## 4.4 However Great the Gift, There Is a Bottom Line

The last three chapters spoke mostly of "loosening," "empty," "not gripping"—all of it from the dismantling side: setting down that tangle of "I, mine, my doing" in the mind.

But dāna-pāramitā also has a side that is built up in the positive. Besides "empty out what should be emptied," there is "grow what should be grown." The *Yogācārabhūmi*—the great treatise on the stages of the practitioner's path—paints, from the front, a complete portrait of the bodhisattva's giving.

### How a Gift Grows Up

The *Yogācārabhūmi* says the bodhisattva's giving has nine facets, and together they form a road of growth—from inner to outer, and back again to inner.

It starts from the innermost place: first, a heart that "holds nothing back, neither self nor possessions"—this is the root of giving, a decision of the will, not a moment's softening of the heart. With this root in place, it grows outward: what can be given grows wider and wider (from things outside the body, to one's own body and life); the harder gifts, too, can be given (having little to begin with, yet enduring poverty to give; even the most beloved thing leaving the hand); and the avenues of giving multiply (one's own wealth, wealth others are persuaded to give, the household's, and giving to whoever comes to ask—all of them open).

Then it gathers inward again, to refine its quality: give with pure faith, give with reverence, give with your own hand, give at the right time, give without wounding the one who receives. Then it spreads outward once more: following beings' needs—food for those short of food, clothes for those short of clothes, shelter for those without shelter, light for those without light—item by item, giving pressed close to people's actual hardships.

And at the last, it returns to the innermost place—"purified giving": washing this giving heart utterly clean.

Look at this road: from the heart, to the conduct, and back to the heart. It begins in a heart that holds nothing back and ends in a heart washed clean. It seems to return to where it started, yet it has walked one great circle and grown into another stature altogether.

### What Clean Giving Looks Like

Then "purified giving," this washed-clean giving heart—what does it look like in the concrete? The *Yogācārabhūmi* lists ten items, and every one of them is a mirror you can hold your own mind up to. Here are a few that cut closest—

Not lingering: given is given; the mind does not stick to the deed, turning it over and over to taste it again.

Not exalting oneself: not letting a little pride of "I am quite something" sprout just because you have given.

Not looking down: not slighting or short-changing the receiver because he is lowly and humble.

Not turning toward and away: not favoring this one and cold-shouldering that one according to who is close and who is distant.

Not hoping for gratitude: not expecting the receiver to turn around and thank me, repay me.

Not wishing for ripened reward: not counting on this gift to trade, someday, for any merit in return.

Read these ten through (only six are given here) and you will find they all speak of one and the same thing, from different faces: **clearing out, corner by corner, the "I" that hides behind the giving.** No sticking, no pride, no slighting, no favoring, no angling for thanks, no fishing for reward—every item is a washing of that "I." This is "the three wheels empty" of the earlier chapters, landed in ten concrete places where you can set your hand, day after day.

### A Line That Can Never Be Crossed

After all this talk of giving "at its greatest," "without edge"—a bodhisattva can give even eyes, even body, even life—the last and weightiest question arrives:

Has this giving, then, no edge at all? Whenever someone asks—whatever they ask, whatever it is for—should one give?

No. Right here, the *Yogācārabhūmi* draws a bottom line that can never be crossed. It says:

> A bodhisattva would sooner, a hundred times, a thousand times, even a hundred thousand times, give up his own body and life in giving to beings, than **ever follow another's command**—to please anyone, to bend to anyone's wishes—and so oppress, harm, or deceive beings against all reason.

Read that sentence slowly. It has two layers.

The first layer: giving may grow without any upper limit—body and life can be given, given a thousand times over.

The second layer is the bottom line itself: and yet, **this giving must never be taken up by anyone as a tool for harming others.** Even if someone comes commanding you in the name of "giving," "devotion," "sacrifice"—directing you to coerce, to injure, to deceive other beings—then however great the letting-go, it stops at once. Sooner give up your life than follow such a command and do harm.

What this line guards is the original heart of giving. The original heart of giving is "loosen what is mine, benefit beings"; the moment it is conscripted and turned around to do harm, it is no longer giving at all—it is evil done in giving's name. So here the bodhisattva yields not an inch: body and life may be given up; this line may not be crossed.

This is a very deep warning: any virtue, however noble—giving, devotion, sacrifice—once captured by power or by cunning, can become a blade that harms. So a truly clean giving keeps the mind loose (the three wheels empty), yet keeps beneath the hand one line cast in iron: never, at anyone's command, harm beings. Loose toward the "I"; hard against the harming. Both are required.

### Part Four, to This Point

Part Four has followed the Mahāyāna and pushed giving forward a whole step.

It has let us see: a gift, with that one touch of seeing-through wisdom added—the three wheels loosened—crosses the river, from ordinary giving into the dāna-pāramitā that can ferry people across (chapter one). Through an eye given away, we saw what "the three wheels empty" is, and saw that "the other shore" is no distant perfection but the end of this one loosening of the hand, this one crossing (chapter two). We then saw that "empty" is not "nothing"—all the more effort in the deed, all the less grip in the mind (chapter three). And last, we saw that this greatest of gifts must grow, on its positive side, all the way into a clean heart—while beneath it runs a line that cannot be crossed (this chapter).

This whole part has been loosening the "I who is giving." Loosened to this point, the work is already deep.

And yet the telling of "the three wheels empty" that carries the most force, the most music—the one that has sunk deepest into people's hearts—is not these treatises of the patriarchs. It is a sūtra. A sūtra of which nearly everyone in this land has heard at least a few lines. It condensed "the three wheels empty" into a single sentence you can probably already recite—"Let the mind arise without abiding anywhere."
