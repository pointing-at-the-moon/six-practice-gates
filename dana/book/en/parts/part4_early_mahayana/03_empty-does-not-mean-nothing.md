## 4.3 Empty Does Not Mean Nothing

The last chapter spoke of "empty" the whole way through—the giver empty, the receiver empty, the gift empty, the three wheels empty. Hearing this, a question will likely rise in the mind, even a touch of unease:

Doesn't giving then come to nothing? If there is no real "I" giving, no real "thing," no real "person" receiving—why give at all? Hasn't this negated giving itself?

That thought is one of the easiest pits to step into on the whole path. This chapter exists to make it clear: **"empty" has never meant "nothing."**

### It Empties the Grip, Not the Existence

First, take the true measure of this word "empty."

When the Dharma says giving is "empty," what gets emptied out is not "the act of giving," but "the notion that the act of giving has an iron-cast, grippable self of its own."

Take that piece of cloth from the last chapter again. To say "the cloth is empty" is not to say "there is no cloth"—the cloth is plainly in your hand; you can touch it, you can give it away. To say it is "empty" is to say: this cloth holds no "cloth" that could stand on its own apart from the threads, apart from the weaving, apart from all its conditions. It is the shape of many conditions met together—gathered, it is there; scattered, it is gone. It exists—in that way.

The Dharma gives this kind of "existing" a name: "conventional existence"—existing by way of a borrowed name. The "borrowed" does not mean false or deceiving; it means simply on loan. The cloth exists conventionally: it is really and truly there—usable, givable, a benefit to people—only it does not exist as "cast in iron, there all by itself."

So "empty" and "exists" are not in the least at war here. The giving is still really and truly done—the rice goes out, the hungry are fed; all of that is real, useful, of benefit to people. Only, in the middle of it, there is no dead-gripped "I," "he," "thing." **Do what should be done; grip nothing that should not be gripped**—that is what "the three wheels empty" really means. It is not telling you to stop giving. Quite the opposite: it is telling you to give **more cleanly**.

### What the Prajñā Sūtra Itself Says

For this posture—doing as before, gripping nothing—the *Great Prajñā Sūtra* itself has one sentence, and it says it most beautifully:

> "Although they practice giving, they do not grasp at what is given."

"Although they practice giving"—the giving goes on being done, not one bit less. "They do not grasp at what is given"—yet in the mind there is no clinging to "I have let go of something." The hand gives, and the mind retains no trace of "what I have given away."

The same passage continues, line after line, in exactly this key: "although they practice morality, they do not grasp at the mark of morality; although they practice patience, their mind does not cease…"—the precepts are kept, but without hanging up the mark "I am keeping precepts"; patience is practiced, but the mind keeps no knot. Every practice takes this same posture: **the deed, done full and complete; the mind, clean, retaining nothing.**

And please notice one thing: this sentence—"although they practice giving, they do not grasp at what is given"—was spoken by the Buddha Himself, in the sūtra. "The three wheels empty" is not some novelty a later patriarch dreamed up out of thin air—what the patriarchs did (Nāgārjuna, who composed the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra*, among them) was to take this sentence of the Buddha's from the prajñā sūtras and open it out, carefully and thoroughly. The source is the Buddha's own lips.

### Should You Still Choose Whom to Give To?

At "the three wheels empty," someone will ask again: if the receiver too is "empty," and giving to anyone is equally empty, then was the "choosing of the field of merit" back in Part Two—picking out those worthy of offering—all said for nothing? If it is all empty anyway, why not just give to whoever happens by?

This too is thinking gone crooked.

"The three wheels empty" is **view**—the eye with which you see the matter, the non-clinging within the mind. "Choosing the field of merit" is **conduct**—the wisdom in the doing, the arrangement in the hand. These two not only do not quarrel; they must walk together. In the mind, no gripping of "I gave to someone magnificent"; in the hand, the gift still placed where it can truly bear benefit. Loose in the seeing, unmuddled in the doing.

It is just as Kāḷigodhā said all those years ago: she shared "with those of virtue, those of goodness"—her mind was loose (no stinginess, no angling for return), and her hand was clear (giving to the right place). Loose does not mean muddled; empty does not mean anything-goes. One who truly understands "empty" acts, if anything, with more clarity than anyone—because the calculations of "I must look generous," "how much merit will this trade for" are gone from his mind, his eye sees truer, and his hand sets things down steadier.

### Falling Off at Neither End

So this chapter has been describing a "middle way"—walking between two pits.

The pit on one side is "too solid"—gripping "I, the other, the thing" until giving becomes the bookkeeping of "I have done a great good deed." This is what Parts One through Three were loosening all along. The pit on the other side is "too empty"—hearing "the three wheels empty" and sliding into the nihilism of "it is all empty anyway; giving means nothing, do it or don't."

The Dharma wants neither end. What it wants is this: **the giving goes on—done more diligently, more widely, more cleanly—while in the mind, those three wheels are loosened utterly clean.** Full in the deed, empty in the mind. Only with both at once have you truly understood dāna-pāramitā.

"Empty" has never told you to drop your hands and quit. It tells you: when you reach out your hand, gently set down that tangle of "I, mine, my doing" inside. The hand busier, the mind more at ease—that is its true intent.

### Walking On

With "empty does not mean nothing" made clear, the posture of this giving stands firm: full effort in the deed, no grip in the mind.

But this giving—so vast, so empty—has it then no edge at all, nothing it will not do? A bodhisattva can give even body, even life—does that mean that whenever someone asks, whatever they ask, whatever it is for, one should give?

The next chapter looks at how this seemingly boundless giving has, in fact, a bottom line that can never be crossed.
