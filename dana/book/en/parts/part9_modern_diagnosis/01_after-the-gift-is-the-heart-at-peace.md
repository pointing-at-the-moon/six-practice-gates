## 9.1 After the Gift, Is the Heart at Peace?

Having walked the long road from the Pāli to the Huayan, we come back to our own age—and meet something faintly ironic.

Today the chances to do good, to donate, to serve, are more plentiful than at any time before. One tap on the phone and the donation is made; fundraisers stand on every street; appeals of every kind arrive at year's end. By rights, this is a good thing. And yet, in so many places, the act of giving is being quietly made back into a piece of **business**—

Merit is made in order to trade for blessings, like depositing money into an invisible account. Donations are made for the naming rights, for being seen, for that little reputation of "I am a good person." Even giving itself gets worked over for its "return on investment."

And this is exactly the knot this book has been loosening for us since its first page. Part One said it already: the truth of giving lies not in "how much you give, what you get for it," but in "whether that 'mine' in the heart can be loosened."

The trouble is, this bargaining mind hides deep. It often wears the fine mask of "doing good," and fools even us. We are plainly doing a good deed—and underneath, quietly reckoning the return, minding the reputation, savoring that small satisfaction of "I'm really rather good." **Then how do I tell—is this gift of mine a true opening of the hand, or a transaction in disguise?**

The patriarchs of the generations left a touchstone—very plain, and very accurate.

### A Touchstone: After Giving, Is the Heart Settled, or Restless?

The Dharma teaches that the true joy in a practitioner's heart—*pīti*—carries one telltale mark: it **sinks downward, moves toward rest**.

The sūtras describe it in fine detail: when that pure gladness truly arises in a person, it does not stop at excitement and agitation; it goes one step further down, and turns into a resting of body and mind—the person loosens, quiets, settles. The masters of old called this resting *passaddhi*, "tranquillity"—the body light, the mind at ease. True joy always draws forth tranquillity. Joy first, rest after, like water reaching the channel and flowing downward of its own accord.

And the other way around—a "joy" with bargaining mixed in?

That gladness of "I did a good deed, I banked some merit, people will praise me" is another thing altogether. It stops at excitement; it cannot sink. After the gift, the heart is not quieter but more restless—minding: has this merit been recorded? When does the return arrive? Did anyone see me do it? That "joy" is an upward-floating exhilaration, not a downward-settling rest. It cannot give birth to tranquillity.

And so the touchstone is clear:

**After you have given, grow still and feel for yourself—is the heart settled, or restless?**

If it has loosened, settled, stopped hanging on—then most likely the hand truly opened. If it is still minding, still reckoning, still waiting for something to come back—then under this gift, the odds are, a deal is still being clenched.

This touchstone asks no deep learning; anyone can use it on the spot. What it shows is not how much you gave, but the shape of the heart after the giving.

### The Patriarchs Saw Through This Sickness Long Ago

This bargaining mind is no new sickness of the modern age. The patriarchs of the generations saw clean through it long ago, and handed down heavy verdicts.

Master Shandao of the Pure Land school put it most severely. If a person practices and does good while seeking fame, seeking gain, seeking reward, he said, then this so-called goodness is called **mixed-poison goodness**—goodness with poison stirred in; it is also called **false-and-empty action**—not true work. The "poison" does not mean the person has done something evil; it means the motive has the poison of bargaining mixed into it.

Master Yongming Yanshou caught the symptom of this sickness at its subtlest. Of *nianfo*—reciting the Buddha's name—he said: "**When *nianfo* is cast into a scattered mind, the scattered mind cannot become Buddha.**" Your mouth is on the Buddha's name, your hands are doing good—but if the mind underneath is scattered, bargaining, floating, then the practice cannot transform that scattered mind. The surface motion and the underlying heart have come apart. And this coming-apart is exactly the "joy that cannot sink" we just saw—the mouth moves, and the heart has not followed.

Master Ouyi, in turn, set out the right road most clearly. To truly be born in the Pure Land, he said, the vow must be "**weariness of Sahā and yearning for Sukhāvatī**"—weary of this world of endurance, yearning for the Land of Bliss—a turning of the whole direction. And he said, with no hedging at all: if what you seek is reward in the human and heavenly realms, or some other advantage, then "**this is then not the vow of birth in that land**"—it is no vow of rebirth at all. The moment the bargaining mind that seeks blessings is mixed in, the vow, on the spot, no longer counts.

### And Yet—a Bargained Gift Is Not Worthless

Having said all this, one most important sentence must be added quickly, lest we swing to the other extreme and strike down everyone who seeks blessings with a single blow.

Here the Dharma reads both ends with one gaze—it does not condemn; it only distinguishes the levels.

The one who gives, who recites the Buddha's name, with a bargaining mind does not come away with zero. Master Ouyi, citing the sūtra, says it plainly: such calling of the name "with a mixed and defiled mind" "**gains only worldly good-reward**"—it still trades for real, solid worldly reward—only "**not the vast subtle benefit**." And Master Yongming, also citing the sūtra: even one who recites the Buddha-name with a distracted mind—"**the merit thus earned will not be exhausted**."

So a gift that seeks blessings still has its blessing; it is still a good deed, still trades for good reward in the human and heavenly realms. The error is not in seeking blessings as such—in the Dharma, that is a real step on the staircase, not a false one. The error lies only in **taking this one step for the whole, for the final word**—thinking giving ends here, not knowing that beyond it runs a whole deeper, wider road: loosening "mine," losing the "I," crossing over all suffering and distress.

And this is how the touchstone is used: not to convict anyone, but to hold up a mirror to yourself. If the mirror shows that this gift left the heart restless—no shame, no self-reproach; it only means you are still standing on the step called "blessings." See it clearly, and simply move one small step forward—next time you give, try setting the reckoning down a little, letting the heart sink a little further. The moment the heart sinks, you have moved from the step called "bargaining" toward the step called "the hand opening."

### Walking On

Having looked into this mirror, we turn next to something very new—something many people wonder about.

Today, the forms of giving have changed. Once, giving meant placing a thing into another's hands with your own. Now, a light touch on the phone—five minutes, a single tap—and the money crosses a thousand mountains and rivers to someone you have never met. Does such "one-tap giving" count as true giving? Could it be too light, too fast, too effortless—so effortless it doesn't count?

In the next chapter, we see how the Dharma answers this very modern question.
