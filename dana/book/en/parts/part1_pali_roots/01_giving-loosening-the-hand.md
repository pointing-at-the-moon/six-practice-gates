## 1.1 Giving: Loosening the Hand

The last of something is left in the house—the last bowl of rice, the last piece of bread. And just then someone comes to the door, asking. Inside you, isn't there a slight catch?

That catch is short, perhaps less than a second. Before the hand reaches out, the heart contracts first. That small inward contraction is not evil; it is simply human. But the Dhamma has a special name for this contraction, an account of where it comes from, and a remedy for it. It is called stinginess.

The "giving" this whole book is about comes down, in the end, to dealing with that flinch.

### Not "How Much You Give" but "Whether You Loosen"

First, one thing must be set straight.

When we ordinarily speak of giving, our eyes are mostly fixed on the act of giving—how much was given, to whom, whether anyone knew of it afterward. All of these are visible, and all of them matter. But when the Buddha taught this matter, the weight was never really on the hand reaching out. It was on the loosening in the heart, before the hand ever reached out.

In the Pāli texts that record the Buddha's teaching, more than one word is used for this matter of giving. One word governs the act of giving itself—placing a thing into another's hands. This is giving (*dāna*). The other word governs what lies beneath that act, the loosening in the heart—the mind no longer gripping "mine" and refusing to let go. This is letting-go (*cāga*).

Giving can be seen; letting-go cannot.

A person can perform a very large act of giving while the letting-go inside has not moved at all—out of face, out of repaying a favor, out of fear, he hands the money over; the gesture is handsome, and the "mine" in his heart has not loosened by a hair. And there are those who share out only half a bowl of rice, yet the hand inside has truly come open. When the Dhamma looks at these two people, it looks at the layer underneath.

This is why this book is not a history of giving, not a story of humanity growing ever more generous. It is about how a hand comes open—and how much that hand is holding has never been the point.

### How the Buddha Spoke of This

This center of weight, the Buddha stated very plainly.

There is a passage in the *Itivuttaka* where the Buddha says:

> "Mendicants, if beings only knew, as I know, the fruit of giving and sharing, they would not eat without first giving… Even their last morsel they would not eat without sharing, so long as someone was there to receive it."

Notice where this saying lands. The Buddha did not say "give a great deal," nor "give to renowned recipients." What He spoke of was the last morsel—when this little is all you have left, when keeping it is just enough for yourself and giving it away means going hungry, can the hand still come open? This is the touchstone. A person who can loosen his hand at the last morsel—his letting-go is real. Giving away the spare ends of one's surplus, ornament upon plenty, tests nothing at all.

The Buddha also left a passage to be said silently in the heart when one looks back on one's own letting-go:

> "I am so fortunate, so very fortunate. Among people whose minds are full of the stain of stinginess, I live at home with a mind freed from that stain—with letting-go loosened, with hand washed clean, delighting in relinquishment, responsive to those who request, delighting in giving and sharing."

These lines do not paint the portrait of "a charitable person." They show where a mind that has been trained ordinarily rests. "With letting-go loosened" means the mind no longer grips "mine" to the death. "With hand washed clean" means the hand stands ready at any moment to give. "Delighting in relinquishment" means the mind's default is no longer "keep" but "loose." "Responsive to those who request" means that toward another's asking, the heart stands open. "Delighting in giving and sharing" means that in this loosening and giving there is gladness, not compulsion.

Put the five together, and you have the everyday look of a mind whose hand has come open.

### What Stinginess Actually Is

Then what, exactly, is this stinginess that works against the loosening?

It is not merely the small emotion of "I can't bear to part with it." The treatises define it with precision: stinginess is the mind's inability to allow "what is mine" to become "mine, and also another's." What it catches on is not the amount of money but the boundary line of "mine"—and the moment that line is touched, the heart contracts.

So you will see a strange thing: a person who can hand over ten dollars without blinking, yet cannot allow someone else to sit in his chair. The ten dollars never touched his line of "mine"; the chair touched it. The switch of stinginess is "mine" being challenged, not how costly the thing is.

And for just this reason, the remedy for stinginess is not "force yourself to donate more." Giving that is forced often only hides the stinginess in a new costume. The true remedy is to grow a contrary habit in the heart—and that habit is letting-go. Let the mind see, one time after another, the very moment its hand comes loose; let those moments accumulate in the mind, and the binding of stinginess on the heart loosens, inch by inch. This is work done on the heart, not work done on a ledger.

### One More Layer: Offering

Besides giving and letting-go, the Pāli texts preserve a third word with a register of its own—offering (*yāga*).

In the age when the Buddha taught, India already had a very old culture of sacrifice. People made offerings to the gods—for timely wind and rain, for offspring, for the life to come. The Buddha's teaching arose in that soil, and it did not cut the word "offering" away with one stroke. It took the word over and changed its direction—the gesture remained the same gesture of respectful presenting, but the one presented to was changed: from the gods in the heavens to the field of merit—the Buddha, the Dhamma, the Saṅgha, and virtuous good people. The purpose changed too—no longer the seeking of this world's benefits, but the planting of a cause that tends toward liberation.

So the word "offering" carried, from the very beginning, a measure of reverence and a measure of solemnity. It is not the cold handing-over of a thing; it is presenting it with respect. This layer will open out in full when we come, much later, to Samantabhadra's vast cultivation of offerings in the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*, and become the far end of this long road. For now, only remember this: within giving there has always been the reverence of offering.

Set the three side by side, and a complete act of giving is in fact three layers moving at once: first the loosening of letting-go in the heart, then the giving of the hand, and that giving carrying with it the reverence of offering. Loosening, giving, reverence—only with the three layers together do you have the full shape of what the Buddha meant, back then, by giving.

The layer most easily missed is letting-go—because it is the least visible. Money can be counted; reverence can be seen; only that loosening in the heart is invisible to others, and even you yourself, if not attentive, will miss it. And yet that is precisely the layer that is the heart of the whole matter.

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

Having come this far, there is one thing to see clearly, lest the first step go crooked.

The world often treats giving as a transaction: I donate so much now, to draw back so much blessing later—like depositing money into an invisible account, waiting to withdraw it with interest. The thought is natural, and very common. But it gets the direction of giving entirely backward.

The giving the Buddha taught faces not toward "what I can get back later" but toward "what I can loosen now." That hand clenched around "mine"—what it grips is not only property. It grips an "I," a habit of drawing thing after thing inside the boundary of "mine." And this habit is the very root that keeps a person turning, without rest, through birth and death. Giving is precious because it is one of the few practices that goes straight to that root and loosens it.

In other words, giving is not "do a good deed and wait for the reward." It is "loosen the hand a little, lose a little of the I." As for blessings—will they come? They will—but they fall as something shed along the way when the hand loosens, not as the purpose of loosening it. Just as the soles of your shoes wear down when you walk, yet no one walks in order to wear down their soles. Fix your eyes on the blessings, and this road becomes impossible to walk; set your heart on the loosening, and the road opens of itself.

This principle—that the real substance of giving lies in the loosening and the losing of "I," not in the giving and not in the exchange—is the spine of this entire book. In the twenty-eight chapters ahead, the objects grow larger chapter by chapter and the methods finer chapter by chapter, but underneath, the question being asked is always the same one: has that hand come open?

### Walking On

How much, then, does this loosening matter?

It matters this much—the Buddha listed it among the marks of a person who has "stepped into the stream of liberation." Whether someone has truly entered the way, truly set out on the road: one of the signs looked for is how far this hand of his has loosened. That may sound surprising: how can letting-go stand in the same position as keeping virtue, as wisdom, and serve as a mark of realization?
