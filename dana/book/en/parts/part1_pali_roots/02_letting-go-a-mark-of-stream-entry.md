## 1.2 Letting Go: A Mark of Stream-Entry

Among the Sakyan clan there was a lay disciple named Mahānāma. He was the head of a great household, and he lived in the city of Kapilavatthu. The city was crowded, its streets thick with carts and horses, and once, on his way home, he was nearly run down by a maddened elephant and a horse broken loose. After that fright, a very practical thought rose in him: if some day I die in the street just like that, where will I go?

He brought this worry before the Buddha.

The Buddha's answer contained not one empty word.

### What Has Long Perfumed the Mind Will Float Up

The Buddha told him: if a person, over long years, lets five things perfume their way into the mind—faith, virtue, learning, letting-go, wisdom—then even though his body be eaten by crows and hawks and dogs and crumble to dust, his mind will rise, tending toward a good destination.

The Buddha also gave a simile:

> "It is just as if a man were to sink a jar full of ghee into a deep pool and break it: the shards and gravel of the jar would sink down, but the ghee inside would rise, floating up to the surface."

The body is like the jar's shards—broken, sunk. But what has been perfumed into the mind over long years is like the ghee: it rises of itself. What Mahānāma had to do was not worry over the moment of death, but, while still alive, let these five things steep into his mind, little by little.

Notice that among these five, fourth in line, stands letting-go—the same loosening hand of the last chapter. It stands side by side with faith (confidence in the Three Jewels), with virtue (conduct that holds), with learning (hearing the true Dhamma), and with wisdom (the seeing that sees through)—one of the five roots that perfume the mind. Letting-go is not an optional good deed. It is the backbone of the mind's nurture.

### The Marks of Stream-Entry Can Be Seen from Three Angles

When the Buddha spoke of what counts as having stepped into the stream of liberation, He would often list four marks. The first three are fixed: unshakable confidence in the Buddha, in the Dhamma, in the Saṅgha. The fourth, on different occasions, He filled with different qualities.

In the *Saṃyutta Nikāya* there are three discourses standing one after another. Their openings are identical to the word; the first three marks are identical to the word; only that fourth one changes, three times over: the first discourse fills it with virtue, the second with letting-go, the third with wisdom.

This is not the texts in disarray. This is the Buddha deliberately making the fourth mark a window that can be opened from three directions.

From the direction of virtue: a person who has entered the stream has conduct that holds—unbroken, without leaks. From the direction of letting-go: the hand inside him is loose; it no longer grips "mine" to the death. From the direction of wisdom: he sees through arising and passing away and is no longer deceived by surfaces. Virtue, letting-go, wisdom—these are three sides of one and the same person. In teaching, one may cut in from whichever side suits the listener's capacity; but in a person who has truly entered the stream, all three arise together. Someone whose mind has already loosened around "mine" will not still be leaking in his conduct, nor will his eyes still be shut.

What matters is this: letting-go can stand in that fourth position, on equal footing with virtue and with wisdom. It is not a minor item filed under keeping virtue, nor a good deed done in passing. It carries the kind of weight that qualifies it to mark "this person is already on the road."

### A Laywoman Who Proved It to the Buddha Herself

Doctrine alone still stands at one remove. In the Pāli texts there is a passage where a real person took this very item—letting-go—and confirmed her own attainment before the Buddha, face to face.

In Kapilavatthu there was a Sakyan laywoman named Kāḷigodhā. The Buddha came to her house to receive an offering of food and taught her, in person, these four marks of stream-entry—and for the fourth He used precisely letting-go. When she had heard it, she did not merely nod and receive the instruction. She turned around and reported to the Buddha her own actual condition.

These four things, she said, are present in me; I can see them and verify them for myself. The first three—confidence in the Buddha, in the Dhamma, in the Saṅgha—she stated one by one. When she came to the fourth, letting-go, she did not repeat the formula the Buddha had taught her. She put it in the words of her own daily life:

> "In our household, whatever is a fit thing to be given, all of it, without any discrimination, is shared with the virtuous and those of good qualities."

These words carry great weight. She had not learned a term; she had lived letting-go into her own rice jar, her own cupboard, every meal of her house. Only someone who has truly done it can translate it like that.

And there is one fine detail worth noticing. She said "shared with the virtuous and those of good qualities"—she was not giving to all comers, handing things to whoever appeared. This tells us: true letting-go is not stinginess flipped inside out, scattering things without head or tail. It is that once the mind has loosened around "mine," it sees more clearly, not less—placing things, with full clarity, where they ought to go. Loose does not mean muddled; loosened, the eyes grow brighter.

When the Buddha had heard her, He answered with only three sentences:

> "It is a gain for you, Godhā, a great gain for you, Godhā. You have declared the fruit of stream-entry yourself."

Two sentences of praise, one of confirmation. The Buddha added nothing, corrected nothing, did not say "you have left out such-and-such." A laywoman, using this one item of letting-go alone, declared her own fruit herself, and the Buddha acknowledged it on the spot. This is the most living, most human proof there is that letting-go can serve as a mark of stream-entry.

### Why Letting-Go, of All Things

Here a question may be asked: there are so many wholesome qualities of mind—patience, energy, compassion, mindfulness—why did the Buddha pick out letting-go, of all things, to set among the marks of stream-entry? Is patience not good? Is compassion not good?

They are all good. But each governs its own territory, and what they govern is mostly downstream.

As for the root that keeps a person turning without rest through birth and death, the Dhamma sees it with precision: it is "mine"—the habit, working at every moment, of drawing things inside the boundary of "mine" and then guarding them to the death. The instant "mine" is touched, the heart contracts; that contraction is stinginess. One "mine" after another builds up a solid "I," and that "I" pushes a person on, turning life after life.

Patience handles the unevenness of emotion—but where does the emotion come from? Mostly from "mine" being touched. Compassion handles the heart we turn toward others—but that heart still reaches from the "I" on this end toward the "other" on that end. Mindfulness handles present awareness—but the objects of that awareness are still "my" body, "my" feelings, "my" mind. All of these are good; all are work on the path. But all of them labor above the structure of "mine."

Only letting-go goes straight in to loosen the root. "With letting-go loosened"—the words say literally this: take the taut boundary line of "mine," and loosen it. The other wholesome qualities circle around the "I"; letting-go reaches out its hand and touches "mine" itself.

So when the Buddha set letting-go among the marks of stream-entry, it was no casual placement. If a person has truly loosened, even once, at this root of "mine"—and that one loosening cannot be undone—then he has truly stepped into the stream of liberation. Stream-entry, in the end, is the first crack opening in that root, a crack that will not close again. Letting-go is the name that lies closest to that crack.

### The Same Gift, Two Roads

One more thing, easily confused, must be told apart here. The same act of giving something away can be walking, underneath, two entirely different roads.

Elsewhere the Buddha taught: a person who gives lavishly and keeps virtue well, yet does no work on the mind, can gain better and better rebirths—human blessings, then heavenly blessings, rising layer upon layer, as high as the topmost heaven. That is already splendid. But look carefully: however high, it is still inside the heavens. When the blessings are spent, one falls back down. This road is a circle—you put in merit, you draw out good results, you spend the results, and you are back where you began. The wheel still turns; it is only turning on a more comfortable track.

The other road does not raise the level of your next life. It does something else—it pulls open a gap in the circle itself. And this road rests not on "giving much" but on "mine" coming loose.

What matters is this: on these two roads, the action can be exactly the same—the same handing over of goods, the same amount, the same recipient. The difference is not in the hand; it is in the heart. A heart fixed on "what can I get back" walks the first road, the circle; a heart that has simply "loosened a little of mine" walks the second, the opening. So when the last chapter said giving is not a transaction, it was not because transactions are despised—seeking good results and receiving good results is real in the Dhamma, not false—but because inside that circle alone, no gift, however devout, ever comes back around to liberation itself. The road the Buddha wants to lead people along is the one with the opening in it.

### Having the Marks Is Not Being Home

One last point—the most important, and the most easily overlooked.

Even when a person has all four marks—including this letting-go—he is not yet home. The Buddha said it very clearly: those who have the marks divide into two kinds. One kind, having gained these marks, is satisfied—"I have enough now"—and no longer applies effort by day or sits quietly by night. In such a person, the chain that should arise in the mind—first gladness; from gladness, rapture; from rapture, tranquility of body and mind; from tranquility, bliss; from bliss, concentration; from concentration, the dhammas appearing before one—that chain does not start. The marks are there; the chain stands still.

The other kind gains the marks and refuses to stop—working on as before, heedful as before. Only in such a person does the chain begin to move, link by link: gladness arises, rapture arises, tranquility comes, bliss is gained, the mind concentrates, the dhammas become manifest.

The same holds for letting-go. That the mind has loosened once does not make you, from then on, "a person who delights in giving." Letting-go is not a plaque hung on the wall; it is work that must be done anew each day. Today this hand comes open; tomorrow, facing a new "mine," it tightens again—so tomorrow it must be loosened again. Every act of giving starts this mechanism running, one more time.

So letting-go is not a medal you stockpile until you can rest easy. It is a craft to be practiced every day.

### Walking On

If letting-go matters this much—a root that perfumes the mind, a mark of stream-entry, the one hand that goes straight in to loosen the root of birth and death—then the next question asks itself: how, exactly, is this letting-go to be practiced?

Knowing it matters, with nowhere for the hand to take hold, you still cannot bring your strength to bear. The Buddha in fact left a very concrete, very workable method—turn around, and look at what you have given.

In the next chapter we look at this method: how to take letting-go as a lesson you can look back on every day, and tend every day.
