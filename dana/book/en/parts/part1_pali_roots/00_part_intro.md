# Part One · Pāli Roots · The First Loosening of the Hand

This book is going to walk a long road—from the plainest letting-go the Buddha taught His lay disciples in person while He was in the world, all the way to that dharma-realm offering in the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* which gathers in all things. Along the way we will pass through the eight kinds of giving in the Āgamas, through the field of merit in the Vinaya, through the *Diamond Sūtra*'s three wheels—giver, receiver, gift—all empty, through Samantabhadra's vast cultivation of offerings. The objects will grow ever larger, the ritual forms ever finer, the language ever more solemn. Yet beneath all of it, the same one thing is being walked: loosening, inch by inch, the hand clenched around "mine."

The starting point is in none of those solemn places. The starting point is somewhere earlier, and plainer.

When most people today hear "giving," what comes to mind is donating money, making merit, trading for blessings in return. All of these came later. Before any of them, the Buddha taught one layman at His feet, a man busy with worldly affairs, something simpler and deeper—the weight of this matter has never been on "how much was given," but on whether that "mine" in the heart can come loose.

What Part One does is return to this root.

Three chapters, laid out in order. Chapter one: see clearly what giving is—its real substance lies not in the hand that gives, but in the loosening of the heart. Chapter two: see how much this loosening matters—it is one of the marks of a person stepping into the stream of liberation, standing shoulder to shoulder with virtue and with wisdom. Chapter three: see how to take this loosening and practice it—looking back on what you have given, letting the mind soften along it, one time after another.

Read these three chapters through, and beneath all the seemingly ever-more-elaborate offerings of the twenty-eight chapters that follow, the lines of one same hand will show—the hand that is coming open.

The foundation must be driven firm first. We begin with chapter one.
