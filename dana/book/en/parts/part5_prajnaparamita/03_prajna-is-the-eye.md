## 5.3 Prajñā Is the Eye

The *Heart Sūtra* is so short it never once mentions giving. Yet among the prajñā sūtras stands the longest of them all—the *Great Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra*, a full six hundred fascicles. And it is the *Heart Sūtra*'s exact opposite: from end to end it says "giving" again and again, urging and urging, all but tireless.

One never breathes the word; one repeats it without rest. Are these two sūtras quarreling, or are they the same thing?

### Giving Is the First Step of the Whole Road

First, look at where the six hundred fascicles place giving.

There is a passage in the *Great Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* that draws a road rising layer by layer from the first step of giving. The Buddha says:

> "This bodhisattva, because of giving, takes up and keeps the precepts; because of giving and precept-keeping, gains concentration; because of giving, precepts, and concentration, gains wisdom; because of giving, precepts, concentration, and wisdom, gains liberation…"

Look at where this road begins—not at prajñā, not at concentration, but at **giving**. Layer upon layer: giving lays the base; precept-keeping is built on top of it; concentration on the first two; wisdom on the first three; liberation on the first four. Giving is the first step on this whole long stairway to liberation.

This joins hands, across the distance, with what we saw earlier—giving, virtue, cultivation: begin with the hand. Giving is the door anyone can enter, the first step that most deserves to be taken first. The six hundred fascicles of the *Great Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* spell out the weight of this first step in full.

But notice how the passage ends. The Buddha goes straight on to say:

> "This bodhisattva, though he can practice layer upon layer like this and climb step by step, has, toward all of it, 'no attainment.' Why? Because no dharma has a fixed self of its own."

The climbing, layer by layer, is real practice; and at the same time, "no attainment." It is not "practice enough first, then turn to emptiness." It is "practicing while staying loose—never clenching." Giving still given, precepts still kept, concentration still cultivated—but inside, the clench of "how much have I practiced, how much have I gained" is empty. This is what the six hundred fascicles say over and over: **giving is to be done "with no attainment as the expedient means."**

### Again and Again: "With No Attainment, Practice Giving"

Through the six hundred fascicles, one sentence appears again and again, in changing dress:

> "If a bodhisattva, with no attainment as the expedient means, practices the pāramitā of giving, understanding that giver, receiver, and gift are all unobtainable—such giving, skillful in its means, can bring to fullness the six pāramitās: giving, precepts, patience, vigor, concentration, and prajñā."

"With no attainment as the expedient means"—"no attainment" is not a result that giving eventually produces; it is the key that giving must hold in its hand from the very start. Giver, receiver, gift: all three "unobtainable" (which is to say, the three wheels loosened through, as before). Give with such a mind, and this one act of giving can "bring the six pāramitās to fullness"—from giving alone, the whole bodhisattva path is drawn out.

You see, the six hundred fascicles of the *Great Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* are not one long exercise in demolition, in "empty, empty." With one breath it says "all dharmas are unobtainable"; with the next it pleads, patiently, earnestly: "you should practice giving," "you should practice giving"—again and again.

Is that a contradiction? It is not. What it is saying is exactly this: **precisely because no dharma has a fixed self of its own (emptiness), giving is not trapped inside countable worldly reward, and can become the immeasurable pāramitā of giving that carries beings across.** Emptiness was never a reason not to give—emptiness is the very condition that lets giving break free of its boundaries and become immeasurable. So the more the six hundred fascicles speak of emptiness, the more diligently they urge you: go and give.

### Prajñā Is the Eye; the Five Pāramitās Are the Body

Then within these six, what seat does prajñā actually hold? The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra* gives three superb metaphors:

> "The five pāramitās are like the blind; prajñā is like the eye. The five pāramitās are like an unfired clay jar (pour in water and it dissolves); prajñā is like a jar fired through (it holds the water). The five pāramitās are like a bird without wings; prajñā is like a bird that has grown its wings."

Three metaphors, one point: the first five—giving, precepts, patience, vigor, concentration—without prajñā, are a blind man without eyes, a clay jar that cannot hold water, a bird that cannot fly. **Prajñā is not a sixth item standing in line beside the other five; prajñā is the pair of eyes that lets the five see the road, hold what they carry, and rise into flight.**

And this unties the *Heart Sūtra*'s silence completely. The *Heart Sūtra* names only the eye—prajñā—because once the eye is named, the blind man is blind no longer, and the body (the first five pāramitās) can move, can walk. It need not go on to speak of hands and feet. The six hundred fascicles of the *Great Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra*, meanwhile, take that body now possessed of eyes and act the whole of it out for you, head to toe, one movement at a time.

One names the eye (the brief); one acts out the whole body (the broad)—it is the same seeing person.

### The Broad and the Brief: One and the Same Prajñā

Master Fazang of the Tang put this layer most clearly. He said:

> "Set it all out matter by matter, in full detail, and the words run past two hundred thousand verses (that is the six hundred fascicles of the *Great Prajñāpāramitā*); draw out only what is most pivotal, and the principle is complete in fourteen lines (that is the *Heart Sūtra*)."

Two hundred thousand verses, and fourteen lines. Six hundred fascicles, and two minutes. These are not two sūtras at war; they are the same prajñā, facing different people in different seasons—one speaking in full (the broad), one speaking in essence (the brief).

For someone just setting out, who needs to be led step by step, there is the broad of the six hundred fascicles—starting from giving, holding his arm, climbing one step at a time. For someone already quick, who takes the point at a touch, the fourteen-line brief of the *Heart Sūtra* is enough—emptiness laid bare directly, nothing elaborate. Broad and brief, neither higher nor lower: different medicine, given to different people.

### Part Five, to This Point

Here the three sūtras of Part Five flow together into one complete picture.

The *Diamond Sūtra* tells us how to give—without abiding in marks, abiding in nothing. The *Heart Sūtra* tells us why giving is possible at all—it rests on that emptiness—and lets the word "crossing over," the crossing of "crossed over all suffering and distress," rise to the surface before us. The *Great Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* tells us where giving goes—giving is the first step, prajñā is the eye, and the road leads to all-knowledge.

Taken together, the three are the place where "the three wheels empty" is honed to its sharpest: the hand loosened until it abides in no mark, the mind loosened until it sees the five skandhas are empty—and that loosening is losing the "I"; that loosening is the crossing.

From the Pāli letting-go all the way to here, giving has been loosened to the very bottom in the direction of "I"—loosened until even "I am giving" is empty.

But self-grasping has another face. Everything so far has loosened it from the giving end—handing "mine" away. Self-grasping also has an impulse that runs the opposite way: the urge to **take** what does not belong to me. From the side of "not reaching out to take"—that is, not-taking—the same "I" can be loosened just as well.

In the next Part we turn in a new direction and look, from the reverse side of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, at the other kind of giving hidden inside not-taking.
