## 5.2 The Sūtra That Never Mentions Giving

The *Heart Sūtra* is probably the most widely known sūtra in the Chinese world. Short enough to chant in two minutes, it is a text many people grow up hearing—many can recite the whole of it from memory.

And yet this sūtra, the most distilled teaching of the bodhisattva's prajñā there is, contains, from beginning to end, **not one word about giving.**

Not even a "no giving"—that would at least be a mention, a negation. There is not even the negation; the name itself never appears. Giving is, plainly, the first of the six pāramitās; from the Āgamas to the Mahāyāna it has always stood first in line. Yet when the *Heart Sūtra* condenses the whole bodhisattva path into "no cognition and no attainment," giving—without a sound—is gone.

Does this disappearance matter?

### Not Left Out, but "Spoken All Together"

Master Kuiji of the Tang dynasty gave the most direct answer there is. The *Heart Sūtra*, he said, speaks only of "no cognition and no attainment" and does not list the six pāramitās one by one, because—

> "It is spoken all together."

"Spoken all together"—the six pāramitās, gathered whole, folded into that single line, "no cognition and no attainment."

That is a different thing altogether from leaving something out. Left out means "should have been said, but was forgotten"—like a list missing one item; you just add it back. "Spoken all together" means "one sentence holds the whole"—like a single line that contains an entire book. There is nothing to "add back," because it was all in there from the start.

Kuiji's point is this: for each of the six pāramitās—giving included—to truly become a *pāramitā* (a reaching of the other shore), what is required is one and the same condition: the wisdom of "no attainment." Giving must be with no attainment; keeping the precepts must be with no attainment; patience must be with no attainment… That being so, when the *Heart Sūtra* speaks this "no attainment" straight through, has it not, in one breath, spoken the root of all six?

So the *Heart Sūtra*'s not speaking of giving is not because giving does not matter. Quite the contrary—it does not speak of giving as a thing to do, because it is speaking of the condition under which giving (and every other practice) can stand at all. **Giving without prajñā is merely worldly handing-over; giving with prajñā is the pāramitā of giving that can carry beings across.** What the *Heart Sūtra* means to give you is exactly this root—the root that makes giving giving.

### When the King Comes, the Retinue Has Come Too

The masters also offered a vivid metaphor for this "all together."

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra*—the great treatise on prajñā—says: among all practices, prajñā is the one at the head, the bright guide. And so—

> "It is like a king arriving: his retinue is sure to follow. Mention only the master's name, and the rest are already all obtained."

When a king goes forth, a train of attendants goes with him, always. So you need only say "the king has come," and the whole court of generals and attendants is already contained in that one sentence—no need to call the roll.

The *Heart Sūtra* does exactly this. It names the king—prajñā—and so giving, keeping the precepts, patience… all the retinue that travels with the king have already arrived; there is no need to name them one by one. Say "prajñā," and giving is already in it.

Notice the masters' precision here: prajñā does not *replace* giving; prajñā *completes* giving—it is what turns giving from blind handing-over into giving that has eyes, giving that can reach the other shore. The guide at the head of the road does not need to announce each traveler's name; yet he lights the path under every one of their feet.

### "Crossing Over": The Word This Book Faces

And it is in this shortest of sūtras that the word this whole book has been facing rises to the surface, face on.

The *Heart Sūtra*'s opening line:

> "Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, while practicing deeply prajñāpāramitā, saw that the five skandhas are empty, and **crossed over all suffering and distress**."

Among the six practice gates, what the gate of giving faces is precisely this word: "crossing over"—the crossing of "crossed over all suffering and distress."

Set everything this road has said about loosening the hand against this one word, and it all comes through. From Part One to Part Four, the hand that clenched "mine" was loosened inch by inch; at the *Diamond Sūtra*, loosened to "not abiding in marks"; and here, loosened until even the "I" in "I am giving" is seen through—seen to be "five skandhas, all empty," with no self in there that could be clenched. When the hand is loosened until there is nowhere to abide and nothing to clench, that loosening is the crossing: crossing the river of suffering swollen by self-grasping, over to the other shore.

So the crossing in "crossed over all suffering and distress" is nowhere else: it is right where that hand opens completely. The road of giving, arriving here at the *Heart Sūtra*, joins up with the word "crossing"—giving, it turns out, is itself one way of practicing the crossing. Loosen "mine" and "I am giving" to the very end, and that is "saw that the five skandhas are empty"; that is "crossed over all suffering and distress."

### The Silence Is Itself the Deepest Word

Then why did the *Heart Sūtra* choose to speak of giving by silence, rather than simply say a word about it?

Because sometimes **not saying carries more force than saying.**

Whoever set down the *Heart Sūtra* saw prajñā with utter clarity—no one writes such a sūtra otherwise. And when someone of such clarity, composing the most distilled of all prajñā sūtras, **deliberately does not write of giving**—that not-writing is itself a statement. It is like a master of music composing a piece with only three notes: not because he cannot write a fourth, but because he chose not to.

And there is one more intention folded into this silence: it makes you ask the question yourself. A sūtra on the bodhisattva path—how can it not mention giving? The moment you ask, your mind has moved—and that movement is prajñā waking up in you. Had the *Heart Sūtra* dutifully listed the six pāramitās, you would have taken it as a checklist: six things to do. But it keeps silent—and the silence pushes you back to the root: to ask what it is that makes giving giving. And in that asking you are not being told about prajñā; you have, in person, used prajñā once.

So the *Heart Sūtra*'s silence does not hide giving away. It places giving where it no longer needs to be said—inside the retinue of the king called prajñā, and at the far end of that opening hand where all suffering and distress are crossed.

### Walking On

By its silence, the *Heart Sūtra* gathers giving into prajñā; it tells us that what makes giving giving is that emptiness.

But this raises the opposite question. Among the prajñā sūtras there is also the longest of them all—the *Great Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra*, a full six hundred fascicles. And it is the very opposite of the *Heart Sūtra*: page after page it says "giving," over and over, never tiring of it.

Two prajñā sūtras: one so short it never breathes the word giving, one so long it urges giving again and again. Are they at odds, or are they the same thing? In the next chapter we walk into those six hundred fascicles and see for ourselves.
