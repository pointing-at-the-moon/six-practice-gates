## Transition ⑧ · From the Huayan Dharma-Realm to the Modern Age, and the Coda

Part Eight has walked up to the words "the Dharma itself is giving," and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath—the last one this book will catch.

By now, the long road the book's title names—from Pāli roots to the dharmadhātu—has been walked to its end.

Let us turn back one last time and look over the ground we have covered.

The starting point was that plainest Pāli word, letting-go. A busy householder, in his heart, loosened the hand clenched around "mine"—gently, just a little (Part One). Then the hand reached into the human world—why we give, to whom, what is received here and now (Part Two); it was kept by the Vinaya as a field of merit no one can own privately, able to last a thousand autumns (Part Three); in the Mahāyāna, even the "I who is giving" came loose—loosened to "not abiding in marks," to "seeing that the five skandhas are all empty" (Parts Four and Five); from the reverse side of not-taking, we saw its other face (Part Six); in the *Lotus*, it was pushed to the utmost of offering even the body—and in that fire, what burned was the "I" (Part Seven); and at the last, in the *Huayan*, it was stretched open into an offering that fills the dharma-realm, thought after thought unbroken—stretched until Samantabhadra said it outright: the Dharma itself is giving (Part Eight).

One hand, loosened and loosened again, loosened and loosened again. From one small loosening inside one person's heart, it loosened into the whole dharma-realm.

In principle, this road has reached its peak; there is nowhere higher to go.

And yet—

this resplendent dharma-realm, layer upon layer without end—where, finally, must it land?

It must land back **here**. Back in this room where you and I sit reading at this moment, back in the ordinary day you will live tomorrow, back in this noisy, hurried modern world of ours.

However wide Samantabhadra's dharma-realm, however heroic the Medicine King's fire, however sharp the pain of Śāriputra's eye—if all of it cannot land back in how you, today, loosen that little bit of "mine" in your own hand, then it remains only a fine book read and set down, not a road you have actually walked.

And here, a question is waiting for us.

Our age has more chances to give, to do charity, to donate than any age before it—who knows how many more. And yet in many places it has quietly turned giving back into a **transaction**.

Merit-making, trading for blessings—giving treated as deposits into an invisible account. Naming rights on donations—charity treated as a way to buy a reputation. Calculating the return on investment—benefiting beings run as an optimized business. Even practice, even offering itself, can be packaged into something that makes the "I" higher, better, more worthy of being looked up to.

Do you see it? This is exactly the knot this book has been loosening for us since its first page.

Part One said it already: the true measure of giving lies not in how much is given, not in what is gotten in exchange, but in whether the "mine" in the heart can come loose. Part Two said: giving is not doing a good deed and waiting to collect the reward. Part Four said: even the I in "I am giving" must come loose. Part Six warned: giving can be hijacked into deviant giving, taken and used to fatten that "I."

This whole book, walking from the Pāli to the Huayan, has been saying only one thing: **loosen the hand clenched around "mine"; then loosen the clenching "I" as well.**

And the deepest sickness of our age runs exactly the other way: under all kinds of handsome names (merit, charity, blessings, even "spirituality"), it makes that hand clench tighter—gripping no longer wealth, but "a more impressive me."

So this book cannot end adrift in the resplendent dharma-realm of the Huayan. It must come back down to the ground, back among you and me, and ask, plainly and simply:

having walked this long road from the Pāli to the dharma-realm, what, today, is this hand of ours still gripping? And how does it loosen?

The final part is this book's coda. It invites giving back out of the scrolls and into the world before our eyes—to look at the traps this age has set around giving, and to gather this whole long road into a few of the plainest words, small enough to rest in the palm of your hand.

We turn this last page.
