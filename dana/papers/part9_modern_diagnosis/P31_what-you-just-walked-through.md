## Abstract

With the thirtieth paper concluded, the DĀNA volume comes to a close. The present paper looks back along the volume's seven-stage arc: the Pāli *muttacāga* five-marks formula; the motivational spectrum of the Āgamas; the Vinaya's institutional landing; the early-Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā*; the *prajñāpāramitā*'s three-wheel-empty self-witness; the *Śūraṅgama*'s three reverse layers; the *Lotus Sūtra*'s three forms of body-offering; the *Avataṃsaka*'s five-part Samantabhadra arena; and the modern-diagnosis pair — the *bojjhaṅga* twin-factor instrument and the three-axis grounding of giving in the age of artificial intelligence. Thirty canonical anchors that load-bear each preceding paper's thesis are here re-illuminated in their structural roles within the volume's argument-arc, while the author's prose deliberately restrains its own initiative: in the retrospective, the author steps aside, and the voices of *Shandao*, *Yongming Yanshou*, *Zhixu*, *Yinguang*, *Samantabhadra*, *Nāgārjuna*, and the Pāli canon carry the invitation forward. The seam to Volume Three (*SĪLA*) is not constructed by the author but already laid down in the canon — pre-eminently in the *Bhaiṣajyaguru Sūtra*'s fifth great vow, supplemented by the structural pairing of three-wheel-emptiness and the three-encompassing precepts of the *Bodhisattva-mālā-sūtra*, and by the pre-laid posture of *śīla-pāramitā*'s opening in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle thirteen. The fifth section acknowledges that seam without constructing it. The volume's end indicates the place to begin — and the answer to "where to begin" is given by the Buddha himself in *anupubbi-kathā* (graduated discourse): *dāna-kathā* is always the first step. The volume closes on seven characters from the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, fascicle eleven: *dāna* is the first condition for *nirvāṇa*.

**Keywords:** *muttacāga*, *cāga*-faculty, the giving-arc retrospective, where to begin, *anupubbi-kathā*, *dāna* as the first condition of *nirvāṇa*, three-encompassing precepts, the Bhaiṣajyaguru's fifth vow, volume-seam, three-wheel emptiness, *Samantabhadra*, *Nāgārjuna*, *anumodanā*, Volume Two of the Six Practice Gates

## I. Introduction

When the reader turns to this page, the thirty papers of Volume Two (DĀNA) have already been walked.

From the Pāli *muttacāga* (unsticking-giving) five marks, through the *Āgamas*' motivational spectrum and the *Velāma-sutta*'s "the time of a finger-snap of impermanence-perception" apex; through the institutional landing of the Vinaya's sangha-of-the-four-directions and the establishment of the three-fold giving structure in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle fourteen ("the upholding of precepts as non-harm — this is the giving of fearlessness"); through the early-Mahāyāna assertion that what merely bears the name of giving has not crossed to the far shore, and the *prajñāpāramitā*'s canonical self-witness of three-wheel-emptiness; through the *Śūraṅgama*'s three reverse layers of precept, attainment, and demon; through the *Lotus Sūtra*'s three body-forms of substitution, posture-axiom, and transformation; through the *Avataṃsaka*'s five-part sequence of entry-threshold, vast-cultivation architecture, dharma-offering collection-point, fifty-three-teachers trajectory, and dharma-realm terminus; to the modern diagnosis pair — the *bojjhaṅga* twin-factor instrument and the three-axis *adhipati-pratyaya* grounding for the age of AI — the seven-stage accumulation is now complete.

The present paper has three tasks.

First, to re-illuminate along the arc the thirty canonical anchors that load-bore each preceding paper's thesis, so that the reader sees a spiral rather than a straight line: each later stage revisits the same question from a new face. The *muttacāga*'s "unsticking" quality of *cāga* is re-given by *prajñāpāramitā* as "abiding nowhere"; the first-step status of *dāna-kathā* in the *Āgamas* is expanded by the *Avataṃsaka* into Samantabhadra's autobiographical self-witness that no word, no line of the dharma-sea was obtained except through the relinquishment of cakravartin-thrones. The same path at different altitudes; the same posture seen from successive faces. The connection between stages is not linear but spiral.

Second, the seam between Volume Two (*Dāna*) and Volume Three (*Sīla*) is not constructed by authorial design but already laid down in the canon. The fifth great vow of the *Bhaiṣajyaguru Sūtra* states: "let the *brahmacarya* be cultivated ... the three-encompassing precepts be completely held" (T0450 p.0405a23). One canonical line threads Volume Three's three load-bearing chapters — the three-encompassing precepts (Part IV), the precept-substance question (Part V), and the *Bhaiṣajyaguru* principal-arena (Part VI) — into a single sequence. Coupled with the structural pairing of the three-encompassing precepts in fascicle two of the *Bodhisattva-mālā-sūtra* and the pre-laid opening posture of *śīla-pāramitā* in fascicle thirteen of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, the inter-volume seam is already in the canon. The fifth section of the present paper acknowledges that seam; it does not construct it.

Third, the volume's end indicates the place to begin — and the answer to "where to begin" is not in the author's invitation but in the Buddha's *anupubbi-kathā* (graduated discourse): "the graduated teaching — the discourse on giving, the discourse on virtue, the discourse on heavens" (*Dīrgha-āgama*, fascicle twenty, T0001; Vin Mv I 22). When the Buddha addresses those of newly-aroused aspiration, his teaching invariably begins with *dāna-kathā*; only thereafter come *sīla*, the heavens, and the renunciation that exits desire. This is not a pedagogical device — it is the structure of the path.

The register of this paper accordingly differs somewhat from the doctrinal unfolding of the preceding thirty. In those papers the author set out theses, deployed evidence, addressed objections, and brought traditions into conversation. In this paper, the author steps aside in the retrospective and lets the voices of the canon itself carry the load — *Shandao*, *Yongming Yanshou*, *Zhixu*, *Yinguang*, *Samantabhadra*, *Nāgārjuna*, and the Pāli canon's invitations are allowed to arrive in turn. The reader who has completed thirty papers does not need each paper's argument re-rehearsed; each anchor's load-bearing original speaks for itself, and the author offers only a single sentence on its structural role within the volume's argument-arc.

## II. Looking Back (Part I): From the Pāli Roots to Prajñāpāramitā

The volume begins in the Pāli.

### Part I — Pāli Roots: Three Faces of the Five-Marks Formula

Three papers share one anchor — the *muttacāga* five-marks formula — and each cuts at a different face.

**P01** cuts at the semantic face. The semantic-field divergence among three Pāli terms — *dāna*, *cāga*, *yāga* — releases *cāga* as the disposition-faculty whose path-rooted character unfolds as five marks: *muttacāga* (the giving that does not stick), *payatapāṇi* (with a hand made pure), *vossaggarato* (delighting in relinquishment), *yācayogo* (responsive to those who ask), *dānasaṁvibhāgarato* (delighting in the act of dispensing-and-sharing) (SN 55.32, PTS v.391; AN 6.25, PTS iii.312). The five marks are not five techniques; they are the semantic unfolding of *cāga*'s own inward inclination. Giving's ultimate is in *cāga*'s inward posture — not in the outward magnitude of the thing.

**P02** cuts at the realization face. Among the four limbs of stream-entry (*sotāpattiyaṅga*), the *cāga* limb is realized in the first person by *Kāḷigodhā* — "these dhammas are present in me ... I have declared myself a stream-enterer" (SN 55.39, PTS v.395) — and the Buddha confirms her in the moment. This is the sole lay first-person witness of *cāga*'s root-faculty status: giving is not the transaction of merit; it is the rooted ground of the path's foundation.

**P03** cuts at the recollection face. Among the six *anussati*, the fifth — *cāgānussati* — is presented in a formula whose five marks are exactly identical (AN 6.25, PTS iii.312). *Cāga*'s substance (the faculty) and its function (the object of recollection) co-stand on a single formula — substance-and-function pair within the same five marks.

The three faces of the Pāli stratum — semantic, root-faculty, and recollection — all converge on a single five-marks formula. Each later stage of the volume revisits, deepens, or inverts one face of this formula.

### Part II — The Āgama View: Motive, Apex, Witness, Incommensurability, Container

**P04** sets out the eight-fold motivational spectrum: *dāna* given out of attachment, out of fear, out of debt-return, out of expectation-of-recompense, out of conformity, out of self-aggrandizement, out of reputation-seeking — and the eighth, *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāratthaṁ dānaṁ deti* ("one gives for the ornament-and-equipment of the mind") (AN 8.31, PTS iv.236). The eighth motivation does not seek reward beyond the giving; the giving itself is the ornament of the mind that gives.

**P05** sets out the apex of incommensurability: the great Velāma-offering's incalculable bounty does not compare to a single moment of impermanence-perception sustained for the duration of a finger-snap (*accharāsaṅghātamatta aniccasaññā*) (AN 9.20, PTS iv.395). The apex is not magnitude but the quality of the moment.

**P06** sets out the 4+1 epistemic partition: when the Buddha lays out the five visible-here-and-now benefits of giving, the general *Sīhasenāpati* answers "*ahaṁ jānāmi*" ("I know this") for the first four; only the fifth (after-death) belongs to the Buddha (AN 5.34, PTS iii.39; iv.81). Four are lay-verifiable in this life; one is for the Buddha's testimony alone. The boundary of lay-knowable is set in the canon itself.

**P07** sets the incommensurability-lock at the Sangha level: "I declare in no way (*na kenaci pariyāyena*) that a personal-recipient giving exceeds in fruit a giving directed to the Sangha" (MN 142, PTS iii.253–256). The Sangha-as-field receives the offering not as the sum of its individual members but as a structural field; field-condition outweighs personal-recipient calculation by any method whatsoever.

**P08** sets the three-fold container of merit-making: *dāna*, *sīla*, *bhāvanā* — the three *puññakiriyavatthūni* (bases of merit-making) (AN 8.36, PTS iv.241). *Dāna* is the first of the three; the volume-three forward-pose of *sīla* and the cross-volume bridge to *bhāvanā* are already pre-figured in this enumeration.

### Part III — The Vinaya: Institutional Landing, the Birth of *Abhayadāna*

**P09** lands the practice institutionally. When *Anāthapiṇḍika*, having received the dharma-eye at Rājagaha, paves Jeta's Grove with gold to invite the Buddha, the Buddha redirects the offering to "the sangha of the four directions" (*cātuddisa saṅgha*) (Vin Cv VI 4, Vin ii 158). Personal devotional impulse is structurally re-channeled into community-form: giving's institutional landing was a redirection, not a refusal.

**P10** sets the śāstra-layer term: "the upholding of precepts as non-harm — this is the giving of fearlessness" (持戒不害是則無畏施) (T1509, fasc. 14). *Abhayadāna* (giving-of-fearlessness) is established by the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* as the third member alongside material-giving and dharma-giving. The threefold structure is sealed within the śāstra layer.

### Part IV — The Mahāyāna Turn: Four Movements of *Dāna-Pāramitā*

**P11** sets the transformation condition: giving that has not received the *prajñā-pāramitā* operator merely bears the name of giving and has not crossed to the far shore — "but it bears only the name of giving; it has not reached the other shore" (T1509, fasc. 11; T0220e, fasc. 571). *Dāna-pāramitā* is *dāna* under the transforming gaze of *prajñā*.

**P12** sets the three-wheel-not-apprehended at the śāstra level: in fascicle eleven of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, the giver, the recipient, and the gift are each non-apprehensible. The three wheels stop turning as discrete entities under the prajñā-gaze.

**P13** sets the canonical self-witness: in the abridged *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra*, fascicle five hundred and seventy-one, the three-wheel-empty (三輪體空) is no longer a śāstra-derivation but the Buddha's own dharma-discourse (T0220e, fasc. 571). The śāstra and the sūtra co-witness at the same locus.

**P14** sets the nine-marks construction balance: the *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* presents the *dāna* mnemonic-verse (*uddāna*) of nine marks (T1579, fasc. 3; fasc. 39). The nine marks balance the three-wheel-not-apprehended of P12–P13 with a constructive enumeration of giving's elements — emptiness without losing structural articulation.

### Part V — Prajñāpāramitā: The Giving without Abiding

**P15** sets the *Diamond Sūtra*'s three-character imperative: "abiding nowhere, yet practicing giving" (應無所住而行於布施) (T0235). This imperative is not the negation that would replace giving; it is the structural condition of "abiding nowhere" upon which "practicing giving" rests — *muttacāga*'s "unsticking" quality re-given as "abiding nowhere."

**P16** sets the *Heart Sūtra*'s encompassing silence: *Kuiji* (窺基) explains that the *Heart Sūtra*'s silence about *dāna* is not omission but "stating in the comprehensive [unitary] mode" (總合說故) (T1710). The pair of abridged and expanded recensions is here established by commentary: silence and elaboration carry the same teaching at different scales.

**P17** sets the expanded recension's six-layer graduated unfolding: fascicle five hundred and twenty-eight of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra*'s "Subtle Marks" chapter unfolds the same teaching in six progressive layers (T0220c, fasc. 528). The abridged recension's "comprehensive silence" and the expanded recension's flourishing six layers are, on the prajñā-teaching, a single declaration in two registers.

The seventeen papers from the Pāli through *prajñāpāramitā* share a structural pattern: the five marks of *cāga* are successively re-illuminated through the motivational spectrum, the incommensurability-lock, the institutional landing of *abhayadāna*, the systematic definition of *dāna-pāramitā*, the canonical self-witness of three-wheel-empty, the nine-marks construction, the three-character imperative of "abiding nowhere," and the encompassing silence of "stating in the comprehensive mode." Each step is the unveiling of a new face of the same *cāga* quality.

## III. Looking Back (Part II): From the Śūraṅgama to the Avataṃsaka

### Part VI — The Śūraṅgama: Three Reverse Layers

Three papers reveal giving's boundary and trap from the reverse side.

**P18** sets the inversion of the second pure precept-instruction. The *Śūraṅgama*'s second pure instruction concerns non-stealing; its reverse face is the giving of "alms-leftover offered to the hungry" (乞食餘分施餓眾生) (T0945, fasc. 6). The reverse-mirror of the non-stealing precept is the ascetic's giving — giving's second pure-instruction inverse structure is here completed.

**P19** sets the three-giving placement within the twenty-five gateways: Avalokiteśvara's fourteen fearlessness-giving postures (T0945, fasc. 6) anchor material, dharma, and fearlessness-giving structurally within the twenty-five gateways of perfect penetration. The *dāna*-face of the gateways chapter receives structural placement.

**P20** sets the *dāna*-warning among the fifty *skandha-māra*s. Fascicle nine of the *Śūraṅgama* names the great-self-pride demon of the *vedanā-skandha* — "reverse-encoding of the three wheels" (T0945, fasc. 9). The third of the five *vedanā*-demons is the interpretive warning that the three-wheel structure can be inverted into self-aggrandizement — the *dāna*-typology of the fifty demonic states is here established.

The Śūraṅgama's three layers share a pattern: giving's reverse-revelation — the inversion (the back of non-stealing), the boundary (the gateway's three-giving placement), and the trap (the demonic inversion of the three wheels) — verify from the reverse side the stability of giving's inward posture.

### Part VII — The Lotus Sūtra: Three Body-Forms

Three papers re-frame giving through three body-forms — substitution, posture-axiom, and transformation.

**P21** sets the Dharma-Master chapter's dharma-offering: "there is no further need to install relics ... the entire body of the Tathāgata is here" (T0262, "Dharma Master" chapter). Dharma-offering requires no further installation of relics, for the sūtra itself is the bearer of the dharma-body — the five-fold dharma-master posture's canonical self-witness of the dharma-body's five manifestations is here established.

**P22** sets *Sadāparibhūta*'s reverent-faith giving: the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle twelve names three offerings — material, dharma, and reverent-faith offering (恭敬施) (T1509, fasc. 12). The third offering is the doctrinal anchor for the bodhisattva *Sadāparibhūta*'s *prasāda-dāna* (giving of reverent faith) — bodily prostration is not "a thing-offering of the body" but "the body's bow." The third body-form lies in posture, not in object.

**P23** sets the dual-anchor middle-way correction in the Past-Deeds-of-*Bhaiṣajyarāja* chapter: the *Lotus Sūtra*'s twenty-third chapter declares the body-offering "the most honored and supreme among all offerings" (於諸施中最尊最上) (T0262, ch. 23), and *Zhiyi*'s *Lotus Commentary* (T1718) explains that "true dharma-offering" is "the inner functioning of wisdom-contemplation." The interpretive crisis posed by self-immolation is corrected by the dual anchor of "the most honored and supreme" in the sūtra and "inner functioning of wisdom-contemplation" in the commentary — *dehadāna*'s doctrinal point lies not in the body's flame but in the inner contemplation that gives wisdom its function.

The Lotus's three body-forms share a pattern: the body as bearer of the offering — substitution (the dharma-body as substitute for the relic), posture-axiom (the body's bow as posture-axiom), and transformation (body-life-giving's wisdom-contemplation transformation) — re-give the thing-face of giving through three forms of bodily mediation.

### Part VIII — The Huayan Five-Part: Samantabhadra's Principal Arena

Five papers define giving's final structure in the *Avataṃsaka* — entry-threshold, vast cultivation, dharma-offering, trajectory, terminus.

**P24** sets the Ground of Joy's giving: the *Daśabhūmika*'s Ground of Joy — "to all things without any clinging ... the great relinquishment is accomplished" (T0279, fasc. 34). The first ground's *dāna* is "without-clinging plus great relinquishment" as the entry threshold of the bodhisattva grounds — the *Avataṃsaka*'s entry-portal for giving is here established.

**P25** sets the four-exhaustions of the third great vow: Samantabhadra's third great vow closes in the four-exhaustions formula — "should the realm of space be exhausted, the realm of beings be exhausted, the karma of beings be exhausted, the afflictions of beings be exhausted — only then would my vow be exhausted" (T0293, fasc. 40, third great vow). The four-exhaustions' negative-form perpetual-motion structure completes the constructional definition of vast-cultivation-offering — giving's total structure is here completed by the perpetuum of negative formulation.

**P26** sets the seven kinds of dharma-offering: the third great vow continues — "of all offerings, the dharma-offering is supreme" — and unfolds into seven concrete forms: "the offering of practicing according to the teaching, the offering of benefiting beings, the offering of taking up beings, the offering of bearing beings' suffering, the offering of cultivating roots of goodness diligently, the offering of not abandoning bodhisattva activity, the offering of not departing from the bodhi-mind" (T0293, fasc. 40, p.0845a). The enumeration of seven dharma-offerings unfolds the "dharma equals giving" equation, from Samantabhadra's own statement, into seven concrete forms — the collection-point anchor.

**P27** sets the trajectory rule of *Sudhana*'s fifty-three visits: at *Sudhana*'s departure, *Mañjuśrī* gives the four-noughts — "do not exalt yourself, do not abandon the old and sick, do not act for gain, do not act for fame" (T0293, fasc. 4, Mañjuśrī chapter). The trajectory rule given at *Sudhana*'s departure is the canonical instruction for the subject-posture of giving — giving is the active verb in every visit, and its posture is held by the four-noughts' negative formulation.

**P28** sets the terminus of the *Gaṇḍavyūha*: fascicle eighty's autobiographical self-witness by Samantabhadra — "in my dharma-sea, there is not one word, not one phrase, that was not obtained through the relinquishment of cakravartin-thrones for the sake of seeking it; nor is there one word, one phrase, that was not obtained through the relinquishment of all I possessed" (T0279, fasc. 80). This is the closure-anchor of the entire Part VIII — the "dharma equals giving" equation is self-witnessed by Samantabhadra at the *Avataṃsaka*'s terminus in autobiographical form, so that giving's ultimate form is the obtaining of the dharma itself.

The Huayan five-part shares a pattern: giving's final structure — entry-threshold (the Ground of Joy), construction (the four-exhaustions), collection-point (the seven dharma-offerings), trajectory (the fifty-three visits), terminus (the dharma-equals-giving equation) — has Samantabhadra as principal arena, so that the outward action of giving converges, in the buddha-realm, into a single matter with the obtaining of the dharma-body itself.

## IV. Looking Back (Part III): The Modern Diagnosis

### Part IX — Modern Diagnosis: The *Bojjhaṅga* Instrument and the Three Axes of the Age of AI

Two papers apply the doctrinal tools of the preceding eight stages to the contemporary situation — one as a cross-traditional instrument for the quality of mind, the other as the doctrinal grounding for a new dominant condition.

**P29** establishes the canonical structural-source for the *bojjhaṅga* twin-factor instrument of *pīti* and *passaddhi*. *Saṃyukta-āgama* sūtra seven hundred and eleven states: "the joy factor having been fulfilled, body and mind become tranquillized, then the tranquillity factor is cultivated" (喜覺支滿足已，身心猗息，則修猗覺支) (T0099 SA 711). Fascicle nineteen of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* in turn distinguishes "*pīti* born within actual wisdom" from *pīti* attached to pleasure, and renders the sixth factor as 除覺分 — "the removal factor" — drawing on *passaddhi*'s negative-side semantic register (T1509, fasc. 19). The dual anchor — the sūtra's "having been fulfilled" and the śāstra's "true *pīti* / removal-factor" — co-establishes the structural source of the instrument: the instrument was not coined by the author; the sūtra's three characters "having been fulfilled" lock down the causal direction. With this same instrument, the bargaining-drift of Chinese-tradition *nianfo* (in its merit-bargain register) and the instrumentalization of Western *mindfulness* (in its productivity-tool register) can be measured under one frame, without bias toward either tradition. The systematic failure of the bargaining-mind at the fifth-to-sixth transition — surface joy unable to call forth tranquillity — receives doctrinal diagnosis.

**P30** establishes the three-axis lock of the AI-era's doctrinal grounding — three interlocking points of canonical attestation. Axis One: the *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* fascicle three states "excluding the seed, the remaining supports" (除種子，餘所依) (T1579, fasc. 3). AI falls within the dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*), not the primary cause (*hetu-pratyaya*); the seed of the mind is not replaced. Axis Two: the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle ninety-two states "taking *cetanā* as head — as in sewing a robe, the needle is the lead" (以思為首；譬如縫衣，以針為導) (T1509, fasc. 92). One mind-moment of *cetanā* is itself the karma-seed; five minutes of mind-intention is the starting point of giving. Axis Three: the *Aṅguttara-nikāya*'s 5.36 passage on rejoicing — "those who rejoice therein ... also share in the merit" (*Ye tattha anumodanti ... tepi puññassa bhāgino*, *Kāladāna-sutta*, AN 5.36, PTS iii.41) — paired with the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle sixty-one's "without troubling body or speech ... but by mind alone" (不勞身、口業……但以心方便) (T1509, fasc. 61). *Anumodanā* (rejoicing-in-merit) is the canonical warrant for mediated giving. The three axes are mutually locking: if any one falls away, the slip toward "AI as primary cause" becomes possible and the entire proposition collapses.

The modern-diagnosis stratum shares a pattern: the doctrinal tools of the preceding eight stages — the *bojjhaṅga*'s instrument for the quality of mind, the four-conditions' classification of relational supports, the canonical warrant for mediated giving in *anumodanā* — receive concrete landing in the contemporary situation. The criterion of landing is not "in or out of fashion" but "in or out of the canon."

## V. The Seam to Volume Three: The Canonical Pre-Laying of *Sīla*

The seam to Volume Three (*Sīla*) does not need to be constructed by the author; it is already laid down in the canon.

Carrying forward the *Avataṃsaka* terminus of P28: in the very same Samantabhadra-witness passage of *Sudhana*'s pilgrimage, a great earth-deity (*pṛthivī-devatā*) appears as the donor of *Sudhana*'s twenty-third visit, where she expounds the "cloud-net liberation" gate by jointly bringing forth the *dāna-pāramitā* and the *śīla-pāramitā* (T0293, fasc. 16–17). *Dāna* and *sīla* are already named in parallel within the *Avataṃsaka*; this is not an authorial conscription.

The three-wheel-to-three-encompassing-precepts structural parallel is also already in the canon. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle eleven establishes the non-apprehensibility of the three wheels (giver, recipient, gift), which is the P13 anchor; the *Bodhisattva-mālā-sūtra* fascicle two states "the encompassing precepts of restraint, the encompassing precepts of wholesome dharmas, the encompassing precepts of sentient beings" (攝律儀戒、攝善法戒、攝眾生戒) — the three-fold structure of the three-encompassing precepts (T1485, fasc. 2). The negative formulation of the three wheels and the positive formulation of the three-encompassing precepts form a structural pair — not a philosophical identity but a constructional correspondence.

The pre-laid posture of giving's mind passing into precept-substance is also in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle thirteen at the opening of *śīla-pāramitā*: "*Śīla*, in the language of Qin, means 'naturally good': fond of practicing the good path, and not letting oneself be heedless" (尸羅，秦言性善，好行善道，不自放逸) (T1509, fasc. 13). This single line already pre-lays the continuity of *cāga*'s inward five-marks quality into the substance of precept — the inward posture of giving extended into precept-substance through "naturally good plus non-heedlessness."

And the fifth great vow of the *Bhaiṣajyaguru Sūtra* completes the seam in a single canonical line: "let the *brahmacarya* be cultivated ... the three-encompassing precepts be completely held" (令修梵行……具三聚戒) (T0450 p.0405a23). The canonical text itself threads Volume Three's three load-bearing centers — the three-encompassing precepts (Part IV), the precept-substance question (Part V), and the *Bhaiṣajyaguru* principal-arena (Part VI) — into a single sequence. The inter-volume constructional unity is pre-laid in the canon; the author merely acknowledges it.

## VI. The Place to Begin: The Buddha's First Step in *Anupubbi-kathā*

The reader has walked the thirty papers — and yet still stands at giving's place-of-entry. A reasonable question: where to begin?

The answer to this question is not in the author's invitation but in the Buddha's *anupubbi-kathā* (graduated discourse). The Vinaya's *Mahāvagga*, chapter one, section twenty-two states: "the graduated teaching — the discourse on giving, the discourse on virtue, the discourse on heavens, the danger and lowness and defilement of sense-pleasures, the benefit of renunciation" (*dāna-kathaṁ sīla-kathaṁ sagga-kathaṁ kāmānaṁ ādīnavaṁ okāraṁ saṅkilesaṁ nekkhamme ānisaṁsaṁ pakāsesi*) (Vin Mv I 22, Vin i 35–36). The *Dīrgha-āgama*, fascicle twenty, attests the parallel in Chinese: "the graduated teaching — the discourse on giving, the discourse on virtue, the discourse on heavens" (T0001, fasc. 20). When the Buddha addresses one of newly-aroused aspiration, his teaching invariably opens with *dāna-kathā* (the discourse on giving), and only thereafter passes to *sīla*, then to the heavens, then to the renunciation that exits desire.

This is not the accidental arrangement of a pedagogical technique; it is the structure of the path itself. *Dāna*'s first-step status is established directly by the Buddha himself — the reader does not need to seek out a place to begin, for the place to begin is already laid down.

So the question "where to begin," answered by the canon, is no longer the active question "where do I start" but the receptive posture "the Buddha has already laid the first step; I walk over to it." Receptive does not mean passive — it means recognizing that this first step has been laid, and then rising.

And here the author steps aside. The grammatical subject of the invitation now becomes the voice of the canon itself.

A reader who approaches *dāna* through the post-Christian, secular, or charitable-impact frame will recognize the question in a particular form: how is giving rightly to be calibrated when so much depends on where the gift goes? The *Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅga-sutta* (MN 142, PTS iii.253–256) opens with Mahāpajāpatī Gotamī, the Buddha's foster-mother, bringing him a new pair of robes she has personally spun and personally woven, asking him to accept them for her sake. The Buddha redirects her: "*Saṅghe, gotami, dehi; saṅghe te dinne ahañceva pūjito bhavissāmi saṅgho ca*" — "give it to the Sangha, Gotami; with the gift given to the Sangha, both I and the Sangha will be honored." Three times she asks, three times he redirects. The doctrinal point is not that the Buddha is less worthy than the Sangha; it is that the giving is a moral relation, and the field that receives it is the field of the relation. Personal craftsmanship — the woven cloth, the spinning hand — meets a structural field of recipients; the relation makes the offering. To approach *dāna* as if it were an impact-arithmetic of marginal expected value is to miss the canonical fact that the field receiving is not the sum of its individuals; it is a structural locus that the offering, rightly directed, enters.

The Vinaya's Cūḷavagga, chapter six, section four (Vin ii 158–159), narrates the founding of the Jetavana grove by Anāthapiṇḍika. The image known abroad is the gold-paving — the householder covering Jeta's grove with kahāpaṇas until the prince relented. The canonical narrative places the gold-paving second. First comes the dharma-eye: at Rājagaha, hearing the Buddha, "*virajaṁ vītamalaṁ dhammacakkhuṁ udapādi*" — "the dust-free, stainless dhamma-eye arose" in him. Only after the recognition does the gold appear. Capital follows recognition; the order is fixed in the canon. For a reader formed by the institutional-philanthropy framework's typical sequence — first the gift, then perhaps the understanding — the canonical order is precisely reversed. The understanding is the founding gate; the wealth is the consequent material.

And one further canonical line, addressing the question of the giver's accounting: in the abridged *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra*, fascicle five hundred and seventy-one, the Buddha himself expounds that "neither giver, nor recipient, nor gift is to be apprehended" (T0220e, fasc. 571). This is not an exotic metaphysical claim; it is the structural relaxation that the reader's own giving — at its best moments — already half-knows. The moment a giving is given without the giver's inward audit of the giver's own role, of the recipient's deservingness, or of the gift's own magnitude, the three wheels are already unobstructed for an instant. The canonical line names what the practitioner's best instinct already does.

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, fascicle eleven, closes this entire volume on seven characters that belong equally to both readers: "**giving is the first condition for *nirvāṇa*"** (檀為涅槃之初緣) (T1509, fasc. 11). *Dāna* is not the path itself; it is the first *pratyaya* — the first conditional support — for entering the path. Joined to P29's *bojjhaṅga* path-structure (*cāgānussati* → gladness → one-pointedness → impermanence-contemplation → attaining the path), the seven characters lock down giving's structural position in the path with single-line precision.

The companion closure in Volume One (NIAN) at P31, "The Internal Steps of Buddha-Recollection," completes the sister-volume diptych of the *Six Practice Gates*; the cross-reference is in the index. The seam to Volume Three (*Sīla*) was laid in the preceding section.

The reader has walked thirty papers. The author's further invitation here would be redundant. The canon's invitations are already enough — *Shandao*'s three-offerings-complete, four-embraces-gathered; Samantabhadra's four-exhaustions; *Vimalakīrti*'s turning of *Sudatta*'s donation-assembly into a dharma-donation-assembly; *Nāgārjuna*'s seven characters; the Pāli *anumodanā*; the Mahāpajāpatī robe-pair; the Anāthapiṇḍika dharma-eye-before-gold; the three-wheel-not-apprehended. Take whichever meets the reader where the reader stands: that is the first step of *dāna*.

The rest is your hand — and the loosening behind it.
