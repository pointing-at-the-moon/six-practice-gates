# The Emergence of *Abhayadāna*
**The Millennium-Long Substance–Name Asymmetry and the Taxonomic Axis-Switch from Recipient-Axis to Content-Axis**

*中文主題：無畏施的出現——實體早於名稱的千年不對稱與從受者軸到內容軸的分類學軸轉換*

---

## Abstract

Western moral traditions classify "not killing" as a *prohibition* — a negative duty. Early Buddhism's mid-fifth-century Chinese translation window reclassifies the same act as a gift: *abhayadāna*. This paper argues the reclassification is neither extension nor accident but the product of two structural asymmetries. First, a millennium-long substance–name asymmetry: the substance of *abhayadāna* is already fully operative in the Nikāya/Āgama strata (the five precepts reframed as *pañca mahādānāni* in AN 9.20 and its direct Ekottara parallel at T0125 *juan* 20 *Bhadrika the householder*) and in the Vinaya institutions (the monastic *vihāra* idiomatically called "the city where fearlessness is given" thrice in T1442; the rain-retreat origin story grounded in non-harming to small creatures, preserved in four of the five Chinese *vinaya*s as a pre-schismatic common ancestor), yet the name *abhayadāna* does not crystallize as a taxonomic category until Kumārajīva's T1509 (~405 CE) and Dharmakṣema's T1488 (~425 CE). Second, an axis-switch: the Nikāya *dāna* taxonomy is ordered by *who receives* (MN 142's fourteen recipients); the fifth-century triad is ordered by *what is given*; *abhayadāna* is the first specimen on the new axis, not an extension of the old. And the third slot is born at the *śīla/dāna* interface — T1509 places the triad not in the *dāna-pāramitā* chapter but in the *śīla-pāramitā* chapter (*juan* 14, T25.163b), showing that *abhayadāna* is *precept-qua-gift* reclassification. The paper closes by handing off to P11: if *abhayadāna* is the third slot, how does the triad get restructured by *śūnyatā* under the *dāna-pāramitā* system?

**Keywords:** *abhayadāna*, fearlessness-giving, *pañca mahādānāni*, five great gifts, *attupanāyika*, self-comparison, the city where fearlessness is given, substance–name asymmetry, axis-switch, recipient-axis, content-axis, interface-derivative, precept-qua-gift, rain-retreat institution, *vassūpanāyika*, pre-schismatic common ancestor, T0125 *Bhadrika* chapter, AN 8.39 Ekottara parallel, AN 9.20 *Velāmasutta* ascending ranking, SĀ 845 five quenchings of fear, SĀ 1044 *Veḷudvāreyyā* self-comparison, T1428 *Vassūpanāyikakkhandhaka*, T1442 *abhayapuradāna* idiom, T1509 *śīla-pāramitā* chapter (*juan* 14), T1488 *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* miscellaneous chapter, T0152 *Liù-dù-jí* narrative-without-name, Kantian universalizability, Wittgensteinian rule-following, moral topology, prohibition-to-gift reclassification, carryover from P09 *cātuddisa saṅgha* institutionalization, forward-pointer to P11 on *dāna-pāramitā* and *śūnyatā*.

## I. Framing

In a recension of the *Mūlasarvāstivāda-vinaya* transmitted some twenty-five centuries after its recorded events, a pursuer asks a household where their man has gone. The answer comes back: "He is among the Śākyan recluses." The household replies with a rhetorical question: "Is that place the city where fearlessness is given (*abhayapuradāna*)? I shall go there and deal with him by law, doing the unprofitable deed."[^1] The same rhetorical counter-move recurs three times in the same *vinaya* — in fascicle 6, as a householder reports to his wife; in fascicle 28, as one man reports to his friend; and in fascicle 38, in an inverted re-use within a prophecy-formula ("the first Tathāgata named Dharma-drum-sound, the second named the Fearlessness-Giving Tathāgata").[^2] The rhetorical structure of the counter-move repays close reading. The pursuer negates the monastic *vihāra*'s status as a sanctuary against pursuit; and the negation must take the form of a rhetorical question precisely because "the city where fearlessness is given" is already a settled idiom in the surrounding lay society. Only a presupposed commonplace can be answered by counter-assertion in the interrogative mood. Before the pursuer's negation supervenes, the monastic *vihāra* is indeed, in the social imagination, *a city in which fearlessness is given*.

This idiom is the paper's entry-point. A city does not give. A city receives. What logic, then, makes a particular space into a giver of "fearlessness"? In the early *vinaya*, the entity named in this way is a body of real property — the *cātuddisa saṅgha* holdings, the class-beneficiary institutional instrument that P09 established as the DĀNA volume's first structural landing — invested by social imagination with the function of "giving fearlessness." The idiom operates stably in *vinaya* narrative, in lay speech, even in the prophecy-formula for future Buddhas, and yet it is **nowhere in the Nikāya / Āgama or the *vinaya* proper named as "a class of *dāna*."** The question the paper takes up follows from this: when "giving fearlessness" is already a commonplace social idiom, when the *pañca-sīla* abstention from killing, the *Pārājika* 3 rule on homicide, and the *vassūpanāyika* (rain-retreat) institution are all fully operative in their institutional proto-forms, why does the category name *abhayadāna* have to wait until the mid-fifth-century Chinese translation window of Kumārajīva and Dharmakṣema before it crystallizes as a formal taxonomic slot?

The obverse of this question is the implicit assumption a contemporary Western reader brings to the early Buddhist corpus. The Western moral tradition — from the Decalogue through to Kantian *Pflichtenlehre* — treats "not killing" as a *prohibition*: a negative duty, a bounding edge, an action withheld. Early Buddhism's mid-fifth-century naming act reclassifies *the same action* — the keeping of the precept of non-killing — as a **gift**: a positive giving. What the classificatory move changes is not the *content* of the action but its *moral topology* — who is the giver, who is the receiver, which direction does the act run, and under what category is it stored in the moral memory. The paper's central thesis is cast from this observation. The reclassification's enabling logic is one face: a millennium-long substance–name asymmetry, in which the Nikāya / Āgama and *vinaya* layers are substantively complete while the name is absent, and the fifth-century Chinese translation window crystallizes the name. Its other face is: a taxonomic axis-switch, in which the Nikāya *dāna* taxonomy is ordered by "who receives" (recipient-axis) and the fifth-century triad is ordered by "what is given" (content-axis); the third slot (*abhayadāna*) is *not* an extension of the old axis but the first specimen on the new. And the third slot on the new axis does not emerge by internal expansion within the *dāna* category; it is *derived at the śīla/dāna interface* — T1509 *Dà zhìdù lùn* 《大智度論》 places the first systematic appearance of the triad not in its *dāna-pāramitā* chapters but in its *śīla-pāramitā* chapter (*juan* 14)[^3], and this location is itself the philological proof that *abhayadāna* is a *precept-qua-gift* reclassification.

This observation carries forward from P09. There, the paper read the institutionalization of *cātuddisa saṅgha* property as the first landing of Part III's volume-thesis: how the self-loosening of *ahaṃkāra* (I-making) in material giving is first inscribed at the legal layer. P10 now turns to the earliest structure of non-material giving — when there is nothing material to transfer and no legal dative to suspend, how does *abhayadāna* happen *as a gift* at all? The *vinaya*'s answer lies in the institutional skeleton — *Pārājika* 3 combined with the *pācittiya* on taking animal life, the rule on water-with-living-creatures, and the four branches of the rain-retreat. The answer from the Mahāyāna commentarial tradition lies in the crystallization of the three-gift triad. Between the two layers lies a millennium's gap, and that gap is itself the structure of *emergence*: emergence is not a generation from nothing, but a crossing from latent structure to named surface.

§II lays down five strata of evidence: the Āgama substance-anchor, the Āgama mechanism-anchor, the *vinaya* institutional-anchor, the naming-absence anchor, and the dual-track crystallization-moment anchor. §III unfolds the core argument in four layers — the closure of the recipient-axis, the three-stratum completeness of the substance, interface-derivation, and the philosophical stakes of the axis-switch. §IV opens a dual-track cross-disciplinary conversation with Kantian universalizability and Wittgensteinian rule-following. §V addresses three misreadings and two cross-disciplinary cautions. §VI restates the essentials, offers the practical implication, and hands off to P11 *From Dāna to Dāna-pāramitā*.

## II. Canonical Sources

This section lays out evidence in five strata corresponding to the four-layer core argument of §III. Following the dual-track discipline of the NIAN-A and DĀNA volumes, every passage that belongs to the Nikāya / Āgama stratum is cited with both the Taishō number (Chinese transmission) and the PTS reference (Pāli); Pāli materials use Bhikkhu Sujato's CC0 rendering and the Mahāsaṅgīti edition; Chinese materials are cited by Taishō volume and fascicle, with blockquoted originals followed by my own English rendering beneath. Italicized Pāli / Sanskrit terms carry a Chinese gloss on first occurrence, then stand alone.

### 2.1 The Āgama Substance-Anchor

The clearest Pāli precursor to the structural content of *abhayadāna* is preserved at AN 9.20 *Velāmasutta* (PTS AN iv 393). The Buddha takes as his starting point his own previous life as the brahmin Velāma and the vast material offering Velāma made (eighty-four thousand golden bowls, and so on); he then lays out an ascending sequence of meritorious acts, each greater than the last. To feed a single stream-enterer is greater than Velāma's total gift; to feed the Buddha at the head of the saṅgha is greater than feeding a stream-enterer; to build a dwelling for the *cātuddisa saṅgha* is greater than feeding the Buddha at the head of the saṅgha; to go for refuge to the Three Jewels with a heart of pure faith is greater than building a dwelling; and — at the summit — **to undertake the five training rules (*sikkhāpadāni samādiyati*) — to abstain from killing, from taking what is not given, from sexual misconduct, from false speech, and from intoxicants — is greater than going for refuge**.[^4]

The structural decisiveness of this ranking lies in its verdict: the keeping of the five training rules is placed higher than every form of material giving and every act of refuge-taking. At AN 9.20 the verdict is already fixed. What AN 8.39 *Abhisaṇḍasutta* adds is only the lexicalizing move — the same act of precept-keeping is re-named *pañca mahādānāni* (the five great gifts), with explicit gloss: this "great gift" is precisely the giving, to immeasurable beings, of *abhayaṃ dadāti* (freedom from fear), *averaṃ dadāti* (freedom from enmity), and *abyāpajjaṃ dadāti* (freedom from injury). The entire weight of the ranking (five precepts > material gift) is already carried by AN 9.20; AN 8.39 completes only the act of naming.

This ranking has a direct Chinese parallel. T0125 *Zēngyī āhán jīng* 《增一阿含經》 *juan* 20 — the *Bhadrika-the-householder* chapter 〈跋提長者品〉 — preserves the nearest Ekottara-parallel to AN 8.39 in the episode of Maudgalyāyana guiding the householder Bhadrika:

> 「如來說二施，法施及財施，今當說法施，專心一意聽。」……目連報言：「長者當知，如來說五事大施，盡形壽當念修行。」……「一者不得殺生，此名為大施，長者！當盡形壽修行之。二者不盜，名為大施，當盡形壽修行。不婬、不妄語、不飲酒，當盡形壽而修行之。是謂，長者！有此五大施，當念修行。」[^5]
>
> "The Tathāgata teaches two kinds of giving: the gift of Dharma and the gift of material things. I shall now speak of the gift of Dharma — listen single-mindedly." [...] Maudgalyāyana said: "Householder, know that the Tathāgata teaches five great gifts (*五事大施*), which you should keep in mind and practice for as long as life endures." [...] "First, not killing — this is called a great gift; householder, practice it for as long as life endures. Second, not taking what is not given — this is called a great gift; practice it for as long as life endures. Not engaging in sexual misconduct, not speaking falsely, not taking intoxicants — these are to be practiced for as long as life endures. These, householder, are the five great gifts; keep them in mind and practice them." (translation mine)

The structural key to this passage is that the "five great gifts" (*五事大施* — i.e., *pañca mahādānāni*) and the "two gifts" (*dhammadāna* plus *āmiṣadāna*) are **juxtaposed but not integrated**. Maudgalyāyana first lays out the two-gift system; he then *adds* the five great gifts as a reinforcing statement of what "the great gift" actually consists in. The five precepts are identified here as a great gift, but they are **not explicitly absorbed into the two-gift system as a third slot**. This un-integrated state is the perfect specimen of substance-before-name: the *abhayadāna* content of the five precepts is fully available at the ethical level (keeping the precepts *is* giving fearlessness to every being one meets), but the taxonomy has not yet crystallized the name.

### 2.2 The Āgama Mechanism-Anchor

The internal mechanics of this substantively complete substrate are visible in three passages from the *Saṃyuktāgama* (T0099, the Sarvāstivāda recension). The *Saṃyuktāgama* at *juan* 30 sūtra 845 (SĀ 845, parallel to SN 12.41 *Pañcaverabhaya* and AN 10.92 *Verasutta*) is titled "the five quenchings of the enmity-producing fears" and makes the causal link between the five precepts and fear explicit:

> 「若殺生因緣罪怨對恐怖生，若離殺生者，彼殺生罪怨對因緣生恐怖休息。若偷盜、邪婬、妄語、飲酒罪怨對因緣生恐怖；彼若離偷盜、邪婬、妄語、飲酒罪怨對者，因緣恐怖休息，是名罪怨對因緣生五恐怖休息。」[^6]
>
> "If, by the causal condition of killing, the fear of offense-and-enmity arises — then for one who abstains from killing, the fear arising by the causal condition of the offense-and-enmity of killing is quenched. The fears arising by the causal conditions of the offense-and-enmity of stealing, sexual misconduct, false speech, and intoxicants — for one who abstains from these, the fears arising from those causal conditions are quenched. This is called the quenching of the five fears that arise by the causal condition of offense-and-enmity." (translation mine)

SĀ 845 preserves the mechanical side of the five precepts (abstention from killing → the causally-linked fear of enmity is quenched) and records in full the receiving-side structure of *abhayadāna* — the quenching of the fivefold fear *is* the condition of the recipient's fearlessness — **but it never names this as "*dāna*."**

T0099 *juan* 37 sūtra 1044 (SĀ 1044, parallel to SN 55.7 *Veḷudvāreyyā*) provides the ethical-reasoning side: the method of *attupanāyika* — applying the case to oneself, the reflective analogy that moves from one's own experience to the experience of others:

> 「我作是念：『若有欲殺我者，我不喜；我若所不喜，他亦如是。云何殺彼？』作是覺已，受不殺生，不樂殺生。」[^7]
>
> "I reflect thus: 'If someone were to want to kill me, I would not be pleased. What is unwelcome to me is unwelcome to another. How then should I kill him?' Having understood this, I undertake non-killing, and I do not take pleasure in killing." (translation mine)

The passage re-reads the five precepts as not a "list of rules" but as *action-limitations that follow naturally from the reverse analogy of one's own experience*: I do not wish to be harmed; neither does he; therefore I do not harm him. This reasoning already contains the directional structure of "giving" in embryo — if the other fears death as I do, my non-killing confers upon him the assurance of *freedom from being killed by me*. This is the moral-metaphysical precursor of *abhayadāna*. Yet SĀ 1044 still closes the passage with "these seven are called the noble precepts" (*如是七種，名為聖戒*)[^8], reaffirming: the conceptual substrate is fully in place, but the step of *naming as dāna* has not yet been taken.

T0099 *juan* 43 sūtra 1039 (the *Cunda-sūtra*) preserves the fullest *ahiṃsā* pericope in the Chinese definition of the five precepts: "abstains from killing, having laid down the rod and the weapon, with conscience and with a heart of compassion for all living beings" (*不殺生，離殺生，捨刀杖，慚愧，悲念一切眾生*).[^9] The four-fold unfolding — abstention from killing, laying aside weapon, conscience, compassion for all beings — maps directly onto the Pāli formula *nihita-daṇḍa nihita-sattha … sabbapāṇabhūta-hitānukampī*. "Compassion for all beings" is precisely the subjective face of the "fearlessness" that *abhayadāna* transmits (not-to-cause-fear-by-my-very-existence); yet the passage remains a *description of precept-conduct*, never crowned with the term *dāna*.

### 2.3 The Vinaya Institutional-Anchor

The third stratum of evidence moves from the Āgamas into the *vinaya* corpus, where the pre-schismatic common ancestor has left its hardest trace. The origin-story of the rain-retreat is attested in four of the five Chinese *vinaya*s — T1428 *Sìfēn lǜ* 《四分律》 *juan* 37, T1435 *Shísòng lǜ* 《十誦律》 *juan* 24, T1421 *Wǔfēn lǜ* 《五分律》 *juan* 19, and T1425 *Móhē sēngqí lǜ* 《摩訶僧祇律》 *juan* 27 — with phrasing that differs in detail but converges in structure.[^10] The origin is grounded neither in climate nor in soteriological retreat-practice but *directly in the non-harming of small creatures*:

> T1428: "Even heterodox practitioners observe a three-month retreat. Yet these Śākyan sons wander among men in spring, summer, and winter alike. The heavy rains have come and the waters are in flood; […] they tread upon living grasses and sever the lives of other beings. Even insects and birds have nests and lairs in which they remain …"[^11]
>
> T1435: "The bhikkhus in the summer months wander through the countries, treading down living grasses and taking the lives of living creatures. […] From now on, they should observe the summer retreat."[^12]
>
> T1421: "Constantly we are told to have few desires and to guard all beings with a heart of compassionate care; yet now they trample without any heart of mercy. This is not the conduct of a *śramaṇa*; this is the breaking of the *śramaṇa*-Dharma!"[^13]
>
> T1425: "Even the ninety-six kinds of wanderers know how to take up a retreat — just as birds take shelter in their nests and remain. Yet the Śākyan sons, who proclaim themselves good and wholesome, do not observe a retreat."[^14] (all translations mine)

Among the four, T1421 *Wǔfēn lǜ* presents **the most direct textual line connecting the rain-retreat to compassion as such** ("constantly we are told to have few desires and to guard all beings with a heart of compassionate care"). The rhetorical move across all four is uniform: heterodox ascetics and even birds and small creatures know to retreat during the rains so as to protect small lives; the saṅgha, by contrast, has been injuring small creatures by travelling through the rainy season — and so the Buddha institutes the three-month retreat. The *Mūlasarvāstivāda-vinaya* (T1442) at this juncture reorganizes its presentation, distributing the compassion-grounded rationale into the neighbouring training-rule on water-with-living-creatures (*juan* 30)[^15]; but the underlying narrative motive is unchanged.

The institutional significance of the rain-retreat rule is decisive: ***the institutional substance of abhayadāna is pre-schismatic***. The "small differences in wording, full identity in structure" across the five *vinaya*s meets the standard criterion, in *vinaya* text-critical practice, for a pre-sectarian common ancestor. Three further *abhaya*-adjacent rules operate in parallel with the rain-retreat: *Pārājika* 3 (the rule against taking human life, attested in all five *vinaya*s, its origin-story grounded in the sixty bhikkhus of Vesālī who, after mis-directed foulness-of-the-body meditation, took their own lives or had Migalaṇḍika the mendicant kill them)[^16]; the *pācittiya* rule against taking animal life (T1428 *juan* 16, T1435 *juan* 16, T1421 *juan* 8)[^17]; and the rule against using water with living creatures — paired with the water-strainer allowance — whose origin-narrative is preserved around the same Chanda / Chandaka protagonist across two sectarian recensions (T1435 *juan* 11 and T1442 *juan* 30)[^18]. Taken together, these four rules constitute the daily spatial operationalization of *abhayadāna* across the monastic routine.

One further passage deserves attention: the single direct enumeration of "*abhayadāna*" in the main body of the five *vinaya*s, preserved at T1435 *juan* 44 in the *Bhikkhunī-vastu*. A past-life Buddha-figure announces: "I have given you food, firewood, lamps, clothing — and fearlessness" (*我與汝等飲食、薪草、燈燭、衣服及無畏施*).[^19] This is the **sole instance** of *abhayadāna* as a category-term in the main *vinaya* body across all five recensions; yet even here, its context is a jātaka-narrative enunciation by a past-life Buddha, the phrase functioning as a conventional coordinate-listing rather than as a rule-formulation term. The location of this lone instance (jātaka narrative, not rule-body) provides its own counter-evidence: the *vinaya*, **at the level of its rules**, never names *abhayadāna*. The institutional substance is in place; the classificatory name is still absent at this layer.

### 2.4 The Naming-Absence Anchor

The fourth stratum of evidence is naming-absence itself: in the most mature *dāna*-taxonomies of the Nikāya / Āgama corpus, *abhayadāna* still does not exist. MN 142 *Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅgasutta* (PTS MN iii 253) has its Chinese parallel at T0026 *Zhōng āhán jīng* 《中阿含經》 *juan* 47, the *Gautamī-sūtra* 《瞿曇彌經》, which preserves the most structurally complete early *dāna*-taxonomy in Chinese translation:

> 「復次，阿難！有七施眾，有十四私施，得大福，得大果，得大功德，得大廣報……有信族姓男、族姓女布施如來，施緣一覺，施阿羅訶，施向阿羅訶……布施畜生。阿難！布施畜生得福百倍，施不精進人得福千倍……復次，阿難！有四種布施，三淨施。」[^20]
>
> "Further, Ānanda, there are seven kinds of giving to the saṅgha and fourteen kinds of giving to individuals; [such gifts] yield great merit, great fruit, great quality, and a great and wide reward. […] There are gifts made by a faithful man or woman of good family: giving to the Tathāgata, giving to a *pratyekabuddha*, giving to an arhat, giving to one on the path to arhatship, […] giving to an animal. Ānanda, giving to an animal yields merit a hundredfold; giving to an unstriving person yields merit a thousandfold. […] Further, Ānanda, there are four kinds of giving, three kinds of purified giving." (translation mine)

The absence in this passage is decisive: **there is no *abhayadāna* category**. The verse-section makes the point sharper still: "to give as one detached from craving, to one detached from craving — this is the foremost gift of material things" — the summit-position is occupied by the highest form of *material* giving, not by a separate fearlessness-giving along another axis. MN 142 and T0026 *juan* 47 organize their taxonomy by the recipient (seven categories of saṅgha, fourteen categories of individual, four combinations of purity-and-impurity of giver-and-receiver), not by the content of the gift. The axis itself is **closed**: there is no operational room for a third (content) axis.

T0152 *Liù dù jí jīng* 《六度集經》 (translated by Kāng Sēnghuì 康僧會, Wu dynasty, ca. 251 CE) offers the most precise specimen of "substance complete, name absent." This corpus is the earliest Chinese anthology of jātaka-tales (*bĕnshēng* 本生), collecting dozens of narratives in which kings sacrifice their lives to save others, deer-kings take the place of the condemned, bodhisattvas give away their bodies, and so on. The narrative substance of *abhayadāna* is here **abundant**. Yet across all eight fascicles, the character-strings *wúwèi-shī* 無畏施 and *shī-wúwèi* 施無畏 never appear: zero hits. All these narratives are classified under the single category *bùshī dù wújí zhāng* 〈布施度無極章〉 ("The Chapter on the Perfection of Giving without Limit").[^21] This precise misalignment between the fullness of the narrative and the nothingness of the category-name is the most exact philological surface of the paper's thesis.

The broader negative evidence across the Āgama corpus reinforces the point: across the complete four-Āgama collection (T0001 *Dīrgha*, T0026 *Madhyama*, T0099 *Saṃyukta*, T0125 *Ekottara*, T0100 the second *Saṃyukta* recension) — more than a hundred and fifty fascicles in total — the classificatory term *abhayadāna* yields zero hits. The closest single approximation is T0099 *juan* 40 sūtra 1108 (SĀ 1108), in a passage involving the *asura* king Vepacitti: "The sages have come here to ask the gift of fearlessness; if you can give them fearlessness, you will grant the munificent teaching of the sage."[^22] But the phrase *shī wúwèi* 施無畏 here is a verb-object construction (*abhayaṃ dadāti* — "gives fearlessness"), situated in a verse-narrative rather than a doctrinal classification; the donor is the *asura* king (being supplicated) and the recipient is the sages (supplicants) — this is not the nominal taxonomic category *abhayadāna*, but rather a colloquial reflection of the ancient Indian royal idiom of "the king's granting of *abhaya*." The singleton location of this instance is itself evidence that *lexicalization* of the term *abhayadāna* has not yet been completed at the Āgama stratum.

### 2.5 The Dual-Track Crystallization Moment

The fifth stratum of evidence is the crystallization moment of the name itself. T1509 *Dà zhìdù lùn* 《大智度論》 (translated by Kumārajīva, Later Qin, ca. 404–406 CE), in *juan* 14 "Explanation of the Remainder of the *Śīla-pāramitā* Section" (釋初品中尸羅波羅蜜義之餘), presents the first systematic appearance of the three-gift taxonomy as the answer to a structured question:

> 「云何持戒能生於檀？檀有三種：一者、財施，二者、法施，三者、無畏施。持戒自撿，不侵一切眾生財物，是名財施。眾生見者，慕其所行，又為說法，令其開悟……如是種種，名為法施。一切眾生皆畏於死，持戒不害，是則無畏施。」[^23]
>
> "How can the keeping of the precepts give rise to *dāna*? — *Dāna* is of three kinds: first, the gift of material things (*cái-shī* 財施); second, the gift of Dharma (*fǎ-shī* 法施); third, the gift of fearlessness (*wú-wèi-shī* 無畏施, *abhayadāna*). One who keeps the precepts restrains himself and does not infringe upon the possessions of any being — this is called the gift of material things. Those who see him admire his conduct; and he further teaches them the Dharma, awakening them to understanding. […] All such things are called the gift of Dharma. All beings fear death; to keep the precepts and not to harm — this is the gift of fearlessness." (translation mine)

Three features of this passage carry the paper's thesis. **First**, the triad of gifts does *not* first appear in the *dāna-pāramitā* chapters of the treatise (*juan* 11–12, which operate throughout on the *two*-gift schema of material-gift plus Dharma-gift)[^24], but in the *śīla-pāramitā* chapter, and there only as the *answer to a posed question*. The framing of the question itself — "how can precept-keeping give rise to *dāna*?" — presupposes the direction *śīla → dāna*. **Second**, the definition of the fearlessness-gift turns on "all beings fear death" (the receiving-side) and "keeping the precepts, not harming" (the giving-side) in tandem; this doubling inherits the logic of AN 8.39 and the T0125 *Bhadrika* passage's *pañca mahādānāni*, but here deploys it as a *third slot* alongside the gifts of material things and Dharma. **Third**, the definitional substrate of all three gifts is precept-derivative: the gift of material things is defined as *not taking what is not given* (the second precept); the gift of Dharma is the combination of exemplary precept-keeping and instruction; the gift of fearlessness is *non-harming through precept-keeping* (the first precept). The structural ground of the triad is *śīla*, not material object.

T1488 *Yōupósāi jiè jīng* 《優婆塞戒經》 (*Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra*, translated by Dharmakṣema, Northern Liang, ca. 421–433 CE) crystallizes the same combination in parallel, with a different ordering and gloss. Its *juan* 4, Chapter 19 (雜品), reads: "There are also three kinds of giving: first, the gift of Dharma; second, the gift of fearlessness; third, the gift of material things" (*復有三施：一、以法施，二、無畏施，三、財物施*).[^25] The ordering (Dharma → fearlessness → material) differs from T1509's (material → Dharma → fearlessness), and T1488 glosses *abhayadāna* as the external act of rescuing beings from worldly terrors — from kings, lions, tigers, floods, bandits, and so on — in contrast to T1509's internalizing ethical gloss (non-harming through precept-keeping). This *independent difference* in both order and gloss is significant: the triad is not derived from a single Sanskrit source via a single Chinese translation window, but **crystallizes through two independent tracks** in the early-fifth-century Chinese translation corpus. This independence is itself evidence that the triad was already a common commentarial framework in the Indian Mahāyāna treatise tradition by that time.

## III. Core Argument

This section consolidates the five strata of §II into four layers of argument: the closure of the recipient-axis (§3.1), the completeness of the substance across three strata (§3.2), interface-derivation (§3.3), and the philosophical stakes of the axis-switch (§3.4). The first three layers are philological–structural; the fourth unfolds the philosophical stakes. Together they support the paper's compound thesis: the emergence of *abhayadāna* is the joint product of the millennium-long substance–name asymmetry and the taxonomic axis-switch from recipient-axis to content-axis, with the name of the third slot born at the *śīla/dāna* interface.

### 3.1 The Closure of the Recipient-Axis

The recipient-organized *dāna*-taxonomy of MN 142 and T0026 *juan* 47 is not a casual editorial choice. It is a **computationally closed** system. Its operating rule is: the merit of a gift equals the purity of the giver's *cetanā* multiplied by the dignity of the recipient (*dakkhiṇeyya* — the worthy of offering) — two variables, both of them on the recipient side.[^26] The seven saṅgha-categories are layered by *saṅgha*-type; the fourteen individual-categories are ranked by attainment (Tathāgata > *pratyekabuddha* > arhat > one-on-the-path-to-arhatship > … > animal); the four *purified-gift* categories combine the purity or impurity of giver and recipient into their four possible combinations. Not a single variable pertains to the content of the gift.

This closure is not a lexical gap; it is a *structural* closure. To introduce "what is given" as a third variable would require re-forging the entire calculus. The system's requirement is: "recipient fixed, gift variable" — and this has **no operational space** within a taxonomy whose entire equation is *recipient × cetanā*. AN 8.36 *Puññakiriyavatthusutta*, with its threefold parallel of *dāna* (giving), *sīla* (precept-keeping), and *bhāvanā* (cultivation)[^27] — the baseline already treated in P08 — further reinforces the closure: *sīla* is not a sub-category of *dāna*; it is a parallel, co-ordinate category. To reclassify the abstention from killing (a *sīla* item) as one of the *pañca mahādānāni* (a *dāna* item) is therefore an *intentional transgression* of the parallel structure of AN 8.36. The lexicalizing move at AN 8.39 (five precepts = five great gifts) is precisely one step of that transgression: it leaps from the *sīla* column of the AN 8.36 baseline into the *dāna* column, and the leap itself *makes the point* that no absolute boundary separates the two columns in practice — that the recipient-axis's inability to host a third axis is not a practical fact but a limit of the classificatory tool. The Nikāya corpus itself preserves two incompatible taxonomies (the AN 8.36 three-parallel and the AN 8.39 transgressive lexicalization) side by side; this very tension becomes the driving force of later Mahāyāna re-organization.

### 3.2 Substance-Completeness Across Three Strata

From the closure of the recipient-axis we turn to the completeness of the substance along the content-axis. The substance of *abhayadāna* — the phenomenon that "the very existence of a precept-keeping person is the giving of fearlessness to all beings" — is fully in place in the early Buddhist Āgama and *vinaya* corpus along three faces.

**The first face, at the level of precept.** The five precepts operate with the "quenching of the five enmity-fears" structure articulated in SĀ 845; the very existence of a precept-keeping person causes, along five vectors (of being killed, robbed, violated, deceived, and intoxicated by this person), **the quenching of the enmity-arising fears** in every being he encounters. The receiving face of this operation is *the being's fearlessness*; the giving face is *the person's action-limitation*. SĀ 1044's *attupanāyika* provides the moral-metaphysical base for the operation: if the other fears death as I do, my non-killing gifts him the assurance of *freedom from being killed by me*. SĀ 1039's "having laid down the rod and the weapon, with conscience, compassion for all beings" completes the subjective face. Taken together, the three passages show that the **complete conceptual substrate** of *abhayadāna* is already in place in the *Saṃyuktāgama*; only the single step of lexical naming has not yet been taken.

**The second face, at the level of institution.** The four-vinaya convergence on the rain-retreat's origin-narrative ("even insects and birds have nests in which to shelter") shows that the saṅgha's collective-action boundary is **institutionalized on the grounds of non-harming to small creatures**. The non-killing of the individual bhikkhu is *sīla*; the three-month non-movement of the collective saṅgha is *institution* — the latter is the corporate-level substantialization of *abhayadāna*. *Pārājika* 3 raises non-injury of human life to the highest offence-schedule (expulsion from the saṅgha); the *pācittiya* on taking animal life raises non-injury of animal life to a *pāyantika* offence; the rule on water-with-living-creatures raises non-injury of microscopic life to an unignorable daily boundary; the rain-retreat raises non-injury-across-a-season to a three-month freeze on collective movement. These four together constitute an institutional network whose base is *non-harm to every life-bearing being*. Its receiving face (the beings' fearlessness) and its giving face (the saṅgha's action-boundary) are both fully in place; the **institutional substance** of *abhayadāna* is wholly operative.

**The third face, at the level of social idiom.** The thrice-attested "city where fearlessness is given" (*abhayapuradāna*) idiom of T1442 shows that *the monastic vihāra as a law-outside space of non-retaliation* was a **commonplace** of contemporary Indian society — only a presupposed commonplace can be countered in the interrogative mood. This idiom positions the saṅgha's *abhayadāna*-substance as a *known social fact* in the lay world.

Taken together, the three faces show the substance of *abhayadāna* to have been **fully operational before the sectarian split** — yet at none of the three levels (precept, institution, social idiom) is this substance *named as a class of dāna*. The T0152 *Liù-dù-jí* pattern of "narrative present, name absent" is the paper's most precise exhibit: dozens of jātaka-tales packed with "saving beings from terror by the gift of one's own life," all classified under a single undifferentiated category 〈布施度無極章〉[^28], with zero hits on the term *abhayadāna*. The juxtaposition of the fullness of the substance and the absence of the name stands here as a single philological fact — and this substance-name gap is itself the structural definition of *emergence*.

### 3.3 Interface-Derivation

The third layer of the argument addresses a structural anomaly in the crystallization moment itself: T1509 introduces the three-gift triad not in its *dāna-pāramitā* chapters (*juan* 11–12) but in *juan* 14, the *śīla-pāramitā* chapter. This location is itself the paper's sharpest philological evidence: the third slot of the triad is not generated by *internal expansion within the dāna category*; it is *derived at the śīla/dāna interface*.

T1509's architecture is unambiguous on this point. *Juan* 11–12, the *dāna-pāramitā* chapter, operate throughout on the **two**-gift schema (material-gift + Dharma-gift) and quote the Buddha's saying, "Among the two gifts, the gift of Dharma is superior"[^29] — a direct inheritance from the Nikāya idiom. It is only in *juan* 14, the *śīla-pāramitā* chapter, that the treatise poses the question "how can precept-keeping give rise to *dāna*?" and generates the triad as the answer. The directionality of "*can give rise to*" (*能生*) is the key — the vector is *śīla → dāna*, not the reverse. And by the time the treatise reaches *juan* 81 "The Mutual Inclusion of the Six Pāramitās" (〈釋六度相攝品〉), the triad has been retroactively absorbed into the definition of *dāna-pāramitā*: "the bodhisattva constantly practices the three kinds of giving without ever setting them aside — the gift of material things, the gift of Dharma, the gift of fearlessness; this is called *dāna-pāramitā*."[^30] This single treatise's internal developmental arc is itself philological evidence: T1509 does not present the triad as a pre-made formula to be received, but crystallizes it through a cross-chapter line of argument.

The definition of *abhayadāna* within the triad is wholly precept-derivative: "all beings fear death; to keep the precepts and not to harm — this is the gift of fearlessness" — "keeping the precepts, not harming" functions here as the giving face, perfectly aligned with the *śīla*-theme of *juan* 14 as a whole. The definition of the gift of material things is likewise precept-derivative ("not infringing upon the possessions of any being" = the second precept); the definition of the gift of Dharma is a combination of precept-generated exemplarity and instruction. The definitional substrate of the *entire triad* is *śīla* — this is the complete structural signature of the *precept-qua-gift* reclassification.

The exposition in T1488 *juan* 4, though framed through an externalizing gloss (worldly rescue from terrors, rather than T1509's internalizing "non-harming through precept-keeping"), produces an analogous structural result: its three-gift triad also appears within the broader *upāsaka-śīla* (layperson's precepts) theme of the chapter — again, the crystallization is not autonomous but neighbours the precept-matter. That *two* independent tracks derive the third slot from the *śīla* ground reinforces the interface-derivation thesis: the move is not a single author's editorial preference but a **shared conceptual move across the mid-fifth-century Mahāyāna commentarial tradition rendered into Chinese**.

A Sarvāstivāda Āgama contrast completes the structural picture. T0099 *juan* 31 sūtra 874, in its recitational appendix, carries: "As with the five precepts, so also with the five-attribute schemas of faith, precept, giving, hearing, and wisdom — it is taught in the same way" (*如五戒，如是信、戒、施、聞、慧經，亦如是說*).[^31] This recitational appendix sets "the five precepts" and "faith, precept, giving, hearing, and wisdom" as two *parallel* schemas — within which "giving" (*cāga*) stands as a single member, co-ordinate with "precept" (*sīla*) but not absorbed into it. This is a Sarvāstivāda-Chinese editorial trace of "precept is not a kind of giving" — contrasting with the Theravāda lexicalizing move at AN 8.39 that renames the five precepts as *pañca mahādānāni*. The two schools handle the same material differently: the Sarvāstivāda preserves the AN 8.36 three-parallel baseline (hence: precept is not a gift), while the Theravāda crosses the tension between AN 8.36 and AN 8.39 and chooses the latter. What T1509, in the early fifth century, accomplishes is a *synthesis* of the two Āgama inheritances: it preserves the column-separation of AN 8.36 (hence precept is not simply a gift) while, *above* the separation, it rebuilds a bridge (hence the triad is derived *at the interface* in the *śīla* chapter). This is the conceptual move of Kumārajīva-era Chinese commentarial treatises: *interface-derivation*, not *intra-axis expansion*.

### 3.4 The Philosophical Stakes of the Axis-Switch

The first three layers are structural and philological. This final layer unpacks the philosophical stakes. What changes when the taxonomic axis shifts from recipient (Āgama) to content (fifth-century triad)?

**First change: a shift in the centre of the giver's agency.** In the Āgama *dāna*-taxonomy, the giver's agency is centred on **choice of field** — selecting a recipient along the ranked hierarchy of worthiness (Tathāgata > *pratyekabuddha* > arhat > … > animal) in order to maximize return. The agency demanded of the giver is ritual discernment; its ethical centre is the recipient's identity. After the fifth-century axis-switch, the giver's agency re-centres on **choice of content** — deciding whether the giving is of material, of Dharma, or of fearlessness. The agency demanded of the giver is ethical discernment; its ethical centre is the quality of what is given. The first agency requires the presence of a concrete recipient (without recipient, no field to select); the second is exercisable **at every moment of precept-keeping** (with no concrete recipient present, precept-keeping in itself is the landing of *abhayadāna*). The practical consequence of this shift in the centre of agency is decisive: the practice of giving is no longer confined to the moment of an act of donation, but extends to *the continuing existence of every precept-keeper*.

**Second change: the emergence of a type of gift that does not require a concrete recipient.** As the third slot of the triad, *abhayadāna* has a distinctive structural feature: **the recipient need not be concretely individuated**. When one keeps the five precepts, "immeasurable beings" (AN 8.39's formula) receive the gift simultaneously, and no concrete worthy-of-offering need be singled out. Its structural difference from the gift of material things (requires a concrete recipient to receive the object) and the gift of Dharma (requires a concrete listener to hear the teaching) is: the reception of *abhayadāna* is passive and class-extensive — any being who *would* be spared being killed, robbed, violated, deceived, or intoxicated by this precept-keeper, is a recipient. This is a mirror, at the level of individual precept-keeping, of the class-beneficiary structure that P09 established at the level of the *cātuddisa saṅgha*: in P09, the legally-constituted class-beneficiary is the "arrived-and-not-yet-arrived" saṅgha; in P10, the ethically-constituted class-beneficiary is "every being whose fear this person's non-harming would render absent." The two layers are isomorphic; both exhibit the structural asymmetry between "the concrete recipient the giver perceives at this moment" and "the class-recipient the taxonomy constitutes."

**Third change: the opening of an operational space for *śūnyatā*.** This is the deepest layer of the philosophical stakes, and the philological basis for §VI's handoff to P11. *Trimaṇḍala-pariśuddhi* — the three-wheel purity, the emptying of giver, gift, and recipient — requires an axis on which the three "wheels" can actually be turned. This emptying operation can only be performed **on the content-axis**. Why? Because the *śūnyatā* operation requires the substance of the gift itself to be deconstructible — material things may be empty, Dharma may be empty, fearlessness may be empty; but *the recipient's identity* (the tiered worthy-of-offering hierarchy), if made the classificatory axis, cannot survive its own emptying: if recipients are empty, the seven-saṅgha / fourteen-individual rankings lose the ground that anchored their calculus. The Āgama *dāna*-taxonomy's recipient-axis is therefore *structurally closed* to *śūnyatā* deconstruction; the fifth-century triad's content-axis is *structurally open* to it. What P11 (*From Dāna to Dāna-pāramitā*) will take up — the *śūnyatā* recasting of the triad under the *pāramitā* system — has its philological ground already laid here at P10: the *pāramitā*-level emptying is **not a novel Mahāyāna move**, but the **necessary extension** of a taxonomic axis-shift that has already, in the fifth-century Chinese translation window, moved from recipient to content. The opening of the content-axis already reserves the operational space that *śūnyatā* will need.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Engagement

This section supplements §III's axis-switch argument and the §3.2 substance–name asymmetry with a dual-track conversation: Kantian universalizability and Wittgensteinian rule-following. The frame is *structural homology* — not reduction, not theoretical equivalence; where the homology holds, it is named as homology, and where it diverges, the divergence is marked.

### 4.1 Kantian Universalization and *Attupanāyika*

SĀ 1044's *attupanāyika* — "what is unwelcome to me is unwelcome to another" — shares a structural homology with the first formulation of the categorical imperative (*kategorischer Imperativ*) in Kant's *Grundlegung zur Metaphysik der Sitten*: *"Handle so, daß die Maxime deines Willens jederzeit zugleich als Prinzip einer allgemeinen Gesetzgebung gelten könne"* ("Act so that the maxim of your will might at the same time serve as the principle of a universal legislation"). Both test the legitimacy of an action by a *reverse universalization* from self to other.

The directionality, however, is opposite. Kant's universalization takes **a priori rationality** as its starting point: practical reason is itself the legislator, rational testability precedes experience; "I do not wish to be killed," when universalized as a law, must self-contradict (if all men kill, the killer is himself among the killed; universal willing cannot will itself), and so non-killing is a necessity of reason. *Attupanāyika*, by contrast, starts from **embodied experience of fear**: "I reflect thus: if someone were to want to kill me, I would not be pleased" — this "not pleased" is not a logical impossibility of *a priori* reason but a bodily, affective experience; the extension "as he likewise" is an **analogy, not a logical necessity**. Kant's universalization terminates in the *autonomous rational subject* legislating to itself; *attupanāyika* terminates in the *natural extension of embodied empathy*.

The philosophical stakes of this directional opposition: early Buddhist ethical reasoning does **not** posit an autonomous rational subject. In *attupanāyika*, the agent starts not from *a priori* reason but from the bodily apprehension of fear-of-death — a shared empirical fact for every sentient being (*sabbasatta*), not a postulate of inference. Kant's universalization's failure-cases (where the agent has no concrete will at a moment, the universalizing test has nothing to attach to) do not occur for *attupanāyika* — the embodied perception of "not wishing to be killed" is always available in the body of a sentient being. This difference will be returned to in §V as a cross-disciplinary caution: structural homology does not entail reduction; Buddhist ethics stands on its own moral-metaphysical ground.

### 4.2 Wittgensteinian Rule-Following and the Emergence Structure

Wittgenstein's treatment of rule-following in the *Philosophische Untersuchungen* — that the content of a rule does not exist complete prior to its application, but is *articulated* in the continuing practice — shares a second structural homology with the substance–name asymmetry of *abhayadāna*. The precept of non-killing as a rule has been operative in early Buddhism for centuries; its *articulation* as "the gift of fearlessness" is completed only at the fifth-century Chinese translation window. The rule's content was already fixed in the practice — the very existence of a precept-keeper is the quenching of enmity-fears among beings — but its **articulation** as a category of *dāna* is a late move. This structure is homologous to Wittgenstein's insight: practice precedes articulation, and articulation is the crystallization *after* practice, not its precondition.

The homology needs careful handling. Wittgenstein's rule-following has been intensely contested within analytic philosophy — Kripke's sceptical reading against Baker-Hacker's commonsensical reading is a live dispute[^32]; this paper does not enter that dispute, and takes up only the generic structural analogy of *practice-preceding-articulation* without committing to either party's specific interpretation. The value of the structural analogy is: to offer Western readers a familiar linguistic frame — the logic of a rule whose content is not complete prior to its application but is named in the course of continuing practice. Under this frame, the millennium-long substance–name asymmetry of *abhayadāna* is not a philological accident but a necessary rhythm of conceptual articulation.

The convergence-point of the two tracks is this: both axis-switch (§3.4) and articulation (§4.2) point to the logic of *emergence* — *emergence is not generation but naming*. The fifth-century Chinese translation window did not *create* the substance of *abhayadāna*; the substance was already operative at all three layers (precept, institution, social idiom). What that window accomplished was to articulate, in the form of a taxonomic name, a content already complete in practice. And the same naming-act simultaneously accomplished both structural moves — the axis-switch (from recipient to content) and the interface-derivation (the third slot born at the *śīla/dāna* interface). One naming act, two layers of structuration. This is the precise structure of *emergence* as the paper claims it: the substance long complete, the naming suddenly crystallized, the classificatory axis simultaneously shifted, the interface-derivation precisely located — four faces of one and the same act.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section addresses three common misreadings and two cross-disciplinary cautions.

**❌ Misreading 1: *Abhayadāna* is a Mahāyāna novelty.**

This reading treats the three-gift triad as a Mahāyāna expansion on early Buddhism and takes *abhayadāna* to be a distinctively Mahāyāna ethical product. This misreading ignores the five-stratum evidence laid out in §II — the substance of *abhayadāna* is fully operative in the Āgama + *vinaya* corpus (the five great gifts as ethical identification of the five precepts, the rain-retreat institution's collective life-protection, the "city where fearlessness is given" social idiom), and what the Mahāyāna Chinese translation window accomplished was *naming*, not *creation*. The precise formulation is: *abhayadāna* **as substance** belongs to the pre-schismatic common ancestor; **as name** it crystallizes in the fifth-century Chinese translation window. This substance-name distinction prevents reading Mahāyāna ethics as a discontinuous expansion of early Buddhism.

**❌ Misreading 2: The five precepts simply *are* *abhayadāna*.**

This reading identifies the two, taking the complete set of five precepts as a synonymous restatement of *abhayadāna*. The misreading conflates the **operational layer** and the **classificatory layer**. The five precepts are the *operational substrate* — five specific action-boundaries (non-killing, non-stealing, non-misconduct, non-false-speech, non-intoxication), functioning within the *śīla* category of early Buddhism. *Abhayadāna* is the *classificatory layer* — a re-description, under a different taxonomic axis, of that same operation as a kind of giving. Their relation is "the same operation, re-described under a different classificatory axis" — not synonymy. Precisely stated: the five precepts are the operational substrate of *abhayadāna*; *abhayadāna* is one classificatory reading of the five precepts; to collapse the two into identity is to lose the axis-switch structure the paper has just established.

**❌ Misreading 3: The "seven kinds of giving" are a sub-class of *abhayadāna*.**

A modern popular reading in Chinese-Buddhist devotional circles tends to treat the *seven kinds of giving* — eye-gift, gentle-face gift, kind-word gift, body-gift, heart-gift, seat-gift, lodging-gift — preserved at *Zá bǎozàng jīng* 《雜寶藏經》 T0203 *juan* 6 as a sub-category of *abhayadāna*.[^33] The reading is a category error. The organizing principle of the seven gifts is *non-expenditure of material resources* (giving without outlay of goods), not *conferral of freedom from fear*; the two taxonomies are **orthogonal**, not inclusion-related, on the classificatory axis. The correct position of the seven gifts is as a *further axis* of non-material giving, coordinate with (not subordinate to) *abhayadāna* — their differences lie not in set-relation but in classificatory principle. Folding the two into one is a rhetorical conflation of later-Chinese-Buddhist popular exposition, not a fact of the canonical taxonomies.

**⚠️ Cross-disciplinary caution 1: Kantian universalizability and *attupanāyika* are structurally homologous but directionally opposite.**

§4.1 has developed this conversation; the caution here reaffirms the limits of the analogy. Structural homology (both use reverse universalization as the core move of ethical reasoning) is not theoretical equivalence (Kant's *a priori* rational subject and *attupanāyika*'s empirical empathetic subject stand on radically different moral-metaphysical grounds). The analogy's function is *heuristic bridge* — a linguistic entry-point for readers familiar with Kant to approach *attupanāyika* — not theoretical reduction. It cannot be used to claim that Buddhist ethics is an Indian version of Kantianism; nor, in reverse, that Kantian ethics can be explicated by *attupanāyika*.

**⚠️ Cross-disciplinary caution 2: *Fàngshēng* (the later Chinese Buddhist practice of ritual animal release) is not the *vinaya*-era *abhayadāna*.**

*Fàngshēng* 放生, as a Chinese Buddhist custom, developed primarily from the Tang dynasty onward in sinophone regions, drawing scriptural warrant from *Fànwǎng púsà jiè jīng* 《梵網菩薩戒經》, the "Liúshuǐ the Merchant's Son" chapter of the *Suvarṇaprabhāsa-sūtra* 《金光明經》, and similar texts; its broad institutional practice dates from the Song dynasty and later. The *abhayadāna* the present paper addresses is the classificatory name crystallized at the fifth-century Chinese translation window, whose substantive substrate lies in the Āgama-level five precepts and the *vinaya*-level rain-retreat institution — temporally *five to seven centuries earlier* than the *fàngshēng* tradition. Identifying the two is an *anachronism*. The paper offers no valuative judgment on *fàngshēng* (the modern practice has its own ecological-ethical contestations, which are not this paper's concern); it flags only the caution that *abhayadāna* as an early Buddhist institutional concept and *fàngshēng* as a later Chinese ritual practice differ markedly along the axes of time, sectarian lineage, and doctrinal rationale, and ought not to be read as continuous.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essentials**

The emergence of *abhayadāna* is the joint product of two structures: the millennium-long substance–name asymmetry, and the taxonomic axis-switch. The substance is fully operative across three strata before the sectarian split — in the Āgama (the five great gifts as ethical identification of the five precepts; *attupanāyika* as moral-metaphysical ground), in the *vinaya* (*Pārājika* 3 + the *pācittiya* against animal-killing + the water-with-living-creatures rule + the four-fold rain-retreat, convergent across four of the five Chinese *vinaya*s as a pre-schismatic common ancestor), and in the social idiom (the "city where fearlessness is given" as a commonplace). The name, however, must wait until the early-fifth-century Chinese translation window, where Kumārajīva's T1509 *juan* 14 and Dharmakṣema's T1488 *juan* 4 crystallize the triad on two independent tracks. This naming-act simultaneously accomplishes two structurations: the axis-switch from the Āgama recipient-axis to the fifth-century content-axis, and the interface-derivation of the third slot at the *śīla/dāna* interface — "*abhayadāna*" *as the reclassification of precept-qua-gift*, with its very location in T1509's *śīla-pāramitā* chapter (not its *dāna-pāramitā* chapter) being the philological proof.

**Practical implication**

The most immediate implication of this doctrine for the contemporary reader is: **the landing of *abhayadāna* does not require a concrete recipient's presence**. Every moment of precept-keeping is a live occurrence of *abhayadāna*; when the practitioner chooses, at this moment, not to harm any sentient being, every being who would have been spared harm-by-this-person at this moment becomes, simultaneously, the recipient of this gift. This mode of landing requires no material resource, no specific audience for Dharma-transmission, no particular moment of ritual donation — it is open at *the very existence of every precept-keeper*. The doctrine is particularly meaningful in the age of commodified mindfulness (*McMindfulness*): when mindfulness is therapized, commodified, and de-religionized, the face of precept-keeping as *the giving of fearlessness to every being* opens a different entry-point — precept is not self-restraint upon the individual self, but *a gift to every other being*. This is one door through which a Western reader may re-enter early Buddhist ethics via *abhayadāna*.

**Open question → handoff to P11**

If *abhayadāna* is the third slot of the triad, how is the triad restructured by *śūnyatā* under the *dāna-pāramitā* system? The *trimaṇḍala-pariśuddhi* requirement of emptying all three wheels — giver, gift, recipient — can operate only on the content-axis (§3.4). P11 *From Dāna to Dāna-pāramitā* will take up the structure of that *śūnyatā* restructuring: when *abhayadāna* enters the *pāramitā* system, how are its three wheels — the giving face (precept-keeping non-harming), the receiving face (beings' fearlessness), and the content (the "fearlessness" given) — recast under the three-wheel-emptying operation? P10 has laid the philological groundwork in its axis-switch thesis; P11 will build the second Mahāyāna move on that ground. If P10 is the *crystallization* of the naming, P11 is its *deconstruction*.
