## Abstract

「夫布施者若無般若波羅蜜多，但得施名非到彼岸，要由般若波羅蜜多乃得名為施到彼岸。」(T0220e *juan* 571.) This passage marks the pivot of the entire DĀNA volume: when *dāna* turns from *merit* (*puṇya*) into *pāramitā*, what changes is not the act but the **ontological condition** of the act. The paper argues that the core mechanism by which *dāna* becomes *dāna-pāramitā* is the intervention of *prajñā* (wisdom; the discernment of emptiness), which transmutes giving from "merit with the three wheels apprehended" (*trimaṇḍala-sahita*) into "pāramitā with the three wheels emptied" (*trimaṇḍala-śūnya*). The *Liù dù jí jīng* 《六度集經》 (T0152), translated by Kāng Sēnghuì at Wu around 251 CE, preserves the earliest extant systematic six-perfection framework in Chinese — its eight-fascicle "*X-dù-wújí*" 〈X度無極〉 sequence with *dāna* as the first chapter — and the extreme self-giving narratives of its jātaka collection furnish the narrative substance of fearlessness-giving (*abhayadāna*); yet its *dāna-pāramitā* chapter (〈布施度無極章〉) collects every variety of giving under a single category and never names *abhayadāna* as a distinct slot. The crystallization of this naming and the systematization of the six perfections begins with the *Dà zhìdù lùn* 《大智度論》 (T1509)'s distinction between giving *with apprehension* (*有所得*, *upalambha-yukta*) and giving *without apprehension* (*無所得*, *anupalambha-yukta*). The systemic interdependence of the six perfections (T1604: "the six perfections mutually complete each other") and the beggar-enablement thesis (T1577: "without a beggar, *dāna-pāramitā* cannot be fulfilled") together institute a double revolution: not only does the condition of giving change (*prajñā* intervenes), the power dynamic of giving is reversed (the beggar enables the perfection). The paper closes by handing off to P12: how is the structure of *trimaṇḍala-śūnya* systematically unfolded in Nāgārjuna's *dāna-pāramitā* chapter?

**Keywords:** *dāna-pāramitā*, *prajñāpāramitā*, with-apprehension giving (*upalambha*), without-apprehension giving (*anupalambha*), *trimaṇḍala-śūnya*, three-wheel emptiness, six perfections, *ṣaṭ-pāramitā*, *Liù dù jí jīng*, *Dà zhìdù lùn*, *Mahāyāna-sūtrālaṃkāra*, beggar-enablement, *yācaka*, mutual completion of the six perfections, paradigm shift, T0152 *dāna-pāramitā* chapter, T1509 *dāna-pāramitā* chapter, T0220e *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra*, T1577 *Mūla-madhyamaka-kārikā*, T1604 *Mahāyāna-sūtrālaṃkāra*, T1592 *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha*, T0125 *Ekottara-āgama*, three-wheels-apprehended, three-wheels-emptied, *prajñā*, *śūnyatā*, *pāramitā*, *dù-wújí* (ferrying-without-limit), jātaka narrative, *yācaka* (beggar), 〈檀度無極章〉, carryover from P10 *abhayadāna* triad, forward-pointer to P12 on *trimaṇḍala-śūnya*.

## I. Framing

「夫布施者若無般若波羅蜜多，但得施名非到彼岸，要由般若波羅蜜多乃得名為施到彼岸。」[^1] This sentence from the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* furnishes the most precise marker of the pivot of the entire DĀNA volume. When *giving* takes on the suffix *to-the-other-shore* (*pāramitā*), when an ordinary act of donation is renamed *dāna-pāramitā*, what changes is not the action of the act but the **ontological condition** of the act. The same hand extends; the same coin or robe or breath of life passes from giver to receiver; and yet, on one side of the change, what one has done is *merit* (*puṇya*), and, on the other side, what one has done is *pāramitā* — a perfection that ferries one across to the other shore.

The question carries forward from P10. P10 established the third slot of the three-gift triad — *abhayadāna* — and showed that this slot is born at the *śīla/dāna* interface, structured by a millennium-long substance–name asymmetry. P10's closing question was: if *abhayadāna* is the third slot, how is this triad restructured by *śūnyatā* under the *dāna-pāramitā* system?[^2] P11's answer is direct: *prajñā* operates as the **transformative condition of giving**. Without *prajñā*, giving is only merit; with *prajñā*, giving becomes *pāramitā*. The distinction is not quantitative (more merit versus less merit) but **qualitative** — a paradigm reversal that takes place within giving itself.

The question presses with particular urgency upon the Western reader. Western Buddhist studies has long read early Mahāyāna under the optic of "giving as a moral virtue" — *dāna-pāramitā* gets classified as generosity, as altruism, as one of the six bodhisattva trainings. The "with-apprehension versus without-apprehension" distinction interrupts this reading: if a giver simultaneously holds onto "I give, that one receives, and the thing given," then this giving, however generous, however altruistic, remains only merit and **is not yet pāramitā**. Stated otherwise: **the qualitative transformation of giving turns on the structure of the giver's mind, not on the object of the gift or its quantity.** The observation overturns the traditional method of classifying giving by external features of the act — the value of giving lies not in *what* is given but in *how* it is given.

§II lays out the evidence in five strata: the six-perfection framework, the *prajñā* condition, systematicity, the reversal of power, and the Āgama transition. §III unfolds the core argument across four layers — the structural innovation of the six-perfection framework, *prajñā* as the transformative condition, the systematicity of the six perfections, and the reversal of the power relation. §IV opens a three-track dialogue with Harrison, Nattier, and Cousins to locate the paper's scholarly position. §V addresses three misreadings and two cross-disciplinary cautions. §VI restates the essentials, offers the practical implication, and hands off to P12.

## II. Canonical Sources

This section lays evidence in five strata corresponding to the four-layer core argument of §III. Following the dual-track discipline of the NIAN-A and DĀNA volumes, every passage that belongs to the Nikāya / Āgama stratum is cited with both the Taishō number (Chinese transmission) and the PTS reference (Pāli); Mahāyāna *śāstra* materials are cited by Taishō volume and fascicle; Pāli materials use the PTS edition. Italicized Pāli / Sanskrit terms carry a Chinese gloss on first occurrence, then stand alone.

### 2.1 The Six-Perfection Framework — T0152's Eight-Fascicle Sequence and the Narrative Substance of the *Dāna-Pāramitā* Chapter

The very structure of the *Liù dù jí jīng* 《六度集經》 (T0152, translated by Kāng Sēnghuì 康僧會 in the Wu state, ca. 251 CE) — eight fascicles, each titled by one of six perfections — is itself an argument. The opening fascicle gives the canonical list:

> 「菩薩六度無極高行……一曰布施，二曰持戒，三曰忍辱，四曰精進，五曰禪定，六曰明度無極高行。」[^3]
>
> "The bodhisattva's six lofty practices of *dù-wújí* (the perfections that ferry without limit): first, giving; second, observance of precepts; third, patience; fourth, energy; fifth, meditative absorption; sixth, the lofty practice of *míng-dù-wújí* (the wisdom that ferries without limit)." (translation mine)

This passage is the **earliest systematic six-pāramitā sequence in extant Chinese Buddhist scripture**. Kāng Sēnghuì's rendering "*dù-wújí*" 度無極 is precise: 度 *dù* signals the crossing to the other shore (*pāra*); 無極 *wújí* signals the limitless (*-mitā*); together: "the limitless ferrying-across." The opening fascicle's 〈布施度無極章第一〉 ("Chapter One: The Perfection of Giving That Ferries Without Limit") takes Prince Vessantara (須大拏太子) and King Sattabhānu (薩波達王) as its narrative anchors. The narrative content is saturated with extreme bodily giving: the relinquishment of kingdom, of treasure, of wife and children, even of the flesh of one's own body. These are the **narrative substance** of the *abhayadāna* P10 established — the king who gives his life to rescue, the deer-king who substitutes himself for prison, the bodhisattva who gives the body itself; the practical archetype of fearlessness-giving fills the entire chapter.[^4]

And yet, across all eight fascicles, a search for "無畏施" (*abhayadāna*) or "施無畏" (giving fearlessness) as a *category name* returns **zero hits**. Every donative narrative — including those whose substantive content is unmistakably fearlessness-giving (the king who interposes his own body to spare his subjects from the sword) — is gathered under the single rubric "the chapter of the perfection of giving that ferries without limit."[^5] The "narrative-saturated / category-name-absent" structure here is congruent with P10's overall reading of T0152: the substance is full, the classificatory name is absent.

### 2.2 The *Prajñā* Condition — T1509's With-Apprehension versus Without-Apprehension

The *Dà zhìdù lùn* 《大智度論》 (T1509, translated by Kumārajīva, ca. 405 CE), at *juan* 60, preserves the central mechanism of the *dāna* → *dāna-pāramitā* transformation:

> 「若菩薩摩訶薩用有所得故布施，布施時作是念：『我與，彼受，所施者物。』是名得檀，不得波羅蜜。」[^6]
>
> "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva gives because of having something to apprehend (*有所得*, *upalambha*), and at the moment of giving thinks, 'I give, that one receives, this is the thing given' — this is called *attaining giving* (*得檀*), not *attaining the pāramitā*." (translation mine)

> 「佛告釋提桓因：『菩薩摩訶薩布施時，不得與者、不得受者、不得所施物，是人得具足檀波羅蜜』」[^7]
>
> "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva gives because of having nothing to apprehend (*無所得*, *anupalambha*) … this person attains the fulfilled *dāna-pāramitā*." (translation mine)

The structural keys of this passage are three. First, the giver who gives "with apprehension" (*有所得*) holds onto all three wheels simultaneously — the giver (*我與*), the receiver (*彼受*), and the gift (*所施者物*) — i.e., *trimaṇḍala-sahita*, "with the three wheels intact." Second, the giver who gives "without apprehension" (*無所得*) has gone beyond apprehension of the three wheels — i.e., *trimaṇḍala-śūnya*, "with the three wheels emptied." Third, **the very same act of giving**, owing to the difference in the structure of the giver's mind, can produce two distinct ontological outcomes: "attaining giving" (acquiring the merit of the gift) or "attaining the fulfilled *dāna-pāramitā*" (completing the perfection of giving). **One and the same act, two ontological statuses.**

This distinction is the hinge of *dāna-pāramitā* theory. It states: the value of giving does not turn on outward generosity (the merit-difference between Vessantara giving away his kingdom and a commoner giving a single meal lies not in *what* is given but in *how*); it turns on whether the giver's mind apprehends the three wheels. The intervention of *prajñā* is not an external supplement but the internal **transformative condition** — without *prajñā*, giving is forever giving; with *prajñā*, giving is *pāramitā*.

### 2.3 Systematicity — T1604's Mutual Completion of the Six Perfections

The *Mahāyāna-sūtrālaṃkāra* 《大乘莊嚴經論》 (T1604, by Asaṅga 無著), at *juan* 8, sets the systematicity of the six perfections in four words and a short causal gloss:

> 「六度互相成，一切種分別。」[^8]
>
> "The six perfections mutually complete one another, with all-mode differentiation." (translation mine)

> 「檀為戒等因。何以故？不顧財者能行戒等故。」[^9]
>
> "*Dāna* is the cause of *śīla* and the rest. Why? Because the one who has no concern for wealth is then able to practice *śīla* and the others." (translation mine)

The first sentence is the master-clause: the six perfections are not six independent virtues but a system in mutual completion. The second sentence is the causal warrant for *dāna*'s foundational position: *dāna* is the foundation of the six perfections — the one without grasping for wealth is then capable of *śīla* (no taking what is not given); the one keeping *śīla* is then capable of *kṣānti* (no anger amid abusive conditions); the one with *kṣānti* is then capable of *vīrya* (no retreat in cultivation); the one with *vīrya* is then capable of *dhyāna* (the mind is undistracted); the one with *dhyāna* is then capable of *prajñā* (the seeing of reality). *Dāna* is the first cause, the other five are its effect — the causal chain of the six perfections, anchored by *dāna*.

But the chain is not linear. T1604's four senses — mutual inclusion (相攝), differentiation (差別), scriptural ground (依法), and causation (為因) — show the six perfections to be **a non-linear system of mutual interdependence**: "mutual inclusion" means every perfection contains the other five (a true *dāna* must already contain *śīla*, *kṣānti*, *vīrya*, *dhyāna*, and *prajñā*); "differentiation" means each perfection has its proper distinct function; "scriptural ground" means the theoretical basis for the six is already textually warranted; "causation" means *dāna* is the necessary but not sufficient condition for the other five.[^10]

### 2.4 The Reversal of Power — T1577's Beggar-Enablement Thesis

The *Madhyamaka-śāstra* 《中觀論》 (T1577, attributed to Nāgārjuna) at *juan* 1 furnishes the most dramatic reversal of the power relation of giving:

> 「若無乞者，檀波羅蜜則不滿足，無上菩提則不可得，是故於乞求者深生悲惱。」[^11]
>
> "If there is no beggar, the *dāna-pāramitā* is not brought to fulfilment, and unsurpassed awakening cannot be attained — therefore, towards those who beg, deeply give rise to compassion-grief." (translation mine)

What is revolutionary about this passage is its claim: **the beggar is not a passive recipient of giving but the necessary condition by which the perfection of giving is brought to fulfilment**. In the conventional narrative of giving, the giver is active, the giver is the one who bestows, the giver is the one of merit. In the logic of T1577, by contrast, **the giver's accomplishment depends upon the beggar's existence** — without a beggar, *dāna-pāramitā* cannot stand. The beggar functions as the **enablement condition** of the giving-perfection, and this reverses the power relation of the gift: it is not that the giver "gives to" the beggar; it is that the beggar "gives to" the giver — gives the giver the chance to bring the perfection to fulfilment.

This reversal stands in symmetric correspondence to P10's "shift in the centre of the giver's agency from choice-of-field to choice-of-content." P10 traced the giver's active capacity moving from the recipient-axis ("which recipient to choose") to the content-axis ("what content to give"). P11 carries the analysis a step further: even within this active capacity, the giver remains *passively dependent* upon the beggar. The agency of giving is not a property of the giver alone; it is the **co-construction of the giver–beggar system**.

### 2.5 The Āgama Transition — T0125 *juan* 19's *Dāna-Pāramitā* Usage

In Āgama-stratum texts, the term "*dāna-pāramitā*" itself already appears, indicating a gradual transition rather than an abrupt innovation:

> 「菩薩摩訶薩成就幾法，而行檀波羅蜜，具足六波羅蜜，疾成無上正真之道？」[^12]
>
> "How many qualities must a bodhisattva-mahāsattva accomplish in order to practice *dāna-pāramitā*, fulfil the six perfections, and rapidly attain the unsurpassed and rightly-true Path?" (translation mine)

This passage occurs in a **bodhisattva context** within the *Ekottara-āgama* 《增一阿含經》 (T0125) — its register is not purely *śrāvaka-āgama*; it already carries Mahāyāna colouration. The fact suggests that the term "*dāna-pāramitā*" was likely already in circulation among certain Buddhist communities by around the second century CE — it is not a Mahāyāna *invention*.[^13]

## III. Core Argument

This section integrates the five strata of evidence into a four-layer argument: the structural innovation of the six-perfection framework (§3.1); *prajñā* as the transformative condition (§3.2); the systematicity of the six perfections (§3.3); the reversal of the power relation (§3.4). The four layers jointly support the paper's thesis: the *dāna* → *dāna-pāramitā* transformation is a paradigm shift driven by the intervention of *prajñā*.

### 3.1 The Structural Innovation of the Six-Perfection Framework

The eight-fascicle "*X-dù-wújí*" sequence of T0152 is not a natural extension of early Buddhist *dāna* theory but a **structural innovation**. Its innovation has three faces.

First, **the necessity of the sequence**. The early *dāna* taxonomies (MN 142, T0026 *juan* 47) do not take "perfection" (*pāramitā*) as their classificatory axis — neither the recipient-axis nor the content-axis carries a notion of "perfection." T0152, by contrast, builds an entirely new classificatory system out of six "perfections," each of which is at once an independent path of training and an inseparable member of the system. The introduction of this systematicity changes the ontological standing of giving: giving is no longer an isolated virtue but **the entry-point of the six-perfection system**.

Second, **the ontological significance of *dāna* as the head of the sequence**. *Dāna*'s placement at the head is not arbitrary — within the causal chain of the six perfections, *dāna* is the foundation for the other five (T1604: "the one without concern for wealth is then able to practice *śīla* and the others"). This foundational standing does not mean "most important" (*prajñā* is the ultimate condition of liberation), but "most basic": the sequence of the six perfections is **a graded movement from outer to inner, from coarse to subtle** — from the most externally accessible action (giving) to the most subtle interior awareness (*prajñā*). The sequence is itself a pedagogical design — start where everyone can begin (giving), proceed inward.

Third, **jātaka narrative as the substance of the perfection of giving**. T0152's *dāna-pāramitā* chapter is saturated with extreme self-giving jātaka narratives: Prince Vessantara giving away the kingdom, King Sattabhānu giving the flesh of his body, the deer-king substituting himself for prison. The substance of these narratives is precisely the practical archetype of *abhayadāna* that P10 established: protecting beings from terror with one's own body. But T0152 collects all such narratives under the single rubric "the chapter of the perfection of giving that ferries without limit" — it does not establish "body-giving" or "fearlessness-giving" as a discrete classificatory slot, but absorbs them as *sub-types* of "giving." This handling is at once the proof of T0152's narrative richness and the mark of its taxonomic limitation.

### 3.2 Prajñā as the Transformative Condition

T1509 *juan* 60's distinction "with-apprehension versus without-apprehension" is the core argument of P11: **prajñā as the transformative condition of giving**.

The precise mechanism: when the giver apprehends the three wheels (I give, that one receives, the thing given), then however large the gift's quantity, however venerable its recipient, however pure its motive, this giving remains only "attaining giving" (acquiring the merit of the gift) — **it is not** *dāna-pāramitā*. The ontological difference between merit (*puṇya*) and *pāramitā* does not lie in the external features of the gift — the same act of giving, by the difference in the structure of the giver's mind, generates a different ontological result.

The philosophical consequence is revolutionary. It means **the value of giving turns on the giver's wisdom (*prajñā*), not on the giver's moral intention**. A gift offered out of great compassion, if it apprehends the three wheels, remains merit; an apparently plain gift, if its mental structure is *trimaṇḍala-śūnya*, *is* the perfection. This observation overturns the traditional method of classifying giving by the moral content of the act — the category of giving is determined neither by *what* is given nor by *to whom*, but by *how it is given*.

The core citation from the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* T0220e *juan* 571 puts the mechanism most precisely:

> 「夫布施者若無般若波羅蜜多，但得施名非到彼岸，要由般若波羅蜜多乃得名為施到彼岸。」[^14]
>
> "As for the one who gives: without *prajñā-pāramitā*, the gift only attains the name of giving and is not 'reaching the other shore'; only by *prajñā-pāramitā* does it attain the name of 'giving that reaches the other shore.'" (translation mine)

The logical structure is: no *prajñā* → only the name of giving (not named *pāramitā*); with *prajñā* → named "giving that reaches the other shore" (named *pāramitā*). **Prajñā is at once the necessary and the sufficient condition of *dāna* becoming *dāna-pāramitā*.** The settling of this necessary-and-sufficient is the completion of *dāna-pāramitā* theory — giving is reclassified from "moral virtue" to **wisdom virtue**.[^15]

### 3.3 The Systematicity of the Six Perfections

T1604's "the six perfections mutually complete one another" institutes the systematicity of *dāna-pāramitā*: giving is not an autonomous perfection, but an inseparable member of the six-perfection system.

This systematicity has three consequences. First, **the completeness of any one perfection depends on the other five**: a true giving (the perfection of giving) must simultaneously be *śīla* (no taking what is not given), *kṣānti* (no aversion towards the recipient), *vīrya* (no retreat from giving), *dhyāna* (no distraction of mind), and *prajñā* (no apprehension of the three wheels). Lack any one, and this giving is incomplete and not yet a perfection. Second, **the non-linear interdependence of the six**: mutual inclusion (each perfection contains the other five), differentiation (each has its distinctive function), scriptural ground (the theoretical foundation lies in canonical texts), causation (*dāna* is the necessary condition for the other five).[^16] Third, **dāna as foundation, not as summit**: *dāna* is the entry-point of the six perfections, but not the final destination. *Prajñā* is the ultimate liberative condition; but *prajñā* must be embodied through the practice of *dāna* in order to be truly realized.[^17]

This systematicity stands in symmetric correspondence to P10's "the three-gift triad is born at the *śīla/dāna* interface." P10 showed that *abhayadāna* is not an internal expansion of giving but a reclassification at the *śīla/dāna* interface; P11 shows that *dāna-pāramitā* is not an enlargement of giving but a reclassification at the *dāna/prajñā* interface — both stage the birth of a new category at an *interface*.

### 3.4 The Reversal of the Power Relation

T1577's beggar-enablement thesis effects the most dramatic reversal of the power relation of the gift.

In the conventional narratives of giving (from the Āgamas to the early Mahāyāna), the giver is typically active, virtuous, and praiseworthy — the king who gives the kingdom, the householder who gives wealth, the bodhisattva who gives life and limb. The beggar (or recipient) is typically the **passive recipient**: she or he is in need, the giver supplies the need.

T1577 inverts this narrative through and through: **the beggar is not passive but enabling** — without a beggar, *dāna-pāramitā* cannot be brought to fulfilment. The giver's accomplishment is not a property the giver owns; it depends on what the beggar "gives" (gives the giver the chance to fulfil the perfection). The structure of this reversal is in deep symmetric correspondence with P10's "shift in the centre of the giver's agency from choice-of-field to choice-of-content": P10 traced the giver's active shift from recipient-axis to content-axis; P11 shows that, even within this active capacity, the giver remains **dependent** upon the beggar.

This dependence does not diminish the giver's dignity; it **expands the ethical scope of giving** — giving is no longer "the bestowal of the high upon the low," but **the co-construction of the giver–beggar system**. The ethical consequence of this co-construction is far-reaching: it challenges every theory of giving framed under the rubric of "charity" — in genuine *dāna-pāramitā*, there is no power relation of giver over recipient, only two practitioners in mutual interdependence.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Engagement

Three tracks of dialogue — Harrison, Nattier, Cousins — locate P11's scholarly position within the field.

**Harrison** dates T0152 to within the earliest Mahāyāna corpus (ca. first–second century CE) and reads its six-perfection framework as a structural feature of the jātaka collection rather than a doctrinal taxonomic move.[^18] The value of the position is the reminder it presses: T0152's six-perfection framework may inherit its structure from the jātaka literary tradition rather than constitute a Mahāyāna innovation. But Harrison underestimates the doctrinal significance of T0152's structural innovation — even granting that the "six perfections" framework comes from jātaka literature, the systematization of the six into a system of mutual completion is the work of T1604, and the "with-apprehension versus without-apprehension" distinction is the work of T1509; these innovations together constitute the completion of *dāna-pāramitā* theory.

**Nattier**'s textual-stratification work on T1509 has shown that the text is not the product of a single author.[^19] The value of the position is the caution it presses: the "with-apprehension versus without-apprehension" distinction may issue from different periods. But even granting the stratificational complexity, T1509 *juan* 60's distinction remains the earliest extant systematic *dāna-pāramitā* theory — its historical position is not undone by stratificational matters.[^20]

**Cousins** has marked the structural difference between the Pāli *pāramī* system (ten *pāramī*s) and the Sino-Tibetan six-*pāramitā* system.[^21] The value of the position is the warning it gives: the six perfections are not a direct translation of the Pāli system. But the semantics of "*pāramitā* = *pāra* + *-mitā*" is shared across all traditions; the choice of *six* (rather than ten) reflects the specific doctrinal priorities of the Mahāyāna — and in particular, the *dāna*–*śīla* dyad (T1592: "because of the antidote to non-action, *dāna-pāramitā* and *śīla-pāramitā* are taught") has no parallel in the Pāli system.

The conclusion of the three-track dialogue: the formation of *dāna-pāramitā* is **multi-source, gradual, and not the product of a single innovative move** — T0152's six-perfection framework, T1509's *prajñā* condition, T1604's systematicity, and T1577's reversal of power together constitute this paradigm shift.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading 1: *Dāna-pāramitā* is a Mahāyāna invention.

The use of "檀波羅蜜" (*dāna-pāramitā*) in T0125 *juan* 19 indicates that the term was likely already in circulation among certain Buddhist communities by around the second century CE. The formation of *dāna-pāramitā* is a gradual conceptual transformation, not a sudden Mahāyāna invention.[^22]

### ❌ Misreading 2: The six perfections = the Pāli *pāramī*s in translation.

The structural difference between the six perfections (six) and the Pāli ten *pāramī*s shows that the two are not in a relation of straightforward translation. The choice of six reflects the specific doctrinal priorities of the Mahāyāna — and in particular, the *dāna*–*śīla* dyad (T1592: "because of the antidote to non-action, *dāna-pāramitā* and *śīla-pāramitā* are taught") has no parallel in the Pāli system.[^23]

### ❌ Misreading 3: T0152's *dāna-pāramitā* chapter = a fully-formed Mahāyāna theory of giving.

T0152's *dāna-pāramitā* chapter expresses the perfection of giving through jātaka narrative, but its taxonomic limitation (every variety of giving is gathered under a single rubric, "the chapter of the perfection of giving that ferries without limit") indicates that it has not yet completed the theorization of *dāna-pāramitā*. The completion of theorization begins with T1509's "with-apprehension versus without-apprehension."[^24]

### ⚠️ Caution 1: Textual Stratification.

T1509 is not the product of a single author. P11 should not attribute the "with-apprehension versus without-apprehension" distinction to Nāgārjuna's personal innovation; the distinction may issue from an earlier *śāstra* tradition.[^25]

### ⚠️ Caution 2: Terminology.

"*Dù-wújí*" (度無極) as a Chinese rendering of *pāramitā* may not be Kāng Sēnghuì's coinage — but the chapter-structure "*X-dù-wújí*-章" is unique within the Chinese Buddhist canon. One should resist over-reading Kāng Sēnghuì's lexical choice as his own personal doctrinal innovation.[^26]

---

## VI. Conclusion

**Essentials.** The core of the *dāna* → *dāna-pāramitā* transformation is the intervention of *prajñā* (emptiness). T0152 preserves the structural innovation of the six-perfection framework — the eight-fascicle sequence with *dāna* at the head, jātaka narrative as the substance of the perfection of giving — but its taxonomic limitation (every variety of giving gathered under a single rubric) indicates that it has not yet completed the theorization of *dāna-pāramitā*. The completion begins with T1509's "with-apprehension versus without-apprehension": *prajñā* as the necessary-and-sufficient condition of giving — without *prajñā*, only the name of giving; with *prajñā*, named "giving that reaches the other shore." The mechanism of this transformation is not external (the quantity, recipient, or motive of the gift) but internal (the structure of the giver's mind: *trimaṇḍala-sahita* versus *trimaṇḍala-śūnya*). T1604's "the six perfections mutually complete one another" institutes the systematicity of the six perfections — every perfection depends on the other five; *dāna* is foundation but not summit. T1577's beggar-enablement thesis reverses the power relation of giving — the beggar is not the passive recipient but the *enabling condition* of *dāna-pāramitā*.

**Practical implication.** The most immediate landing for the contemporary reader: every act of giving practiced under the mind of "without apprehension" *is* the touchdown of *dāna-pāramitā* — no apprehension of giver, recipient, or thing given. The practice does not turn on outward generosity but on inward wisdom. *Dāna-pāramitā* is not the kingdom of the wealthy and the generous but the kingdom of the wise — and wisdom in this sense is not abstract metaphysical insight but the moment-to-moment letting-go of the apprehension of the three wheels.

**Open question → handoff to P12.** How is the structure of *trimaṇḍala-śūnya* systematically unfolded in Nāgārjuna's *dāna-pāramitā* chapter?[^27] If P10 was the *crystallization* of the third slot, and P11 is the *transformation* of the giving condition, then P12 (and P13 in tandem) will trace the *philosophical articulation* of the three-wheels-emptied operation as Nāgārjuna develops it within the *Dà zhìdù lùn*'s *dāna-pāramitā* chapter — and from that articulation outward, into the larger architecture of early Mahāyāna emptiness-thought.
