**The *Dà zhìdù lùn*'s Treatise on the Perfection of Giving I**

## Abstract (中文版)

「渡河者未達而返，名為不到彼岸。」[^1] 《大智度論》卷十二以舍利弗六十劫行菩薩道、因乞者索眼而退轉小乘的故事，為「不到彼岸」繪製了一幅精準的肖像。當施者執著「我與、彼受、所施者財」，即便六十劫的極端身施，依然墮入魔境界。反之，「三種清淨，無此三礙」，即便一縷布、一碗飯的布施，也是檀波羅蜜。此區分不是功德多寡的量的差異，而是**施的本體論條件的質的轉換**——此即 P11 所確立的「般若條件」的具體展開。

本文論證：T1509 檀波羅蜜章（卷十一至卷十二）是漢傳佛教第一個完整的施波羅蜜理論體系。它以卷十一的四章結構（檀波羅蜜義→讚檀波羅蜜義→檀相義→法施義）建立了從定義、功德、分類到定位的完整框架，並以卷十二「三事不可得」的完整歸謬論證確立了三輪體空的哲學基礎。T1509 的核心貢獻有三：（1）將 P11 確立的「般若條件」從抽象命題展開為具體的因明式論證（名相論→三有→分破空→觀空）；（2）將「有所得/無所得」從施擴展為六度的共通條件；（3）以「阿羅漢到彼岸（涅槃）≠ 菩薩到波羅蜜（一切物一切時一切種的無限條件）」的嚴格區分，破除了將 *pāramitā* 理解為道德完善的誤讀。dāna-pāramitā 在本卷的核心定義不是「更多的施」，而是「三輪體空的施」。

**關鍵詞：** 檀波羅蜜、dāna-pāramitā、三輪體空、三事不可得、有所得、無所得、涅槃之初緣、六度、假名有、分破空、觀空、T1509 檀波羅蜜章、T1509 卷60 校量法施品、T1604 大乘莊嚴經論、三輪有、彼岸雙重含義、阿羅漢到彼岸、P11 般若條件、六度普遍化

## Abstract

"The river-crosser who turns back before reaching the other shore is called 'one who has not reached pāramitā.'" (T1509 vol 12) The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509) fascicles 11–12 constitute the first systematic treatise on dāna-pāramitā in Chinese Mahāyāna. This paper argues that T1509's four-chapter structure (檀波羅蜜義 → 讚檀波羅蜜義 → 檀相義 → 法施義) establishes a complete framework of definition, merit, taxonomy, and positioning, while fascicle 12's reductio ad absurdum argumentation of 三事不可得 (giver, receiver, and gift all empty of inherent existence) provides the philosophical foundation for 三輪體空. T1509's core contribution is threefold: (1) transforming P11's abstract "prajñā as condition" into a step-by-step argument (name → three kinds of existence → decomposition → contemplative emptiness); (2) universalizing the with-apprehension/without-apprehension distinction from dāna alone to all six perfections; (3) rigorously distinguishing "arhat reaching the other shore (nirvāṇa)" from "bodhisattva reaching pāramitā (the infinite condition of giving everything, at all times, in all ways)"—this semantic correction defeats the Western reduction of *pāramitā* to moral perfection. dāna-pāramitā is not "more giving" but "giving with the three wheels emptied."

**Keywords:** dāna-pāramitā, 檀波羅蜜, trilakṣaṇa-śūnya 三輪體空, 三事不可得, 有所得, 無所得, pratham-pratyaya-nirvāṇasya 涅槃之初緣, ṣaṭ-pāramitā 六度, prajñapti-sat 假名有, deśana-śūnyatā 分破空, upalambha-śūnyatā 觀空, T1509 檀波羅蜜章, T1509 vol 60 校量法施品, T1604 大乘莊嚴經論, trilakṣaṇa-sahita 三輪有, dual meaning of pāra 彼岸, arhat pāra vs bodhisattva pāramitā, P11 prajñā condition, six-perfection universalization

## I. Framing

"The river-crosser who turns back before reaching the other shore is called 'one who has not reached pāramitā.'"[^2] This passage from the opening of fascicle 12 of the *Dà zhìdù lùn* 《大智度論》 (T1509) is not the beginning of a philosophical argument but a story: Śāriputra practiced the bodhisattva path for sixty kalpas, with the perfection of giving as the first. A beggar came and asked for his eye. Śāriputra gave it; the beggar, after smelling it and finding it offensive, spat on it and trampled it underfoot. Śāriputra reflected: "Such wretched beings are hard to save! The eye is truly useless and yet was demanded; once received, it was cast aside and trampled. What degradation! Beings like this cannot be saved. Better to train oneself and escape saṃsāra." And so he turned back to the *śrāvakayāna*.[^3]

T1509 uses this story as the defining illustration of "not reaching pāramitā."[^4] It then states positively: "If giving has three obstructions — 'I give, that one receives, the thing given' — this is falling into Māra's domain, not yet freed from difficulty. If a bodhisattva gives with three kinds of purity, free of these three obstructions, reaching the other shore, praised by the buddhas — this is called dāna-pāramitā."[^5]

This is the most precise anchor for the paper. The distinction between "not reaching pāramitā" and "reaching pāramitā" does not turn on the quantity, intensity, or even duration of giving (sixty kalpas of extreme bodily giving). It turns on the **structure of the giver's mind**: apprehending the three wheels (giver, receiver, gift) as inherently existent — falling into Māra's domain; seeing the three wheels as empty — reaching the other shore.

This observation carries forward from P11. P11 established "prajñā as the transformative condition of giving" — without prajñā, giving is only merit; with prajñā, giving becomes pāramitā.[^6] But "how exactly does prajñā transform giving into pāramitā?" — this question, anchored by T0220e *juan* 571 "the one who gives, without prajñāpāramitā, attains only the name of giving and is not yet 'reaching the other shore,'" was handed to P12.[^7] P12's answer is: **T1509, in its fascicles 11–12, establishes a complete system from philosophical foundation to practical method, answering "how to implement the prajñā condition."**

§II lays out seven strata of evidence: the four chapters of fascicle 11 (檀波羅蜜義 → 讚檀波羅蜜義 → 檀相義 → 法施義); fascicle 12's argumentation of the unobtainability of the three matters and the definition of reaching the other shore; the application of with-apprehension / without-apprehension to all six perfections in *juan* 60; and T1604 *juan* 8's six-aspect system as a symmetric counterpart. §III unfolds the core argument across four layers — the four-chapter structure of the dāna-pāramitā treatise, the complete argumentation of the three-wheel emptiness, the universalization of the six perfections, and the dual meaning of "the other shore." §IV opens a three-track dialogue with Nattier, Harrison, and comparative Buddhist studies. §V addresses three misreadings and two cautions. §VI restates the essentials, offers practical guidance, and hands off to P13.

## II. Canonical Sources

This section lays evidence in seven strata corresponding to the four-layer core argument of §III. Following the discipline of the DĀNA volume, Mahāyāna *śāstra* materials are cited by Taishō volume and fascicle. Italicized Sanskrit terms carry a Chinese gloss on first occurrence, then stand alone.

### 2.1 Dāna-Pāramitā as Definition — T1509 Fascicle 11, Chapter 17

The Dà zhìdù lùn 《大智度論》 (T1509) opens its treatment of dāna-pāramitā at fascicle 11, chapter 17, with a single defining formula:

> 「菩薩摩訶薩以不住法住般若波羅蜜中，以無所捨法具足檀波羅蜜，施者、受者及財物不可得故。」[^8]

> "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, dwells in prajñāpāramitā without clinging to any dharma; fulfills dāna-pāramitā through non-apprehension of the given — because the giver, the receiver, and the gift are all unobtainable." (translation mine)

This is the most precise definition of dāna-pāramitā in the text. Three key terms: "不住法" (not clinging to any dharma), "無所捨法" (not apprehending the given), and "三事不可得" (the three matters — giver, receiver, gift — all unobtainable). The three actually point to one thing — **trilakṣaṇa-śūnya** (三輪體空, the three wheels emptied).

The definitional structure is causal: because the three matters are unobtainable, dāna-pāramitā is fulfilled. The "unobtainable" does not negate the existence of giving; it negates the inherent existence of the three wheels of giving (giver, receiver, gift). This is the operation of emptiness-wisdom within giving itself — not a reflection appended after the act, but **the necessary and sufficient condition of giving becoming pāramitā**.

### 2.2 Praising Dāna-Pāramitā — T1509 Fascicle 11, Chapter 18

Fascicle 11, chapter 18 (讚檀波羅蜜義) lists a function-catalog of giving:

> 「檀為寶藏，常隨逐人；檀為破苦，能與人樂；檀為善御，開示天道；檀為善府，攝諸善人；檀為安隱，臨命終時心不怖畏；檀為慈相，能濟一切；檀為集樂，能破苦賊；檀為大將，能伏慳敵；檀為妙果，天人所愛；檀為淨道，賢聖所遊；檀為積善福德之門；檀為立事聚眾之緣；檀為善行愛果之種；檀為福業善人之相；檀破貧窮、斷三惡道；檀能全護福樂之果；**檀為涅槃之初緣**；入善人聚中之要法；稱譽讚歎之淵府；入眾無難之功德；心不悔恨之窟宅；善法道行之根本；種種歡樂之林藪；富貴安隱之福田；得道涅槃之津梁；聖人大士智者之所行；餘人儉德寡識之所效。」[^9]

> "Giving is a treasury that always follows the giver; giving breaks suffering and gives joy; giving is a good chariot that opens the heavenly path; giving is a good vault that gathers all the good; giving is安稳, without fear at the time of death; giving is a mark of compassion that saves all; giving gathers joy and destroys the thief of suffering; giving is a great general that subdues the enemy of stinginess; giving is a wondrous fruit loved by gods and humans; giving is a pure path walked by sages; giving is the gate of accumulated merit; giving is the condition for building affairs and gathering followers; giving is the seed of good conduct and beloved fruit; giving is the mark of a good person engaged in meritorious deed; giving destroys poverty and severs the three evil paths; giving preserves the fruit of fortune and joy; **giving is the initial condition for nirvāṇa**; the essential dharma for entering the assembly of the good; the abyss of praise and acclaim; the merit of entering crowds without difficulty; the home of a heart without regret; the root of all good dharmas and the path of cultivation; the forest of all kinds of joy; the good field of wealth and安稳; the ford for attaining the Path and nirvāṇa; what sages and great beings practice; what others of small virtue emulate." (translation mine)

The structure of this catalog is significant: the first half describes worldly merit (wealth, security, reputation), the middle shifts toward transcendent liberation (initial condition for nirvāṇa, root of good dharmas), and the final section closes with "what sages and great beings practice." This is a **continuous spectrum from the worldly to the transcendent** — giving is not a moral virtue opposed to the世俗, but a channel **贯通 between the two**.

The most critical item here is "檀為涅槃之初緣" (giving is the initial condition for nirvāṇa). T1509 establishes a causal chain: recollecting giving → joy → one-pointed mind → contemplation of arising and ceasing → attaining the Path.[^10] Giving is not the replacement for liberation (a transaction of merit for liberation) but the **initial cause** — without the joy that giving generates, the subsequent samādhi and contemplation cannot arise.

### 2.3 The Characteristics of Dāna — T1509 Fascicle 11, Chapter 19

Fascicle 11, chapter 19 (檀相義) establishes a taxonomy of giving:

> 「問曰：云何名檀？答曰：檀名布施。心相應善思故。」[^11]

> "Q: What is called dāna? A: Dāna means giving. It is the wholesome thought associated with the mind." (translation mine)

T1509 first defines dāna as a psychological event (wholesome thought associated with the mind), not merely an external act. It then describes the世俗 conditions of dāna with "five matters converging": "having faith, having a field of merit, having wealth — when these three converge, the thought of letting-arise, and stinginess is destroyed."[^12]

A crucial question arises: if dāna requires "five matters converging" (giver, receiver, gift, field, timing), how can T1509 simultaneously assert "the three matters are unobtainable"?

T1509's response is the core argument of fascicle 12 — first acknowledging the three matters as **conventionally existent** (prajñapti-sat, 假名有), then deconstructing their **inherently existent** (dharma-sat, 法有) status through dependent-origins analysis. This is standard Mādhyamaka methodology, but its **systematic application to dāna theory**, T1509 is the first.

### 2.4 Dharma-Giving Surpasses Thing-Giving — T1509 Fascicle 11, Chapter 20

Fascicle 11, chapter 20 (檀波羅蜜法施義) argues that dharma-giving surpasses thing-giving:

> 「施之法，有佛無佛，世間常有；如法施者，唯有菩薩。」[^13]

> "The dharma of giving — whether buddhas appear or not — exists in the world. But the one who gives dharma — only a bodhisattva." (translation mine)

The primacy of dharma-giving does not lie in it being "higher" than thing-giving but in it being **the bodhisattva's unique giving** — thing-giving is shared by the three vehicles (śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, and bodhisattva can all practice it), while dharma-giving belongs exclusively to the bodhisattva. This establishes the bodhisattva-character of giving: not the giving of things but the giving of dharma.

### 2.5 Filling Dāna-Pāramitā — T1509 Fascicle 12: The Complete Argumentation of Three Matters Unobtainable

Fascicle 12 contains the longest philosophical argumentation on trilakṣaṇa-śūnya in the entire treatise. Its central question:

> 「檀波羅蜜中言：『財、施、受者，三事不可得。』問曰：三事和合，故名為檀；今言三事不可得，云何名『檀波羅蜜具足滿』？今有財、有施、有受者，云何三事不可得？如所施疊實有。」[^14]

> "The dāna-pāramitā chapter says: 'wealth, giving, and receiver — the three matters are unobtainable.' Question: The three matters converge, therefore it is called dāna; now you say the three matters are unobtainable, how is 'dāna-pāramitā fulfilled and complete'? There is wealth, there is giving, there is a receiver — how can the three matters be unobtainable? The given cloth is surely real." (translation mine)

T1509's argumentation proceeds in four steps:

**Step 1: Name-argumentation.** Against "because there is a name, there is an entity," T1509 replies with counterexamples: "A certain grass is named 'zhulì' (thief), but the grass does not steal or rob — it is not truly a thief but called 'thief.' Again, rabbit horns and tortoise hair — they have names but no substance."[^15] Name existence does not entail entity existence.

**Step 2: Three kinds of existence.** T1509 distinguishes three levels of "existence": **apekṣā-sat** (相待有, existence by relativity, like long/short), **prajñapti-sat** (假名有, conventionally existent by the convergence of conditions, like curd), and **dharma-sat** (法有, existence by inherent nature). The gift (using cloth as example) belongs to the second level — prajñapti-sat: because conditions converge, it exists; because conditions scatter, it does not.[^16] This does not say the gift does not exist; it says the gift's mode of existence is not inherent nature but dependent origination.

**Step 3: Decomposition-emptiness (deśana-śūnyatā, 分破空).** Using atomistic analysis to deconstruct the last thread of inherent existence of the gift: if the gift has inherent nature, it should be indivisible; but if divisible, it has no independent inherent nature. Further, if indivisible (paramāṇu / atom), the atom must have spatial extension (十方分) to possess color, smell, taste, and touch — but if it has spatial extension, it is divisible, therefore not an atom.[^17] **This is the sharpest argumentative step** — it cuts off any possibility of using paramāṇu (atom) as a basis for inherent nature.

**Step 4: Contemplative emptiness (upalambha-śūnyatā, 觀空).** Using the observer's cognitive variation to demonstrate that the attributes of the gift do not reside in the object itself but in the observer. "To a lustful person, a beautiful form appears pure and delightful; to one practicing impurity-contemplation, the same form appears as various foulnesses" — the same object, different observers, different attributes. The value, meaning, and even the存在感 of the gift depend on the observer's psychological construction.[^18]

The argumentation for the giver proceeds identically: "Four elements surrounding emptiness, called body; the bodily consciousness moves, comes, goes, sits, rises — conventionally called 'person.'"[^19] The giver has no inherent nature. The receiver is the same.

### 2.6 With-Apprehension / Without-Apprehension — The Universal Condition of the Six Perfections

*Juan* 60 (校量法施品) extends the with-apprehension / without-apprehension distinction from dāna alone to all six pāramitā:

> 「若菩薩摩訶薩用有所得故布施，布施時作是念：『我與，彼受，所施者物。』是名得檀，不得波羅蜜；『我持戒，此是戒。』是名得戒，不得波羅蜜；『我忍辱，為是人忍辱。』是名得忍辱，不得波羅蜜；『我精進，為是事懃精進。』是名得精進，不得波羅蜜；『我修禪，所修是禪。』是名得禪，不得波羅蜜；『我修慧，所修是慧。』是名得慧，不得波羅蜜。」[^20]

> "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva gives with apprehension, thinking at the moment of giving, 'I give, that one receives, the thing given' — this is called *attaining dāna*, not *attaining the pāramitā*... 'I observe precepts, this is the precept' — this is called *attaining śīla*, not *attaining the pāramitā*... 'I practice patience, for this person I practice patience' — this is called *attaining kṣānti*, not *attaining the pāramitā*... 'I practice energy, for this matter I practice energy' — this is called *attaining vīrya*, not *attaining the pāramitā*... 'I practice dhyāna, what I practice is dhyāna' — this is called *attaining dhyāna*, not *attaining the pāramitā*... 'I practice wisdom, what I practice is wisdom' — this is called *attaining prajñā*, not *attaining the pāramitā*." (translation mine)

This is the most crucial of the extensions: trilakṣaṇa-śūnya is not a condition unique to dāna but **the common necessary-and-sufficient condition of all six pāramitā**. Each perfection operates in exactly the same way — apprehending the three wheels means "attaining X, not attaining the pāramitā"; not apprehending the three wheels means "fulfilling the pāramitā of X."[^21] The universalization of prajñā over all pāramitā is thus completed.

### 2.7 The Six-Aspect System — T1604 as Symmetric Counterpart

T1604 *juan* 8 (二度攝品) offers a six-aspect analysis of dāna-pāramitā:

> 「施彼及共思，二成亦二攝，具住不慳故，法財無畏三。」[^22]

> "Giving to others and the thought with it; two-fold attainment and two-fold grasping; abiding constantly without stinginess; the three of dharma, wealth, and fearlessness." (translation mine)

The six aspects are: svabhāva (giving to others = offering one's own wealth to recipients), hetu (the thought-with = non-greed wholesome root and citta-saṃskāra arising together), phala (two-fold attainment = wealth-attainment and body-attainment), karma (two-fold grasping = self-grasping and other-grasping both satisfied), samprayukta (abiding constantly without stinginess = continuously dwelling in the heart of one who does not sting), and prakāra (the three of dharma-giving, wealth-giving, fearlessness-giving).[^23]

This positive-taxonomy six-aspect system stands in symmetric correspondence to T1509's trilakṣaṇa-śūnya — T1509 defines pāramitā through **deconstruction** (negation), T1604 through **construction** (positive taxonomy). Their intersection is "non-greed wholesome root": T1509's "無所捨法" and T1604's "具住不慳" point to the same psychological state — the mind of giving without apprehension.

## III. Core Argument

This section integrates the seven strata of evidence from §II into a four-layer argument: the four-chapter structure of the dāna-pāramitā treatise (§3.1); the complete argumentation of trilakṣaṇa-śūnya (§3.2); the universalization of the six perfections (§3.3); the dual meaning of "the other shore" (§3.4). The four layers jointly support the paper's thesis: T1509's dāna-pāramitā treatise constitutes the first systematic treatise on dāna-pāramitā in Chinese Mahāyāna — with trilakṣaṇa-śūnya as its core mechanism, with-apprehension / without-apprehension as its discriminative standard, and initial-condition-for-nirvāṇa as its causal positioning.

### 3.1 The Four-Chapter Structure of the Dāna-Pāramitā Treatise

The dāna-pāramitā treatise of T1509 fascicle 11 consists of four consecutive chapters: chapter 17 (檀波羅蜜義), chapter 18 (讚檀波羅蜜義), chapter 19 (檀相義), and chapter 20 (檀波羅蜜法施義).[^24] These four chapters are not a random arrangement but a carefully designed theoretical system:

**Chapter 17 is the definition** — establishing the ontological condition of dāna-pāramitā through "the three matters are unobtainable." This step is **necessary but not sufficient**: knowing trilakṣaṇa-śūnya alone is not enough to practice dāna-pāramitā; one also needs to understand why and how.

**Chapter 18 is the warrant** — answering "why practice dāna-pāramitā?" through an exhaustive catalog of its merits. T1509's strategy begins with worldly merit (treasury, breaking suffering,安稳), gradually shifts to transcendent merit (initial-condition-for-nirvāṇa, root of good dharmas), and culminates in "what sages and great beings practice." This is a **continuous spectrum from the ordinary to the saintly** — giving is not a "noble act" opposed to the世俗 but a channel **贯通 between the two**.

**Chapter 19 is the taxonomy** — the transition from "five matters converging" to "three matters unobtainable" establishes the **transition method** from世俗 giving to pāramitā. This is not jumping from one world to another but re-viewing the same giving event — from the perspective of apprehension, it is five matters converging; from the perspective of emptiness, it is three matters unobtainable.

**Chapter 20 is the positioning** — dharma-giving surpasses thing-giving, establishing the bodhisattva-character of giving. This is the final positioning: giving is not an independent virtue but inextricably bound to dharma-giving as a bodhisattva practice.

The internal logic of the four chapters is: definition → warrant → taxonomy → positioning. This is a complete theoretical system — from "what is dāna-pāramitā?" to "what position does dāna-pāramitā hold in the bodhisattva path?"

### 3.2 The Complete Argumentation of Trilakṣaṇa-śūnya

The "prajñā condition" established by P11, in T1509 fascicle 12, is no longer a proposition but a complete argument. Its core is answering a seemingly simple question: "The three matters converge, therefore it is called dāna; now you say the three matters are unobtainable, how is dāna-pāramitā fulfilled and complete?"[^25]

T1509's response is a four-step reductio — each step building on the previous, each progressively deeper:

**Step 1: Name-deconstruction.** The most intuitive argument is "because there is a name, there must be an entity" — if the word "gift" exists, then the gift must exist. T1509's counter: name existence does not entail entity existence. Using "grass named thief" — grass is not a thief, merely called thief; using "rabbit horns and tortoise hair" — name without substance.[^26] The key move is cutting the direct mapping between language and existence.

**Step 2: Three-existence analysis.** T1509 proposes three levels of "existence": existence by relativity (apekṣā-sat, 相待有), conventional existence by condition-convergence (prajñapti-sat, 假名有), and existence by inherent nature (dharma-sat, 法有). The gift belongs to the second level — prajñapti-sat.[^27] This does not negate the gift's existence but reclassifies its mode: existing because conditions converge, ceasing because conditions scatter.

**Step 3: Decomposition-emptiness.** Using atomistic analysis to deconstruct the last thread of inherent existence of the gift: if the gift has inherent nature, it should be indivisible; but if divisible, it has no independent inherent nature. Further, if indivisible (paramāṇu), the atom must have spatial extension (十方分) to possess color, smell, taste, and touch — but spatial extension entails divisibility, therefore not an atom.[^28] **This is the sharpest step** — it cuts off any possibility of using paramāṇu (atom) as the basis for inherent nature.

**Step 4: Contemplative emptiness.** Using the observer's cognitive variation to demonstrate that the attributes of the gift do not reside in the object itself. "To a lustful person, beautiful form appears pure and delightful; to one practicing impurity-contemplation, the same form appears as foulness" — the same object, different observers, different attributes. The value, meaning, and even the存在感 of the gift depend on the observer's psychological construction.[^29]

The structure of this four-step argument is: first acknowledge世俗 existence (prajñapti-sat), then demonstrate emptiness of inherent nature (deśana-śūnyatā), finally proving non-inherence through contemplative emptiness (upalambha-śūnyatā). This is the standard Mādhyamaka argumentative methodology — but before T1509, **no text had systematically applied this methodology to dāna theory**.

### 3.3 The Universalization of the Six Perfections — Trilakṣaṇa-śūnya as the Common Condition

The most crucial extension of *juan* 60: extending the with-apprehension / without-apprehension distinction from dāna alone to all six pāramitā.

The theoretical significance is enormous. P11 established prajñā as the transformative condition of dāna, but did not yet answer: if prajñā itself is the sixth pāramitā, how do the first five acquire prajñā? If the six perfections are circular — dāna needs prajñā to become pāramitā, prajñā needs dāna as its object — then where lies the systematicity of the six?

T1509's response: **trilakṣaṇa-śūnya is the common necessary-and-sufficient condition of all six perfections.**[^30] Each perfection operates identically — apprehending the three wheels means "attaining X, not attaining the pāramitā"; not apprehending means "fulfilling the pāramitā of X."[^31] This means the six perfections are not six independent virtues but **one transformation mechanism applied across six domains**.

This systematization is revolutionary. Before T1509 (such as T0152's Liù dù jí jīng), the six perfections were six parallel practices. In T1509, the six perfections become **one mechanism with six aspects** — trilakṣaṇa-śūnya. This also answers the question P11 left open: dāna's priority among the six perfections is not temporal (give first, then keep precepts) but **logical** — giving with trilakṣaṇa-śūnya is the entry-point of all bodhisattva practice.

### 3.4 The Dual Meaning of "The Other Shore" — Defeating the Moral-Perfection Misreading

Another key contribution of T1509 fascicle 12: distinguishing "arhat reaching the other shore" from "bodhisattva reaching pāramitā":

> 「阿羅漢、辟支佛渡彼岸，與佛渡彼岸，名同而實異。彼以生死為此岸，涅槃為彼岸，而不能渡檀之彼岸。所以者何？不能以一切物、一切時、一切種布施。」[^32]

> "The arhat and pratyekabuddha reaching the other shore, with the buddha reaching the other shore — the name is the same but the reality differs. They take saṃsāra as this shore and nirvāṇa as the other shore, but cannot cross the other shore of dāna. Why? Because they cannot give everything, at all times, in all ways." (translation mine)

This is the most crucial insight for the Western reader. In Western Buddhist studies, *pāramitā* is typically translated as "perfection," leading to a deeply ingrained misreading: pāramitā is "higher moral perfection."

T1509 explicitly deconstructs this misreading. **The arhat does reach the other shore — but the arhat's other shore is nirvāṇa (leaving saṃsāra), while the bodhisattva's other shore is pāramitā (the infinite condition).**[^33] The本义 of pāramitā is not "higher moral standard" but "**the infinite condition**" — everything (inner and outer), all times (without exception), all ways (all methods).[^34]

"The other shore" (pāra) has three definitional levels in T1509:[^35] (1) crossing the river to the other shore (metaphor); (2) the task accomplished (世俗义); (3) this shore = stinginess, the river = dāna, the other shore = buddhahood (教義义).[^34] The third level is the most crucial: the other shore is not the opposite shore (a separate place from this shore) but **the capacity to cross the river itself**. Stinginess is the this shore (riverbank), giving is the river (the crossing), buddhahood is the other shore (the destination). The three are not three separate places but three phases of one crossing event.

The implication for the Western reader is profound: *dāna-pāramitā* is not "perfect giving" but **"giving that makes giving an infinite condition"** — everything (inner and outer), all times (uninterrupted), all ways (all methods).[^36] The本义 of "pāramitā" is "the tool for crossing the river" (栰),[^37] not "moral perfection on the other shore of the river."

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Engagement

Three tracks of dialogue — Nattier, Harrison, and comparative Buddhist studies — locate P12's scholarly position within the field.

**Nattier**'s textual-stratification work on T1509 has shown that the text is not the product of a single author or a single period but a multi-layer accumulation.[^38] The dāna-pāramitā treatise of fascicle 11 may have different sources — chapter 17's definition formula may derive from the early prajñā tradition, chapter 18's merit catalog may have circulated independently as a Mahāyāna hymn tradition, chapter 19's argumentation may have absorbed abhidharma-instrumental techniques.

**Even granting complex stratification, T1509's systematization as an extant text is undeniable.** The internal logic of the four chapters of fascicle 11 (definition → warrant → taxonomy → positioning) and the rigorous structure of the four-step reductio in fascicle 12 demonstrate that, even if the materials have diverse sources, the final redactor of T1509 integrated them into a **single theoretical system**. Systematization itself is theoretical innovation — taking scattered dāna theory (the jātaka narratives of T0152, the three-meritorious activities of the Āgama, the僧伽-field of the Vinaya) and integrating them into a theory centered on trilakṣaṇa-śūnya.

**Harrison** positions T1509 as a synthetic text of early Mahāyāna.[^39] This does not mean T1509 has no innovation, but that its innovation lies not in "inventing new doctrine" but in **organizing the existing scattered materials into an operable system**. The dāna-pāramitā treatise of T1509 is not "Nāgārjuna's thought" but Nāgārjuna's (or the redactor's) **systematic reconstruction** of early Mahāyāna dāna theory — from narrative to philosophy, from scattered to system, from plurality to unity.

The value of this systematization is that it provides bodhisattva practitioners with a **clear operational framework**. Trilakṣaṇa-śūnya is no longer a mysterious proposition but a philosophical construction that can be progressively argued, progressively understood.

**Comparative Buddhist studies:** The Pāli tradition's cāgānussati (recollection of giving) is one of the six recollections and has superficial similarity with T1509's trilakṣaṇa-śūnya — both involve reflection on "letting go." But they are fundamentally different in kind.

cāgānussati's moral recollection — "I have given up what I loved" — is a **meditation practice that reinforces the psychology of generosity**. Its premise is the世俗 efficacy of giving: I indeed gave, the other indeed received, merit indeed arose.[^40]

T1509's trilakṣaṇa-śūnya, by contrast, is a **philosophical analysis of the ontological condition of giving** — not denying世俗 existence (prajñapti-sat) but further asking: do the giver, the receiver, and the gift have inherent nature? The answer is no.[^41] This is not a meditation technique but an inferential argument.

The root of the difference lies in methodology: the Pāli tradition's handling of dependent-origination (the twelve-links formula) provides the analytical foundation but never systematically applies this methodology to dāna theory. T1509 is the first text to complete this application.[^42]

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading 1: Trilakṣaṇa-śūnya is Nāgārjuna's "invention."

**❌ Misreading:** Trilakṣaṇa-śūnya is a unique invention of Nāgārjuna (or T1509) with no precedent in prior tradition.

**Correction:** The methodological foundation of trilakṣaṇa-śūnya — dependent-origination of phenomena, absence of inherent nature — was already mature in the Āgama tradition. T1509's innovation lies not in trilakṣaṇa-śūnya itself but in the **systematic application of this methodology to dāna theory**. No prior text had conducted such a complete inferential argumentation on the three wheels of giving (giver, receiver, gift).[^43]

### ❌ Misreading 2: *Pāramitā* = moral perfection.

**❌ Misreading:** Pāramitā is "moral perfection"; dāna-pāramitā is "perfect giving."

**Correction:** T1509 explicitly distinguishes arhat reaching the other shore (nirvāṇa) from bodhisattva reaching pāramitā (infinite condition).[^44] The本义 of pāramitā is "the tool for crossing the river" (栰),[^45] and its core meaning is "everything, at all times, in all ways" — the infinite condition — not a moral standard. Western readers should understand *pāramitā* as "infinite condition" rather than "perfection."[^46]

### ❌ Misreading 3: The Śāriputra-turned-back story is a historical fact.

**❌ Misreading:** Śāriputra indeed practiced the bodhisattva path for sixty kalpas and then turned back to the *śrāvakayāna* because of the beggar's eye-demand — this is a historical event.

**Correction:** Śāriputra's turning back to the *śrāvakayāna* is a common narrative motif in Buddhist literature, appearing across multiple sectarian texts.[^47] T1509's use of this story functions as the defining illustration of "not reaching pāramitā" — a literary device, not a historical record. Reading the narrative as history misses T1509's argumentative intent: Śāriputra turned back not because the beggar was wretched, but because Śāriputra apprehended the three wheels **at the very moment of giving**.[^48]

### ⚠️ Caution 1: Textual Stratification.

T1509 fascicles 11–12 may have layered sources. Chapter 17's definition formula may derive from the early prajñā tradition; chapter 18's merit catalog may have circulated independently; chapter 19's argumentation may have absorbed abhidharma instrumental techniques. Nattier's stratification analysis remains applicable: T1509's dāna-pāramitā treatise is a **multi-layered systematization**, not the original work of a single author.[^49]

### ⚠️ Caution 2: Terminology — "檀" vs "施."

T1509 consistently uses "檀" rather than "施" throughout. This is Kumārajīva's translation convention — "檀" as a phonetic transliteration preserves the original phonetic texture, differing stylistically from the later義translation "施."[^50] P12 follows T1509's original use of "檀" in the Chinese text and distinguishes phonetic transliteration (檀 = dāna) from義translation (施 = giving) in conceptual discussion.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essentials.** T1509's dāna-pāramitā treatise constitutes the first complete theoretical system on dāna-pāramitā in Chinese Mahāyāna. Through its four-chapter structure (檀波羅蜜義 → 讚檀波羅蜜義 → 檀相義 → 法施義), it establishes a complete framework of definition, merit, taxonomy, and positioning; through fascicle 12's four-step reductio argumentation of 三事不可得 (name → three existences → decomposition → contemplative emptiness), it establishes the philosophical foundation of trilakṣaṇa-śūnya. Its core contributions: transforming P11's abstract "prajñā condition" into concrete inferential argumentation; extending with-apprehension / without-apprehension from dāna to all six perfections as a common condition; and defeating the moral-perfection misreading through the rigorous distinction between "arhat reaching the other shore (nirvāṇa) ≠ bodhisattva reaching pāramitā (the infinite condition)."

**Practical guidance.** The most immediate lesson for contemporary practitioners: every act of giving — even the smallest — can become pāramitā, and the key does not lie in the quantity or intensity of the gift but in whether one contemplates trilakṣaṇa-śūnya. T1509 provides a concrete method: beginning with name-deconstruction, progressively understanding that the giver, the receiver, and the gift are all without inherent nature, yet not negating their conventional existence.[^51] This is a **middle way that transcends the世俗 without negating it**.

**Open question → handoff to P13.** Where does the theoretical foundation of trilakṣaṇa-śūnya come from — from where does it arise? P13 will follow this open question, tracing the argumentative development of trilakṣaṇa-śūnya after Nāgārjuna — particularly how it is transformed within the Yogācāra system into the "ten pure aspects" of giving.[^52]
