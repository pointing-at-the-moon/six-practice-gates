# The Sīha Sutta
**Present-Life Visible Fruits of Giving and the Four-Plus-One Epistemic Structure**

*中文主題：師子經——施的現世可見果與四加一的認識論結構*

---

## Abstract

Contemporary research on prosocial spending in positive psychology — Dunn, Aknin, and Norton (2008, 2013); Aknin and colleagues' cross-cultural verification across one hundred and thirty-six countries — has empirically established that "the giver gains well-being in the present moment," while the West simultaneously circulates the stereotype that "Buddhism defers the reward of giving to a future life"; if the contradiction between these two messages is left unaddressed, the present-moment structure of the early Buddhist *dāna* sūtras cannot be honestly presented. Taking AN 5.34 *Sīhasenāpatisutta* as its pivot, this paper argues that early Buddhism, as early as the fifth century BCE, already treated the present-moment fruit of giving through an epistemic partition of **four *sandiṭṭhika* plus one *samparāyika*** — four items visible in this very life, one item belonging to the life to come. In the Buddha's presence Sīha attests the first four items in his own first person (*ahaṁ jānāmi*) and explicitly reserves the fifth as something received on faith (*saddhāya gacchāmi*); the Buddha gives his double endorsement *evametaṁ, sīha, evametaṁ, sīha*. It 26 opens with the Buddha's own declaration that he knows the fruit of giving "as I know it" (*yathāhaṁ jānāmi*) — this is the epistemic premise for the partition. AN 7.57 deepens the same Sīha in the same setting, hardening the partition into the emphatic form "*nāhaṁ saddhāya gacchāmi*" ("I do not proceed on faith"). The argument proceeds along three axes: the two-depth reading of the same Sīha within the Pāli (AN 5.34's five items and AN 7.57's seven items unfolding within a single scene); the divergence-convergence of the Chinese twin-fascicle transmission (T0125 *juan* 24 sūtra 10 "ending fluxes in this very body" as a present-life uptake, and *juan* 51 sūtra 6 "five superiorities over the gods" as a concrete rendering of the heavenly realm); and the temporal-axis juxtaposition (AN 5.36 *Kāladāna* with its five timely gifts and the *anumodanā* clause). The cross-disciplinary section concludes with contemporary prosocial spending research as a structural-twin counterpart, establishing three irreducible differences (fineness of stratification, the 4+1 epistemic partition, and the effect-motive differentiation); the middle-way corrections address four misreadings — effect inverted into motive, AN 5.34 read as transactional calculus, the mistaken inference that giving alone produces a heavenly rebirth, and the wealth-oligarchy misreading. The paper takes up the motivation axis from P04 and the recipient ladder from P05, serving as the third handle of Part II's early **three contemporary handles** (motivation / cost-effectiveness / giver's well-being); where P05 establishes the ceiling of giving, this paper establishes its floor, and hands off to P07 *Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅga* with its fourteen personal recipient-fields.

**Keywords:** AN 5.34, *Sīhasenāpatisutta*, Sīha Sutta, *sandiṭṭhika*, *samparāyika*, four-plus-one epistemic partition, present-life visible fruits, AN 7.57, same Sīha at two depths, *ahaṁ jānāmi* self-attestation, *evametaṁ sīha* double endorsement, *nāhaṁ saddhāya gacchāmi*, threshold of death, It 26 *Dānasutta*, *dānasaṁvibhāgassa vipākaṁ*, *yathāhaṁ jānāmi*, epistemic premise, T0125 *juan* 24 〈Chapter of Good Assembly〉, T0125 *juan* 51 〈Chapter of Mahāprajāpatī's Parinirvāṇa〉, twin-fascicle Chinese parallels, ending fluxes in this very body, five superiorities over the gods, lineage divergence-convergence, AN 5.36 *Kāladāna*, five timely gifts, *anumodanā*, rejoicing as sharing of merit, wealth-oligarchy rebuttal, AN 8.31 effect-motive differentiation, *cittālaṅkāra*, effect inverted into motive, AN 8.32 threefold confluence, *saddhā + hirī + kusaladāna*, *visārado-amaṅkubhūto*, entering assemblies without fear, the lion-king-of-beasts simile, T0026 *juan* 4 *Sīha Sutta*, AN 8.12 conversion, Nigaṇṭha, *saṅghupaṭṭhāka*, *dāyaka dānapati*, Dunn-Norton *Happy Money*, Aknin cross-cultural verification, prosocial spending, structural twin, non-reductionist explanation, Part II's three contemporary handles, dual axes of immediacy, floor of giving, genre asymmetry, NIAN-A dual track.

## I. Problem Consciousness

The short paper Elizabeth Dunn published in 2008[^1] became a landmark in contemporary donation research: she gave University of British Columbia campus subjects five dollars or twenty, with half required to spend the money on themselves that day and half required to spend it on someone else. The self-report forms collected that evening showed the "on someone else" group reported significantly higher well-being than the "on themselves" group — and the difference was independent of amount (five dollars and twenty dollars yielded similar results). The conclusion "the giver gains well-being in the present moment" was re-verified by Lara Aknin and colleagues across one hundred and thirty-six countries[^2], written into Dunn and Michael Norton's 2013 *Happy Money*[^3], and featured on TED, in *The New York Times*, and in *The Atlantic*. By the mid-2020s, a Western reader attentive to charity has essentially come to treat this result as common sense — giving is an immediately effective well-being intervention, not a deferred moral burden.

But the same reader, having skimmed a bit of introductory Buddhism — perhaps a few English-translation primers or a Netflix documentary — is very likely carrying another impression at the same time: that Buddhism defers the reward of giving to the next life, that merit is a next-life affair. *Dāna* looks like a long-horizon inter-life investment, not an immediately verifiable well-being generator. The two messages run in parallel in the contemporary reader's head and nobody notices their contradiction, because readers usually do not expect early Buddhism to address the question "is this life's fruit visible or not?" directly.

But it did. In the fifth century BCE at Vesālī, the Licchavi general Sīha, in the Peaked-Roof Hall of the Great Wood (Mahāvana), asked the Buddha his opening question: "Venerable Sir, is the Blessed One able to point out a fruit of giving visible in this very life (*sandiṭṭhikaṁ dānaphalaṁ*)?" The Buddha answered "he is able," and then laid out five items — four visible in this very life, one belonging to the next — and opened out the five through a structural move that is rare in the Pāli canon: having finished listing, the Buddha is met by Sīha attesting each of the first four in his own first person (*ahaṁ petāni jānāmi*, "these four things I know myself"), explicitly declaring "on these I do not proceed on faith"; only for the fifth, the good destination after death, does he say "this I do not know; on this I proceed on faith in the Buddha." The Buddha then endorses twice: *evametaṁ, sīha, evametaṁ, sīha* — "So it is, Sīha; so it is, Sīha." AN 7.57 deepens this structure with the same Sīha in the same place, its key line harder still: "I do not proceed on faith, and I know these things myself" (*nāhaṁ saddhāya gacchāmi; ahampi etāni jānāmi*, translation mine).

This is not an undifferentiated "doctrine of the merit of giving"; it is an epistemic structure that the Buddha himself explicitly partitions into **four plus one** — four items **visible in this very life (*sandiṭṭhika*)**, one item **belonging to the life to come (*samparāyika*)**. Faith enters only beyond the threshold of death; before death, experience is the sole criterion. This partition can stand in the Buddha's presence because the Buddha himself has already declared in It 26: "the fruit of giving and sharing, as I know it" (*dānasaṁvibhāgassa vipākaṁ yathāhaṁ jānāmi*, translation mine) — the Buddha attests to knowing the fruit of giving, and only because of this can Sīha's self-attestation be received before the Buddha as experiential testimony and be twice endorsed. It 26 is the epistemic premise of the four-plus-one partition.

The Chinese transmission preserves parallels of this structure. *Ekottara Āgama juan* 24, 〈Chapter of Good Assembly〉 (善聚品), sūtra 10[^4] renders the five merits item by item, even inserting a simile corresponding to the *visārado* axis of AN 5.34 — "just as the lion-king of beasts, among a herd of deer, is without fear or trouble" — the tightest Chinese-Āgama correspondence to the entire sutta. *Ekottara Āgama juan* 51, 〈Chapter of the Parinirvāṇa of Mahāprajāpatī〉 (大愛道般涅槃分品), sūtra 6[^5] replays the same Sīha event in a different anthological stratum; its fifth merit is unfolded into "rebirth among the Thirty-Three gods, with five further superiorities over those gods," and its closing section absorbs the knowledge-as-it-is of the four noble truths — showing that the Chinese transmission carries a late-sectarian divergence on the question "does the fifth fruit of giving ultimately point to a rebirth in heaven, or does it point to the path-insight axis?" This paper argues that these two juxtaposed fascicles do not display translation drift but two independent crystallizations in the Chinese transmission of "how *samparāyika* is to be concretely rendered," converging exactly with the two-depth structure of AN 5.34 (five items) and AN 7.57 (seven items) on the same Sīha in the Pāli.

Within the Part II sequence the paper's position is explicit: P04 establishes the **motivation axis** of giving (why one gives); P05 establishes the **recipient ladder** and the **apex of giving** (to whom one gives, up to what). **P05 sets the ceiling of giving — from Velāma's eighty-four thousand down to the finger-snap perception of impermanence; this paper sets the floor — four items visible in this very life, plus one entrusted to faith.** The two papers jointly lay out the structural upper and lower poles of giving in Part II, with P07's unfolding of the fourteen personal gift-fields of MN 142 *Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅgasutta* coming next as the fine-grained breakdown of the recipient categories.

## II. Canonical Foundations

This section's citations proceed along two axes. The first axis is the sūtra axis (A), with five groups: the Pāli prototype of the same Sīha at two depths (AN 5.34 + AN 7.57); the twin-fascicle Chinese parallel (T0125 *juan* 24 + *juan* 51); the disambiguation of same-named Sīha figures (general in *juan* 24 and 51 vs householder in *juan* 45); the epistemic premise and its supports (It 26 + AN 5.36 + AN 8.31 + AN 8.32); and biographical backdrop with an honest-absence statement (T0026 *juan* 4, plus vault gaps). The second axis is the commentarial axis (B), which relies on internal Pāli dual-axis self-reference as a functional substitute. Per NIAN-A dual-track discipline, Pāli materials carry PTS numbering and Chinese materials carry Taishō fascicle and sūtra numbering.

### 2.1 A · Sūtras

**Group 1: The same Sīha at two depths — AN 5.34 and AN 7.57**

AN 5.34 *Sīhasenāpatisutta*[^6] is placed in the *Aṅguttara Nikāya*, *Pañcaka-nipāta* (Book of Fives), *Sumanāvagga*, fourth sūtra; the setting is the Peaked-Roof Hall (Kūṭāgārasālā) in the Great Wood (Mahāvana) near Vesālī. The Licchavi general Sīha approaches the Buddha with the opening question "Venerable Sir, is the Blessed One able to point out a fruit of giving visible in this very life?" (*sakkā nu kho, bhante, bhagavā sandiṭṭhikaṁ dānaphalaṁ paññāpetun*, translation mine); the Buddha answers "he is able," and lays out five merits item by item:

> The giver becomes dear and agreeable to many people (*piyo manāpo hoti bahuno janassa*).
> Good people associate with the giver (*santo sappurisā bhajanti*).
> The giver's good reputation spreads (*kalyāṇo kittisaddo abbhuggacchati*).
> The giver, whatever assembly he enters — of warrior-nobles, of brahmins, of householders, of renunciants — enters *visārado upasaṅkamati amaṅkubhūto*, self-assured and not abashed.
> After the breakup of the body, after death, he is reborn in a good destination, a heavenly world (*kāyassa bhedā paraṁ maraṇā sugatiṁ saggaṁ lokaṁ upapajjati*) — this is a ***samparāyikaṁ*** *dānaphalaṁ*, a fruit of giving belonging to the world to come.

The five-item structural marking is unmistakably clear: each of the first four items terminates with the fixed clause *Yampi … idampi sandiṭṭhikaṁ dānaphalaṁ* ("it is for this reason that this is a fruit of giving visible in this very life"); the fifth switches the qualifier from *sandiṭṭhikaṁ* to *samparāyikaṁ*. This lexical switch is the structural backbone of the sutta and the textual basis for the four-plus-one partition argued in this paper.

AN 7.57 *Sīhasenāpatisutta*[^7] (*Aṅguttara Nikāya*, Book of Sevens, *Abyākatavagga*, same title, same person) is another exchange with the same Sīha at the same place. Identification evidence: same Vesālī–Mahāvana–Kūṭāgārasālā setting; same opening question *sakkā nu kho … sandiṭṭhikaṁ dānaphalaṁ paññāpetun*; same Sīha self-designating as *dāyako kārako saṅghupaṭṭhāka* ("giver, doer, attendant on the Sangha"). The difference lies in how the Buddha answers — AN 5.34 lists five items directly, AN 7.57 instead poses counter-questions to Sīha ("Sīha, if there were two persons, one without faith, miserly, envious, speaking ill of others, and one of faith, delighting in giving…") and through question-by-question induction draws Sīha into answering seven items himself. The seven items are not five-plus-two-new items; they are an unfolding of the first two items of AN 5.34 (dear and agreeable / good people associate) into four *arahant*-relation items (*arahants* show sympathy toward, approach, receive gifts from, and teach Dhamma to the giver), with the last three items corresponding to AN 5.34's items 3, 4, and 5. The key line, uttered after Sīha's answers, is explicit: "*Yānimāni, bhante, sandiṭṭhikāni dānaphalāni akkhātāni, nāhaṁ ettha bhagavato saddhāya gacchāmi; ahampi etāni jānāmi*" — "Venerable Sir, as for these fruits of giving visible in this very life that you have spoken of, on these I do not proceed on faith in the Blessed One; I know these things myself" (translation mine) — a sharper expression than AN 5.34's *ahaṁ petāni jānāmi*, crystallizing the emphatic form "I do not proceed on faith" (*nāhaṁ saddhāya gacchāmi*). The Buddha responds with the double endorsement *evametaṁ, sīha, evametaṁ, sīha*.

The two sūtras read together: **same Sīha, two depths** — not two different Sīhas of divergent report, not an earlier and later textual version. To avoid the item-counting trap, this paper does not conjecture diachronically (version X is earlier, version Y is later); it argues only the 4+1 epistemic structure jointly established by the two depths.

**Group 2: Twin-fascicle Chinese parallels in T0125**

*Ekottara Āgama juan* 24, 〈Chapter of Good Assembly〉 sūtra 10[^4] is the tightest Chinese parallel to AN 5.34. The setting "Vesālī, the Great Wood of Monkeys" (毘舍離獼猴林) corresponds exactly to Pāli Vesālī–Mahāvana; the questioner "General Sīha" (師子大將) matches Sīha *senāpati* in name and rank. The Buddha first questions Sīha — "Well, Sīha, is giving carried on continuously in your household?" — and Sīha reports that at the four city gates and throughout the town he gives freely in season; the Buddha then declares: "Sīha, a giver-donor who gives in season has five merits" — enumerated as a good name spreading in the four directions, entering assemblies without fear ("just as the lion-king of beasts, among a herd of deer, is without fear or trouble"), being revered by all people ("as a son looks on his father, he gazes without weariness"), rebirth at death among gods or humans, and **wisdom rising above all people, ending the fluxes in this very body, not passing on to another life**. The sūtra closes with gāthā verses and a passage on the three truths known as they are, exhorting the listener: "whether one seeks the way of the disciple or of the *paccekabuddha*, it shall be as one wishes."

The structural correspondence of these five items with AN 5.34 is high: item 1 "good name" matches AN 5.34 item 3 *kittisaddo*; item 2 "entering assemblies without fear" matches AN 5.34 item 4 *visārado-amaṅkubhūto*, with the Chinese uniquely preserving the "just as the lion-king of beasts" simile as the concrete rendering of *visārado*; item 3 "revered by all, as a son looks on his father" matches AN 5.34 items 1–2 *piyo manāpo* and *sappurisā bhajanti*; item 4 "rebirth among gods or humans" matches AN 5.34 item 5 *sugatiṁ saggaṁ lokaṁ upapajjati*. **Item 5 "ending the fluxes in this very body" is this Chinese version's unique uptake of the apex** — replacing AN 5.34's *samparāyika* fifth item with **ending the fluxes in this very life** (an upward motion within the *sandiṭṭhika* sense), approaching the *arahant*-orientation axis carried by the expanded version in AN 7.57.

*Ekottara Āgama juan* 51, 〈Chapter of the Parinirvāṇa of Mahāprajāpatī〉 sūtra 6[^5] is another recension replay of the same Sīha event. The setting is the same Vesālī (rendered "Mahāvana Park", 摩訶婆那園); the questioner is the same General Sīha. The five-merit structure is broadly synchronous with *juan* 24, but the internal differences are marked: item 4 is "at the time of giving, he rouses a joyful mind, fixity of volition, and knowledge-as-it-is of the truths of suffering, of its origin, of its cessation, and of the path out" (the four noble truths known as they are, as a present-life fruit); item 5 is "after the breakup of the body, rebirth among the Thirty-Three gods, with five further items of superiority over those gods" (the heavenly-rebirth axis unfolded into five superiorities: beautiful form and high status, freedom to obtain what one wishes, rebirth among humans in a wealthy family, abundant wealth and treasures, words that are heeded and followed). The closing segment, "the gods tell General Sīha that such-and-such a person is an *arahant*, such-and-such has broken the precepts; Sīha does not waver, remembering the Tathāgata's teaching of equal giving, and recites a gāthā on equal giving," is narratively cognate with the material about the householder Sīha's "equal giving" in *juan* 45, 〈Chapter of the Horse-King〉 sūtra 5 (see Group 3 below for disambiguation).

The relation between *juan* 24 and *juan* 51: **two recensions of the same source material**, not translation drift, and not two independently compiled sources. *Juan* 24 is a short-form composite of "five merits + ending the fluxes + gāthā on the four truths"; *juan* 51 is a long-form composite of "five merits + heavenly-rebirth expansion + the gods' announcement + equal-giving gāthā." The two fascicles diverge at item 5 (ending the fluxes in this very body / five superiorities over the gods), precisely reflecting two independent crystallizations in the Chinese transmission of "how *samparāyika* is to be concretely rendered in Chinese," converging with the two-depth structure on the Pāli side (AN 5.34's five items / AN 7.57's seven items).

**Group 3: Disambiguation of same-named Sīhas**

The "General Sīha" (師子大將) of T0125 *juan* 24 and *juan* 51 is the Licchavi general of Vesālī (corresponding to Pāli Sīha *senāpati*, after his conversion from the Nigaṇṭhas); the "Householder Sīha" (師子長者) of *juan* 45, 〈Chapter of the Horse-King〉 sūtra 5 is a householder of the Bamboo Grove at Rājagṛha, designated "foremost of the lay-disciples in equal giving" (corresponding to AN 1.14 *aggaṁ dāyakānaṁ*). **These are not the same person** — the former is marked by forbearance, the latter by equal giving; their life-stories become conflated in the closing segment of *juan* 51 sūtra 6 ("the gods tell"), which is a wandering of recitation formulas, not an intra-sūtra merging of personae. T0125 *juan* 3, 〈Chapter of Lay Disciples〉 sūtra 4, lists "foremost in the practice of forbearance: General Sīha"[^8], which is the Chinese textual basis for the former's character-profile and is internally consistent with Sīha's biography as a former disciple of the Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta who, on conversion to the Buddha, retained his forbearing disposition. The "General Sīha" appearing in *juan* 39, 〈Chapter of the Horse-Blood Deva〉, is a Māra-general bearing the same name and is not cited in this paper.

**Group 4: Epistemic premise and supports — It 26 · AN 5.36 · AN 8.31 · AN 8.32**

It 26 *Dānasutta*[^9] opens with the Buddha's first-person epistemic declaration: "Bhikkhus, if beings knew the fruit of giving and sharing as I know it (*dānasaṁvibhāgassa vipākaṁ yathāhaṁ jānāmi*), they would not eat without having given, nor would the stain of miserliness occupy their minds; even down to their last morsel and last mouthful, if there were a recipient, they would not eat alone but would share" (translation mine). *Yathāhaṁ jānāmi* ("as I know it") is the Buddha's direct epistemic declaration concerning the fruit of giving — the Buddha himself explicitly attests to knowing the fruit of giving. This declaration is the epistemic premise by which Sīha's self-attestation *ahaṁ jānāmi* in AN 5.34 and the Buddha's endorsement *evametaṁ sīha* are able to stand: the Buddha, in It 26, has already established that knowledge of the fruit of giving is something the Buddha knows, so when Sīha comes seeking "let this be made known to me" (*paññāpetun*), only then can the first-person attestation of the first four items in the four-plus-one partition have its epistemic footing. The compound *dānasaṁvibhāga* ("giving and sharing" as a single term), found in It 26, is the higher-order vocabulary under which the five merits of AN 5.34 fall, and in this paper it serves the epistemic-underpinning function between §1 and Group 2 of §2.

AN 5.36 *Kāladāna*[^10] lists five timely gifts: a gift to a guest, to a traveller, to the sick, in times of famine, and the first offering of newly harvested grain and fruit to those of virtue. The gāthā contains the key line: "*ye tattha anumodanti, veyyāvaccaṁ karonti vā, na tena dakkhiṇā ūnā, tepi puññassa bhāgino*" — "those who rejoice therein, or lend personal service, do not thereby diminish the gift; they too share in the merit" (translation mine). The *anumodanā* (rejoicing) clause is the key to refuting the wealth-oligarchy misreading in §V — the fruit of giving is not the exclusive property of the material giver; rejoicers also share in its merit. AN 5.36 together with AN 5.34 constitutes early Buddhism's **dual-axis pairing** of giving: AN 5.34 is the fruit axis (what fruit does the giver obtain here and now), AN 5.36 is the temporal axis (at what time is giving most fitting).

AN 8.31 *Paṭhamadānasutta*[^11] and AN 8.32 *Dutiyadānasutta*[^12] are the basis for §V's effect-versus-motive caution; the detailed treatment has been given in P04 §II Group 1. This paper uses only two points: AN 8.31's seventh position *kittisadda* (giving for the sake of a good name) to refute the misreading "AN 5.34 item 3 *kalyāṇo kittisaddo* is being inverted into a motive for giving," and AN 8.32's gāthā (*saddhā + hirī + kusaladāna → devaloka*) to refute the misreading "the four *sandiṭṭhika* items alone secure rebirth in heaven."

**Group 5: Biographical backdrop and honest-absence statement**

*Madhyama Āgama juan* 4, 〈Chapter of Karma-Correspondence〉 sūtra 18 *Sīha Sutta*[^13], translated by Gautama Saṅghadeva of the Eastern Jin, is the Chinese parallel to Sīha's conversion event, corresponding to Pāli AN 8.12 *Sīhasutta* (not to AN 5.34 / AN 7.57). In this sūtra Sīha, originally a disciple of Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta, having heard the Licchavis praise the Buddha, goes to the Buddha without taking leave of the Nigaṇṭha and enters into an eight-aspect polemical clarification with the Buddha concerning what the Nigaṇṭha reports as "the recluse Gotama's doctrine of non-action" (non-action, action, annihilation, abhorrence, discipline, austerity, non-re-entry into the womb, safety). This paper does not treat the eight-aspect argument (not the topic of AN 5.34); it is cited in §I only as a character-profile backdrop for Sīha (≤200 CJK characters) — he has already undergone conversion and carries the pre-history tension of a former Nigaṇṭha disciple, giving him an internal motive to seek experiential criteria for "is the fruit of giving truly visible in this very life?"

**Honest-absence statement:** The Pāli original of AN 8.12 *Sīhasutta* (Sīha's conversion), AN 4.57 *Suppavāsā*, AN 4.58 *Sudatta*, AN 4.51–53 *Puññābhisanda* (lay version), AN 6.37 *Velukaṇḍakī*, *Udāna* 4.3 *Yasoja*, *Udāna* 2.8 *Suppavāsā*, SN 3.24 *Issatta*, AN 5.38 *Saddha*, *Dhammapada* giving-gāthās, and the Sīha passage at *Vinaya Mahāvagga* VI.31 — all of these are not collected in the current vault-pali. Per the v2.5.2-NIAN discipline, this paper does not cross-check them online and does not reconstruct from memory; Sīha's biography uses T0026 *juan* 4 *Sīha Sutta* (the Chinese parallel) as its entry point, and the Pāli originals are deferred to future vault remediation.

### 2.2 B · Commentary

The commentarial layer is kept light. The commentarial treatment of AN 5.34 and AN 7.57 is scattered across Buddhaghosa's *Manorathapūraṇī* (the *Aṅguttara* commentary), mostly in the form of item-by-item lexical glosses (clarification of the relation between *piyo manāpo* and *sappurisā bhajanti*, allocation of *visārado-amaṅkubhūto* between bodily and verbal action, and so forth) — offering limited structural contribution to this paper's main argument, the four-plus-one epistemic partition. This paper therefore uses the internal Pāli dual-axis self-reference (AN 5.34 / AN 7.57 / It 26 / AN 5.36) as a "commentarial-functional substitute" within the Pāli — the four sūtras juxtaposed already establish the canonical foundation of the four-plus-one structure in full, with no need for external commentarial reinforcement. The full two-voice commentarial dialogue (Buddhaghosa as lead voice, Yogācāra as counter-voice) continues the Part I convention and will be reactivated in Part IV at the Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā* stage.

## III. Core Argument

This section's three-layer argument proceeds in order: the four-plus-one *sandiṭṭhika / samparāyika* partition in the AN 5.34 prototype (3.1), the depth-expansion and emphatic formula of the same Sīha in AN 7.57 (3.2), and the three-axis joint structure of the T0125 twin-fascicle juxtaposition with AN 5.36's temporal axis (3.3).

### 3.1 The AN 5.34 prototype — the four-plus-one *sandiṭṭhika / samparāyika* partition

The sutta's structural move can be read in three steps. The first step is the Buddha's statement: the five fruits are pronounced in sequence, each of the first four items terminating with the fixed clause *Yampi … idampi sandiṭṭhikaṁ dānaphalaṁ*, and the fifth switching lexically to *samparāyikaṁ dānaphalaṁ*. This switch is not rhetorical ornament — *sandiṭṭhika* and *samparāyika* are a structural pair of epistemic terms in the Pāli canon: *sandiṭṭhika* is the same word used in the *Dhammānussati* standard formula "*svākkhāto bhagavatā dhammo, sandiṭṭhiko, akāliko…*," referring to "directly verifiable in this very life, not requiring death to be confirmed"; *samparāyika*, by contrast, refers explicitly to "belonging to the world to come, not within the scope of present-life verification." The Buddha deploys this pair as an epistemic taxonomy for householder meritorious acts in AN 8.54 *Dīghajāṇu*, AN 5.58 *Licchavikumāra*, and other sūtras. AN 5.34 is precisely the application of this taxonomy to the fruit of giving.

The second step is Sīha's self-attestation. The move is extraordinarily rare in the Pāli canon — in the presence of the Buddha and a four-fold assembly, Sīha repeats each of the first four items in the first person as his own experience: "Venerable Sir, I am a giver, a donor; I am dear and agreeable to many people. I am a giver, and good people associate with me. I am a giver, and my good name spreads — 'General Sīha is a giver, a doer, an attendant on the Sangha.' I am a giver, and whatever assembly I enter — of warrior-nobles, of brahmins, of householders, of renunciants — I enter self-assured and not abashed." He then declares emphatically: "Venerable Sir, as for these four fruits of giving visible in this very life that you have spoken of, on these I do not proceed on faith; these four things I know myself." This four-item attestation (*ahaṁ petāni jānāmi*) is not an abstract theological declaration; it is a public experiential report on four specific socio-psychological effects. The first-person four-item attestation constitutes an **intra-sutta public self-checking** — Sīha, in the four-fold assembly, demonstrates four present-life effects jointly verifiable by his own experience and by others' observation.

The third step is Sīha's explicit **scope switch** at the fifth item: "As for the Blessed One's statement to me that 'Sīha, a giver, with the breakup of the body, is reborn in a good destination, a heavenly world' — this I do not know; here I proceed on faith in the Blessed One (*etāhaṁ na jānāmi; ettha ca panāhaṁ bhagavato saddhāya gacchāmi*, translation mine)." This is the explicit declaration of the death-threshold: experience takes precedence over faith; faith enters as a criterion only beyond death. The Buddha's double endorsement *evametaṁ, sīha, evametaṁ, sīha* simultaneously confirms two things — that Sīha's four-item attestation is true, and that the fifth item of heavenly rebirth is also true. The double endorsement is bidirectional confirmation: the Buddha does not ask Sīha to take experientially verifiable matters on faith, and does not negate the reality of the fifth, supra-experiential, item. This is early Buddhism's **epistemic honesty** concerning *dāna* — explicitly distinguishing "what you can verify yourself" from "what you need to take on my authority," and relinquishing neither reality-claim in either region.

The reason this sutta is the pivot text of P06 lies precisely in the **intra-textual self-awareness** of the 4+1 structure. The entire partition is not an interpretive enterprise of later commentators; it is **explicitly established within the sūtra's first-person interaction** between Sīha and the Buddha. The sūtra's rhetorical strategy is not instruction; it is a public allocation of experiential criteria.

### 3.2 AN 7.57 — the same Sīha deepened, the emphatic version of "I do not proceed on faith"

AN 7.57 deepens AN 5.34 at three layers. The first layer is the **unfolding of five items into seven**: AN 5.34's first two items (dear and agreeable / good people associate) are unfolded into four *arahant*-related items (*arahants'* sympathy for the giver, their approach, their receiving gifts, their teaching Dhamma to him), with the last three items (good name, entering assemblies without fear, rebirth in heaven) preserved. The underlying structural move of the seven items is to differentiate AN 5.34's items 1–2, "the giver's social relational network," further by mapping four concrete relations with **arahants** — from the *arahant*'s inner attitude (*anukampā*, sympathy) to the *arahant*'s bodily and verbal action (*upasaṅkamana*, approach; *paṭiggahaṇa*, receiving; *dhammadesanā*, teaching of Dhamma). Item 4 is especially important: *arahants* give priority in Dhamma-teaching to the giver — this opens AN 5.34's socially present-life fruit toward the **Dhamma-giving axis** (*dhammadāna*; cf. It 98, already flagged in P05 §III 3.5), without departing from the giver's present-life domain.

The second layer is the **Buddha's substitution of question for direct statement**. In AN 7.57 the Buddha does not list seven items outright; he guides Sīha through contrast-questions into answering them: "Sīha, if there were two persons — one without faith, miserly; one of faith, delighting in giving — to whom would *arahants*, if they were to extend compassion, first extend it?" Sīha answers each question in turn: "the giver." The seven items come out of Sīha's own mouth, with the Buddha endorsing each answer with *evametaṁ, sīha*. This question-answer structure shifts the seven fruits from "a teaching the Buddha tells Sīha" to **a public showing of the giver's own cognitive structure** — AN 7.57's rhetorical core is not "the Buddha tells Sīha what fruits giving has," but "Sīha knows for himself what fruits giving has, and the Buddha endorses it."

The third layer is Sīha's emphatic declaration. After the seven items have been answered, Sīha reprises the same structure as in AN 5.34: "*Yānimāni, bhante, sandiṭṭhikāni dānaphalāni akkhātāni, nāhaṁ ettha bhagavato saddhāya gacchāmi; ahampi etāni jānāmi*" — here *nāhaṁ saddhāya gacchāmi* ("I do not proceed on faith") is an **explicit negation**, rather than merely an affirmation of self-knowledge as in AN 5.34's *ahaṁ petāni jānāmi*. Read together, the two clauses are bidirectional: I do not proceed on faith, **and** I know these things myself. The double formula is stronger than AN 5.34's — it does not only affirm self-attestation; it actively negates the intrusion of faith.

The two sūtras read together: the complete form of the four-plus-one structure on the Pāli side is — **the count of fruits can be expanded (from five to seven), but the 4+1 epistemic partition is the fixed structure**. The *sandiṭṭhika* cluster is judged by experience before death; the single *samparāyika* item is judged by faith after death; **the intrusion of faith is strictly limited to what experience cannot extend to**.

### 3.3 Chinese twin-fascicle juxtaposition with the AN 5.36 temporal axis — the three-axis joint structure of the present-life fruit

The twin-fascicle juxtaposition of T0125 *juan* 24 sūtra 10 and *juan* 51 sūtra 6 reveals two independent crystallizations in the Chinese transmission at the **apex of the fifth item**. *Juan* 24 moves AN 5.34's *samparāyika* fifth item upward into "wisdom rising above all people, **ending the fluxes in this very body, not passing on to another life**" — this translation choice replaces the fruit of heavenly rebirth (originally belonging to the next-life axis) with "ending the fluxes in this very life," keeping all five merits formally within the single axis of *sandiṭṭhika*. *Juan* 51, by contrast, preserves the heavenly-rebirth axis but expands it into "rebirth among the Thirty-Three gods, with five further superiorities over those gods" — beautiful form and high status, freedom in obtaining what one desires, rebirth among humans into a wealthy family, abundant wealth and treasures, and words that are heeded and followed — these five superiorities being the concrete contents the Chinese translators assign to the *samparāyika* fruit. The two juxtaposed fascicles diverge between *juan* 24's present-life flux-ending axis and *juan* 51's heavenly-realm expansion axis, yet **converge on the common structural skeleton of the four present-life fruits** — both *juan* 24's first four merits (good name, entering assemblies without fear, being revered, rebirth among gods or humans) and *juan* 51's first four merits (good name, entering assemblies without fear, being loved, knowledge-as-it-is of the four noble truths) correspond closely to AN 5.34's four *sandiṭṭhika* items. This divergence-convergence picture is structurally of the same kind as the two-depth structure on the Pāli side (AN 5.34's five / AN 7.57's seven) — **the first four items (present-life fruits, broadly construed) form the common skeleton, and the fifth item (*samparāyika* / apex expansion) is the open region where lineage choice takes place**. The Chinese and Pāli transmissions each crystallize independently within this open region; what is at work is not translation drift but two lineages' independent doctrinal decisions about "how the epistemic apex of giving is to be concretely rendered."

Both of these Chinese fascicles are embedded within the *Ekottara Āgama* anthology, which, together with the stand-alone status of Velāma in T0073 *Fo shuo Xuda jing* discussed in P05, forms a clear **genre asymmetry**: Velāma's enormous giving, as a narrative paradigm, requires free-standing and prominent circulation; Sīha's everyday present-life fruits, as a part of daily Āgama-material, is embedded in anthological transmission. The transmission genre of each matches its doctrinal position: Velāma's apex (a finger-snap of the perception of impermanence) is the ceiling of the *dāna* ladder, its stand-alone prominence answering its status as a paradigm; Sīha's present-life fruit is the floor of *dāna*, its embedded status in daily Āgama-material answering its status as everyday practice.

*Juan* 24 uniquely preserves the "just as the lion-king of beasts, among a herd of deer, is without fear or trouble" simile as the concrete rendering of *visārado-amaṅkubhūto* — the Pāli "entering assemblies without fear" receives, in Chinese, explicit pictorial carriage. This simile clicks with the name of the questioner, Sīha (師子, "lion") himself: Sīha calls himself a giver, and his fruit is precisely to enter assemblies without fear, like the lion for whom he is named. This self-referential structure, though not explicit at the Pāli lexical level (Sīha appears only as a personal name, not as a simile), is explicitly activated in *juan* 24 as a narrative resonance — the translator's choice of this simile may derive from a sensitivity to the structural resonance within the original between the questioner's name and the fruit attributed to him.

AN 5.36 *Kāladāna*, as the **temporal axis**, juxtaposed with AN 5.34's fruit axis, forms the **dual-axis architecture** of early Buddhist *dāna*: AN 5.34 answers "what fruit does the giver obtain here and now"; AN 5.36 answers "at what time is giving most fitting." AN 5.36's five timely gifts — to a guest, to a traveller, to the sick, in famine, and the first offering of newly harvested produce to the virtuous — are **situation-and-recipient composite criteria** (timing × condition of recipient). The fifth item is especially worth noting — the first offering of newly harvested grain and fruit to those of virtue overlays the temporal axis (time of new harvest) with the recipient axis (those of virtue), forming the tightest example in early Buddhism of the fine-grained treatment of the conditions of giving. The closing gāthā's "*ye tattha anumodanti … tepi puññassa bhāgino*" (those who rejoice also share in the merit) is the key to defusing the wealth-oligarchy misreading — the present-life fruit of giving is not a by-product of wealth accumulation; rejoicers (*anumodanā*) and servers (*veyyāvacca*) also share in the merit without any reduction of the giver's own fruit. This *anumodanā* clause is the canonical basis for the ⚠️ cautions in §V.

Three-axis joint structure: the fruit axis (AN 5.34 / AN 7.57), the temporal axis (AN 5.36), and the epistemic premise (It 26) together form early Buddhism's complete narrative structure for the **immediacy** of giving. Two axes are explicit (fruit and time); one axis is epistemic and underlying (the Buddha's knowing). The structural layout of this section — from AN 5.34 prototype, to AN 7.57 depth-expansion, to T0125 twin-fascicle parallel, to AN 5.36 temporal-axis juxtaposition — follows precisely this three-axis layering, finally converging on the structural unity of the four-plus-one. This structural unity is not the doctrinal systematization Western readers may expect (Christian systematic theology's integrative synthesis); it is an epistemic architecture **native to** early Buddhist canonical texts — *dāna* is assigned its epistemic position in the structurally shared 4+1 form across the Pāli and Chinese traditions, without requiring later exegetes to construct it.

## IV. Cross-disciplinary Synthesis: Structural Twinhood and Categorical Divergence between AN 5.34 and Contemporary Research on Prosocial Spending

The most rigorous empirical line of research on the contemporary question of "the giver's present-moment well-being" is the *prosocial spending* research program within positive psychology. Dunn, Aknin, and Norton's 2008 short paper in *Science* laid the groundwork — three experiments demonstrated that subjective well-being generated by spending money on others is significantly higher than spending on oneself, with the effect independent of amount (smaller and larger sums yielding similar results); Aknin and colleagues' 2013 cross-cultural data from one hundred and thirty-six countries showed the effect to be a cross-culturally universal phenomenon (absolute levels differ by country, but the direction is consistent); Dunn and Norton's 2013 *Happy Money* integrated the research into five popular-audience principles of spending (buy experiences, make it a treat, buy time, pay now and consume later, invest in others). This line of research runs broadly in parallel with AN 5.34 on the same question — **the giver gains well-being in the present moment** — and in the front N rungs (if one dares to make the structural analogy, roughly the first four *sandiṭṭhika* items of AN 5.34) it genuinely forms a **structural twin**: both take the present-life effects of giving seriously, both refuse to defer the whole of giving's return to the next life or to the long future, and both provide verifiable experiential criteria.

But this twinhood is **structurally non-reducible in three directions**.

**The first difference is fineness of stratification.** The Dunn–Norton framework measures a single hedonic outcome — subjective well-being, life satisfaction, positive-affect frequency — primarily through self-report scales. AN 5.34, by contrast, stratifies the present-life fruit of giving into **four structurally distinct types**: a relational effect (being dear and agreeable, *piyo manāpo*); a social effect (good people associate, *sappurisā bhajanti*); a reputational effect (good name spreads, *kalyāṇo kittisaddo*); and a situational-psychological effect (entering assemblies without fear, *visārado-amaṅkubhūto*). These four items point respectively to four structurally distinct socio-psychological effects — the first two concern others' attitude toward the giver (inner disposition plus outward approach behavior), the third concerns the spread of the giver's representation through the social network, and the fourth concerns the giver's own psychological state within complex social occasions. These four effects cannot be reduced to a single "rise in happiness" — *visārado-amaṅkubhūto*'s "entering assemblies without fear" especially so: this is not an emotional state but a **social-capacity state**, closer in psychological terms to social confidence than to subjective well-being. Contemporary research, using a single happiness scale to measure all effects of giving, compresses these four structurally distinct effects into one dimension and loses the fineness of stratification preserved in the Buddhist canon.

**The second difference is the 4+1 epistemic partition.** By design, contemporary prosocial-spending research does not differentiate between *sandiṭṭhika* fruits and *samparāyika* fruits — all of its findings lie within the region of present-life verification, because research design itself can only address what is verifiable in this present life. This is a structural limitation of the research design, not a shortcoming in research quality. But the significance of the partition AN 5.34 performs lies precisely in this: **it explicitly marks** which fruits can be verified in this life and which fruits require the intrusion of faith, and restricts the intrusion of faith strictly to what experiential criteria cannot reach. Contemporary research lacks the *samparāyika* axis not because that axis does not exist but because research methodology does not admit entry into it. This difference is not one of refinement but one of **epistemic architecture** — the Buddhist canon preserves its declaration of the limits between "experiential knowing" and "faith-entrusted knowing," whereas contemporary research, within its methodological boundaries, constructs a precise picture of giving's verifiable effects but bears no responsibility for declarations beyond those boundaries.

**The third difference is effect-motive differentiation.** AN 8.31 (unfolded in P04 §III 3.1) explicitly distinguishes the eight-position motivational spectrum: giving for the sake of a good name (the seventh position, *kittisadda*) is a low position in the motivational spectrum; giving for the sake of the adornment of the mind (the eighth position, *cittālaṅkāra*) is the liberation-directed apex. AN 5.34's "*kalyāṇo kittisaddo* as a present-life fruit" and AN 8.31's "giving for the sake of *kittisadda* as a low motive" are not in contradiction — the former concerns **the social effect produced by the act of giving**, the latter concerns **the inner intention that brings about the act of giving**. Effect and motive are distinct dimensions, and their **directions are opposed**: the motivation of right giving points inward (to *cittālaṅkāra*), while its effects point outward (to *kittisadda* and so forth); the effects exist as truly as reported, but taking the effects as the motive causes giving to lose its liberation-directed character. This differentiation is structurally outside the sensitivity of contemporary prosocial-spending research design — the design cannot distinguish the directionality between "giving for the sake of well-being" and "gaining well-being by giving." A contemporary reader, reading AN 5.34 with this research as background, is very liable to read items 1–3 of the present-life fruits as "reasonable motives for giving" — "since giving brings being loved, approached, and reputed, then giving for the sake of being loved, approached, and reputed is reasonable" — a reading that structurally inverts effect into motive and slides back into the low positions of AN 8.31, losing the liberation-directed apex at the eighth position. §V will address this misreading.

**⚠️ Unidirectional analogy caveat:** The Dunn-Aknin-Norton research can help contemporary Western readers quickly identify the contemporary scenes corresponding to the first four items of AN 5.34 — "after I made a donation, I really did feel a lift in mood for a while," "after sustained giving I really did build better social relationships" — this direction of analogy is valid. The reverse does not hold: one cannot use AN 5.34's four-plus-one partition to "optimize" contemporary prosocial-spending research methodology — that is a question within the methodological category of psychology, and Buddhist canonical texts are not designed to answer it. The analogy is structurally isomorphic in the first four items (the contemporary-reachable axis) and breaks at the fifth item (the *samparāyika* axis); it is structurally insensitive in the motivation-effect relation on the contemporary side, and rigorously differentiated on the canonical side by AN 8.31. The entire analysis adheres to **structural twinhood as non-reductionist explanation** — the resemblance is genuine, but resemblance is not identity.

The analytical form of this section continues the syntax of P04 §IV (Andreoni *warm glow* + Bénabou–Tirole *identity signaling* + Harbaugh neural reward, matching the **motivation axis**) and P05 §IV (Singer + MacAskill + GiveWell, matching the **recipient axis**). Read together the three §IV sections constitute the **three contemporary handles** of Part II's early segment — motivation analysis, cost-effectiveness analysis, prosocial-spending research — mirroring respectively the liberation-directed markers of *cittālaṅkāra*, the categorical break at the *vipassanā* apex, and the methodological boundaries of the *sandiṭṭhika* / *samparāyika* epistemic partition. These three handles share structural syntax — **isomorphism in the first N rungs/positions, categorical break at the apex/topmost position** — and this shared syntax is Part II's coherent signature for the structural analysis of *dāna*. Early Buddhism's serious treatment of giving is not an ancient substitute for contemporary research; it is an answer of a different category, and that very difference is what gives it value for the contemporary reader.

## V. Middle-way Corrections

This section addresses two misreadings and two cautions.

**❌ Misreading 1: Giving for the sake of a good name — effect inverted into motive**

The most common misreading is to read items 1–3 of AN 5.34 (dear and agreeable, good people associate, good name spreads) as "reasonable motives for giving" — since giving brings about these, then giving for the sake of these is what the sutta endorses. The error in this reading has been unfolded in §IV's third difference: the sutta describes **effects**, not **motives**. AN 8.31 clarifies this: giving for the sake of a good name (the seventh position, *kittisadda*) belongs to the low region of the motivational spectrum, structurally remote from giving for the sake of the adornment of the mind (the eighth position, *cittālaṅkāra*). Inverting effect into motive is not merely logical misplacement; it causes giving to lose its liberation-directed character — if the motive is to seek a good name, then the giver's mind during the act of giving is turned toward others' eyes on him rather than toward the adornment of his own mind; the faculty of *cāga* (the loosening of "mine-making" established in P02) cannot be activated, and the event cannot serve as an object of *cāgānussati* (P03). This misreading not only violates AN 8.31; it structurally dismantles the faculty-motive-object chain established across P02-P03-P04. Read clearly the sutta's effect-motive distinction, and this misreading automatically fails.

**❌ Misreading 2: AN 5.34 as a transactional formula — the four *sandiṭṭhika* fruits as investment returns**

Another common misreading runs in the opposite direction — Western readers (especially those familiar with the vocabulary of behavioral economics) tend to read AN 5.34 as "the input-output analysis of giving": I invest the gift, I receive five returns — being loved, being approached, good name, entering assemblies without fear, heavenly rebirth. This reading treats the four *sandiṭṭhika* items as a juxtaposition of hedonic rewards and reads the sutta as an ancient version of well-being optimization. The error of this reading lies in **flattening the structural stratification**. §IV has argued that the four items are structurally distinct (relational, social, reputational, situational-psychological), not four versions of emotional uplift. At a deeper level: the structure of AN 5.34 is not a calculation of returns but **a faithful presentation of fruit as it is** — the Buddha himself in It 26 declares "as I know it" concerning the fruit of giving, which is a faithful identification of the natural fruit of giving, not a promise of returns. Sīha's self-attestation *ahaṁ jānāmi* is not the verification of a transaction but the confirmation of experience — he has been a giver, he has observed himself becoming dear to many people and entering assemblies without fear; these events occur not because "the giver deserves returns" but because such are the natural consequences of the event of giving within the socio-psychological structure. Read clearly the distinction between "natural presentation of fruit" and "promise of returns," and the transactional misreading automatically fails.

**⚠️ Caution 1: The four *sandiṭṭhika* fruits alone do not produce heavenly rebirth**

A contemporary reader of AN 5.34 may infer erroneously: "since giving has five fruits, of which the fifth is heavenly rebirth, then giving alone produces heavenly rebirth." This inference has a direct rebuttal in AN 8.32's gāthā — "*Saddhā hiriyaṁ kusalañca dānaṁ, dhammā ete sappurisānuyātā; etañhi maggaṁ diviyaṁ vadanti, etena hi gacchati devalokan*" — "Faith, moral shame, and wholesome giving — these are the dhammas followed by the good; this, they say, is the divine way, by this one goes to the heavenly realm" (translation mine). *Saddhā* (faith) and *hirī* (moral shame) clearly lie outside the scope of giving and belong to other domains of wholesome qualities; giving (*kusala dāna*) appears in this gāthā as the third of three, merging with faith and shame into a **threefold confluence** that produces the path to the heavenly realm. The *samparāyika* heavenly fruit in AN 5.34's fifth item is not the result of giving alone; it requires the cooperation of faith and moral shame as inner qualities to be realized. Readers who read the fifth item as an outcome of giving alone structurally misunderstand early Buddhism's complete treatment of the conditions for heavenly rebirth. This caution also corrects a potential over-reading of the "three-axis parallel" in §IV — even when AN 5.34's fruit axis and AN 5.36's temporal axis are fully arrayed, the *samparāyika* fruit at the fifth item remains constrained by AN 8.32's threefold-confluence condition and cannot be deduced from either axis alone.

**⚠️ Caution 2: AN 5.36's *anumodanā* as the defusal of the wealth-oligarchy misreading**

Another contemporary-liable reading is the **wealth-oligarchy misreading** — "since giving has *sandiṭṭhika* fruits, and giving requires material goods, only the wealthy can give, only the wealthy can obtain such fruits; the poor, having nothing to give, naturally have no fruit to obtain." This reading pushes early Buddhist *dāna* doctrine to an untenable conclusion of class legitimation. AN 5.36's gāthā and its *anumodanā* clause directly rebut this misreading: "*ye tattha anumodanti … tepi puññassa bhāgino*" — "those who rejoice therein also share in the merit" (translation mine). The one who rejoices need not perform a material gift himself in order to share in the giver's merit; the one who lends personal service (*veyyāvacca*) likewise. The teaching is unmistakable: the sharing of giving's fruit is not tied to material conditions but to **resonance of mind** (the rejoicing and service that embody a mind in accord with giving). Sīha himself, in AN 5.34's first item *piyo manāpo*, does not take material quantity as the criterion — "dear and agreeable to many people" arises from the sustained act and attitude of giving, not from its material magnitude. This rebuttal simultaneously prevents another contemporary misreading (the mindful-charity / therapeutic-altruism downgrade) — the downgrading of *cittālaṅkāra* into an MBSR-style emotional technique and the reading of AN 5.34 as an ancient version of wellbeing-through-generosity practice — the structural reasons for which P04 §V has already addressed, this paper's §IV closing has already reaffirmed, and which is therefore not unfolded further here.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essentials restated:** AN 5.34 *Sīhasenāpatisutta* establishes, through the four *sandiṭṭhika* + one *samparāyika* epistemic partition, the **present-life visible fruit structure** of early Buddhist *dāna* — experience precedes; the intrusion of faith is confined to the threshold of death. Sīha's self-attestation *ahaṁ jānāmi* and the Buddha's double endorsement *evametaṁ, sīha, evametaṁ, sīha* constitute the rhetorical backbone of the sutta, displaying the giver's intra-assembly public self-checking and the Buddha's bidirectional confirmation. AN 7.57 deepens the same Sīha and establishes the emphatic form "*nāhaṁ saddhāya gacchāmi*." It 26's declaration "the fruit of giving, as I know it" supplies the epistemic premise for the partition. T0125 *juan* 24 sūtra 10 and *juan* 51 sūtra 6, read together, display two independent crystallizations in the Chinese transmission at the apex of the fifth item (*samparāyika*) — *juan* 24's "ending the fluxes in this very body" as a present-life uptake, *juan* 51's "five superiorities over the gods" as a concrete rendering of the heavenly realm — converging with the two-depth structure of AN 5.34 / 7.57 on the Pāli side. AN 5.36 *Kāladāna*'s temporal axis, juxtaposed with AN 5.34's fruit axis, constitutes early Buddhism's dual-axis architecture for the **immediacy** of giving; its *anumodanā* clause defuses the wealth-oligarchy misreading.

**Practice guidance:** For the contemporary reader, the practical significance of the 4+1 partition lies in **epistemic honesty**. Before and after each act of giving, the practitioner may ask: is the fruit I am now seeking within the *sandiṭṭhika* region of what can be verified? If so, which layers of this fruit have I observed (being dear to others, being approached by good people, a good name, entering assemblies without fear, or otherwise)? If the fruit I seek is *samparāyika* (rebirth in heaven, the completion of a future life), am I honestly acknowledging that this is a judgment beyond experience, entrusted to faith in the Buddha? This honesty does not require the practitioner to "not seek next-life fruit" — early Buddhism does not exclude that axis — but to **distinguish the two axes** and **not to disguise the region of faith as a verdict of experience**. This distinction prevents both over-expectation of present-life fruit (as in "giving must bring good fortune" transactional calculation) and over-faith-direction toward next-life fruit (as in pushing the whole meaning of giving to after death). Sīha's self-attestation, and his explicit reservation at the fifth item (*etāhaṁ na jānāmi; bhagavato saddhāya gacchāmi*), are the narrative prototype of this practice — in the four-fold assembly he neither lets faith overstep into what is verifiable by experience in the first four items, nor lets experience trespass into what faith is entrusted with at the fifth; both regions remain distinct, and the reality-claim is preserved in both.

**Handoff to P07:** This paper treats the present-moment visible fruit of giving (the "when the fruit arises" axis); P07 will turn to the recipient taxonomy axis (the "for whom the fruit arises" axis) with MN 142 *Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅgasutta*'s fourteen kinds of personal gift-field, which, together with this paper, forms Part II's double-faceted analysis of giving along the axes of immediacy and recipient — P05 setting the ceiling of giving, this paper setting its floor, P07 subdividing recipient categories between the two, completing the early segment of Part II (P04–P07).

This paper is the foundational document of the prosocial-spending research program.

[^2]: Aknin, Lara B., et al. "Prosocial Spending and Well-Being: Cross-Cultural Evidence for a Psychological Universal." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 104, no. 4 (2013): 635–652. Using Gallup World Poll data across 136 countries, the paper confirms that the positive correlation between prosocial spending and subjective well-being is a cross-culturally universal phenomenon; replication experiments in Canada, South Africa, and Uganda confirm the behavioral causality of the effect. This paper §I and §IV cite this work as the principal empirical basis for the contemporary reader's expectation of the cross-cultural robustness of "the giver's present-moment well-being."

[^3]: Dunn, Elizabeth, and Michael Norton. *Happy Money: The Science of Happier Spending*. New York: Simon & Schuster, 2013. The book integrates the prosocial-spending empirical research into five spending principles for popular audiences (buy experiences, make it a treat, buy time, pay now and consume later, invest in others). Through broad circulation in TED, *The New York Times*, *The Atlantic*, and other mainstream Western media outlets, "the giver gains well-being in the present moment" has shifted from academic finding to common-sense premise of contemporary Western philanthropic discourse. This paper §I uses it to mark the Western reader's existing cognitive backdrop.

[^4]: *Ekottara Āgama juan* 24, 〈Chapter of Good Assembly〉 (善聚品第三十二), sūtra 10, translated by Gautama Saṅghadeva of the Eastern Jin, *Taishō* vol. 2 No. 125. The sūtra opens with "at that time, the Buddha was in the Great Wood of Monkeys at Vesālī, together with a large assembly of five hundred bhikkhus"; the questioner is General Sīha. The Buddha declares item by item: "a giver-donor who gives in season has five merits" — (i) a good name spreading in the four directions; (ii) when entering the assemblies of warrior-nobles, brahmins, elders, and renunciants, bearing no shame and no fear, "just as the lion-king of beasts, among a herd of deer, is without fear or trouble"; (iii) being revered by all people, "as a son looks on his father, he gazes without weariness"; (iv) after death, rebirth among the gods or among humans, revered by gods where a god and honored by humans where a human; (v) wisdom rising above all people, ending the fluxes in this very body, not passing on to another life. The sūtra closes with a gāthā and a passage on the three truths known as they are (the truths of the origin of suffering, of the ending of suffering, and of the way out), exhorting the listener: "whether one seeks the way of the disciple or of the *paccekabuddha*, it shall be as one wishes." This paper §I, §II Group 2, and §III 3.3 take it as the principal Chinese parallel to AN 5.34.

[^5]: *Ekottara Āgama juan* 51, 〈Chapter of the Parinirvāṇa of Mahāprajāpatī〉 (大愛道般涅槃分品第五十二), sūtra 6, translated by Gautama Saṅghadeva of the Eastern Jin, *Taishō* vol. 2 No. 125. The sūtra is set in the same Vesālī (rendered "Mahāvana Park", 摩訶婆那園), with General Sīha as the questioner; the Buddha declares the "five merits of the giver-donor" — (i) a good name spreading far and wide; (ii) entering the assemblies of warrior-nobles, brahmins, and renunciants bearing no fear and no doubt; (iii) being loved by many, universally revered, "as a child loves its mother, whose heart does not leave it"; (iv) at the time of giving, rousing a joyful mind, fixity of volition, and knowledge-as-it-is of the truths of suffering, of its origin, of its cessation, and of the way out; (v) after death, rebirth among the Thirty-Three gods, with five further superiorities over those gods (beautiful form and high status, freedom in obtaining what one wishes, rebirth among humans into a wealthy family, abundant wealth and treasures, words that are heeded and followed). The sūtra continues with a segment in which gods tell General Sīha of differences among fields of merit, Sīha does not waver, and he recites the Tathāgata's teaching of equal giving as a gāthā — a segment narratively cognate with the equal-giving material of *juan* 45's householder Sīha. This paper §I, §II Group 2, and §III 3.3 use it as the second-recension Chinese parallel to AN 5.34; juxtaposed with *juan* 24 sūtra 10, it displays the two independent crystallizations in the Chinese transmission at the *samparāyika* apex.

[^6]: AN 5.34 *Sīhasenāpatisutta*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya*. PTS AN iii.38. *Pañcaka-nipāta* (Book of Fives), *Sumanāvagga*, fourth sūtra (SuttaCentral catalogues AN 5.34 under Mahāsaṅgīti Tipiṭaka numbering). The setting is the Peaked-Roof Hall (Kūṭāgārasālā) in the Great Wood (Mahāvana) near Vesālī. The Licchavi general Sīha asks the Buddha about "a fruit of giving visible in this very life" (*sandiṭṭhikaṁ dānaphalaṁ*); the Buddha pronounces five items — the first four terminating with the fixed clause *Yampi … idampi sandiṭṭhikaṁ dānaphalaṁ*, the fifth switching to *samparāyikaṁ dānaphalaṁ*. Sīha attests the first four items in his own first person (*ahaṁ petāni jānāmi*) and reserves the fifth on faith (*bhagavato saddhāya gacchāmi*); the Buddha endorses with *evametaṁ, sīha, evametaṁ, sīha*. This paper takes it as the pivot text, with detailed argument in §I, §II Group 1, and §III 3.1. English renderings draw on Bhikkhu Sujato's and Bhikkhu Bodhi's translations in cross-reference; the version in vault-pali is the Mahāsaṅgīti Tipiṭaka Buddhavasse 2500 edition.

[^7]: AN 7.57 *Sīhasenāpatisutta*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya*. PTS AN iv.79. Book of Sevens, *Abyākatavagga*, same title. Same Vesālī–Mahāvana–Kūṭāgārasālā setting; same Sīha self-designating as *dāyako kārako saṅghupaṭṭhāka*. The Buddha guides Sīha through counter-questions into answering seven items himself — four *arahant*-relations to the giver (sympathy, approach, receiving, teaching of Dhamma), plus good name, entering assemblies without fear, and heavenly rebirth. The key line "*Yānimāni, bhante, sandiṭṭhikāni dānaphalāni akkhātāni, nāhaṁ ettha bhagavato saddhāya gacchāmi; ahampi etāni jānāmi*" ("on these I do not proceed on faith; I know these things myself," translation mine) is sharper than AN 5.34. This paper §II Group 1 and §III 3.2 draw on this as the second depth of the same Sīha.

[^8]: *Ekottara Āgama juan* 3, 〈Chapter of Lay Disciples〉 (清信士品第六), sūtra 4, translated by Gautama Saṅghadeva of the Eastern Jin, *Taishō* vol. 2 No. 125. This sūtra is the "etadagga-list of disciples" of T0125 (analogous to the *Etadagga* chapter in AN), in which "foremost in the practice of forbearance — namely, General Sīha" is recorded. This paper §II Group 3 takes it as the Chinese textual basis for General Sīha's character-profile — Sīha was originally a disciple of Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta and, having converted, retained his forbearing disposition; this designation of "foremost in the practice of forbearance" is internally consistent with Sīha's character in the conversion narrative of Pāli AN 8.12 *Sīhasutta*, and distinguishes him from the Householder Sīha of *juan* 45, 〈Chapter of the Horse-King〉 sūtra 5 (designated "foremost of lay-disciples in equal giving"), who is a different person.

[^9]: It 26 *Dānasutta*, *Itivuttaka*. PTS It 18; SuttaCentral (Sujato) catalogues as It 26. The sūtra opens with the Buddha speaking in the first person: "Bhikkhus, if beings knew the fruit of giving and sharing as I know it (*dānasaṁvibhāgassa vipākaṁ yathāhaṁ jānāmi*), they would not eat without having given, nor would the stain of miserliness occupy their minds; even down to their last morsel and last mouthful, if there were a recipient, they would not eat alone but would share" (translation mine). *Yathāhaṁ jānāmi* is the Buddha's direct epistemic declaration concerning the fruit of giving. This paper §I and §II Group 4 take it as the epistemic premise of AN 5.34's four-plus-one partition — the Buddha has already established in It 26 that knowledge of the fruit of giving is what the Buddha knows, and only against this premise can Sīha's *ahaṁ jānāmi* in AN 5.34 and the Buddha's *evametaṁ sīha* endorsement have their epistemic footing. P05 had listed this sūtra as sideline; P06 promotes it to principal citation.

[^10]: AN 5.36 *Kāladāna*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya*. PTS AN iii.41. *Pañcaka-nipāta*, *Sumanāvagga*, sixth sūtra. Lists five timely gifts: to a guest (*āgantukassa dānaṁ*), to a traveller (*gamikassa dānaṁ*), to the sick (*gilānassa dānaṁ*), in famine (*dubbhikkhe dānaṁ*), and the first offering of newly harvested grain and fruit to those of virtue (*navasassāni … sīlavantesu … paṭhamaṁ patiṭṭhāpeti*). The gāthā contains the key line: "*ye tattha anumodanti, veyyāvaccaṁ karonti vā, na tena dakkhiṇā ūnā, tepi puññassa bhāgino*" — "those who rejoice therein, or lend personal service, do not thereby diminish the gift; they too share in the merit" (translation mine). This paper §II Group 4, §III 3.3, and §V caution 2 take this as the canonical basis for the temporal-axis juxtaposition and for the defusal of the wealth-oligarchy misreading.

[^11]: AN 8.31 *Paṭhamadānasutta*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya*. PTS AN iv.236. *Aṭṭhaka-nipāta*, sūtra 31. Lists eight motives for giving: (i) *āsajja* (under duress); (ii) *bhayā* (out of fear); (iii) *adāsi me* (in return for past receipt); (iv) *dassati me* (in expectation of future receipt); (v) *sādhu dānan* (because giving is good); (vi) *pacāmi ahaṁ* (because I cook and they do not); (vii) *kittisadda* (for the sake of a good name); (viii) *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra* (for the adornment and equipment of the mind). The eighth position is the liberation-directed apex of the motivational spectrum. P04 §II Group 1 and §III 3.1 treat this in detail. This paper §II Group 4, §IV third difference, and §V misreading 1 use it as the principal basis for effect-motive differentiation — the seventh position *kittisadda* (giving for the sake of a good name) is a low motive, and if AN 5.34's item 3 *kalyāṇo kittisaddo* (good name as an effect) is inverted into a motive for giving, this slides back into the low positions of AN 8.31.

[^12]: AN 8.32 *Dutiyadānasutta*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya*. PTS AN iv.237. *Aṭṭhaka-nipāta*, sūtra 32, closing with the gāthā: "*Saddhā hiriyaṁ kusalañca dānaṁ, dhammā ete sappurisānuyātā; etañhi maggaṁ diviyaṁ vadanti, etena hi gacchati devalokan*" — "Faith, moral shame, and wholesome giving — these are the dhammas followed by the good; this, they say, is the divine way, by this one goes to the heavenly realm" (translation mine). This paper §II Group 4 and §V caution 1 take this as the canonical basis for "the four *sandiṭṭhika* items alone do not produce heavenly rebirth" — AN 5.34's fifth item, the *samparāyika* heavenly fruit, requires the threefold confluence of faith (*saddhā*) + moral shame (*hirī*) + wholesome giving (*kusala dāna*), and is not deducible from giving alone.

[^13]: *Madhyama Āgama juan* 4, 〈Chapter of Karma-Correspondence〉 sūtra 18 *Sīha Sutta*, translated by Gautama Saṅghadeva of the Eastern Jin, *Taishō* vol. 1 No. 26. The Chinese parallel to Pāli AN 8.12 *Sīhasutta* (not to AN 5.34 / AN 7.57), recording Sīha's conversion event. Sīha, originally a disciple of Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta, having heard the Licchavis praise the Buddha, goes to the Buddha without taking leave of the Nigaṇṭha and enters into an eight-aspect polemical clarification with the Buddha concerning the Nigaṇṭha's report that "the recluse Gotama teaches non-action" (non-action, action, annihilation, abhorrence, discipline, austerity, non-re-entry into the womb, safety). This paper §II Group 5 cites it only as biographical entry-point for Sīha (≤200 CJK characters); the eight-aspect argument (not the topic of this paper) is not unfolded. The Pāli original of AN 8.12 is not collected in the current vault-pali; per v2.5.2-NIAN discipline, this paper uses the Chinese parallel as the replacement entry point and does not cross-check the Pāli original online or from memory.
