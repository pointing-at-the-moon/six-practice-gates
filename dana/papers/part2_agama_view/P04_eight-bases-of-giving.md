**The Motivation Axis and the Quality Axis in Early Buddhism — Two Independent Taxonomies**

## Abstract

Contemporary research on philanthropic behavior has developed a refined taxonomy of donor motivation — Andreoni's *warm glow*, Bénabou–Tirole's *identity signaling*, the fMRI neural-reward models of Harbaugh and colleagues — yet within these frameworks every downstream reward mechanism operates strictly within the welfare economy of this present life, structurally lacking any liberation-directed filter; early Buddhism, by contrast, treated precisely this filter as the structural backbone of its *dāna* taxonomy as far back as the fifth century BCE. This paper argues that early Buddhism's eight-fold classification of giving is not a single fixed structure but a *dual-axis independent development* — Pāli AN 8.31 takes a spectrum of motivational purity as its axis (from *āsajja* [external compulsion], *bhayā* [fear], transactional giving, cultural conformity, and reputation, culminating in *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra* as the inward motivation of adorning and equipping the mind), while the Chinese *Ekottara Āgama juan* 37, "Chapter on the Eight Difficulties," sūtra 9 takes a quality-configuration recipe as its axis (with the seven conditions of timeliness, freshness, personal hand, vow-framing, liberation-orientation, aim-at-cessation, worthy field, and dedication rendering *dāna* complete as *puñña-kriyā*); the two axes are not translation equivalents but parallel contributions by two independent lineages to the structural analysis of *dāna*, converging above the container of AN 8.36's three bases of meritorious action and within the faculty-to-object chain established across P02 and P03, supplying a dual criterion for what counts as an event fit to serve as an object of recollection. The analysis proceeds in three layers: the motivation axis's spectrum from the outward to the inward, grouped structurally into five clusters (with the real categorical watershed running between positions 7 and 8); the quality axis's seven-condition recipe with its compound structure (and positions 5–6 — liberation and cessation — functioning as the operative layer where the liberation-directed filter is embedded); and the convergence of the two axes above AN 8.36's orthogonal matrix (supplying the motivation-generation layer that had been missing between the faculty of P02 and the object of P03). Once this dual-axis taxonomy is read clearly, two opposed contemporary misreadings — the reading of Chinese-tradition giving as "merit-transaction accumulation," and the reading of the Chinese emphasis on quality as "soulless ritualization" — can both be structurally refuted; likewise, AN 8.31's eighth position *cittālaṅkāra* can be kept from being downgraded into a form of MBSR-style mindful charity. The present paper occupies, within Part I's faculty-to-object chain, the motivation-generation layer, and opens Part II *The Āgama View* by initiating the systematic treatment of the Āgama exposition of *dāna*.

**Keywords**: AN 8.31, eight kinds of giving, motivational purity spectrum, *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra*, T0125 *juan* 37, eight bases of giving, quality-configuration recipe, dual-axis taxonomy, AN 8.36, three bases of meritorious action, container gloss, orthogonal matrix, *warm glow*, *identity signaling*, welfare optimization, liberation-directed filter, *puñña-kriyā* completeness, merit-accumulation misreading, mindful-charity caution, Part II *The Āgama View*, NIAN-A dual track, attribution discipline, AN 8.33 vault gap

## I. Introduction

Set the scene in the contemporary world. A Western reader with some passing interest in the ethics of giving — perhaps an MBA graduate, perhaps working in the nonprofit sector, perhaps only a person watching where their year-end donations go — very likely has at hand several ready-made tools for classifying donor motivation. James Andreoni's 1990 *warm glow* — the inner satisfaction generated by the act of giving itself — tells this reader why the pure-altruism model fails to explain actual donation behavior. Roland Bénabou and Jean Tirole's 2006 *identity signaling* — donation as a public signal of identity and values — tells the reader why anonymous donation actually depresses giving for certain people. Harbaugh, Mayr, and Burghart's 2007 fMRI study — generous giving activating the striatal reward circuit — tells the reader why descriptions like "nothing feels better than the moment of signing that check" are not mere rhetoric.

Each of these frameworks is refined in its own right; together they form the most developed contemporary taxonomy of donor motivation. Yet structurally they share a single boundary: all reward mechanisms (warm glow, signal, neural reward) are mapped inside **the welfare economy of this present life** — a better self-image, a more stable social position, more active brain regions. The contemporary framework does not discuss (and was not designed to discuss) one structural question: **which motivations point toward release from "mine-making," and which merely deepen "mine-making" at a more refined layer?** For the contemporary framework, this question is not a blind spot, it is *outside the category* — the grammar of welfare-optimization has no slot for "release from the welfare frame itself."

Early Buddhism already made this question the structural backbone of its *dāna* taxonomy in the fifth century BCE. *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.31, the *Paṭhamadānasutta*[^1], lists eight kinds of giving spoken by the Buddha, in the following sequence: giving because struck or affronted, giving out of fear, giving in return because the other has previously given, giving in expectation of future return, giving because of the cultural consensus that "giving is good," giving because "I am one who cooks, they do not; it is not fitting for a cooker to not give to non-cookers," giving for the sake of reputation, and **giving for the adornment and equipping of the mind**. The eight unfold from external compulsion to inner cultivation; the final position is explicitly marked as **adornment and equipping of the mind** — oriented neither toward an external recipient, nor toward a karmic result, nor toward social honor, but toward the practitioner's own inner *citta* and its cultivation. This last position is not "the noblest motive for charitable giving"; it is **the only motivation in the entire taxonomy that points toward liberation** — it is the motivational precondition for an event fit to serve as an object of recollection, and from there it connects to the fourth limb of stream-entry's *cāga* argued in P02 and to the fifth of the six *anussati*'s *cāgānussati* argued in P03, forming a complete chain from motivation-layer to limb-layer to object-layer.

Looking back from this direction at the limits of the contemporary framework, the structural divide becomes visible. *Warm glow* corresponds to an internalized version of AN 8.31's position 5 "giving is good" — a *sāhu-dānaṁ*-type motivation operationalized as a measurable variable by contemporary behavioral economics; *identity signaling* is the algorithmic rendering of position 7 *kittisadda* (giving for fame); the neural-reward research reveals the physiological substrate of positions 1–7, but has no correspondent for position 8 at all — because the inward orientation of *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra* does not lie within the coordinate system of welfare-optimization. The two systems are structurally isomorphic across the first seven positions; at the eighth they diverge structurally. The divergence is not *supplementation*, it is a **category switch**.

This is the paper's argumentative space. First step: a close reading of AN 8.31's eight positions of motivation as a **purity spectrum** — eight layers sketching, in sequence, how far the activation of *cāga* reaches, from total impossibility to full unfolding. Second step: the eight items listed in the Chinese *Ekottara Āgama juan* 37, "Chapter on the Eight Difficulties," sūtra 9[^2] — timeliness, freshness, personal hand, vow, liberation-orientation, aim-at-cessation, worthy field, dedication — read as **another independent axis**: not as a translation correspondent of AN 8.31, but as a calibration, along the **quality-configuration** dimension, of *dāna*'s completeness as *puñña-kriyā*. The two eight-fold classifications **are not translation equivalents** — they are two parallel classificatory impulses developed in the same early Buddhist *dāna* culture. Third step: the two axes converge within the frame, established in P03, of *cāga* as an object — the motivation axis answers "which motivational structures generate events fit to serve as objects of recollection," and the quality axis answers "which quality-configurations bring such an event to the completeness of *puñña-kriyā*" — together constituting the motivation-generation layer between P02's faculty and P03's object.

The dual-axis route simultaneously answers two default expectations held by Western readers when they encounter "classifications of giving": the moral-psychological reader (motivational analysis) and the ritual-completeness reader (quality recipe) each meet a structure familiar to them. But when the classification is completed, both axes together point to a matter that is structurally absent from the contemporary framework — **among these motivations and these quality-conditions, which genuinely point toward liberation, and which only deepen the circling?** This is not an ancillary contextual consideration, it is the **structural precondition** for reading *dāna* as a limb of the path.

## II. Canonical Basis

This section's citations are organized along two axes: axis one is sūtra material (A), containing two primary sources (AN 8.31's motivation axis, T0125 *juan* 37's quality axis), one complement (AN 8.32 as supplementary gāthā), one adjacent reference (T0125 position axis), and one absence declaration (AN 8.33, vault gap). Axis two is commentarial material (B), with AN 8.36's three bases of meritorious action serving as the container above both axes. In keeping with NIAN-A dual-track discipline, Nikāya material is cited by PTS edition, Chinese material by Taishō volume and sūtra number.

### 2.1 A · Sūtra

**Group 1: AN 8.31 — The motivation axis of eight bases**

*Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.31, the *Paṭhamadānasutta*[^1]:

> *Āsajja dānaṁ deti, bhayā dānaṁ deti, 'adāsi me'ti dānaṁ deti, 'dassati me'ti dānaṁ deti, 'sāhu dānan'ti dānaṁ deti, 'ahaṁ pacāmi, ime na pacanti; nārahāmi pacanto apacantānaṁ dānaṁ adātun'ti dānaṁ deti, 'imaṁ me dānaṁ dadato kalyāṇo kittisaddo abbhuggacchatī'ti dānaṁ deti, cittālaṅkāracittaparikkhāratthaṁ dānaṁ deti.*

The sūtra opens with the short framing clause "Mendicants, there are these eight kinds of giving," directly enumerates the eight motivations, and closes with "These, mendicants, are the eight kinds of giving." There is **no ordering language** — the Buddha does not make explicit whether the eight constitute a rank-order; the structural significance of the sequence is left for the reader to read from the structure itself. The eight in English rendering (translation mine):

1. *Āsajja* — giving when struck or affronted face-to-face
2. *Bhayā* — giving out of fear
3. *'Adāsi me'ti* — giving in return because the other has previously given
4. *'Dassati me'ti* — giving in expectation of future return
5. *'Sāhu dānan'ti* — giving because of the consensus that "giving is good"
6. The cooker-and-non-cooker custom — giving because "one who cooks should share with those who do not"
7. *Kittisadda* — giving for the sake of reputation
8. *Cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra* — giving for the adornment and equipping of the mind

**Group 2: T0125 *juan* 37, sūtra 9 — The quality axis of eight bases**

*Ekottara Āgama* （增一阿含經） *juan* 37, "Chapter on the Eight Difficulties" （〈八難品第四十二之二〉）， sūtra 9[^2]:

> "If a son or daughter of good family gives gifts of material goods, they acquire eight kinds of merit. What are the eight? First, giving at the right time, not at the wrong time; second, giving what is fresh and pure, not what is foul and defiled; third, giving with one's own hand, not having another do it; fourth, giving with a clear vow, without pride or extravagance; fifth, giving out of liberation, not looking for return; sixth, giving aimed at cessation, not seeking rebirth in the heavens; seventh, seeking a worthy field for the giving, not giving to barren soil; eighth, then taking this merit and dedicating it to all beings, not keeping it for oneself." (translation mine)

The sūtra closes with a gāthā: "The wise give at the right time, / Without stinginess or greed; / Having already made their merit, / They turn it all to others. / This giving is most excellent, / Praised by all the Buddhas; / In this body they receive its fruit, / Beyond it they receive celestial blessings"; and with a concluding exhortation: "Whoever seeks this result should practice these eight bases. Its recompense is measureless, beyond all reckoning; they obtain the jewel of the deathless and gradually come to extinction" (translation mine).

The axis of the eight items is clear: timing, substance, mode, resolve, orientation (split into two layers, liberation and cessation), recipient, and the final handling (dedication). These eight **do not translationally correspond** to AN 8.31's eight — AN is a motivation axis, T0125 a quality axis; the two analytical dimensions are fundamentally different. The structural relation is developed in §3.1–3.2.

**Group 3: AN 8.32 as supplementary gāthā, and the absence of AN 8.33**

*Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.32, the *Dutiyadānasutta*[^3], stands adjacent to 8.31 and in past scholarship has often been presumed to be the eight-item parallel or doctrinal extension of AN 8.31. This presumption requires correction: AN 8.32 is not an enumeration of eight items but a four-line *gāthā*:

> *Saddhā hiriyaṁ kusalañca dānaṁ, dhammā ete sappurisānuyātā; etañhi maggaṁ diviyaṁ vadanti, etena hi gacchati devalokan*ti.

Rendered: "Faith, conscience, and wholesome giving — these qualities are followed by the virtuous; this is called the heavenly path by the Buddha, and by this one goes to the world of devas" (translation mine). The three virtues (*saddhā* / *hiri* / *kusala dāna*) point toward the *devaloka*. The relation between this *gāthā* and AN 8.31 is **complementary, not correspondent** — AN 8.31 is a descriptive taxonomy, AN 8.32 a normative *gāthā* compressed to the upper end of the motivational spectrum; the explicit overlap between them amounts only to the shared root *kusala* between AN 8.31's position 5 *sāhu dānaṁ* and AN 8.32's *kusala dāna*. The two further virtues named in the *gāthā*, *saddhā* and *hiri*, are **not** in AN 8.31's eight-item list — they belong to the *devatānussati* pentad of virtues (faith / virtue / learning / letting-go / wisdom), not to the *dāna* taxonomy itself. This paper's citation of AN 8.32 strictly observes the attribution discipline: it does **not** call AN 8.32 a correspondent or extended version of AN 8.31.

According to Bhikkhu Bodhi's AN translation index, the sūtra that actually presents an eight-base doctrinal expansion is AN 8.33, the *Dānavatthusutta*; however, this sūtra is not included in the current state of the collection[^4]. The present paper does not cite AN 8.33, does not cross-check it online, and does not reconstruct it from memory — its formal handling is deferred to a future vault remediation. The absence of AN 8.33 does not damage the main argument: AN 8.31's eight-item spectrum is already self-sufficiently complete.

**Group 4: T0125 *juan* 37, sūtra 8 — The position axis, mentioned for reference**

Immediately adjacent to the quality-axis sūtra, the preceding sūtra records a dialogue between the Buddha and Anāthapiṇḍika (阿那邠邸 長者)[^5]:

> "Of giving in a poor household, unbroken day and night, to the four city gates, to the great market, in the household's passageways, and to the Buddha and the community of monks — these are the eight positions of giving." (translation mine)

The "eight" here is **eight positions / eight locations of giving** — the four city gates, the great market, the household's passageways, the Buddha, and the community of monks. Taken together with sūtra 9's quality axis and AN 8.31's motivation axis, T0125 *juan* 37 presents within a single *juan* two eight-fold taxonomies in parallel (quality axis + position axis), with AN 8.31's motivation axis adding a third — a picture of early Buddhism's structural analysis of *dāna* along at least three parallel axes. To keep the argumentative focus of this paper, the position axis is only flagged here, and does not enter the main argument; this axis may be revisited in Part III, when treating the institutionalization of the *saṅgha* as recipient, but is for now only a structural observation.

### 2.2 B · Commentary

**AN 8.36's three bases of meritorious action as the container above both axes**

*Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.36, the *Puññakiriyavatthusutta*[^6], lists three kinds of *puññakiriyavatthu*: *dānamayaṁ* (based on giving), *sīlamayaṁ* (based on ethical conduct), and *bhāvanāmayaṁ* (based on cultivation). The three are enumerated as the three bases of meritorious action, calibrated by three intensities (*parittaṁ* / *mattaso* / *adhimattaṁ*).

This sūtra does **not** perform any internal decomposition of *dāna* — it does not list eight motivations, does not list eight qualities, does not subclassify *dāna* in any way. *Dāna* appears here as an **unanalyzed whole**, its position fixed by an outer framework (the three-fold juxtaposition) and an outer modulator (the three-intensity calibration). This structural feature makes AN 8.36 the **container** of the two-axis analysis in §2.1 — both axes are internal structural analyses of *dānamayaṁ puññakiriyavatthu*, neither crosses over into *sīlamayaṁ* or *bhāvanāmayaṁ*. *Sīla* has its own internal classification (the eightfold division of *sīla*); *bhāvanā* enters wholesale into other systems (the four establishments of mindfulness, the seven factors of awakening, the noble eightfold path, etc.). The AN 8.36 container therefore delimits the analytical scope of P04: this paper addresses the internal dual-axis structure of *dāna*, without crossing the boundaries of the three bases.

AN 8.36's three-intensity mechanism is **orthogonal** to the two axes of §2.1 — the quantitative calibration axis is independent of the qualitative classificatory axes. An *adhimattaṁ* (great-intensity) giving may correspond to AN 8.31's position 1 external-compulsion motivation, or equally to position 8 *cittālaṅkāra* motivation; a T0125 seven-condition-complete *dāna* may be small-intensity or great-intensity. Intensity is independent of motivation and of quality. This orthogonality is consolidated into P04's structural diagram in §3.3.

**Note on commentarial structure**: as with the first three papers of Part I, the present paper follows the dual-voice commentarial approach (Buddhaghosa as the main voice, Yogācāra as the secondary voice); but the main argument here proceeds through direct structural analysis of the sūtra, and the commentarial layer appears only as an AN 8.36 container-gloss in §3.3, without a dedicated section. Full dual-voice commentarial dialogue is reserved for Part IV's turn to the Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā*.

## III. Core Argument

This section's three-layer argument proceeds in turn: the purity-spectrum structure of AN 8.31's motivation axis (3.1), the configuration-recipe structure of T0125's quality axis (3.2), and the convergence of the two axes under the P03 frame of *cāga* as object (3.3, with AN 8.36's container as scaffold).

### 3.1 The AN 8.31 purity spectrum — the eight-layer structure from outer compulsion to inner cultivation

§2.1 Group 1 has already listed AN 8.31's eight motivations. This section argues that their internal structure is a **purity spectrum**: the eight not only have a sequence, but the sequence itself carries a structural judgment — the further a motivation is from an external referent and the more it orients toward inner cultivation, the more the *dāna* event it generates is capable of serving as a stable internal-structural trace, the kind of *nimitta* required by *cāgānussati* to enter the path of meditation.

This structural judgment can be presented by **clustering the eight into groups** (the clustering does not change the sūtra's sequence, it only makes explicit the structural arc that is already there):

- **Group One (positions 1–2) · Outer compulsion**: *āsajja* (being struck or affronted) and *bhayā* (fear). Giving is a reactive movement under external pressure; the *cāga*-faculty is **actively suppressed** — the mind's original inclination is not to give but to defend "mine-making," and giving appears only as the cost of removing the threat. Events generated under this motivation bear the imprint of compulsion at the root and cannot serve as the stable internal-structural trace that *cāgānussati* requires.
- **Group Two (positions 3–4) · Transactional**: *'adāsi me'ti* (past return) and *'dassati me'ti* (future expectation). Giving is a transactional movement within a loop of reciprocity; the *cāga*-faculty is **short-circuited into transaction** — the mind's giving is not a loosening of "mine-making" but a movement of ownership inside a network of debts and exchanges. Events generated from this motivation bear calculative traces; in recollection, one encounters the structural residue of "I gave so that the other would return," and it cannot become a clean *nimitta* of letting-go.
- **Group Three (positions 5–6) · Cultural custom**: *'sāhu dānan'ti* (the normative judgment that "giving is good") and the cooker-and-non-cooker custom. This group **partially activates** the *cāga*-faculty — the mind indeed has an inclination to give, but its engine is external normativity (ethical consensus, community belonging), not an inner loosening. Events generated bear the imprint of normative compliance; in recollection one can identify "I gave because this is what a good person does" — the object is usable, but carries a layer of social contamination.
- **Group Four (position 7) · Reputation**: *kittisadda* (giving for the sake of fame). This motivation remains outward, but has contracted from normative consensus to **personal reputation** — the engine is signal transmission (the giver's own image in the community's eye). This is the most refined form of outward motivation, closest to turning inward; events generated bear performative traces, and recollection taking such an event as *nimitta* will meet the awkward mirroring of "I did this to be praised."
- **Group Five (position 8) · Inner cultivation**: *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra* (giving for the adornment and equipping of the mind). This group is the **only inward** motivation — the *cāga*-faculty is activated for the cultivation of its own inner *citta*. *Alaṅkāra* (adornment) refers to the already-accomplished equipping of the mind (the inner beauty already attained); *parikkhāra* (equipment) refers to the mind's path-going equipage (the inner gear being accumulated); together they point to the practitioner's own *citta* as **the object receiving cultivation**. The events generated by this group are pure enough in object-character to serve as the stable *nimitta* of *cāgānussati*, linking further to P02's fourth limb of stream-entry *cāga* and P03's fifth of the six recollections.

The **watershed** of the five groups runs between positions 7 and 8 — not between 4 and 5, nor between 5 and 6. The first seven motivations differ in **degree of outwardness** along the sequence, but all belong to the same **category of direction** (oriented toward an external object: threat, debt, norm, reputation); position 8 performs a **category switch**, orienting toward the inner *citta*. AN 8.31's ordering of the eight is therefore not a linear gradient but a structure of "seven outward items of a spectrum + an eighth as an inward mutation."

The structural contrast with contemporary taxonomies of donor motivation will be developed in §IV. This section establishes only: AN 8.31's eight items constitute a purity spectrum of motivation whose endpoint is not "the purest altruism," nor "the most socially mature giving," but **the sole motivation compatible with liberation** — giving for the cultivation of the mind.

### 3.2 The T0125 *juan* 37 quality-configuration recipe — the *puñña-kriyā* structure of seven conditions co-present

§2.1 Group 2 has already listed T0125 *juan* 37 sūtra 9's eight bases. This section argues that its structure is **not a spectrum but a recipe** — the eight bases are not eight gradient positions, but eight categories of condition; when all eight categories are **co-present**, a single *dāna* is brought to completeness as *puñña-kriyā*.

The evidence for the recipe structure lies in the sūtra's grammar. Sūtra 9 does not say "from low to high, progressively more excellent"; it says "**acquires eight kinds of merit**" — a complete *dāna* by a single giver can simultaneously acquire all eight effects of merit, none of which substitutes for another; missing any single one means that one specific merit does not appear, but the other items are not prevented from being realized. This differs grammatically from AN 8.31's "eight kinds of giving" — AN 8.31's eight are mutually exclusive (eight mutually incompatible motivations, of which a giver can possess at most one per act of *dāna*), whereas T0125's eight are co-present (eight categories of condition, ideally all present at once, the giver configuring as many as possible in a single act of *dāna*). Mutually-exclusive eight is a spectrum; co-present eight is a recipe — this is the **deepest logical-structural difference** between the two axes.

The eight bases can be organized into **seven categories of condition** (positions 5 and 6, though presented as two items, are in fact two sub-items of a single class of orientation-condition):

- **Timing** (position 1): "at the right time" vs "at the wrong time" — not the quantity of the gift, but whether this very moment is fit; whether the recipient is ready to receive now, whether the community needs this thing now.
- **Substance** (position 2): "fresh and pure" vs "foul and defiled" — the dimension of the gift-matter's own quality, not "expensive vs cheap," but "is it of a quality that deserves to be received."
- **Mode** (position 3): "with one's own hand" vs "having another do it" — the personal-hand dimension of bodily action; to delegate is to leave the act structurally incomplete.
- **Resolve** (position 4): "with a clear vow" vs "with pride or extravagance" — a pre-committed framing vs a random flash of superiority; commitment confers structural continuity on the action.
- **Orientation** (positions 5–6, in two layers): "out of liberation" vs "looking for return"; "aimed at cessation" vs "seeking birth in the heavens" — these two layers of orientation-condition explicitly *exclude* motives of circling reward; this is the **liberation-directed filter** within the recipe.
- **Recipient** (position 7): "a worthy field" vs "barren soil" — the recipient as a field of merit; the wisdom of choosing a field is not snobbery, it is a judgment about "whether this thing can actually produce benefit at the hands of this recipient."
- **Handling** (position 8): "dedication" vs "keeping for oneself" — the handling of the merit after the giving; dedication to all beings converts personal fruit into shared karmic provisioning.

The **two-layer orientation-condition** at positions 5–6 deserves particular attention. A contemporary reader who supposes that "the Chinese emphasis on quality-conditions = ritualizing *dāna* into soulless procedure" will lose focus on these two positions — they are not ritual procedure, they are the marks by which the Chinese translation **deliberately embedded the anti-*saṃsāric* filter into the quality-configuration itself**. What T0125 shows here is not the Chinese text substituting a quality classification for the motivation classification (which would be a degeneration of axis into translation), but the Chinese text **writing the liberation-directed filter of motivation directly into the operative layer of quality-conditions** — the two axes are **structurally communicative here but axially different**: AN 8.31 marks liberation-directed motivation with the endpoint of its spectrum (position 8 *cittālaṅkāra*); T0125 marks it by *excluding* circling motivation at two quality-conditions (positions 5–6: liberation, cessation). One is a positive mark (pointing toward liberation), the other a negative mark (not pointing toward rebirth); the two are structurally complementary, **not** mutually exclusive, **not** substitutive.

Position 8, "dedicating to others, not keeping for oneself," is the **completing condition** of the recipe. Once the prior seven conditions have generated a complete event of *dāna*, the way the merit-fruit is handled decides whether this event enters the individual's karmic loop or is converted into shared karmic provisioning. Dedication toward all beings is the **outward mirror** of AN 8.31's position 8 **inward cultivation of mind** — the final positions of both axes point to the structural slot of "exit from personal *saṁsāric* reward," differing only in vector (inward *cittālaṅkāra* vs outward *pariṇāmanā*). This mirror-symmetry constitutes a structural meeting-point between the two axes at the final position; it is not a translation residue, but a parallel solution by two independent lineages to the question of "how the structurally highest position of *dāna* should be configured."

When the seven categories of condition are co-present, the *puñña-kriyā* completeness of one *dāna* event is achieved. This completeness is not a subjective judgment of "the most excellent giving," but a structural criterion — when timing is fitting, substance is pure, the hand is one's own, resolve is clearly framed, orientation is toward liberation, the recipient is worthy, and the merit is dedicated, the event's structural completeness reaches its maximum along these seven dimensions simultaneously. *Cāgānussati* (P03) can take such an event as *nimitta* without encountering structural anomalies in the recollection (such as wrong time, foul substance, delegation, random flash, seeking return, unworthy recipient, private retention) — these seven dimensions precisely exclude the seven event-features unfavorable to recollection.

### 3.3 Convergence of the two axes — AN 8.36 as container, and the faculty-motivation-object chain P02 → P04 → P03

With this the independent structure of each axis is established: AN 8.31's motivation axis (mutually exclusive spectrum, oriented toward *cittālaṅkāra*) and T0125's quality axis (co-present recipe, oriented toward seven conditions complete). This section treats the axes' convergence — they do not substitute for each other, are not in a translation relation, but are **two analytical dimensions of the same *dāna* limb**, working together, within the container of AN 8.36 and above the faculty-to-object chain established in P02 → P03, to take up the structural role P04 plays in the series.

**The structural leverage of the AN 8.36 container**. §2.2 has already indicated that AN 8.36's three bases of meritorious action and three intensities form a matrix of **qualitative classification + quantitative modulation**. The three bases (*dāna* / *sīla* / *bhāvanā*) position *dāna* as the most easily externalized *puñña-kriyā* — the act of *dāna* has a visible transfer of matter and an explicit spatiotemporal locus; compared to the normative holding of *sīla* or the purely inner training of *bhāvanā*, *dāna* is the base with the lowest resistance to classification. This explains why the internal content of *dāna* could develop eight-fold and even multi-axial classifications, whereas the eight-fold classification of *sīla* exists but is narrower in significance, and *bhāvanā* turns wholesale into other systems. The AN 8.36 container thereby delimits the legitimacy boundary of the two axes: both the motivation axis and the quality axis are internal analyses of *dānamayaṁ puññakiriyavatthu*, not crossing the border of the three bases.

**From container to the orthogonal unfolding of the qualitative axes**. The motivation axis answers "why the event is generated" — under which motivational structures the *cāga*-faculty is activated into an observable *dāna* event. The quality axis answers "how the event is structurally complete" — whether a generated *dāna* event has all seven conditions co-present, whether it reaches *puñña-kriyā* completeness. The two questions are logically independent: the same event may be "motivation *cittālaṅkāra* + seven conditions complete" (the ideal case), or it may be "motivation of outer compulsion + seven conditions complete" (giving under threat but with the object selected in a timely manner, fresh and pure, with one's own hand, with a resolve, etc. — this case is empirically possible, e.g., a person coerced who still selects an appropriate gift). The motivation axis and the quality axis are **orthogonal**; each independently describes one dimension of the event, and neither entails the other.

This orthogonality further aligns with AN 8.36's three-intensity axis as orthogonal. Intensity is independent of motivation, and intensity is independent of quality — a high-intensity giving can arise from any motivation and exhibit any quality-configuration; the same for low-intensity. The three axes (motivation / quality / intensity) constitute a **three-dimensional matrix**, in which every particular *dāna* event can be located.

**The faculty-motivation-object chain P02 → P04 → P03**. Part I of this series has by now formed a progressive structure. P02 treats *cāga* as a **faculty** — the inner configuration of one already entered into the stream, with the fourth limb of stream-entry as its structural position and the loosening of the "mine-making" root-construction as its functional definition. P03 treats *cāga* as an **object** — an already-accomplished *dāna* event as *anussati nimitta*, with the fifth of the six recollections as its structural position and event-hood as its distinctive feature. P04 treats *cāga* as a **motivation-generating condition** — under which motivational structures the *cāga*-faculty is activated into an observable *dāna* event, which event is then taken by recollection as object. The three-layer chain:

```
Faculty (P02)
    ↓ activated under some motivational structure
Event generated by motivation (motivation axis · P04 §3.1)
    ↓ structurally complete with some quality-configuration
Structurally complete event (quality axis · P04 §3.2)
    ↓ taken by recollection as nimitta
Object (P03)
    ↓ held by recollection as ārammaṇa
Upacāra samādhi (P03 §3.2)
```

In this three-layer chain, P04 lies at the middle segment — the bridge from P02's faculty to P03's object. Without the dual-axis analysis that P04 performs, P02's faculty jumps directly to P03's object, and the criterion for "which events are fit to serve as objects of recollection" is left hanging; P04 supplies this criterion — the motivation axis's *cittālaṅkāra* (position 8) and the quality axis's seven conditions together form the structural filter for what counts as an event fit to serve as an object of recollection.

**Further refinement of the Chinese-translation layering judgment made in P03 §3.3**. P03 §3.3 already refined the previous paper's verdict of "systematic softening in T0099" into a layered one — "softening at the doctrinal-finalization layer, preservation at the object-description layer." This section adds a further layer on that basis: **the contrast between T0125 and T0099**. T0099 *juan* 33, as the Chinese parallel to the 55th *Saṃyutta* collection, does at the structural-doctrinal layer (limbs of stream-entry, five-virtue foundation, six recollections) cover *cāga* with "giving" (施); T0125 *juan* 37 sūtra 9, however, with the independent development of a quality axis, **exists not as a translation of AN 8.31 but as a parallel taxonomy** — this is not "the Chinese softening the AN," but the Chinese Āgama lineage's **own independent structural doctrinal contribution**. The earlier "systematic softening" verdict thus becomes further refined: the softening exists only where T0099's doctrinal-layer correspondence runs; T0125's quality axis is not explained by the softening model — it is the Chinese Āgama's **independent doctrinal contribution**, neither a translation of the Pāli nor a softening of it. This refinement gives Western readers a more precise coordinate for evaluating the Chinese tradition: the Chinese is not consistently tilting toward *dāna*; it makes different structural choices at different textual layers — in places it tilts toward *dāna* (T0099's doctrinal layer), in places it stands independently as a parallel taxonomy (T0125's quality axis). This layering is closer to the actual doctrinal diversity of the Chinese Āgama than the single verdict of "systematic softening."

Putting the four together — the two axes, the container, the faculty-motivation-object chain, and the Chinese-translation layering — P04's structural position is clear: it occupies the **motivation-generation layer** in Part I's progressive chain, linking P02's faculty layer and P03's object layer; and on both traditions (Pāli / Chinese) it treats two eight-fold classificatory axes in parallel, opening Part II *The Āgama View* toward the systematic treatment of the Āgama exposition of *dāna*.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Interchange: The Structural Divergence between AN 8.31's Motivation Axis and Contemporary Research on Donor Motivation

Contemporary research in charitable ethics and philanthropic behavior presents a taxonomy of donor motivation composed mainly of three frameworks. James Andreoni's 1989/1990 *warm glow giving* model challenges the pure-altruism model by indicating that part of the utility of giving comes from the inner satisfaction generated by the act of giving itself[^7]. Roland Bénabou and Jean Tirole's 2006 *identity signaling* theory models pro-social behavior as a mechanism of publicly signaling a pro-social identity — donors maintain self-concept and social position through observable acts of generosity[^8]. William Harbaugh, Ulrich Mayr, and Daniel Burghart's 2007 fMRI study demonstrates that voluntary generous giving activates the same striatal reward circuits as receiving monetary rewards — the so-called "neural aesthetics of giving" shares substrate architecture with the neural base of receiving a gain[^9]. Together these three constitute the mainstream contemporary taxonomy of donor motivation: warm glow (affective reward) + signal (social reward) + neural reward (physiological substrate).

This framework corresponds with precision, along **the first seven positions**, to the AN 8.31 motivation axis: *warm glow* corresponds to position 5 *sāhu dānan'ti* (the internalized affect of "giving is good") — Andreoni's warm glow is *sāhu dānan'ti* operationalized in the grammar of contemporary behavioral economics; *identity signaling* corresponds to position 7 *kittisadda* (giving for the sake of fame) — Bénabou–Tirole's signal-transmission refines the social-individual bidirectional mechanism of *kittisadda*, wherein "the other observing my generosity" constitutes the social structure of identity-stabilization; the Harbaugh–Mayr–Burghart fMRI study supplies the physiological substrate for positions 1–7 — warm glow, signal, transactional return, and their kin all have corresponding neural-circuit activity. The structural correspondence across the first seven positions is clear, verifiable, mutually translatable — the contemporary framework in fact **covers** AN 8.31's first seven positions.

But at position 8, *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra*, the correspondence **breaks**. The measurement tools and modeling vocabulary of the contemporary framework are built within the coordinate system of welfare optimization — every motivational utility is oriented toward some form of this-life welfare (affect, social standing, neural pleasure); position 8's **adornment and equipping of the mind** does not enter this coordinate system. It is not "some still more refined welfare" — not self-actualization, not spiritual growth, not moral pleasure — it **points toward liberation itself**. As an ultimate end, liberation does not lie within the vocabulary of welfare optimization, because the welfare model's own boundary condition is the continuation of the circling subject — improvement of welfare presupposes the subject's continued existence; liberation presupposes the **loosening** of subject-structure. Two ultimate ends categorically incompatible.

**Three non-reducible differences**:

- **Difference in ultimate end**: welfare optimization points toward the maximization of subject-welfare; *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra* points toward the loosening of subject-structure and the influx of liberation. The former presupposes continuation of subject, the latter prepares for the dissolution of subject-continuation. The two stand not in a relation of "more refined vs. cruder" but in a fundamental categorical difference of ultimate ends.
- **Difference at the limb layer**: the motivations in the contemporary framework are the computed outputs of an individual utility function; AN 8.31 position 8's motivation is the activation of the *cāga*-faculty (the "substance" side of the substance-function pair of P02) — the **activation** of a faculty differs from the **computation** of a utility. Utility describes the outputs of behavior, a faculty describes the path-structural configuration from which behavior emerges; the former describes the yield of action, the latter describes the inner structure out of which action arises.
- **Difference downstream**: the downstream of welfare maximization is donor satisfaction (the striatal activation recorded by Harbaugh et al.); the downstream of *cittālaṅkāra* is *upacāra samādhi* (the access concentration flagged by Buddhaghosa in P03 §3.2) — access concentration is a ladder toward *bhāvanā*, not some higher welfare state. Satisfaction and access concentration may both be **phenomenologically positive inner states**, but their structural teleologies are wholly different.

**⚠️ Unidirectional analogy caveat**: the contemporary framework of donor motivation can help a Western reader newly encountering AN 8.31 quickly identify how the first seven positions present themselves in contemporary life — how *warm glow* operates, where *identity signaling* activates, how fMRI evidence supports the physiological substrate of positions 1–7. This direction of analogy is valid. The reverse does not hold: the concept of *cittālaṅkāra* at position 8 cannot be deployed to "optimize" the research paradigm of contemporary philanthropic studies — that is a question within the category of behavioral science, and AN 8.31 position 8 was not designed for that. The analogy is deeply isomorphic at the first seven positions and structurally divergent at the eighth; the place of divergence is the categorical boundary between the science of liberation and behavioral science.

The analytical form of this section — using the contemporary framework as an "external mirror" to reflect the liberation-directed mark of AN 8.31's structure — is complementary to the §IV forms of P01 (scarcity mindset), P02 (costly signaling), and P03 (intra-Buddhist structural). Part I's first paper turned to psychology, the second to evolutionary biology, the third to intra-Buddhist structure, and this one to behavioral economics; taken together, the four §IV angles give Part I reasonable coverage in its interdisciplinary boundary-survey of *dāna*, enough to hand off to the systematic treatment of the Āgama exposition of *dāna* in Part II *The Āgama View*.

## V. Middle-way Corrections

This section treats two misreadings and one caution, all relating to the paper's dual-axis architecture.

**❌ Misreading 1: Giving = Transactional Merit / Merit-Accumulation**

When Western readers encounter Chinese-tradition Buddhist language of "eight kinds of merit," the most common misreading is to read giving as transactional merit-accumulation: I put in matter, I get merit-credit back; the credit accumulates to some threshold at which it can be exchanged for a better rebirth or for rebirth in the heavens. This reading takes AN 8.31's positions 3–4 (*'adāsi me'ti* / *'dassati me'ti*, return-motivated giving) as the summary of the whole *dāna* structure, ignoring the other positions of the spectrum.

This reading errs by **substituting a part for the whole**. Among AN 8.31's eight motivations, transactional return occupies only positions 3–4; positions 1–2 are compulsion, positions 5–6 are cultural conformity, position 7 is reputation, position 8 is inner cultivation — transactional motivation is not the backbone of the taxonomy, it is only one middle segment of the spectrum. To read the whole of *dāna* as merit-accumulation is to see only positions 3–4 of the spectrum and block out positions 5–8 (especially position 8's *cittālaṅkāra*). Any reader attentive to the AN 8.31 original will not make this error — the sūtra clearly places transactional motivation alongside five other motivations, granting it no structural privilege. This paper's dual-axis analysis is itself a structural rebuttal of this misreading: the motivation axis contains eight positions, of which transaction occupies only two; among the seven conditions of the quality axis, positions 5–6 (liberation / cessation) precisely **exclude** circling-reward motivation. Read the structure clearly, and the merit-accumulation misreading falls apart on its own.

**❌ Misreading 2: The Chinese emphasis on quality = Soulless ritualization**

An opposite common misreading: a Western reader encounters T0125's quality-conditions of "at the right time, fresh and pure, with one's own hand, with a clear vow" and may read this as "the Chinese translation ritualizes *dāna* into external procedure, losing the inner-spirit dimension." This reading rests on the false equivalence "quality-conditions = external procedure = non-inward."

The error of this reading lies in **misreading the structural layering of the quality-conditions**. §3.2 has already indicated that among T0125's eight bases, positions 5–6, "giving out of liberation, not looking for return" / "giving aimed at cessation, not seeking rebirth in the heavens," are **not** external ritual procedures — they are inner orientation conditions explicitly excluding circling-motivation. The Chinese translation here embeds the liberation-directed filter directly into the quality layer rather than separating the quality layer from the motivation layer. To read the eight quality-bases as "purely external procedure" is to miss the liberation-directed character of positions 5–6 entirely; this reading equates a recipe containing inner conditions with a procedure having only outer steps. At a deeper level: quality-conditions as **structural criteria of completeness** are not, in their nature, a binary distinction of "inner vs outer," but a tool for judging "whether the event-structure is complete." Timing fitness (position 1) is both an outer condition (the objective moment) and an inner judgment (whether the giver perceives this moment as appropriate); personal hand (position 3) is both a bodily movement and a marker of inner presence. To binarize the quality-conditions into "outer vs inner" is itself a false dichotomy — the Chinese quality axis refuses this dichotomy, emphasizing the multi-dimensional calibration of *dāna* as a complete event (outer act + inner resolve + liberation-directed orientation). Read the structure clearly, and the soulless-ritualization misreading falls apart on its own.

**⚠️ Caution: AN 8.31 position 8 *cittālaṅkāra* is not branded mindful charity**

When contemporary Western readers encounter the translation "giving for the adornment and equipping of the mind," they are very prone to read it as "mindful charity" in the MBSR / contemporary mindfulness tradition — maintaining present-moment awareness during giving, attending to one's own inner feelings, experiencing the affective satisfaction of giving. This reading absorbs AN 8.31 position 8 into the affective-well-being frame of contemporary therapeutic mindfulness, downgrading *cittālaṅkāra* into a technique of affective self-regulation.

This downgrade is an error. Both compounds of *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra* are rooted not in affective regulation but in **the cultivation of the mind as an instrument of the path to liberation**. *Alaṅkāra* (adornment) refers to the already-accomplished equipping of the mind as the bearer of the noble path — making the mind "worthy to bear" the noble path; *parikkhāra* (equipment) refers to the mind's **path-going equipage** — equipping the mind with the configurations required to walk the noble path. Both point to *cāga* as a limb (the fourth limb of stream-entry as P02 established), to *cāgānussati* as a gateway into *upacāra* (as P03 established), and ultimately to the path of liberation. The affective well-being of MBSR-style mindfulness belongs to the category of contemporary psychotherapy — its ultimate aim is psychological health (individual welfare), not liberation. The two **may look similar on the behavioral surface** (sitting, awareness, attention inward), but **their structural teleologies are entirely different**. To read *cittālaṅkāra* as mindful charity is to degrade the motivation of the path of stream-entry into a technique of therapeutic application — the directions are exactly opposite (the former toward the loosening of subject-continuity, the latter toward the stabilization of subject-continuity).

This caution is especially relevant to certain dialogic interfaces between contemporary Engaged Buddhism and secular mindfulness — some constructs deliberately mix mindfulness and charity to drive contemporary social-engaged spirituality. Such constructs may have their practical value, but if AN 8.31 position 8 is appropriated as their scriptural basis, they will structurally misread this teaching. The present paper's treatment of *cittālaṅkāra* holds firmly to its original liberation-directed orientation, and does not divert it to serve as rhetorical capital for contemporary applications.

## VI. Conclusion

**Recapitulation**: early Buddhism's eight-fold classification of *dāna* is not a single fixed structure but a **dual-axis independent development** — Pāli AN 8.31 takes a spectrum of motivational purity as its axis (the eight positions running from outer compulsion to inner cultivation, culminating in the liberation-directed motivation of *cittālaṅkāra*); the Chinese *Ekottara Āgama juan* 37 sūtra 9 takes a quality-configuration recipe as its axis (the seven categories of condition rendering *dāna* complete as *puñña-kriyā*, with the liberation-directed filter of liberation / cessation embedded within the quality-configuration). The two axes are not translation correspondents; they are parallel contributions of two independent lineages to the structural analysis of *dāna*. The two axes converge above the container of AN 8.36's three bases of meritorious action and within the faculty-object chain established by P02 → P03 — the motivation axis filters the generation conditions by which the *cāga*-faculty is activated into an observable event, the quality axis calibrates the structural completeness of already-generated events, and together they form the dual criterion for events fit to serve as objects of recollection, offering structural assurance for the purity of the object held by *cāgānussati*. The present paper thus occupies the motivation-generation layer within Part I's progressive chain, bridging P02's faculty and P03's object, and opens Part II *The Āgama View* toward the systematic treatment of the Āgama exposition of *dāna*.

**Practice directive**: for the contemporary reader, the practical significance of the dual axis lies in providing a **dual self-examination criterion**. Before and after every act of *dāna* about to occur, one can self-examine with the two axes. Motivation-axis self-examination: why am I giving at this moment? Is it position 1, compulsion? Positions 3–4, transactional expectation? Position 7, reputation-maintenance? Or position 8, cultivation of the mind? There is no need to force oneself to have to be at position 8 — the very existence of the spectrum acknowledges that the first seven are the real spectrum of human giving-motivation, and does not shame them. But the self-examination is useful: seeing clearly the rung of motivation at this moment prevents the self-deception of misreading position 3 as position 8. Honestly seeing, again and again, one's own position on the spectrum is itself the beginning of movement toward position 8. Quality-axis self-examination: is this giving seven-fold complete? Is the timing fitting? Is the matter pure? Is the hand one's own? Is there a clear commitment? Is it oriented toward liberation and not toward circling reward? Is the recipient worthy? After the act, is the merit dedicated? The seven conditions are not required to be complete each time (perfect occasions are rare in daily *dāna*), but each time a condition is missing, one can understand the specific location of this event along the spectrum of *puñña-kriyā* completeness. This kind of structural awareness is itself a fine training on *dāna*.

Both axes together serve the *cāgānussati* established in P03 — a *dāna* event with pure motivation and all quality-conditions in place is the most stable *nimitta* for *cāgānussati* to hold as *ārammaṇa*. Conversely, an event with mixed motivation or missing quality-conditions will meet structural anomalies during recollection, making *upacāra* hard to stabilize. Dual-axis self-examination is therefore not the ethical requirement of "being a better donor," but the path-equipment of "cultivating a clean *nimitta* for meditation."

**Open question**: §II Group 4 has already noted T0125 *juan* 37 sūtra 8's Anāthapiṇḍika "eight positions of giving" as the adjacent reference of a position axis — the existence of a third axis hints that early Buddhism's structural analysis of *dāna* may have still other axial directions (a recipient-category axis, a spatiotemporal axis, an object-type axis, etc.). The present paper does not enter the position axis as a main argument; the next paper, P05, will treat the recipient-hierarchy structure in the *Velāma* case[^10] — that is the direct development from the position axis into the recipient axis: from "eight positions" (eight locations for giving) refined into a "recipient hierarchy" (a liberation-ward hierarchy of recipients). *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 9.20 *Velāmasutta*'s eleven-tier ladder, running from offering to worldly persons up to offering to the noble ones, is early Buddhism's most complete scriptural ground for treating **the recipient as a field-of-merit quality variable**; P05 will use it to open Part II's systematic treatment of the recipient axis, forming, together with this paper's motivation / quality axes, a three-axis parallel structure in the Chinese Āgama's exposition of *dāna*.

**Closing structural observation**: the motivation / quality dual axis treated in this paper, together with the recipient hierarchy to be treated in P05, forms Part II's early-segment **inner structural analysis of *dāna*** — the inner side of the giver (motivation), the structure of the giving-act (quality), the structure of the recipient (*Velāma* ladder). This triadic inner structural analysis will, in the latter segment of Part II (P06 present-life benefits, P07 personal field-of-merit), form a contrast with the **outer effect-structure** of *dāna* (benefit / fruition), and will in P08's section on the three bases of meritorious action be integrated into the complete picture of the early Buddhist exposition of *dāna*. As the opening paper of Part II, the present paper establishes a methodological stance of dual-axis / multi-axis structural analysis for the series of arguments to follow.

## Cross-reference index to the series

- **This volume, P01 *What Giving Ultimately Does***: §I, §III, and §IV borrow from P01's three-word semantic field (*dāna* / *cāga* / *yāga*) the inner-cultivation vector of *cāga*, providing background for the anchoring of AN 8.31 position 8's *cittālaṅkāra* motivation within that field. P01 locates *cāga*'s unique semantic position by distinguishing terms; this paper refines, at the motivation-generation layer, the structural conditions under which *cāga* is activated.
- **This volume, P02 *Cāga as a Faculty of Stream-Entry***: the preceding link at the faculty layer. P02 establishes *cāga* as the fourth limb of stream-entry, the faculty (substance) — the inner configuration by which the root-construction of "mine-making" is loosened. This paper supplies the motivation-generation layer between P02's faculty and P03's object — under which motivational structures the faculty is activated into an observable *dāna* event. §3.3 explicitly marks this paper's middle position in the three-layer chain (faculty → motivation-generated event → object).
- **This volume, P03 *Cāga as an Object of Recollection***: the following link at the object layer. P03 establishes *cāga* as the fifth of the six recollections, the object (function) — an already-accomplished *dāna* event as *anussati nimitta*. This paper provides a structural filter for P03's object — AN 8.31 position 8 *cittālaṅkāra* (motivation axis) and T0125's seven conditions co-present (quality axis) form together the dual criterion for an event fit to serve as an object of recollection. §3.3 adds a third layer of refinement to P03 §3.3's "systematic softening of T0099" judgment: the softening exists only where T0099 has a doctrinal-layer correspondent; T0125's quality axis is the Chinese Āgama's independent doctrinal contribution.
- **This volume, P05 *The Velāma Case and the Recipient Hierarchy***: §VI's open question points explicitly here. This paper treats the two axes of "giver motivation / giving-act structure," and P05 will open the recipient axis with AN 9.20 *Velāmasutta* — merit-field quality as the third independent variable of the *dāna* structure. The three axes together form Part II's early-segment inner structural analysis of *dāna* (inner side of the giver + structure of the giving-act + structure of the recipient).
- **This volume, P08 *The Three Bases of Meritorious Action as Container***: §2.2 and §3.3's AN 8.36 container analysis is a preview of P08. The present paper treats AN 8.36 as the container above the two axes and as an orthogonal matrix; P08 will integrate Part II's full-segment multi-axial analysis of recipient / quality / motivation by means of AN 8.36's three bases × three intensities, completing the full structural picture of the early Buddhist exposition of *dāna*.
- **This volume, P11 *From Dāna to Dāna-Pāramitā***: §IV's inward, soteriologically-vectored character of position 8 *cittālaṅkāra* runs in structural continuity with the *dāna-pāramitā* "three-sphere purity" (三輪體空) structure to be argued in P11 of Part IV. This paper serves as the Pāli-layer anchor for AN 8.31 position 8, and P11 will be the early-Mahāyāna-layer thoroughgoing unfolding of that anchor.
- **NIAN volume Part I, P03 *The Six Recollections as a System***: within the same six-recollection material, the full skeleton of the positional structure of *cāgānussati* is unfolded by NIAN P03; the present paper (DĀNA P04) supplies the structural analysis of the preconditions for object-generation (motivation axis / quality axis). The two volumes are complementary at the *cāgānussati* node: NIAN P03 treats the anussati verb-perspective, DĀNA P04 treats the motivation-quality configuration preceding object-generation.
