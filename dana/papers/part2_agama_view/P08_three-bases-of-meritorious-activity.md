# The Three Bases of Meritorious Activity
**Dāna, Sīla, Bhāvanā as the Three-Layer Container of Merit — AN 8.36 as Part II's Closing Container and the Structural Case for Dāna in First Place**

*中文主題：三福業事——施、戒、修的三層福業容器——AN 8.36 作為 Part II 收束容器與施居首之結構性證成*

---

## Abstract

The three bases of meritorious activity (*puñña-kiriya-vatthu*) established in AN 8.36 *Puññakiriyavatthusutta* are a three-fold classificatory axis — merit made of giving (*dānamaya*), merit made of virtue (*sīlamaya*), merit made of cultivation (*bhāvanāmaya*) — and constitute a stable cross-transmission structure of early Buddhism as three **material types** of merit. This paper takes that triad as the **closing container** of Part II, integrating the four-axis analysis of giving's internal structure laid down in the four preceding papers. That giving stands in first place is a cross-transmission factual stability: AN 8.36 enumerates the list three times without inversion; the Chinese *Ekottarikāgama juan* 12 *Three Jewels Chapter* sūtra 2 and the *Dīrghāgama Saṅgītisūtra* both match the three items precisely as 施業 (*shīyè*, "giving-action") / 平等業 (*píngděngyè*, "impartial-action") / 思惟業 (*sīwéiyè*, "reflection-action"). This paper builds its argument on two anchors: the **twin-engine observation of the sense-sphere realm** in AN 8.36 (the panorama of fruits across the six sense-sphere heavens is driven by the twin axes of giving-and-virtue together, while cultivation remains at the "does not reach" level throughout this terrain) and the **cross-sectarian translation tension around 平等業** ("impartial-action" as the Chinese rendering of *sīla*). From these two anchors the paper derives four structural reasons for giving's first place — outwardly observable, accessible to householders, *cetanā* flowing in two directions, and the standing order of graduated discourse (*anupubbikathā*). Giving in first place is not the floor of a value-ladder but the **entrance to the three-base container**. This argument reassigns the four axes of P04–P07 (motivation and quality, the rank of the giver, the temporal axis of fruit, the category of the recipient) as the internal unfolding of *dānamaya* — the first base — and hands off to Part III's ninth paper *The Sangha as a Field of Merit*, where the institutionalization of the field-of-merit turns this entrance into a sustainable communal undertaking.

**Keywords:** three bases of meritorious activity, *puñña-kiriya-vatthu*, merit made of giving, *dānamaya*, merit made of virtue, *sīlamaya*, merit made of cultivation, *bhāvanāmaya*, AN 8.36 *Puññakiriyavatthusutta*, *Ekottarikāgama juan* 12 〈Three Jewels Chapter〉 sūtra 2, *Dīrghāgama Saṅgītisūtra*, 平等業 *píngděngyè*, impartial-action, twin-engine observation of the sense-sphere realm, graduated discourse, *anupubbikathā*, two-directional flow of *cetanā*, the entrance-function of giving, Part II's closing container, *-maya* construction as material classification, the six sense-sphere heavens, *adhimatta-mattaso-paritta* stratification, *sīla* as *samacariyā*, equal-conduct, the four immeasurable minds reassigned to the virtue base, the seven factors of awakening as cultivation, cross-sectarian twin-crystallization, T0026 *Madhyamāgama* structural lacuna, Itivuttaka 3.19 non-inclusion, honest-absence declaration, Aristotelian *aretē* and habit, contemporary behavioral-habit science, minimum-viable-action, structural-isomorphism without doctrinal reduction, *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra*, first-base inward unfolding of P04–P07, reassignment of the four axes, hand-off to P09 *The Sangha as a Field of Merit*, *cātuddisa saṅgha* in forward view.

## I. Problem Consciousness

Contemporary English-speaking Buddhist readers typically carry an implicit preconception about the relative ranks of giving (*dāna*) and meditative cultivation (*bhāvanā*): cultivation is taken to be the deep practice at the core of the path, giving a moral starting-point or a first-rung meritorious activity in the department of rewards. The operational conventions of a ten-day *vipassanā* retreat make this preconception concrete: participants pay for the venue, sit in meditation throughout, and at the end offer a freewill *dāna* to the teacher and the center as a gesture of respect; meditation is framed as the practice itself, giving as a logistical posture or gesture of gratitude around the practice. The industrial critique of the mindfulness-commodification has already challenged the simplistic narrative that "mindfulness equals authentic Buddhism," but the parallel intuition about the internal ordering of the three bases — the high-low sorting of giving, virtue, and cultivation — has not yet received a symmetrical correction.

Early Buddhism, however, orders these three bases explicitly and repeatedly. AN 8.36 *Puññakiriyavatthusutta* of the Aṅguttara Nikāya takes the three bases of meritorious activity (*puñña-kiriya-vatthu*) as its skeleton, enumerating the three items in sequence: merit made of giving (*dānamaya*), merit made of virtue (*sīlamaya*), merit made of cultivation (*bhāvanāmaya*); this sequence is listed three times in the sūtra and never inverted, and it recurs in the same order in the three-fold sets of DN 33 *Saṅgītisutta*. On the Chinese side, the *Ekottarikāgama juan* 12 Three-Jewels Chapter sūtra 2 matches the three items in the order 施業 (giving-action) / 平等業 (impartial-action) / 思惟業 (reflection-action) precisely with the Pāli; the *Dīrghāgama Saṅgīti Sūtra*, in its three-*dharma* section, also lists them in the same order within Śāriputra's catalogue of the saṅgīti items. Giving in first place is a stable structural fact internal to early Buddhism, not a random translator's choice.

If this sequence is read as a value-ladder ascending from shallow to deep, with giving as the shallowest first rung, it aligns with the contemporary reader's intuitive direction: cultivation above, giving below. But that reading runs into structural obstacles within the text itself. The passage on the fruits of the six sense-sphere heavens in AN 8.36 shows no "strong" (*adhimatta*) intensity of *bhāvanāmaya* anywhere in the panorama; the fruits across the sense-sphere heavens are driven, rung by rung, by "strong giving and strong virtue, cultivation not reaching." If giving were the first shallow rung and cultivation the terminal deep one, why should the upward motion across the entire sense-sphere panorama be powered by the twin engine of giving and virtue? And if giving were merely an introductory virtue, why should the Buddha, in the standard order of discourse to householders (the graduated discourse, *anupubbikathā*), always place the talk on giving (*dānakathā*) first? These structural observations together point to a different reading: giving in first place is **not** the floor of a value-ladder but **the entrance to the three-base container** — outwardly observable, accessible to householders, structurally source-flowing at the motivational layer, and always standing first in pedagogical order.

The four preceding papers of Part II have already laid down four axes of giving's internal structure. P04 handled the motivation and quality axes via AN 8.31, AN 8.32, and the sevenfold complete causation in *Ekottarikāgama juan* 37; P05 handled the rank axis of the giver via AN 9.20 *Velāma Sutta*; P06 handled the epistemic temporal axis of fruit via AN 5.34 *Sīhasenāpatisutta*; P07 handled the recipient-category axis via MN 142 *Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅgasutta*. All four axes treat giving's internal structure — why one gives, how one gives, when the fruit ripens, to what class of recipient — but none of them frontally answers a more basic question: why does giving stand in first place within the larger structure of the three bases of meritorious activity? That question can be answered only by stepping back to the three-base container of AN 8.36.

The structural task of this paper is therefore threefold. **First**, to unfold frontally the cross-sectional classificatory sense of the three bases of meritorious activity — to establish that the three items are not three parallel meritorious projects that admit comparison in size, but three **material types**: the "*-maya*" (made of) construction gives the three items the sense "merit whose material is this very item," making the triad a classificatory axis that can hold any good action, rather than a ranked ladder of merits. **Second**, to derive the four structural reasons for giving's first place — outwardly observable, accessible to householders, *cetanā* flowing in two directions, and the graduated discourse — anchored in two observations: the structural hint embedded in the fruit-passage of the six sense-sphere heavens in AN 8.36 (the twin-engine of the sense-sphere realm) and the Chinese translation tension around 平等業 ("impartial-action" rendering *sīla*). **Third**, to use the three-base container as the integrative frame that reassigns the four-axis analysis of P04–P07, and to hand off to P09 *The Sangha as a Field of Merit*: giving being the entrance, how does the institutionalized field-of-merit (*cātuddisa saṅgha*, the property of the sangha of the four quarters) turn this entrance into a sustainable communal undertaking?

The argument unfolds across six sections. §II takes AN 8.36 as its Pāli proto-form and adds three Chinese witnesses — T0125 *juan* 12 Three-Jewels Chapter sūtra 2 (the Chinese master anchor), T0001 *juan* 8 *Saṅgīti Sūtra* (sectarian parallel), and T0099 *juan* 30 SĀ 841 (the verse-form transmission in the Chinese) — and positively acknowledges three absences: the structural lacuna of T0026 *Madhyamāgama*, the non-inclusion of the Itivuttaka version in the working corpus, and the limits on Abhidhamma-layer expansion. §III unfolds the structural case for giving's first place in four moves: AN 8.36's textual formula and the twin-engine observation of the sense-sphere realm; the Chinese parallels and the translation tension around 平等業; the four structural reasons for giving's first place; and the reassignment of Part II's four axes. §IV gives a light cross-disciplinary resonance with Book II of Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* and contemporary behavioral-habit science; the cross-disciplinary registration in Part II's closing paper stays minimal as a matter of principle. §V addresses three common misreadings — reading giving's first place as first-rung merit, reading "impartial-action" as a translation error, and reading the T0026 lacuna as a defect in the canon — plus one cross-disciplinary misreading risk. §VI recapitulates the essentials, offers practice guidance, and hands off to Part III.

## II. Textual Foundations

The citations in this section are arranged along two tracks: the first is **sūtra** (甲), in four groups — the AN 8.36 Pāli proto-form (Group 1), the T0125 *juan* 12 Three-Jewels Chapter sūtra 2 as Chinese master anchor (Group 2), the T0001 *juan* 8 *Saṅgīti Sūtra* as sectarian parallel (Group 3), and the T0099 *juan* 30 SĀ 841 as verse-form transmission (Group 4) — followed by an "honest-absence declaration" that positively acknowledges the T0026 structural lacuna, the non-inclusion of the Itivuttaka version, and the limits on Abhidhamma-layer expansion. The second track is **commentary** (乙), handled briefly with the four sūtra-layer texts functioning as internal cross-glosses in lieu of a separate commentarial layer. Following the NIAN-A dual-track discipline, Pāli materials carry PTS references; Chinese materials carry Taishō volume and sūtra numbers.

### 2.1 Sūtra （甲）

**Group 1: AN 8.36 *Puññakiriyavatthusutta* — the Pāli proto-form**

AN 8.36[^1] is the sixth sūtra of the fourth *Dānavagga* (Chapter on Giving) of Book Eight in the Aṅguttara Nikāya. The sūtra adopts the standard "heading-list / unfolding-items / closing-refrain" sūtra-and-*mātikā* structure, addresses the bhikkhus, and places the core formula three times: in the opening enumeration, in the item-by-item unfolding, and in the closing refrain. The opening enumeration:

> "Bhikkhus, there are these three bases of meritorious activity. Which three? The base of meritorious activity consisting of giving, the base of meritorious activity consisting of virtue, the base of meritorious activity consisting of cultivation." (*Tīṇimāni, bhikkhave, puññakiriyavatthūni. Katamāni tīṇi? Dānamayaṁ puññakiriyavatthu, sīlamayaṁ puññakiriyavatthu, bhāvanāmayaṁ puññakiriyavatthu.*) [translation mine]

The item-by-item unfolding runs through the three bases along three intensity grades — "slight" (*paritta*), "moderate" (*mattaso*), "strong" (*adhimatta*) — and assigns a corresponding fruit-rank to each combination. The crucial structural observation lies in the fruit-ranking: when the practice has reached only "strong giving, strong virtue, cultivation not reaching," the fruits rise in sequence across the six sense-sphere heavens — the Four Great Kings, the Thirty-three, Yāma, Tusita, the gods Delighting in Creation, the gods Wielding Power over the Creations of Others — covering the entire panorama of the sense-sphere heavens. At no point in this passage does the "strong" intensity of *bhāvanāmaya* appear; cultivation remains throughout at "not reaching" (*nābhisambhoti*). This is the sūtra's own structural signal: every upward ascent whose fruit is the sense-sphere heavens is driven by the twin engine of giving and virtue; the terrain in which *bhāvanāmaya* takes effect in its own full intensity (the form realm and above) lies outside the explicit scope of this sūtra. This is the textual basis for the core "twin-engine of the sense-sphere realm" observation of this paper, unfolded in §III.1.

The suffix "*-maya*" (made of) is the backbone concept of this sūtra. Its sense is "with X as material, made of X"; *dāna-maya puñña-kiriya-vatthu* does not mean "the reward of giving" but "the base of meritorious activity whose very constitutive material is the act of giving itself" — the substance of merit is not separable from the concrete action of giving. This construction turns the three bases into three **material types** rather than three parallel meritorious projects: any good action can be assigned to one of the three classes according to the material of its nature. When later Abhidhamma expands the three bases of meritorious activity into the ten bases of meritorious activity (adding seven items: service, reverence, dedication of merit, rejoicing in merit, hearing the Dharma, teaching the Dharma, straightening out of views), the ten items are each distributed into the three classes without a fourth axis being set up — which positively demonstrates that the three bases, as a material classification, are structurally complete. The present paper limits its argument to the three bases at the sūtra layer.

**Group 2: T0125 *juan* 12 Three-Jewels Chapter sūtra 2 — the Chinese master anchor**

The *Ekottarikāgama*, *juan* 12 "Three-Jewels Chapter" sūtra 2[^2], translated by Gautama Saṅghadeva of the Eastern Jin, is this paper's Chinese master anchor — the only Chinese sūtra whose three items correspond in full to AN 8.36. The opening:

> "Thus have I heard. At one time, the Buddha was staying at Jeta's Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika's Park, in Śrāvastī. Then the Blessed One addressed the bhikkhus: 'There are these three activities of merit. What three? Giving is an activity of merit; impartiality is an activity of merit; reflection is an activity of merit.'" [translation mine]

The three items each receive their own unfolding. *Giving as an activity of merit* unfolds as "with an open heart giving to *śramaṇas*, brahmins, the destitute, the lonely, and those with no refuge," with a list of goods (food, drink, clothing, bedding, medicine, incense and flowers, and lodging "as suits the recipient") that matches the recipient-list of the Pāli AN 8.36. *Impartiality as an activity of merit* unfolds most richly of all — it contains the basic armature of the Five Precepts (no killing, no stealing, no sexual misconduct, no false speech, no intoxicants) **together with** the cultivation of the four immeasurable minds (loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity) pervading the eight directions, above and below. *Reflection as an activity of merit* unfolds as cultivation of the seven factors of awakening (mindfulness, investigation-of-dharmas, energy, joy, tranquility, concentration, equanimity) "relying on dispassion, relying on non-observation, relying on cessation, relying on relinquishment." The closing verse recapitulates: "Giving and impartiality, / a loving-kind mind guarding reflection: / these three bases, / are frequented by the wise." [translation mine]

The unfolding of this sūtra exhibits two crucial structural features, both of which §III.2 treats frontally. **First**, the Chinese renders *sīla* as 平等 ("impartial"/"equal") rather than the traditional 戒, 淨戒, or 尸羅 (transliteration). This rendering is rare; scholarship ordinarily back-translates it to *samacariyā* (equal conduct) or *samatā* (equality/evenness), reflecting the functional definition of *sīla* in the translators' sectarian tradition — the essence of *sīla* as the positive function of "acting as equal conduct" rather than the reduced sense of "prohibitive regulation." **Second**, the Chinese redistributes the four immeasurable minds (loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity) into the "impartiality" (virtue) item rather than placing them, as the Pāli does, under merit-of-cultivation as *brahmavihāra-bhāvanā* (the cultivation of the divine abodes); this is a structural reorganization of the functional division of the three bases by the Chinese transmission. *Reflection as an activity of merit* is delimited through the seven factors of awakening (*bojjhaṅga*) rather than through the pairing of *samatha* and *vipassanā* privileged in the later Theravāda: the seven factors of awakening and *samatha-vipassanā* both belong to the thirty-seven aids to awakening and point in the same direction, but they adopt different structural frames.

**Group 3: T0001 *juan* 8 *Saṅgīti Sūtra* — sectarian parallel**

The *Dīrghāgama juan* 8 *Saṅgīti Sūtra*[^3] (*Saṅgītisutta*, *Dīrghāgama* 9), translated by Buddhayaśas and Zhu Fonian of the Latter Qin, is another witness to the three bases of meritorious activity in a distinct sectarian transmission — the translated stratum belongs to the Dharmaguptaka lineage. This sūtra does not treat the three bases of meritorious activity on its own; rather, the three items appear as one entry in Śāriputra's catalogue of *saṅgīti* items delivered at Pāvā, within the *Saṅgīti Sūtra*'s recursive enumerations ("again three items…": the three unwholesome roots / the three wholesome roots / the three unwholesome courses of action / the three wholesome courses of action…). Among these, the three bases of meritorious activity appear as:

> "Again, three items: the three activities of merit — giving-action, impartial-action, reflection-action." [translation mine]

The listing is terse, without any unfolding of fruits. Three structural differences from AN 8.36 stand out: **a difference in sūtra-form** (AN 8.36 is an independent sūtra, while the *Saṅgīti Sūtra* is a catalogue-of-*saṅgīti* entry); **a difference in content** (no ranked unfolding of the fruits across the six sense-sphere heavens); and **a consistency in terminology** (the three items 施業 / 平等業 / 思惟業 match T0125 verbatim).

The third point is especially crucial. T0125 and T0001 belong to different sectarian transmissions; yet the two crystallize independently around the same rendering of the three bases — both using 平等業 for *sīla* and 思惋業 for *bhāvanā* — which cannot be the idiosyncratic choice of an isolated translator and must be read as a **cross-sectarian common functional definition**. This commonality is the core support for the "impartial-action translation tension" argument of §III.2; the two Chinese transmissions both using 平等業 rule out the "translation-error" reading. The crystallization of the three bases of meritorious activity is located along two pathways: AN 8.36 and DN 33 *Saṅgīti Sūtra*; other catalogue-form schematizations (e.g. DN 34 and *Dīrghāgama* 10 *Daśottarasūtra*, which replaces this three-item list with the three *samādhis*)[^4] are differences of catalogue-form, not doctrinal discontinuity.

**Group 4: T0099 *juan* 30 SĀ 841 — the verse-form transmission in the Chinese**

The *Saṃyuktāgama juan* 30 sūtra 841[^5], translated by Guṇabhadra of the Liu-Song, is the verse-layer witness to the three bases of meritorious activity in the Chinese transmission. The sūtra belongs to the "fourfold unshakable faith" (於佛不壞淨, 於法不壞淨, 於僧不壞淨, 聖戒成就) string of sūtras, and uses the simile of "the five great rivers merging" to analogize the noble disciple's "four moistening merits" to the hundred rivers returning to the sea. The verse at the close:

> "…all the great rivers, / upon which living beings depend, / all return to the great ocean: / this body too is like that — / **the merits of giving, virtue, and cultivation, / are where the hundred merits flow to meet**." [translation mine]

The closing line "the merits of giving, virtue, and cultivation, are where the hundred merits flow to meet" is the condensed formula of the three bases of meritorious activity at the verse layer — not an independent *puñña-kiriya-vatthu* doctrinal exposition, but a three-character gathering of the accumulated merit of fourfold-unshakable-faith attainment in this verse. The three-character order 施戒修 matches the Pāli *dāna / sīla / bhāvanā*. This verse circulated very widely in later East Asian Buddhist literature; the phonetic-sense glossaries and the commentarial-exegetical literature frequently cite this phrase as the standard Chinese formulation of the three bases, and the three-character phrase 施戒修 takes its key locus classicus here. This paper uses T0125 as the master anchor for the doctrinal unfolding and SĀ 841 as an auxiliary anchor for the verse-layer transmission.

**Honest-absence declaration**

Three points must be stated frontally. **First**, T0026 *Madhyamāgama* has no direct correspondence to the three bases of meritorious activity[^6] — a full-canon search for 三福業, 施業, 平等業, and 思惟業 returns no matches. This is not an indexing defect but a structural lacuna: the crystallization of the three bases into a formula belongs to the catalogue-of-*saṅgīti* style of the *Dīrgha* collection (enumeration by number as a memorandum against disputation), while the *Madhyama* collection favors the long-form narrative-argumentative style and does not carry this three-item formula. The *Madhyamāgama* preserves many giving-themed narrative sūtras (*Pāyāsi Sutta* MĀ 71, *Soṇa Koṭikaṇṇa Sutta* MĀ 123, *Raṭṭhapāla Sutta* MĀ 132, *Uposatha Sutta*, and others), but in these sūtras the subject of giving unfolds in narrative scenes rather than being gathered by the three-item formula; this is a divergence in sectarian *saṅgīti* style, acknowledged positively at the end of §III.2. **Second**, the *Itivuttaka* sūtra 60 *Puññakiriyavatthu Sutta* (It 3.19) is another Pāli version of the three bases of meritorious activity, which weights *mettā* (loving-kindness) as the core content of merit-of-cultivation[^7]; this version is not present in the current working corpus and is not directly cited by this paper — the related structural connection (the junction of the three bases with the cultivation of loving-kindness) is left for future treatment. **Third**, the Abhidhamma expansion to the ten bases of meritorious activity (*dasa-puñña-kiriya-vatthu*) in the *Dhammasaṅgaṇi* and the *Vibhaṅga* is a later development; this paper remains at the sūtra-layer three bases and does not develop the Abhidhamma-layer expansion.

### 2.2 Commentary （乙）

The commentarial layer of this paper is treated briefly. Buddhaghosa's *Manorathapūraṇī* (the commentary on AN) treats AN 8.36 through scattered phrase-level glosses — a doctrinal disambiguation of the *-maya* construction, a judgment on the criteria of the "slight / moderate / strong" grading of practice, and a doctrinal underpinning for the fruit-ranking across the six sense-sphere heavens — whose structural contribution to the "giving as entrance to the three bases" thesis of this paper is limited. This paper takes the sūtra-layer unfolding of the three bases across the four sūtra-texts (AN 8.36 + T0125 + T0001 + T0099) as a "commentary-function substitute" of the Pāli-to-Chinese internal cross-gloss — the four texts gloss one another; the sūtra layer already exhibits in full the structural role of the three bases as material classification. The full two-voice commentarial dialogue (Buddhaghosa as main voice, Yogācāra as counter-voice), following the Part I convention, will be restarted at the Part IV stage of the Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā*.

## III. Core Argument: The Structural Case for Giving in First Place

This section unfolds the structural case for giving's first place in four layers. §III.1 derives the "twin-engine of the sense-sphere realm" hint from the textual formula of AN 8.36 — the entire upward motion across the sense-sphere panorama is driven solely by the twin axes of giving and virtue; merit-of-cultivation exists in this terrain only at the level of "not reaching." §III.2 treats the Chinese translation tension around 平等業, showing that the two Chinese transmissions crystallize independently yet share a complete functional definition, forming a structural complement with the Pāli commentarial tradition's understanding of *sīla* in its prohibitive face. §III.3 answers the "why giving in first place" question frontally through four structural reasons — outwardly observable, accessible to householders, the two-directional flow of *cetanā*, and the graduated discourse. §III.4 uses the three-base container as the frame through which to revisit the four axes of P04–P07, showing that all four axes are the internal unfolding of *dānamaya* as the first base, and that the three-base container is their structural home.

### 3.1 AN 8.36's Textual Formula and the Twin-Engine Observation of the Sense-Sphere Realm

The inner structural layer of AN 8.36 operates through the three intensity-grades running across the three bases. The sūtra uses "slight" (*paritta*), "moderate" (*mattaso*), and "strong" (*adhimatta*) as the three intensity modifiers of each base, and each combination carries a corresponding fruit-rank. These three grades are not a simple quantitative gradient but a marker of the depth of practice at a given material type — a practitioner may reach "strong" intensity in the giving-base, only "moderate" in the virtue-base, and even "not reaching" (*nābhisambhoti*) in the cultivation-base; each base is graded independently, and they do not substitute for one another.

The fruit-ranking structure of the item-by-item unfolding deserves close looking. When the practice stands at "merit-of-giving slight, merit-of-virtue slight, merit-of-cultivation not reaching," the fruit rises to the lower positions of the human realm; as each base strengthens in turn, the fruit rises to the Four Great Kings, then to the Thirty-three, and so on, all the way to the gods Wielding Power over the Creations of Others — the full panorama of the six sense-sphere heavens. The whole rank-structure reads as a continuously ascending line, easily mistaken for "a linear ladder from low to high." But the decisive property of this ladder is recognized only from what is **absent**.

**Throughout the entire fruit-ranking across the six sense-sphere heavens, the "strong" intensity of merit-of-cultivation (*bhāvanāmaya*) never once appears.** Cultivation remains at "not reaching" (*nābhisambhoti*, "not accomplished"). In other words: the full dynamics of the ascent through the sense-sphere realm are powered by only two bases — giving and virtue. Merit-of-cultivation is present in this stretch, but as "not reaching" — present rather than absent, but simply never at "strong." Its "strong" state arrives only when the fruit-realm enters the form realm (the first through fourth *jhānas*) or the formless realm. The sūtra itself thus makes a structural declaration **by way of absence**: **the sense-sphere realm is the operating terrain of giving and virtue; the full operation of merit-of-cultivation lies above the sense-sphere**.

This twin-engine observation has two consequences for the main thesis. **First**, it dissolves the linear-ladder reading. If the three bases were a linear ladder from shallow to deep (giving shallowest, cultivation deepest), then the fruits across the sense-sphere realm should exhibit a structure in which "giving is primary, virtue secondary, cultivation highest"; in fact the opposite holds — the fruits across the sense-sphere realm are driven by the twin axes of giving and virtue, and cultivation, in this terrain, stays at "not reaching." The observation cannot be dismissed by the shortcut that "cultivation is too deep for the sense-sphere to carry it," because the text explicitly says that cultivation exists at "not reaching" in the sense-sphere layer — existing rather than absent, merely never at "strong." **Second**, it reveals the **functional differentiation** of the three bases, which is not a ladder-differentiation. Giving and virtue are the twin engine in the sense-sphere realm; cultivation exists at "not reaching" in the sense-sphere but becomes the main engine in the form realm and above. The three bases are each assigned their own terrain — giving as entrance, virtue as co-operating at the same layer, cultivation as an upward extension — a functional division of labor, not a "ranking by shallow and deep." This observation is the structural premise for the first of the four reasons in §III.3 (outwardly observable): giving's first place is significant not because it is "shallow" but because of its distinctive function as the entrance to the three-base container — the twin-engine operation in the sense-sphere realm is launched by giving, virtue co-operates at the same layer, and cultivation extends upward into higher terrain.

### 3.2 The Chinese Parallels and the Translation Tension Around 平等業

As §II Groups 2 and 3 showed, both the *Ekottarikāgama* and the *Dīrghāgama Saṅgīti Sūtra* render AN 8.36's *dānamaya / sīlamaya / bhāvanāmaya* as 施業 / 平等業 / 思惟業. Among the three items, 施業 and 思惟業 are not difficult to read on the Chinese side — 施業 directly renders *dāna-maya*; 思惟業 (a standard Chinese rendering of *cintā* or *manasikāra*) covers, through the semantic field of "reflection," the basic face of *bhāvanā* as "cultivation, development." The single rare item is 平等業 (*píngděngyè*, "impartial-action") rendering *sīla* — traditional Chinese Buddhist translations for *sīla* use 戒 (precepts), 淨戒 (pure precepts), or 尸羅 (transliteration), all centered on the negative-face sense of "prohibition," "restraint," or "regulation." The rendering 平等 — a manifestly positive-function word — used for this base implies that the tradition inherited by the translators understood *sīla* not as prohibitive regulation but as the establishment of a positive function.

The etymological back-translation of 平等業 has attracted scholarly attention. Taken at its face, 平等 corresponds to the functional definitions *samatā* (equality/evenness) or *samacariyā* (equal conduct) — the essence of *sīla* lies not in "what it forbids" but in "the protection of self and others through the equality of conduct, without differentiating among beings." This reading has structural support in the early Vinaya: the setting of the Five Precepts is not a naked list of taboos but the equal protection of others' lives (no killing), property (no stealing), bodily integrity (no sexual misconduct), trust in speech (no false speech), and clarity of mind (no intoxicants); the inner kernel of *sīla* is the equal structure of "non-differential non-harming." The translation chamber of Gautama Saṅghadeva (Taiyuan era of the Eastern Jin) and the translation chamber of Buddhayaśas and Zhu Fonian (Hongshi era of the Latter Qin) are separated by about two decades, and their underlying texts come from different transmission streams — the former probably from the Mahāsāṃghika or Sarvāstivāda line, the latter from the Dharmaguptaka line. The independent crystallization of 平等業 in both translation chambers at two different sites, from two different source texts, through two different translators standing in two different sectarian transmissions, argues forcefully against the "translation-error" reading: a choice kept in common across such differences must be read as a **cross-sectarian shared understanding of the functional face of *sīla***.

The structural reorganization in T0125 is the second-layer tension. As §II Group 2 noted, the *Ekottarikāgama juan* 12 assigns the four immeasurable minds (loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity) to the "impartiality" (virtue) base; in the standard Pāli structure the four immeasurables belong to merit-of-cultivation as *brahmavihāra-bhāvanā* (the cultivation of the divine abodes). This reassignment is often read in Chinese translation history as a "mistaken Chinese pairing" — as if the translator were unclear on the boundary between *bhāvanā* and *sīla*. That reading overlooks a structural fact: if the essence of *sīla* is "equal conduct" (*samacariyā*), then the cultivation of the four immeasurable minds — "an immeasurable extending of impartiality toward all beings" — sits at the functional core of "equal conduct." The assignment of the four immeasurables to the virtue base in T0125 is not a translation error but a structural extension of inner content — when *sīla* is functionally defined as "equal conduct," the four immeasurables fall within that functional category. Similarly, the delimitation of reflection-as-merit through the seven factors of awakening (*bojjhaṅga*) rather than through the later-Theravāda-privileged *samatha-vipassanā* pairing: the seven factors of awakening belong, no less than *samatha-vipassanā*, to the thirty-seven aids to awakening and point in the same direction, though within a different structural frame. The reorganization in T0125 reflects an independent sectarian plan of the functional division of the three bases, not a translator's infidelity to a Pāli original.

The lacuna in T0026 *Madhyamāgama* is a structural lacuna rather than an indexing oversight (as the honest-absence declaration of §II already stated). The *Madhyamāgama* is the Chinese transmission of the *Majjhima Nikāya*, whose primary register is long-form narrative-argumentation rather than catalogue-of-*saṅgīti* structure — the crystallization of the three bases of meritorious activity into a formula requires the catalogue-of-*saṅgīti* sūtra-form (the *Dīrgha Saṅgīti Sūtra* and *Aṅguttara* Book 8 Chapter 4 Sūtra 6) to surface. The *Madhyamāgama* preserves the narrative sūtras just mentioned — the *Pāyāsi Sutta*, *Soṇa Koṭikaṇṇa Sutta*, and others — but within them the relation among the three bases is handled in narrative scenes rather than gathered into a three-item formula. This is not a canonical defect but a divergence in sectarian *saṅgīti* style — the compilers of the *Majjhima* collection chose the narrative unfolding rather than the catalogue unfolding of the three-base relation. The lacuna itself is therefore a positive structural observation about the distribution of the three-base formula: the crystallization of this doctrine depends on the catalogue-of-*saṅgīti* sūtra-form, and that sūtra-form differentiates across sectarian and redactional paths, with the formula consequently distributing unevenly.

### 3.3 Why Giving in First Place: Four Structural Reasons

The sequence-fixing in AN 8.36 (§III.1) and the cross-echo of the Chinese renderings (§III.2) together establish the cross-transmission structural fact of the three bases of meritorious activity; this section unfolds the four structural reasons for giving's first place, answering the main thesis raised in §I — giving as entrance, not as the floor of a ladder. The four reasons address four dimensions: **observability**, **accessibility**, **motivational two-directionality**, and **pedagogical order**.

**Reason 1: Outwardly observable**

Giving has an external behavioral trace — an object changes hands, a recipient receives; this trace is an event observable by a third party. Virtue is self-restraint, whose core lies in *not doing* rather than in doing; though there are behavioral faces — not killing, not stealing — the full shape of virtue does not manifest externally. Cultivation is even more inward — the attuning of the mental continuum is without external manifestation at all. The three bases line up in descending observability: giving (fully observable) → virtue (partially observable) → cultivation (unobservable). This gradient makes giving the **sole base of the three that can serve as a pedagogical entrance for beginners** — for those just encountering the Dharma, the practice of giving can be seen, learned, and given feedback on; the practice of virtue and cultivation, by contrast, requires self-observation and full interiorization respectively. Giving stands first not because it is shallowest but because it is the most teachable, the most imitable, the most transmissible through a teacher-student relation. This has nothing to do with the terminus of the path; it concerns only the design of the entrance.

**Reason 2: Accessible to householders**

The practice of giving has no prerequisite: anyone at any time can give. A bowl of rice, a piece of clothing, a kind word are all giving; the poor give what the poor can give; the wealthy give what the wealthy can give. The full practice of virtue has a prerequisite — the undertaking of the Five Precepts follows the Three Refuges; the Eight-Precept observance is a one-day-and-night temporary undertaking of monastic deportment. The full practice of cultivation has even more substantial prerequisites — a stable base in virtue, guidance from a wise teacher, and a quiet environment, three conditions not usually available at any moment in ordinary household life. The three bases line up in ascending thresholds: giving (no prerequisite) → virtue (with prerequisite) → cultivation (with heavier prerequisite). This threshold-gradient makes giving the **standard starting step for householders** — the Buddha's teaching to Anāthapiṇḍika, Visākhā, and the other great householder-donors always opens with giving, not because they cannot take on the precepts or cultivate concentration, but because giving presupposes nothing.

**Reason 3: The two-directional flow of *cetanā***

The motivational structure of giving contains a double seed. **First**, "non-differentiating giving" — when the giver, in the act of giving, lets go of the distinctions between "me / mine," that undifferentiating intention is itself the seed of the functional core of virtue (equal conduct, *samacariyā*). Not deliberately calculating who is worthy of gift, not calculating the return on the fruit of the gift, is a motivational tendency that, brought to maturity, forms the foundation of the virtue of "equal treatment of all beings." **Second**, "purification of motive" — the process of giving is at once the observation and re-tuning of motive itself, filtered gradually from "gift for fruit" through "gift without stinginess," "gift without expectation of return," and "gift as the ornament of the mind" (*cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra*, as P04 §III.1 has already established). That purification is the seed of the motive-refining face of merit-of-cultivation.

Giving, as the entrance base at the motivational layer, has *cetanā* flowing in two directions into virtue and cultivation — outward into the ground of virtue as equal conduct, inward into the ground of cultivation as motive-refinement. This two-directional flow makes giving's first place not a temporal "do this before you can do that" but a structural **motivational source**: virtue and cultivation alike trace back to the *cetanā* structure cultivated in giving. Giving stands first because it is the shared source of the three bases' motivational layer, not because it is the earliest meritorious project among the three.

**Reason 4: Graduated discourse**

The Buddha's standard order of discourse to householders is fixed in the Vinaya as *anupubbikathā* (graduated discourse) — the *Cullavagga* Chapter 6 records the Buddha's six-step opening address to Anāthapiṇḍika: talk on giving (*dānakathā*) → talk on virtue (*sīlakathā*) → talk on heavens (*saggakathā*) → disadvantage of sensual pleasures → benefit of renunciation → the Four Noble Truths[^8]. The talk on giving always stands first. This order is not ad hoc but a fixed pedagogical sequence recorded in the Vinaya. P05 *Velāma Sutta* has already established *anupubbikathā* as the pedagogical concretization of the three bases of meritorious activity; this paper does not re-unfold that argument, noting only that giving's first place in the graduated discourse and giving's first place in the three bases belong to the same structural root: giving's observability and accessibility make it the reasonable entrance for discourse — the listener first establishes confidence and merit through giving, then is led step by step toward the deeper teachings of virtue, meditation, and liberation.

Taking the four reasons together, the structural case for giving's first place is complete. None of the four rests on a value-judgment that "giving is shallowest" or "giving is highest"; all rest on the **entrance-function of giving** — observable, accessible, motivational source, pedagogical order — four functions constituting the structural ground of giving as the entrance to the three-base container. Giving in first place is not the floor of a ladder but the step of entry into the three-base container.

### 3.4 Revisiting Part II's Four Axes: Integration Under the Three-Base Container

P04–P07's four axes are an **internal** structural analysis of giving; this section revisits them with the three-base container as the frame, showing that all four axes are the internal unfolding of *dānamaya* as the first base, and that the three-base container is their structural home.

**P04 — motivation and quality axes**: The eightfold motivation-lineage of AN 8.31 (from external coercion to internal cultivation, terminating in *cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra*) and the sevenfold complete causation of T0125 *juan* 37 (timeliness, cleanness, own hand, vow, liberation, search for cessation, good field, dedication) together form the paired motivation-and-quality axis of giving. This axis treats the question "under what psychological configuration does a gift-event become a *dānamaya puñña-kiriya-vatthu*?" — that is, the internal psychological structure of the first base. Both axes remain in the first base and do not extend to *sīlamaya* or *bhāvanāmaya*.

**P05 — the rank axis of the giver**: The ladder of AN 9.20 *Velāma Sutta* (Velāma's eighty-four-thousandfold giving → the Five Precepts → a fingersnap of loving-kindness → a moment of the perception of impermanence) exhibits, within the three-base container, a **cross-base ascent** — the floor of the ladder is *dānamaya*, the middle section is *sīlamaya* (the Five Precepts), and the apex enters *bhāvanāmaya* (loving-kindness as *mettā* and the perception of impermanence as *anicca-saññā*). The P05 ladder is the narrative concretization of the three-base container: the giver ascends toward *sīlamaya* and *bhāvanāmaya* along the pedagogical path of *anupubbikathā*.

**P06 — the temporal axis of fruit**: The four *sandiṭṭhika* plus one *samparāyika* epistemic partition of AN 5.34 *Sīhasenāpatisutta* operates inside *dānamaya* — the four present-life visible fruits form the immediately verifiable structure of giving, the future-life fruit is what is committed to faith. Virtue and cultivation each have their own epistemic structures (the fruit of virtue arising in the signs of concentration, the fruit of cultivation arising in *jhāna* or in insight-knowledge), which are outside the scope of P06.

**P07 — the recipient-category axis**: The category-switch between the fourteen personal gifts and the seven sangha-bound gifts of MN 142 *Dakkhiṇāvibhaṅgasutta* operates inside *dānamaya* — this axis is proper to giving alone. Virtue has no "to whom does one keep precepts" (virtue is self-kept); cultivation has no "whom does one cultivate" (cultivation is self-tuning); the recipient-category axis maps precisely onto Reason 1 of §III.3 (outwardly observable): giving has an observable external structure, and therefore admits the category-differentiation of recipients; virtue and cultivation have no such demand.

Taking the four axes together, P04–P07 are the unfolding along four internal axes of *dānamaya* as the first base; the three-base container is their structural home. The earlier stretch of Part II — the internal analysis of giving — closes here into the first-base operational argument of the three-base container, and opens outward into Part III: from "to what category of recipient is gift given" to "how the recipient is institutionalized as a sustainable field of merit" — that is, from the recipient axis of *dānamaya* to the vinaya-side institutional stabilization of *cātuddisa saṅgha*, the property of the sangha of the four quarters.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Structural Resonance: The Cultivation of Virtue and Behavioral Activation

The "giving in first place" structure of the three bases of meritorious activity has two parallels in cross-disciplinary view — the "virtue acquired by habit" thesis of classical virtue ethics and the "behavioral activation" thesis of contemporary habit science — resonating with the entrance-structure of the three-base container in form, though not in sense.

Aristotle, in Book II Chapter 1 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, advances the thesis that virtue (*aretē*) is "neither by nature nor contrary to nature, but acquired by habit": virtue is not inherent in the substance of our nature nor in contradiction to it, and must become a stable character through the internalization of repeated concrete actions. This thesis is structurally isomorphic with the entrance-structure of the three bases — the internalization of a virtue must begin with an accessible, concrete action, and that beginning cannot be a deep contemplation but an operation available in the present moment. Aristotle articulates this as "doing just deeds precedes becoming a just person," which resonates with giving's function as the entrance to the three bases: an outwardly observable concrete action precedes the internalized stable character.

Contemporary behavioral-habit science takes as its core principle "the minimum viable behavior activates the maximum habit-formation" — situational cue, micro-behavior, immediate positive feedback: three loops driving persistence. These three loops are isomorphic with the "observable, accessible, *cetanā* two-directional flow" structure of giving; B. J. Fogg's Tiny Habits framework can serve as a contemporary parallel of this structural isomorphism.

But the resonance is strictly **structural isomorphism**, not **doctrinal reduction** — the *cetanā*-seed theory and the liberation-oriented terminus of the three bases of meritorious activity cannot be reduced to behavioral-layer or virtue-formation contemporary frames; the three-base container cannot be equivalently reconstructed within these theories. This chapter takes only the structural parallel as mutual illustration.

## V. Misreading Corrections

This section addresses four common misreadings: three doctrinal misreadings marked ❌ and one cross-disciplinary analogy risk marked ⚠️.

**❌ Misreading 1: "Giving in first place is first-rung merit; virtue and cultivation are the advanced stages."**

This reading takes the three-base sequence as a linear ladder from shallow to deep, supposing that the practitioner should begin with giving, cross over gradually to virtue and cultivation, and finish with meditative absorption as the completed shape. It is especially widespread in the English-speaking Buddhist context — influenced by the "meditation as the core of the path" culture of ten-day *vipassanā* retreats, readers easily read giving as introductory, virtue as transitional, and cultivation as terminal.

This reading fails at three layers. **First**, the twin-engine observation of §III.1 has already refuted it frontally: the panorama of fruits across the six sense-sphere heavens is driven by the twin axes of giving and virtue, while merit-of-cultivation persists at "not reaching" in this terrain; if the three bases were a linear ladder, the sense-sphere fruits should exhibit a "giving low, virtue middle, cultivation high" ranking, but in fact giving and virtue co-lead in this layer and cultivation exists only at "not reaching." **Second**, the four reasons of §III.3 all build giving's entrance-function — observable, accessible, motivational source, pedagogical order — none of which rests on giving's "introductory character" but on its **structural entrance-function**. **Third**, the three bases are a classificatory axis of three material types (the sense of the *-maya* construction), not a ranked ladder of merits; the ascent of virtue and cultivation is not "surpassing giving" but an extension upon the *cetanā* structure cultivated in giving. Each of the three bases has its own terrain — giving and virtue in the sense-sphere realm, cultivation in the form realm and above — a functional division of labor, not a shallow-to-deep ranking. Giving stands first not because it is shallow but because it is the entrance to the three-base container.

**❌ Misreading 2: "Impartial-action is a mistranslation of virtue-action."**

This reading takes the rarity of 平等業 in traditional Chinese Buddhist translation as grounds for judging the shared rendering of both Chinese transmissions as the translators' unfamiliarity with the core sense of *sīla*, and holds that 戒, 淨戒, or 尸羅 would have been the correct rendering. The reading has a historical cause in Chinese Buddhist translation studies — later Sinitic Buddhism fixes 戒 as the standard translation of *sīla*, and when readers look back from the later standard to the earlier Eastern Jin / Latter Qin renderings, they easily read the rare choice as the translators' immaturity.

This reading overlooks the cross-sectarian crystallization fact of §III.2. The source texts of the Gautama Saṅghadeva chamber and the Buddhayaśas / Zhu Fonian chamber come from different sectarian transmissions (the former probably from the Mahāsāṃghika or Sarvāstivāda line, the latter from the Dharmaguptaka line); that the two chambers crystallize independently yet hold 平等業 in common is not the coincidence of individual translators' choices but a structural reflection of a cross-sectarian common understanding of the functional face of *sīla*. 平等業 corresponds to the positive-function definitions *samacariyā* (equal conduct) or *samatā* (equality/evenness), forming a **structural complement** to — not a replacement of, or a mistranslation from — the traditional reading of *sīla* through the negative face of "prohibition" and "restraint." The inner kernel of the Five Precepts is the equal structure of "non-differential non-harming," not an isolated list of taboos; the rendering 平等業 for *sīla* captures exactly this positive function. T0125's assignment of the four immeasurables to the impartiality base and its delimitation of reflection-as-merit through the seven factors of awakening are likewise independent sectarian plans of the functional division of the three bases, not translator's categorization errors. Reading the shared rendering of the two Chinese transmissions as "mistranslation" is pushing the later standard translation backward onto the judgment of the earlier translators — a historiographically untenable move.

**❌ Misreading 3: "The absence in T0026 *Madhyamāgama* means a defect in the canon."**

This reading sees the full-canon search return no correspondence in T0026 and judges it an indexing defect or a transmissional damage, supposing that if the three bases of meritorious activity were a core teaching of early Buddhism, all four Āgamas should carry it in full. The reading is common in modern scholarship's handling of canon-consistency — an implicit assumption that "a core teaching should be present in full in every collection" leads to the inference "absent, therefore damaged."

This reading overlooks the divergence-fact of sectarian *saṅgīti* style. The crystallization of the three bases of meritorious activity as a formula depends on the catalogue-of-*saṅgīti* sūtra-form — the *Dīrgha Saṅgīti Sūtra* and *Aṅguttara* Book 8 Chapter 4 Sūtra 6 both house this teaching in a "number + sub-items" catalogue structure. The *Majjhima* collection favors the long-form narrative-argumentative style and does not adopt this catalogue structure; the compilers of the *Majjhima* chose to unfold giving themes through narrative (the *Pāyāsi Sutta* MĀ 71, *Soṇa Koṭikaṇṇa Sutta* MĀ 123, *Raṭṭhapāla Sutta* MĀ 132, *Uposatha Sutta*, etc.), and the relation among the three bases surfaces naturally in narrative without being gathered by a three-item formula. The lacuna reflects the **structural divergence of sectarian *saṅgīti* style**, not a canonical defect. The lacuna itself is a structural observation about the distribution of the three-base formula: the crystallization of this teaching depends on the specific catalogue-of-*saṅgīti* sūtra-form, which differentiates across sectarian and redactional paths, with the formula consequently distributing unevenly — present in the *Dīrgha* and *Aṅguttara*, absent in the *Madhyama* / *Majjhima*.

**⚠️ Cross-disciplinary analogy risk: "Giving is the Buddhist version of behavioral activation."**

This warning addresses a possible misreading of the cross-disciplinary resonance of §IV. §IV has already warned in its limit-declaration that the resonance is "structural isomorphism, not doctrinal reduction"; the warning is strengthened here, lest readers fit the Aristotelian virtue-formation or contemporary behavioral-habit-science frames back onto the doctrinal structure of the three bases, producing a category-displacement.

The *cetanā*-seed theory of the three bases of meritorious activity (§III.3 Reason 3: the two-directional flow of the giving-motivation into virtue and cultivation) and its liberation-oriented terminus are a structure proper to early Buddhism; neither behavioral-habit science nor Aristotelian virtue ethics has this seed-theory structure — the former lacks the mechanism-theory of "the giving-motivation flowing as branches into virtue and cultivation," the latter lacks the terminal-theory of "the Four Noble Truths leading to liberation." Aristotelian virtue-formation is an ethical terminus of "becoming a virtuous person within the *polis*"; contemporary behavioral-activation science is a psychological terminus of "establishing a stable behavior pattern at the individual level"; both are of a different category from the three-base terminus of "exit from the sense-sphere realm and ascent toward liberation." The cross-disciplinary analogy takes only the shared premise at the entrance face — "the entrance must be observable, accessible, and self-reinforcing" as a general principle of path-design — and cannot be pushed to doctrinal equivalence. The resemblance between giving's entrance-function in the three bases and contemporary behavioral-habit science belongs to the structural consensus of path-design, not to the reading of the three bases' full doctrinal structure as a Buddhist variant of behavioral formation. This boundary must be kept clear in the reader's mind, else the deep doctrinal frame collapses into an introductory self-cultivation strategy, losing the liberation-oriented core of the three bases.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essentials**

The three bases of meritorious activity (*puñña-kiriya-vatthu*) form the closing container of Part II. Giving, virtue, and cultivation, through the sense of the *-maya* construction ("made of"), constitute a stable cross-transmission structure of early Buddhism — a classificatory axis of three material types, not a ranked ladder of merits. Giving in first place is not the floor of a value-ladder but the entrance to the three-base container; four structural reasons together constitute its entrance-function: outwardly observable, accessible to householders, *cetanā* flowing in two directions, and the standing order of graduated discourse (*anupubbikathā*). The twin-engine observation of the sense-sphere realm in AN 8.36 (the entire panorama of fruits across the six sense-sphere heavens is co-driven by giving and virtue, while cultivation persists at "not reaching" in this terrain) and the cross-sectarian translation tension around 平等業 (the shared functional definition that the *Ekottarikāgama* and the *Dīrghāgama Saṅgīti Sūtra* crystallize independently) form the twin anchors of this structure. The four axes of Part II's preceding four papers (motivation and quality, the rank of the giver, the temporal axis of fruit, the category of the recipient) are all the internal unfolding of *dānamaya* as the first base; the three-base container is their structural home.

**Practice guidance**

This structure points contemporary practice toward two common-judgment corrections. **First**, the correction of the high-low intuition that "meditation is superior to giving" — the three bases are a material classification, not a ranked order. In the path, giving is the entrance as motivational source, not a beginner's exercise; a householder's path, absent meditative absorption, is not thereby low-rank but on the proper terrain of the twin-engine operation of the three bases. The "strong" intensity of cultivation indeed awaits the form realm and above, but at the sense-sphere layer of the path, giving and virtue already supply the full driving power — this is not a compromise at the lower rung but the proper trajectory answering the terrain of the sense-sphere realm. **Second**, the correction of the ladder reading of "giving as first-rung merit" — in the moment of giving itself, the *cetanā*-shift of "letting go of stinginess," "giving without expectation of return," and "giving as the ornament of the mind" (*cittālaṅkāra-cittaparikkhāra*) is simultaneously the seed-planting of the equal-conduct (*samacariyā*) seed of virtue and the motive-refinement seed of cultivation. The three bases operate at once rather than step by step: the two-directional flow of *cetanā* in giving makes a single qualified act of giving already the co-cultivation of three seeds at once. This is the basic discipline of the path's structure: cultivate the three bases in the moment of giving, rather than step on giving in the sequence of the three bases.

**Open questions · Handoff to P09**

Giving being the entrance to the three bases, how does the institutionalized field of merit turn this entrance into a sustainable communal undertaking? An individual-level giving-event can be one-off, accidental; if the entrance-function of the three-base container is to operate long-term within the sangha institution, it must pass through the institutional structure of the field of merit — how does the legal-doctrinal-practical structure of *cātuddisa saṅgha* (the sangha of the four quarters) both inherit the entrance-function of giving and extend into the vinaya-side institution of the sangha? How do the *cetanā* of the giver and the stability of the field together form a sustainable giving-practice? This is the starting point of Part III's ninth paper, *The Sangha as a Field of Merit*; this paper hands off here.

The *Madhyamāgama* preserves giving-themed narrative sūtras (the *Pāyāsi Sutta* MĀ 71, *Soṇa Koṭikaṇṇa Sutta* MĀ 123, *Raṭṭhapāla Sutta* MĀ 132, *Uposatha Sutta*, etc.) but does not close them with the three-item formula.

[^7]: *Itivuttaka* 3.19 (It 60) *Puññakiriyavatthu Sutta*, PTS It 51. Another version of the three bases of meritorious activity held in the Pāli canon, weighting *mettā* (loving-kindness) as the core content of merit-of-cultivation. The full version is not held in the current working corpus and is not directly cited by this paper; the related structural connection (the junction of the three bases with the cultivation of loving-kindness) is left for future treatment.

[^8]: *Cullavagga* Chapter 6 *Senāsanakkhandhaka* records the six-step opening address of the Buddha to Anāthapiṇḍika: *dānakathā* (talk on giving) → *sīlakathā* (talk on virtue) → *saggakathā* (talk on heavens) → *kāmānaṃ ādīnava* (disadvantage of sensual pleasures) → *nekkhamme ānisaṃsa* (benefit of renunciation) → the Four Noble Truths. This is the crystallized record of *anupubbikathā* (graduated discourse) in the Vinaya. P05 *Velāma Sutta* §III has already laid out the full analysis of this axis; this paper merely cites it.
