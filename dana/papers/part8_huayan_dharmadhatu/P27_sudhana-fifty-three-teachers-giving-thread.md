# Sudhana's Fifty-Three Teachers and the Giving Thread

**Dāna as Walk-Through in the Entry into the Dharma-Realm**

*中文主題：善財五十三參是布施作為「入法界之走過」的多音複調展演——五十三位善知識構成並奏的聲部而非高下的階級；文殊「回返、過一百一十由旬按頂」所構成的交叉鎖鏈結構，與「四勿」規則所揭示的雙向布施機制，是反階梯讀法的文本內證。*

*English: Sudhana's pilgrimage of fifty-three teachers is not a graded ascent but the polyphonic performance of dāna as a "walk-through" of the entry into the dharma-realm — the fifty-three spiritual friends are voices sounding together, not ranks set high and low. The chiastic structure formed by Mañjuśrī's return — his reaching across one hundred and ten yojanas to lay a hand on Sudhana's crown — together with the bidirectional giving-mechanism disclosed by the "four-prohibitions" rule, is the text's own internal evidence against the staircase reading.*

## Abstract

Sudhana's pilgrimage of fifty-three teachers is commonly understood by modern readers as a cultivational staircase rising from low to high. This paper argues that this is a fundamental misreading of the structure of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" chapter, and proposes instead: the fifty-three visits are the polyphonic performance of dāna as a "walk-through" of the entry into the dharma-realm, the fifty-three spiritual friends being voices that sound together rather than ranks set high and low. Working from the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka-sūtra*, the paper observes that the three hermeneutic-test cases — the fire-ascetic Brahmin, King Anala, and Vasumitrā — are placed at structurally equidistant points along the journey with steadily rising ethical pressure, and that this deliberate disposition is itself the text's internal evidence against the staircase reading. It then analyzes the chiastic structure formed by Mañjuśrī's "return, reaching across one hundred and ten yojanas to touch the crown," and the bidirectional giving-mechanism disclosed by the "four-prohibitions" rule. By way of the principal and supporting wings of the Sino-Indic commentarial tradition — Fazang's "rolling-up and unrolling at ease," Chengguan's "mutual inclusion of the two sages," and Li Tongxuan's "without departing from a single thought" — the paper establishes that this polyphonic reading is a view native to the tradition rather than a retrospective projection of modern scholarship, and, in dialogue with the three Western readings of Osto, Granoff, and Gómez, restores the doctrinal dimension of "giving as a recursively encompassing walk-through." This is the fourth paper of the Samantabhadra arena, continuing the discussion of entry-architecture running through the papers of Part VIII, the Huayan Dharmadhātu.

## I. Introduction: Is Sudhana's Path a Staircase, or a Polyphony?

In the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" chapter of the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra*, Mañjuśrī, to the east of the city of Prosperity (福城), first stirs the youth *Sudhana* (善財) to generate the mind of awakening — yet he does not lay out for him the whole ocean of omniscience. He instructs him, rather, to journey south and to visit teachers widely. Fazang (643–712), in his *Huayan jing tanxuan ji*, poses the question of why the arrangement is set this way, and answers it with eight intentions, the eighth of which reads:

> Why does Mañjuśrī only instruct [Sudhana] to seek out spiritual friends, and not expound the teaching for him directly? In answer there are eight intentions ... the first, to show that spiritual friends are hard to encounter ... the second, to show that this is not a domain within the reach of ordinary feeling ... the eighth, to show that all fifty-three visits are themselves dharma-gates.[^1]

The single phrase "all fifty-three visits are themselves dharma-gates" is the starting point of this paper's argument. It signals that the fifty-three spiritual friends (*kalyāṇamitra*) Sudhana encounters are neither something Mañjuśrī alone could expound on their behalf, nor a sequence of cultivational ranks rising in steady order; they are the unfurling of the dharma-gates themselves — each visit a dharma-gate, the fifty-three visits a side-by-side performance of fifty-three dharma-gates. Sudhana states his purpose plainly enough:

> Good man, I have already generated the mind set upon unsurpassed, perfect awakening, yet I do not know: how is a bodhisattva to train in the bodhisattva's practice? How is one to cultivate the bodhisattva's path? I have heard that you, sage, are skilled at guiding and instructing; I pray you, expound it for me.[^2]

The mind of awakening has been generated; what is wanting is the concrete unfolding of "how to train, how to cultivate." Mañjuśrī's response is not a single synoptic treatise but a road.

Faced with the number "fifty-three," however, a modern reader — and a Western scholarly reader in particular — has a nearly instinctive way of reading it: as a cultivational staircase. Fifty-three steps, low to high, Sudhana climbing them rung by rung, advancing one grade with each teacher met, until at Samantabhadra he reaches the summit. This "linear hierarchical" supposition looks natural, but it is hard to sustain in the face of several structural facts in the text. This paper holds that the staircase reading is a fundamental misreading of the chapter's structure.

The most direct counter-evidence comes from the placement, within the itinerary, of three "unnameable" spiritual friends. The tenth teacher, the fire-ascetic Brahmin, manifests through scorching the body with the five fires; the eighteenth, King Anala (無厭足王, "Insatiable"; "Nectar-Fire King" in the forty-fascicle recension), manifests through the duress of punishment; the twenty-sixth, *Vasumitrā* (婆須蜜多), manifests through deliverance by way of contact. The teachings of these three all exceed ordinary feeling, and all require interpretation if they are not to be misread; this paper therefore calls them three "hermeneutic-test cases." It is worth noting that the three are not clustered in a single stretch of the journey but seated, respectively, at roughly one-fifth, one-third, and one-half of its full length — an equidistant placement — with their ethical pressure rising step by step: from suffering inflicted on oneself, to suffering inflicted on others, to the transgression of an ethical taboo. Were the fifty-three visits truly a quality-staircase rising low to high, these three cases could not have been disposed so deliberately. Their equidistant placement is itself textual evidence that what we have is polyphony, not a staircase.

This paper takes up where its predecessor left off — the predecessor having shown, in its treatment of "the dharma-offering supreme among all offerings," that the seven kinds of dharma-offering are seven faces of one contemplative practice, gathered to a convergence-point by the single item "non-departure from *bodhicitta*" (see DĀNA P26). The present paper asks the further question: if offering can be encompassed in this way, then how is the longer journey of Sudhana's fifty-three visits to be structurally understood?

## II. The Three Translation-Recensions and the Tang Stabilization of "Fifty-Three"

The "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" chapter has three principal recensions in the Chinese translation tradition, their formation spanning three and a half centuries across three transmission moments. The earliest is the sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0278), rendered by *Buddhabhadra* in 418–420, where the chapter occupies fascicles forty-four through sixty; next is the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0279), rendered by *Śikṣānanda* in 695–699, where the chapter occupies fascicles sixty through eighty; latest is the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0293), rendered by *Prajñā* in 795–798, the whole of which is a variant recension of this one chapter, with a segment on Samantabhadra's vast vows of practice appended at fascicle forty.[^3]

With the three recensions set side by side, one easily overlooked fact must first be clarified: the count "fifty-three" is not native to the Sanskrit "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" (the *Gaṇḍavyūha-sūtra*) but the outcome of an ordering and stabilization performed by the Chinese commentarial tradition. In the Sanskrit manuscripts, the enumeration of the spiritual friends fluctuates between fifty-two and fifty-three; the pivot is the "return" of Mañjuśrī at the journey's end — Sudhana, having completed his visit to Maitreya, contemplates Mañjuśrī afresh, and Mañjuśrī reaches out his right hand from afar and touches his crown. Grammatically, this segment can be read either as a single independent "visit" or as a structural sealing gesture. If counted as an independent visit, the total is fifty-three; if regarded as a sealing gesture, fifty-two. The number "fifty-three" was stabilized in the Chinese tradition through the ordering performed by the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* and by Chengguan's (738–839) *Huayan jing shu*.[^4]

It must be said plainly here: the round number "fifty-three" cannot be assigned to the Sanskrit original. But it must equally be pointed out that this paper's argument does not hang on the precision of that number — whether fifty-two or fifty-three, the structure is in either case polyphonic; and the very reason Mañjuśrī's return causes the count to fluctuate is precisely that it is not "the next rung up" but a structural device that bends the journey back upon itself. The fluctuation of the count is itself collateral evidence against the staircase reading: a staircase produces no ambiguity when one counts its steps; a loop does.

This paper takes the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0279) as its primary base text, for two reasons. First, with respect to the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" chapter itself, the eighty-fascicle recension is the most stable and complete Chinese rendering extant, its sequence of spiritual friends clear, its Mañjuśrī-return segment fully preserved. Second, where the visit to Vasumitrā is concerned, the corresponding passage in fascicle fifteen of the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* carries an interpolated stretch of conservative, gender-disparaging prose ("because she is a woman she cannot swiftly attain," and the like) not found in the eighty-fascicle recension; to keep this late, translation-bureau-specific framing from interfering with the doctrinal reading of that visit, this paper uses the eighty-fascicle recension throughout the Vasumitrā segment.[^5] As for the Samantabhadra-vows segment of fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle recension, which belongs to the augmentation stratum of Prajñā's Tang-era bureau, a dedicated paper has treated it already (see DĀNA P25); the present paper cross-links it only where necessary. The sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* is used chiefly for cross-recensional collation: three recensions of common origin with divergent renderings allow their differences to throw the stable structural core of the chapter into relief — the sequence of the fifty-three spiritual friends and the sealing of Mañjuśrī's return are consistent across all three, with only the manner of counting and the presence or absence of the vows-stratum differing. The constancy of the structure and the fluctuation of the incidentals coexist, and this observation itself supports the judgment that structure precedes count.

## III. The Chiastic Structure and the Nine-Voice Polyphony of Modalities

The overall trajectory of the fifty-three visits is not a straight line but a chiasmus. Its four structural anchor-points are: the first visit, Mañjuśrī's introduction; the fifty-one mid-journey spiritual friends, the polyphony proper; Mañjuśrī's return after Maitreya's tower at the fifty-second; and Samantabhadra's summary-and-seal of the vows of practice at the fifty-third. Of these, the one most charged with structural significance — and the one that most decisively excludes the staircase reading — is Mañjuśrī's "return." Fascicle eighty of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* records:

> Then Sudhana, the youth, following the instruction of the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Maitreya, journeyed onward by degrees; having passed through more than one hundred and ten cities, he arrived at the city of Sumanā in the land of Samantamukha, and stood at its gate, contemplating Mañjuśrī, observing in accord, turning about in his searching, longing to behold him. At that moment Mañjuśrī, from afar, extended his right hand across one hundred and ten yojanas and laid it upon Sudhana's crown, saying: "Well done, well done, good man! If one departs from the faculty of faith, the mind grows feeble, anxious and full of regret, its meritorious practice incomplete, falling away from diligence ... unable skillfully to set the vows of practice in motion ..." Then Mañjuśrī, proclaiming this dharma — instructing, benefiting, and gladdening him — caused Sudhana the youth to accomplish incalculable dharma-gates ... and caused him to enter the arena of Samantabhadra's practice.[^6]

Mañjuśrī has already appeared at the first visit; this "return" is not the entrance of a new spiritual friend but the reappearance of the same one. A single hand reaching across one hundred and ten yojanas to rest on Sudhana's crown — this gesture performs the chiasmus directly at the level of the text: the journey's far end folds back to its origin. Mañjuśrī does not insert a step-and-a-half between the fifty-second and the fifty-third; he gathers the whole road up and hands it over to Samantabhadra. The Sino-Indic commentarial tradition settled this long ago. Both Fazang and Chengguan read this loop in terms of "the mutual inclusion of Mañjuśrī and Samantabhadra, the two sages": Mañjuśrī, presiding over the beginning of faith and wisdom, and Samantabhadra, presiding over the fullness of the vows of practice, are not the two endpoints of a single straight line but the two faces of a "rolling-up and unrolling," a "mutual inclusion" — gather the road in and it is Mañjuśrī, unroll it and it is Samantabhadra. The polyphony of the fifty-three visits has its gathering principle precisely in this "mutual inclusion of the two sages." The figure who opened the path now closes it; the initiator returns to gather the path before handing it to the seal-figure. This structural reading of Mañjuśrī's return-and-crown-touch is a view native to the Sino-Indic commentarial tradition, not a retrospective projection of Western scholarship onto the text.

Within the chiasmus, how do the fifty-one mid-journey spiritual friends operate? The answer lies in an operative rule Mañjuśrī sets down at the introduction. In fascicle four of the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, Mañjuśrī instructs:

> To draw near to and make offerings to spiritual friends is the very first cause and condition for gathering omniscience ... Good man, one who would seek the bodhisattva's path should resolutely seek a true spiritual friend: in seeking spiritual friends, give rise to no weariness; in meeting spiritual friends, give rise to no satiety; toward all the instruction of a spiritual friend, accord with it entirely; toward the skillful means of a spiritual friend, see no fault.[^7]

"Give rise to no weariness, give rise to no satiety, do not go against the instruction, see no fault" — these four negative formulations this paper calls the "four-prohibitions" rule. Its decisive feature is this: the four prohibitions are something Sudhana hands over to each teacher *before* the visit begins. Before he has heard a word of dharma, Sudhana first offers up "no weariness, no satiety, no opposition, no fault-finding." This is a soundless giving — the giving of the suspension of judgment. By this, giving in the fifty-three visits flows in both directions: the teacher gives Sudhana a dharma in one modality, and Sudhana gives the teacher the suspension of judgment. Bidirectional giving is precisely the operative mechanism by which the polyphony can sound at all. For the three spiritual friends who are hermeneutic-test cases — the Brahmin, the king, and Vasumitrā — this mechanism is especially load-bearing: if Sudhana, on seeing their conduct exceed the ordinary, were at once to "see a fault," the doctrinal function of the test would collapse on the spot; only with "see no fault" set first can the five-fires asceticism, the manifestation of punishment, and deliverance by contact be read as dharma-gates rather than as transgressions.

With "see no fault" as its premise, the giving demonstrated by the fifty-three spiritual friends can present an enormous span of modalities. This paper sorts them into nine kinds: (i) the giving of goods and material offering; (ii) the giving of dharma, the display of a dharma-gate; (iii) the giving of body and life, the manifestation of burning the body; (iv) the giving of skillful means; (v) the manifestation of non-action, the demonstration of silence; (vi) the giving of contemplative visualization; (vii) the giving of fearlessness (*abhaya-dāna*); (viii) self-other exchange (*parātma-parivartana*); (ix) the "unnameable" hermeneutic-test. These nine modalities are not nine grades rising from shallow to deep but nine voices — simultaneously present, answering one another, composing a single polyphonic piece. Read as a staircase, the ninth kind, the "unnameable," would have to be ranked at the highest step; yet its three cases in fact fall at the one-fifth, one-third, and one-half marks of the journey. The nine, plainly, are not nine steps but nine voices running through the whole course, sounding and falling silent by turns. The next section takes five "load-bearing" spiritual friends and shows concretely how this polyphony operates in the text.

## IV. Five Sample Voices: How the Polyphony Sounds in the Mid-Pilgrimage

From among the fifty-one mid-journey visits, this paper selects five as concrete illustrations of the span of modalities. The five giving-gates differ markedly; none can be reduced to another, and none can be ranked above another — this is precisely the text's internal proof that what we have is polyphony, not a staircase.

First, the third teacher, the monk Haiyun (海雲), who manifests the giving of "a way of seeing." Haiyun has dwelt by the ocean for twelve years, taking the great sea as his field of contemplation:

> Good man, I have dwelt in this place for twelve years, always taking the great ocean as my field of contemplation — that is, contemplating the great ocean as vast and measureless, contemplating it as profound and hard to fathom, contemplating it as deepening and widening by degrees ... [And I further reflected:] in all the world, is there anything broader than this ocean? Anything more measureless? Anything more profound? Anything more singular?[^8]

What Haiyun gives Sudhana is neither an object nor a set of doctrinal propositions but a way of seeing — the contemplative act itself, by which the ocean before one's eyes is seen as vast, fathomless, deepening, singular. This may be named the giving of contemplative visualization: what is given is the capacity for contemplative regard.

Second, the eighth teacher, the householder Vimuktika (解脫長者), who manifests the giving of "the display of a dharma-gate." Vimuktika describes his own samādhi of buddha-recollection:

> Good man, if I wish to see the Tathāgata Amitābha of the world of Bliss, I see him at will ... yet that Tathāgata does not come here, nor do I go there; I know that all Buddhas and my own mind are alike a dream; I know that all Buddhas are like a reflected image and my own mind like water; I know that the formed appearances of all Buddhas, and my own mind, are alike an illusion; I know that all Buddhas, and my own mind, are alike an echo.[^9]

Vimuktika does not "hand" the Tathāgata Amitābha to Sudhana; he displays, on the spot, how a dharma-gate operates — Buddhas and one's own mind, like dream, like reflection, like illusion, like echo. What is given is a contemplative structure that can be directly beheld.

Third, the twenty-second teacher, the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, who manifests the giving of fearlessness. Avalokiteśvara describes his liberation-gate of the swift practice of great compassion:

> Good man, I cultivate the bodhisattva's liberation-gate of the practice of great compassion ... if sentient beings [suffer] fears of every kind, grief and sorrow and anguish, bondage and confinement, the duress of punishment and execution, the affliction of royal sanction, the harm of enemies ... for all of them I can act as a great refuge, causing them to be freed from these fears ... Good man, with this bodhisattva-gate of the practice of great compassion I teach and transform all sentient beings equally, in unbroken continuity.[^10]

What Avalokiteśvara gives is the freedom from fear itself. Here the object of giving is neither a thing nor a doctrinal sense but the dissolution of beings' state of fear. This is of one lineage with "the giver of fearlessness" in the Universal Gate chapter of the Lotus, though this paper does not unfold the Lotus material and only cross-links it.

Fourth, the twenty-third teacher, the great deity Mahādeva (大天神), who manifests a giving of a particular "seam" character. Mahādeva describes his "cloud-net liberation," and from it draws out an enumeration of the six perfections:

> Good man, I have accomplished the liberation of the cloud-net ... whatever I do, I do for the rescue and protection of all sentient beings ... Good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should cultivate the six perfections: namely the perfection of giving (*dāna-pāramitā*) ... the perfection of moral discipline (*śīla*-pāramitā) ... the perfection of patience ... the perfection of vigor ... the perfection of meditative absorption ... the perfection of wisdom. Good man, I know only this cloud-net liberation.[^11]

The Mahādeva segment deserves a special mark: in all the fifty-three visits, this is the one place where, from the mouth of a spiritual friend, "the perfection of giving" and "the perfection of moral discipline" are explicitly named side by side. Here giving and the precepts are joined. This paper's conclusion will point out that this segment is the seam by which the volume of giving opens onto the volume of the precepts; here it is enough to mark its modality — what Mahādeva gives is the intimation of a larger structure, "the perfections in unbroken succession."

Fifth, the Spring-Harmony Night Goddess, who manifests fearlessness-giving on a cosmic scale. The night goddess describes her "liberation-gate of the light of the dharma that breaks the darkness of all sentient beings":

> I have attained the bodhisattva's liberation-gate of the light of the dharma that breaks the darkness of all sentient beings ... with this dharma-gate, in every portion of the night, everywhere throughout the worlds of the ten directions ... I rescue and protect all sentient beings on perilous roads ... acting as their refuge, acting as their illumination ... and in accordance with the differing karma of body, speech, and mind of each being, I give comfort to all.[^12]

The night goddess and Avalokiteśvara both belong to fearlessness-giving, but their modalities differ again: Avalokiteśvara's release from fear is the swift great compassion that appears as the occasion calls; the night goddess's release from fear is a constant protection reaching across "every portion of the night" and "the worlds of the ten directions." The same fearlessness-giving — one swift, one constant and pervasive — shows that even within a single modality there is no single standard form.

Five voices set side by side: Haiyun gives a contemplative seeing, Vimuktika the structure of a dharma-gate, Avalokiteśvara the release from fear, Mahādeva the intimation of the perfections in succession, the night goddess a constant and pervasive protection. The reader cannot — and need not — rank these five high and low: one cannot say that "giving a contemplative seeing" is lower or higher than "giving the release from fear." They are five voices, not five steps. This is the direct presentation, in the body of the text, of the polyphonic structure.

## V. The Three Hermeneutic-Test Cases, the Sino-Indic Commentary Wings, and Dialogue with Western Scholarship

This section takes up first the most easily misread of the three hermeneutic-test cases — Vasumitrā — then, by way of the principal and supporting wings of the Sino-Indic commentary, establishes its doctrinal reading, and finally enters into dialogue with three Western scholars. One framing must be stated first: the three commentators this paper draws on — Fazang, Chengguan, and Li Tongxuan — are not three stages of a historical development but three anchor-points in one logical space — the principal wing (Fazang, Chengguan) setting the architecture, the supporting wing (Li Tongxuan) supplying the point of entry. The three are set side by side so that the doctrinal space of the chapter may be fully opened, not so that a line of transmission may be ranked in sequence.

Vasumitrā is the twenty-sixth teacher. Fascicle sixty-eight of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* records her self-description:

> Good man, I have attained a bodhisattva's liberation ... if there are sentient beings bound by the intention of desire who come to my place and conceive the utmost reverence toward my person, I expound the dharma for them, and having heard the dharma they part from craving ... some, merely seeing me, part from craving ... some, merely speaking with me, part from craving ... some, merely taking my hand, part from craving ... some, merely ascending to my seat, part from craving ... some, merely beholding me, part from craving ... Good man, I know only this bodhisattva's liberation-gate of the boundary at which craving ends.[^13]

This is the case of highest ethical pressure among the three. The Sino-Indic commentary's handling of it must be understood through that tradition's own cluster of terms — "reverse-cultivation," "stained yet unstained," "in desire cultivating *dhyāna*" — and not replaced with later, externally imposed frames. Its Sino-Indic doctrinal anchor in fact rests on Chengguan alone. Chengguan glosses it thus:

> Ordinary beings are stained by desire; those of the two vehicles see desire as something separable; the bodhisattva attains liberation without severing craving, because wisdom understands its essential emptiness — and so desire itself is the path. Thus "stained yet unstained," and this is why it is called the bodhisattva's boundary at which craving ends.[^14]

Chengguan's reading runs in three tiers: ordinary beings are stained by and attached to desire; those of the two vehicles see desire as a thing to be set aside; the bodhisattva, by contrast, "attains liberation without severing craving" — the pivot being "wisdom understands its essential emptiness": with wisdom one penetrates to the intrinsic emptiness of desire, and so desire is itself the path. The phrase "stained yet unstained" is precisely the doctrinal sense of Vasumitrā's name, "the boundary at which craving ends." One documentary fact must be honestly marked here: Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* (T1733) and Li Tongxuan's *Xin Huayan jing lun* (T1739) contain, in the extant canon, no direct doctrinal commentary on this teacher; the doctrinal anchor of the Sino-Indic pre-modern tradition for this visit is Chengguan alone. This paper neither patches in a reading from Fazang or Li Tongxuan, nor imports a later ethical-critical frame; modern Western readings of Vasumitrā fall outside this paper's scope. To judge the Vasumitrā visit, by a modern ethical standard, as a "problem passage" is to set a dharma-gate that the tradition reads unambiguously as "an expedient manifestation, wisdom penetrating to emptiness" upon a tribunal to which it does not belong — and it is precisely this judgment that the rule "see no fault" was framed to suspend.

The principal wing of the Sino-Indic commentary falls on Fazang's and Chengguan's reading of the whole chapter's structure. Fazang, in the *Tanxuan ji*, glosses the relation of Mañjuśrī and Samantabhadra by "rolling-up and unrolling at ease":

> Mañjuśrī's interfusion of the three matters is precisely Samantabhadra's interpenetration of the three matters ... rolling-up and unrolling at ease, mutually fused without obstruction.[^15]

Chengguan, in the *Huayan jing shu*, takes this up and makes it plainer still:

> Mañjuśrī's interfusion of the three matters, running subtly throughout, is precisely Samantabhadra's interpenetration of the three matters, layered over and over ... the two sages mutually including one another — only this is the contemplation of the dharma-realm.[^16]

Fazang says "rolling-up and unrolling at ease," Chengguan says "the two sages mutually including one another" — and what both say is precisely the doctrinal articulation, in Sino-Indic terms, of the chiastic structure set out in section III of this paper. Mañjuśrī (the beginning of faith and wisdom) and Samantabhadra (the fullness of the vows of practice) are not the two ends of a single straight line but the two faces of a "rolling-up" and a "mutual inclusion": gather the road in and it is Mañjuśrī, unroll it and it is Samantabhadra. The gathering principle of the polyphony of the fifty-three visits lies in just this "mutual inclusion of the two sages." The supporting wing takes Li Tongxuan's *Xin Huayan jing lun* (T1739 — to be kept firmly distinct from Zongmi's *Yuanjue jing lüeshu*, X0260, a different work altogether, not even by the same author) and supplies one further angle, that of the "point of entry":

> Without departing from a single thought one yet passes through a single lifetime; without departing from a single place one yet reaches everywhere in the ten directions ... each one of them complete in itself possesses the bodhisattva's practice.[^17]

Li Tongxuan's reading folds the chiasmus into "a single thought," "a single place": Sudhana lives out a lifetime and reaches everywhere in the ten directions without ever departing from the present single thought, the present single place. This is an immediate reading turned toward the householder and toward the present — the fifty-three visits are not a matter of walking off elsewhere, but of "each one complete in itself possessing the bodhisattva's practice." The prefatory segment of Chengguan's *Xingyuanpin shu* may serve to seal this principal-and-supporting wing:

> To accord with Mañjuśrī's wondrous wisdom — it is, just so, the initial mind; to enter Samantabhadra's profound gate — there was never a separate substance.[^18]

Zongmi, in the *Subcommentary*, glosses this as "the substance of wisdom was from the first not separate." Mañjuśrī's wondrous wisdom is itself Sudhana's initial mind; Samantabhadra's profound gate has no other substance — and this single line gathers the whole chiasmus of "Mañjuśrī — the fifty-one between — Mañjuśrī's return — Samantabhadra" back into the four words "never a separate substance." This is of one bloodline with the architecture of vast cultivation of offerings and the convergence of the dharma-offering treated in the two preceding papers (see DĀNA P25, P26); the present paper only cross-links it.

One easily confused locution must be clearly distinguished here. Fascicle eighty of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, in describing Samantabhadra, will produce the phrase "not amounting to a hundredth part"; the preceding paper, treating "the dharma-offering supreme among all offerings," found "not amounting to a hundredth part" in the dharma-offering segment of fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* as well. Though the same six characters appear in both contexts, the doctrinal grammar is distinct. The preceding paper's "not amounting to a hundredth part" is a hierarchy metric — the dharma-offering, set against material offering, is a quantitative gradient of the form "this exceeds that" (see DĀNA P26); the present paper's "not amounting to a hundredth part" (treated in the next section) is a containment formula — everything Sudhana enters across the whole journey is encompassed within a single thought in a single pore of Samantabhadra. The former speaks of high and low; the latter of containment. The reader must not, on the strength of the identical wording, equate their senses.

Last, dialogue with three Western scholars. First, Douglas Osto's *Power, Wealth and Women in Indian Mahāyāna Buddhism: The Gaṇḍavyūha-sūtra* (2008) is the sole Western monograph devoted to the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm," and argues that the structure of the fifty-three visits is a textual articulation, in economic terms, of a "circulation of giving" — the distribution of the fifty-three spiritual friends across social strata (kings, householders, youths, maidens, monks, nuns, non-Buddhist practitioners, deities, night-goddesses, bodhisattvas) being the structural proof that giving has "no class-floor."[^19] This paper gladly grants Osto's observation of social distribution as established scholarly ground — it is the doctrinal proof that dāna is the universal ground, admitting no lower bound of class, and it offers the polyphonic reading an entry-point a Western scholarly reader can accept. Yet Osto's economic frame stresses the horizontal social distribution of giving and does not closely capture the recursive structure of Sudhana as a "walker" — that is, the self-referential loop of "seeing his own body within Samantabhadra's body" treated in the next section. This paper restores that vertical, self-referential dimension here: Osto's horizontal-circulation reading must be completed by the Sino-Indic commentary's vertical structure of "the mutual inclusion of the two sages."

Second, the several essays of Phyllis Granoff treat the place of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" within the wider Indian narrative tradition, attending in particular to the precedents of the "spiritual friend" as a category in Indian hagiographical literature, and setting it against corresponding narrative genres in Jain and Hindu materials. This paper grants this observation of an "Indian narrative grammar" as a useful piece of documentary excavation — it signals that the fifty-three visits are not an isolated Buddhist invention but a narrative genre running across Indian religious literature. Yet Granoff's comparative-philological frame stresses formal resemblance and treats only lightly the doctrinal content proper to the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" — the first generation of the mind of awakening, the maximization of great compassion, the gathering architecture of contemplative practice. This paper restores that doctrinal layer here: the narrative grammar is the "form," and the walk-through of giving is the "content" that form carries; the narrative form of the fifty-three visits is in service of giving's architecture of entry into the dharma-realm, and is not merely one instance of a narrative genre.

Third, the several essays of Luis Gómez (1943–2017) argue, from the standpoint of textual stratification, that the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is an independent stratum of early Mahāyāna, formed roughly contemporaneously with the *Sukhāvatīvyūha* line, and, from its contrast with the "vision-structure" of the Pure Land corpus, suggest that the chapter represents an early-Mahāyāna paradigm of "entry-into-position — vision — pilgrimage." This textual-stratification work is standard Western scholarly procedure, and this paper grants its value. Yet the frame Gómez stresses is the cross-textual form of the vision, and it does not closely capture the particular thread of "giving" that recurs through the fifty-three visits. This paper restores that giving-thread here: Gómez's "entry-into-position — vision — pilgrimage" paradigm is a cross-textual structure, while this paper's reading of "the walk-through of giving" is the unfolding of a thematic strand that, within that cross-textual structure, is proper to the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" alone.

Of what the three see: Osto captures the distributional space of giving, Granoff the narrative form of the pilgrimage, Gómez the cross-textual paradigm of the vision; each sees something, and none closely captures the recursive, encompassing dimension of "giving as a walk-through of the entry into the dharma-realm." For that dimension, one must return to the chiastic reading of the Sino-Indic commentary.

## VI. Conclusion: The Trajectory's Closure and the Seam to the Volume of Precepts

The fifty-third teacher, Samantabhadra, is the gatherer of this trajectory. What Sudhana sees on reaching Samantabhadra's arena is the looped closure of the whole road:

> He further saw Samantabhadra, within each and every ocean of worlds, sending forth clouds of transformation-bodies of Buddhas numerous as the dust-motes of all Buddha-fields, pervading all the worlds of the ten directions, teaching and transforming sentient beings, causing them to turn toward unsurpassed, perfect awakening. And then Sudhana the youth saw his own body within Samantabhadra's body, teaching and transforming sentient beings throughout all the worlds of the ten directions.[^20]

"He saw his own body within Samantabhadra's body" — this is the most emblematic closure of the whole trajectory of the fifty-three visits. Sudhana did not walk up to a point "in front of" Samantabhadra; he found that he had been walking within Samantabhadra's body all along — the road he walked turns out to be encompassed by the body that walked it. By this, the "not amounting to a hundredth part" of fascicle eighty of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* attains its proper reading:

> The wholesome roots and the light of wisdom that Sudhana the youth attained by drawing near to spiritual friends numerous as the dust-motes of the Buddha-fields, compared with the wholesome roots attained on seeing the bodhisattva Samantabhadra, do not amount to a hundredth part ... the oceans of Buddha-fields that he entered in the interval from his first generation of the mind down to his seeing Samantabhadra — the oceans of Buddha-fields he now enters in a single thought within a single pore of Samantabhadra exceed the former by an unspeakably unspeakable multiple of the dust-motes of the Buddha-fields.[^21]

As section V distinguished, the "not amounting to a hundredth part" here is a containment formula, not a hierarchy metric: everything Sudhana entered across the whole of his fifty-three visits is encompassed within a single thought in a single pore of Samantabhadra. The whole trajectory is gathered into one thought, one pore — and here the chiasmus closes completely. The closing scene of fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, where Mañjuśrī, Maitreya, and Samantabhadra stand together as the three foremost, is the final imprint of this closure at the level of the sūtra's own structure.[^22]

With this, the four papers of the Samantabhadra arena — the architecture of vast cultivation of offerings, the analysis of the dharma-offering, the deep treatment of the unity of doctrine and Chan, and the walk-through of the fifty-three visits — are gathered to a close as a tetralogy. What the present paper has established is the sense of "trajectory": the entry into the dharma-realm is not a staircase but a road that is walked, and encompassed. The road ahead points toward the final closure of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" chapter — the full unfolding of the dharma-realm, the realm of interpenetration without obstruction — which is the ground of the next paper. And the closing of the volume of giving points not only toward the terminal horizon of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" but back, as well, toward the entry-point of volume three, the precepts: the segment in which the great deity Mahādeva, the twenty-third teacher, names "the perfection of moral discipline" alongside "the perfection of giving" is the cross-volume seam, where the closing of the giving-volume hands off to the entry-point of the precepts-volume; the walk-through of the fifty-three visits, walked to its end, has for its next step the precepts.

**Volume Three (SĪLA) Forward Pointer:** the segment in which the great deity Mahādeva, the twenty-third teacher, names "the perfection of moral discipline" alongside "the perfection of giving" (section IV and the conclusion of section VI) is the cross-volume seam from the giving-volume toward the precepts-volume; the papers of Volume Three will take up this seam, entering by way of the sense of "the perfections in unbroken succession" demonstrated in that segment.
