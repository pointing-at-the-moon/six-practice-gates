# The Joy of the First Ground

**Dāna as the Entry-Threshold of the Bodhisattva-Bhūmis**

*中文主題：歡喜地之施——十地修證之入口*

*A Study of the First Bhūmi in the Daśabhūmika Sūtra and Its East Asian Commentary Tradition*

**Author:** 釋慧鏡 (Shi Huijing)
**Date:** May 2026
**License:** CC BY-NC-SA 4.0
**Version:** 1.0

## Abstract

This paper examines the first *bhūmi* (*pramuditā-bhūmi*, the Joyful Ground) of the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* as the entry-threshold of the bodhisattva path, arguing that *dāna* is not merely first in sequence among the perfections but constitutive of the threshold itself. Reading T0279 fascicle 34 (Śikṣānanda's eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*) against T0278 (Buddhabhadra's sixty-fascicle parallel), Nāgārjuna's ranging exegesis in the *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*, Vasubandhu's systematic *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, and four East Asian commentators — Zhiyan, Fazang, Chengguan, and Li Tongxuan — the thesis is this: the door to the first *bhūmi* is neither metaphysical contemplation of Indra's net, nor a catalogue entry in a perfection-to-ground mapping, nor an etymological correlate of the "Joyful Practice" in the Ten Practices Chapter. It is rather a triangular entry-threshold: *mahā-karuṇā* as the cause of entry, *mahā-tyāga* as its praxis, and the ten great vows (*daśa-mahā-praṇidhāna*) as its vow-structure. *Dāna* in the first *bhūmi* is not first in sequence; it is the constitutive structure of what makes entry possible. The paper opens Part VIII by establishing this threshold as the source-structure of the Samantabhadra material to follow.

**Keywords:** *pramuditā-bhūmi*, *Daśabhūmika-sūtra*, *dāna-pāramitā*, the great giving, great compassion as forerunner, the ten great vows, East Asian Huayan, the emptiness of the three spheres

## I. Introduction: What Is the Door to the Joyful Ground?

When the Western reader first encounters Huayan, what meets the eye is Indra's net — the net of Indra, a thousand jewels reflecting one another, one jewel mirroring all jewels, all jewels mirroring the one, mutually containing and mutually entering, without end. Cook, in his classic monograph, distilled this configuration as Huayan's distinctive contribution: the systematization of conditioned interdependence into a thoroughgoing metaphysics of mutual containment.[^1] This description is accurate — but what does it describe? The fruition-state, or the entrance?

If Huayan's ultimate vision is the jewelled net of a thousand reflecting jewels, then what is the door into that net? This is not a defensive question but the sūtra's own opening question. The Ten Bhūmis Chapter, fascicle 34, in Śikṣānanda's eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, takes the following sentence as the establishing verse of the first ground:[^2]

> "Sons of the Buddha! The bodhisattva arises with such a mind: having great compassion as forerunner, augmented by wisdom, embraced by skillful means, sustained by the most profound resolve, [endowed with] the Tathāgata's immeasurable power, the power of skillful discernment, the power of valiant exertion, and the power of cognition; with unobstructed cognition manifest before him, conforming to spontaneous cognition, able to receive all the Buddha's teachings, teaching and transforming through wisdom — vast as the realm of dharma, ultimate as space, exhausting the bounds of the future... When the bodhisattva abides in such a dharma, this is called abiding in the bodhisattva's Joyful Ground, because he accords with the immovable."

This passage contains every structural element of entry — "great compassion as forerunner," "augmented by wisdom," "skillful means," "the most profound resolve," "vast as the realm of dharma," "ultimate as space," "exhausting the bounds of the future" — and closes upon "abiding in the bodhisattva's Joyful Ground, because he accords with the immovable." This is not the result of contemplating Indra's net; it is entry achieved by acting upon *dāna* with the emptiness of the three spheres of giving (三輪體空) as the configuration of mind — great compassion as the cause of entry, the great giving (廣大施) as the praxis of entry, the ten great vows (十大願, *daśa-mahā-praṇidhāna*) of Samantabhadra as the vow of entry — the three forming the triangular structure of the threshold.

This triangular relation can be read in skeletal form in the parallel entry passage of Buddhabhadra's sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*:[^3]

> "This mind has great compassion as forerunner, augmented by wisdom, guarded by skillful means; the upright mind and the profound mind, pure and complete, equal in measure to the Buddha's power... When the bodhisattva abides in such a dharma, this is called abiding in the Joyful Ground, because of the immovable dharma."

"Great compassion as forerunner" is, in both the sixty- and the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, the first condition of entering the ground. But the sixty-fascicle version lacks the doctrinal-classificatory line "among the ten perfections, the perfection of giving is augmented" — this line appears only in the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, and its conceptual crystallization is rooted not in the sūtra-text itself but in the influence of the Indian commentarial layer upon the translation hall.[^4]

This is the paper's central claim: the door to the Joyful Ground is not a metaphysical contemplation of Indra's net (the Cook line — the cosmological reading's fixation upon the end-state), not the first perfection in a textbook's sequential ordering (the Williams line — the catalogue reading), nor an etymological correlate of the "Joyful Practice" (歡喜行) of the Ten Practices Chapter — whenever Zhiyan's gloss "because giving accomplishes joy in self and other, the first is named the Joyful Practice" is applied without distinction as a gloss upon the Joyful Ground, that misplacement is triggered — but rather the triangular entry-threshold structure of great compassion as cause, the great giving as praxis, and the ten great vows as vow. *Dāna* in the first *bhūmi* is not merely first in sequence; it is the constitutive structure of the threshold itself.

Why does this threshold matter? Because it is the source-structure of the Samantabhadra arena that occupies Part VIII. From P21 (dharma-offering in the Dharma-Teacher Chapter, the substitution-move) → P22 (Sadāparibhūta's *prasāda-dāna*, the axiomatic move) → P23 (Bhaiṣajyarāja's *dehadāna*, the transformation-move), the typological arc of Part VII closes here; P24 opens the architectural move of Part VIII — not treating the full unfolding of the ten great vows (that is P25's territory), not treating the verse "among all offerings, the dharma-offering is supreme" (that verse issues from the Chapter of Samantabhadra's Vows of Practice in the forty-fascicle text, T0293 fascicle 40, outside the territory of the Ten Bhūmis Chapter[^5]), but rather the question: what is the entry-structure of the Samantabhadra arena?

This requires treating two layers at once: the two wings of Indian commentary (Nāgārjuna's ranging exegesis and Vasubandhu's systematic commentary) and the three East Asian commentators (Zhiyan-Fazang, Chengguan, Li Tongxuan). But concerning the three East Asian commentators, one discipline must be established at the outset:[^6]

Zhiyan opens interpretive space through multi-structural decomposition; Fazang closes it into a system through his analysis of essential nature and his sevenfold framework; Chengguan gathers it into Samantabhadra-style full integration through the four dharma-realms and the Lotus-Treasury world; Li Tongxuan gathers it into "entering faith is itself attainment" through the One Vehicle's Buddha of Immovable Wisdom. The three are not a chronological chain of "draft — maturation — synthesis" but a logical space in which three interpretive postures stand side by side. Were Zhiyan to compose a new commentary on the Ten Bhūmis Chapter later than Fazang, his posture would still incline toward multi-decomposition; were Fazang to complete his work earlier than Zhiyan, his posture would still incline toward systematic closure. The three are temperaments, not stages.

The dates of birth and death run: Zhiyan (602–668), Fazang (643–712), Li Tongxuan (635–730), Chengguan (738–839). Li Tongxuan is earlier than Chengguan and roughly contemporary with Fazang; the linear narrative "Fazang → Chengguan → Li Tongxuan" is a chronological misplacement.[^7]

The four sections that follow treat, in order: textual stratification and the doctrinal-classificatory line; the two Indian wings; the three East Asian commentators; and the Madhyamaka correction in dialogue with Western scholarship.

## II. Translation Layers and the Doctrinal Crystallization of "the Perfection of Giving Augmented"

The Chinese rendering and naturalization of *pramuditā* spans five hundred years:[^8]

| Translation | Translator | Date | Rendering of *pramuditā* |
|---|---|---|---|
| T0285 *Jianbei yiqie zhide jing* | Dharmarakṣa | 297 CE | 悅豫地 (Ground of Gladness) |
| T0278 *Avataṃsaka* (60 fasc.) | Buddhabhadra | 418–420 CE | 歡喜地 (Joyful Ground) |
| T0286 *Shizhu jing* | attrib. Kumārajīva + Buddhayaśas | 408 CE | 歡喜地 (Joyful Ground) |
| T0287 *Foshuo shidi jing* | Śīladharma | 790 CE | 極喜地 (Ground of Supreme Joy) |

From "gladness" to "joy" to "supreme joy" — five centuries of fossil-strata in the Chinese naturalization of *pramuditā*. The old T0285 rendering still uses "kindness" and "gentleness" as its affective vocabulary, with no paired terminology of "great compassion / great relinquishment"; T0286 (Kumārajīva–Buddhayaśas, 408 CE) is the first to present the pairing "the mind of great compassion, the mind of great relinquishment."[^9] The implication: the affective frame of the first ground's perfection of giving (great compassion as cause → great relinquishment as praxis) is a fifth-century Chinese conceptual consolidation, not already present in Dharmarakṣa's original rendering — and at the same time a marker of the maturation of Mahāyāna thought in Chinese.

This conceptual consolidation is accompanied by the crystallization of doctrinal classification. The eighty-fascicle Tang rendering alone carries the following line —[^10]

> "This bodhisattva, among the ten perfections, has the perfection of giving augmented; it is not that he does not cultivate the remaining perfections, but he does so according to his capacity and his portion."

— a line with no corresponding sentence at all in the sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0278). This line is not native to the Indian layer of the sūtra-text; it is a doctrinal crystallization within the eighty-fascicle translation hall, formed under the influence of Vasubandhu's commentary, T1522. The *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, fascicle 3, had already established this classification within Vasubandhu's translation workshop.[^11] This is not merely a translation difference but a fossil stratum, within the Chinese rendering, of the doctrinal crystallization of the Indian commentary — Vasubandhu raised the priority of *dāna* for the first *bhūmi* from an implicit textual tendency to an explicit doctrinal-classificatory statement.

The binding of the paired terms "great compassion + great relinquishment" is likewise a conceptual consolidation of the fifth-century Chinese translation system. In the entry passage of the sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, T0278 fascicle 23, "great compassion as forerunner" stands alongside "the profound mind, pure and complete"; the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, T0279 fascicle 34, expands it into:[^12]

> "Sons of the Buddha! When the bodhisattva-mahāsattva, conforming to such great compassion and great loving-kindness, abides in the first ground with a mind of profound gravity, he begrudges nothing among all things... He cultivates the great relinquishment: whatever he possesses, he is able to give. That is to say: wealth and grain, storehouses and treasuries, gold and silver, maṇi-jewels and pearls...; servants and subjects, cities, towns and villages, gardens and groves, terraces and pavilions, wives and consorts, sons and daughters, inner and outer retinue, and all his other articles of delight; head and eyes, hand and foot, blood and flesh, bone and marrow — every part of the body he holds without begrudging, in order to seek the vast wisdom of the Buddhas. This is called the bodhisattva's accomplishment of the great relinquishment while abiding in the first ground."

This passage is the strongest single sentence in P24's doctrinal grounding — "he begrudges nothing among all things" is not ordinary moral exhortation but the constitutive condition of the door of entry. It is not that joy is gained after giving; rather, the begrudging-nothing is itself the entry-marker of the Joyful Ground. "Great compassion" as the cause of entry, "great relinquishment" as the praxis of entry: in the sūtra-text the passage opens with "conforming to such great compassion" and closes with "this is called the bodhisattva's accomplishment of the great relinquishment while abiding in the first ground," forming a complete causal chain.

The significance of "great compassion as forerunner" is not merely affective priority. In the parallel sentence of the sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*:[^13]

> "This mind has great compassion as forerunner, augmented by wisdom, guarded by skillful means; the upright mind and the profound mind, pure and complete, equal in measure to the Buddha's power..."

"Great compassion as forerunner" (大悲為首, *mahā-karuṇā-pūrvaṅgama*) stands alongside "augmented by wisdom" and "guarded by skillful means" — three matters of equal weight: great compassion as the root of motivation, *prajñā* as contemplative observation, skillful means as the gate of praxis. This is the sūtra-text skeleton of the mind-configuration of the emptiness of the three spheres: arising from the compassionate mind, illumined by wisdom, enacted through skillful means. Not a single-faceted cultivation, but the simultaneous operation of the three.

Vasubandhu's commentary, in T1522 fascicle 2, restates this passage as:[^14]

> "This mind has great compassion as forerunner, augmented by wisdom, embraced by skillful means, the upright mind and the profound mind pure and complete, the Tathāgata's immeasurable power... able to receive all the Buddha's teachings, teaching and transforming through wisdom, vast as the realm of dharma, ultimate as space, exhausting the bounds of the future."

Vasubandhu does not alter the skeleton of the sūtra-text; he supplies a gloss upon it: "great compassion as forerunner" is the cause of secure abiding — all that the bodhisattva does is for the sake of sentient beings; compassion is the root of practice, hence it is called the forerunner.[^15] This sentence is the most central proposition of the Dilun school: entering the Joyful Ground is not a personal milestone of cultivation-and-attainment but a structural transformation premised upon sentient beings.

## III. Indian Commentary in Two Wings: Nāgārjuna's Ranging Exegesis and Vasubandhu's Systematic Commentary

The Indian commentary treats the giving of the first ground in two ways: Nāgārjuna's ranging exegesis (the *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*, T1521) and Vasubandhu's systematic commentary (the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, T1522). The two are complementary — Nāgārjuna locates giving from the vantage of the bodhisattva path as a whole; Vasubandhu analyzes *dāna* from within the internal structure of the first *bhūmi*.

### III.1 Nāgārjuna's Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā: Giving as Provision

Nāgārjuna's treatment begins from a basic positioning:[^16]

> "This mind is adorned, because merit is its provision. This mind is discriminating, because wisdom is its provision. This mind is rich and full, because giving is its provision. This mind is greatly resolved, because moral discipline is its provision. This mind is hard to thwart, because patience is its provision. This mind is hard to overcome, because energy is its provision. This mind is quiescent, because meditation is its provision. This mind is without affliction or harm, because wisdom is its provision."

This is the correspondence of the six perfections at the opening of T1521 fascicle 1 — "giving as its provision" is the first of eight "provisions." Provision here is not a supplementary good deed but a necessary precondition of entering concentration. Nāgārjuna, through the provision-frame, positions *dāna* as the foundational resource of the bodhisattva path — not because *dāna* takes priority over the other five perfections, but because meritorious provision is the material of all subsequent cultivation-and-attainment.

Great compassion, in Nāgārjuna's system, is the deeper source of motivation:[^17]

> "Some say: the *trāṇaka-samādhi* and great compassion are called the family of the Buddhas. From these two dharmas the Tathāgatas are born; herein the *trāṇaka-samādhi* is the father, and great compassion is the mother."

The *trāṇaka-samādhi* as father, great compassion as mother — this is not poetic rhetoric. What Nāgārjuna establishes here is a twofold generative structure: the *trāṇaka-samādhi* supplies the methodology (the father, the formal cause), great compassion supplies the motivation (the mother, the material cause). Neither can be dispensed with: lacking great compassion, *samādhi* becomes self-liberation alone; lacking *samādhi*, great compassion becomes an empty vow.

The Chapter on Purifying the Ground, the second, transforms this structure into the first of the twenty-seven curative dharmas:[^18]

> "The power of faith turns and is augmented; he deeply practices the mind of great compassion, with loving-kindness pities the classes of sentient beings, and cultivates good without weariness in mind... this then cures the Joyful Ground."

"Deeply practicing the mind of great compassion" is the first of the curatives for the Joyful Ground. Not contemplating Indra's net, not holding the name, not seated meditation — but deeply practicing the mind of great compassion. This is Nāgārjuna's praxis-positioning of the first *bhūmi*: great compassion is not an ordinary virtue but the concrete method that cures the obstructions of the first ground.

The Chapter Distinguishing Giving, the sixth, gives the causal chain of great compassion and *dāna*:[^19]

> "If the bodhisattva, in this way, deeply follows the mind of loving-kindness and compassion, severs all craving and begrudging, and is diligent and energetic in giving."

"Deeply following the mind of loving-kindness and compassion → severing craving and begrudging → opening the gate of the perfection of giving" — the three steps are irreversible. It is not that giving comes first and then the compassionate mind is produced; rather the compassionate mind exists first, craving and begrudging are naturally severed, and giving naturally opens. Here *dāna* is not a method of cultivation but the natural result of the compassionate mind.

Concerning the structural archetype of Samantabhadra's ten great vows, Nāgārjuna, in the Chapter Explicating the Vows, the fifth, has already supplied the mother-pattern at the Indian commentarial layer:[^20]

> "These ten great vows have ten ultimate articles... the realm of beings, the realm of the world, the realm of space, the realm of dharma, nirvāṇa, the realm of the Buddhas' arising, the realm of all Buddhas' cognition, [each] reaching its end... As for 'the realm of beings reaching its end': were all sentient beings utterly extinguished, my vow would then come to rest... But since these ten articles — the realm of beings and the rest — are in truth inexhaustible, my merit and roots of goodness are likewise inexhaustible and do not come to rest."

This passage, in T1521 fascicle 3, lays out the logical structure of the ten great vows as "ten ultimate articles": ten "realms" serve as the ultimate condition of the vows, and the condition for the vows' continuation is precisely the inexhaustibility of these ten "realms." Here "the realm of beings" (*sattva-dhātu*), in this context, does not refer to a "nature" but to the category and totality of beings; it stands alongside "the realm of dharma" (*dharma-dhātu*) and "the realm of space" as a concept of collectivity and totality, not a "nature" in the ontological sense. The discipline of the English rendering is established from this point — see the note.[^21]

The attribution of T1521 to Nāgārjuna is received as the standard view in the Chinese tradition; Western text-critical scholarship (Lamotte 1944, Hirakawa) maintains a continuing discussion concerning the compositional history of certain passages;[^22] this paper adopts the standard acceptance of the Chinese tradition and passes over the matter with a single note, without unfolding an adjudication.

### III.2 Vasubandhu's Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna: The Systematization of "Great Compassion as Forerunner"

Vasubandhu's commentary is systematic and analytic. In T1522 fascicle 2 he gives a ninefold classification of "joy":[^23]

> "Joy is called the joy of mind, the joy of essence, the joy of faculty. This joy is of nine kinds: first, the joy of reverence, because of reverence amid the Three Jewels; second, the joy of love, delighting to contemplate the dharma of suchness; third, the joy of gladness, self-aware that what one has attested surpasses upon comparison; fourth, the joy of pliancy...; fifth, the joy of exultation...; sixth, the joy of receptivity...; seventh, the joy of the unbreakable...; eighth, the joy of the unafflicted...; ninth, the joy of the unangered."

These nine kinds of joy are not an etymological conjecture about the naming of the "Joyful Ground" but an analytical dissection of the affective architecture of the first *bhūmi*. Among them, "the joy of love, delighting to contemplate the dharma of suchness" connects directly to the theoretical aspect of *pramuditā* — delight toward suchness; "the joy of receptivity" connects to the degree of receptivity — the capacity to receive all the Buddha's teachings; and "the joy of the unafflicted" and "the joy of the unangered" connect to the first ground's transcendence of the afflictions.

The causal analysis of the five fears (*pañca-bhaya*) is the core of Vasubandhu's yogic reading of *dāna*:[^24]

> "These five fears are obstructions of the first ground; furthermore the benefits of the ground are said to be superior... The causes of these fears are, in brief, of two kinds: first, because of the deluded grasping of false cognition, the attachment of conceiving and viewing; second, because the roots of goodness are slight and few."

The five fears — the fear of not surviving, the fear of bad reputation, the fear of death, the fear of evil destinies, and the fear of intimidating assemblies — are overcome at the first ground; their cause is not the accumulation of merit but rather "the deluded grasping of false cognition, the attachment of conceiving and viewing" (false cognition leading to the grasping of a "self") and "the roots of goodness being slight and few" (the roots of goodness not being deep enough). The key to overcoming the five fears lies in the mind-configuration of giving — the emptiness of the three spheres, the severing of attachment to "what is mine."

The six "greats" of the first great vow are the overall architecture of the offering-vow:[^25]

> "Within this first vow there are six kinds of greatness, named the great vow: first, the greatness of the field of merit...; second, the greatness of the matter offered...; third, the greatness of mind...; fourth, the greatness of the merit gathered...; fifth, the greatness of the cause...; sixth, the greatness of time, as the sūtra says, 'exhausting the bounds of the future.'"

The greatness of the field of merit, the greatness of the matter offered, the greatness of mind, the greatness of the merit gathered, the greatness of the cause, the greatness of time — six kinds of greatness constitute the complete architecture of the first great vow. Among them, "the greatness of mind" (the breadth of motivation) and "the greatness of time" (the length of "exhausting the bounds of the future") are the two most crucial: the greatness of mind expands the motivation of *dāna* from personal deliverance to all sentient beings; the greatness of time extends the time of *dāna* from a single lifetime to the exhaustion of the bounds of the future.

Vasubandhu's classification of "all offerings":[^26]

> "As for all offerings, there are three kinds of offering: first, the offering of sustenance — namely robes, bedding, and the like; second, the offering of reverence — namely incense, flowers, banners, canopies, and the like; third, the offering of practice — namely cultivating the practices of faith and moral discipline, and the like."

The offering of sustenance (material), the offering of reverence (ritual), and the offering of practice (praxis) — this threefold classification of offering has already received in-depth treatment in P21 (dharma-offering in the Dharma-Teacher Chapter).[^27] Here Vasubandhu's significance lies in connecting the classification of offering with the *dāna*-architecture of the first *bhūmi*: the giving of the first-ground bodhisattva is not only material giving but at the same time includes the offering of reverence (toward the Three Jewels) and the offering of practice (toward the praxis of the emptiness of the three spheres).

The inner and outer kinds of the great giving, together with the eight kinds of joy-of-benefit, are the concrete unfolding of *dāna*:[^28]

> "Among these, 'all things' are, in brief, of two kinds: first outer, second inner. The outer is again of two kinds: first what is used, second what is stored... Depending upon this there are two joys: first the joy of what is stored and gathered, second the joy of benefit. The joy of benefit is again of eight kinds: first, the joy of benefit of adornment — namely jeweled ornaments and the like; second, the joy of benefit of conveyance — namely elephants, horses, and the like; third, the joy of benefit of amusement — namely gardens, groves, terraces, and pavilions, and the like; fourth, the joy of benefit of relieving suffering — namely servants and the like; fifth, the joy of benefit of sovereignty — namely lands and villages, and the like; sixth, the joy of benefit of retinue — namely wife and children, and the like; seventh, the joy of benefit of firm attachment — namely all that is loved, and the like; eighth, the joy of benefit of satisfaction — namely head, eyes, ears, nose, and the like."

Inner giving (head, eyes, hand, foot) and outer giving (wealth, grain, storehouses) — together with the eight kinds of joy-of-benefit — constitute the complete map of the first-ground bodhisattva's giving. Among them, "the joy of benefit of relieving suffering" (substituting oneself for the suffering of sentient beings) and "the joy of benefit of satisfaction" (head, eyes, ears, nose — the things most dear) are the two most extreme categories: they are the doctrinal precursors of P23 (Bhaiṣajyarāja's *dehadāna*), but P24 here mentions them only structurally and does not unfold them.

The six characteristics are the most influential conceptual legacy:[^29]

> "Within all the ten phrases that are spoken, there are six kinds of gate of distinguishing characteristics. This verbal explication should be understood as excepting 'things' — 'things' meaning the aggregates, the elements, the sense-fields, and the like. The six characteristics are: the total characteristic, the particular characteristic, the characteristic of identity, the characteristic of distinction, the characteristic of formation, the characteristic of dissolution."

The total, the particular, identity, distinction, formation, dissolution — the six characteristics became the direct source of Fazang's six-characteristic full integration in the *Tanxuan ji*.[^30] The six characteristics are not a description of *dāna* but a method of analysis for the causal relations that *dāna* produces: a single act of giving is at once total (the whole), particular (the individual), identity (the shared), distinction (the differentiated), formation (the established), and dissolution (the non-abiding). The seed of six-characteristic full integration is already present here.

T1522 → the Dilun school (the Northern and Southern paths) → the Zhiyan-Fazang Huayan school: this is the basis for the Chinese reception history treated in P24's fifth section. The six characteristics of T1522 fascicle 1 are the direct source of Fazang's six-characteristic full integration in the *Tanxuan ji*; the line in T1522 fascicle 3, "name-and-form arises together with the *ālayavijñāna*," is the textual point of origin for the millennium-long divergence between the Northern path (Daochong's defiled-consciousness theory) and the Southern path (Huiguang's pure-consciousness theory).[^31]

## IV. The East Asian Three-Cornered Logical Space: Zhiyan-Fazang, Chengguan, Li Tongxuan

This section treats the three East Asian commentators on the first ground. The three are not a chronological development — they are a logical space standing side by side.[^32]

### IV.1 The Zhiyan-Fazang Corner: Foundational Systematic Exegesis

Zhiyan's treatment of the Ten Bhūmis Chapter opens interpretive space through multi-structural decomposition:[^33]

> "The Ten Bhūmis Chapter — it is by the principle of enumeration that the name is made manifest... The first ground has eight divisions, the second ground two divisions, and so on, the tenth ground eight divisions; the distinctions taken together, the one chapter has forty-eight divisions in all. This sūtra-text, compared with the sūtra-recension used in the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, has many additions and deletions."

Zhiyan decomposes the first ground by the method of "enumeration": the first ground in eight divisions, forty-eight divisions in all. The decomposition into multiple structures is not for the sake of dissecting the sūtra-text but for the sake of liberating the sūtra-text's interpretive space. Each division is an independent gate of entry for praxis.

The following sentence from Zhiyan's *Kongmu zhang*, T1870 fascicle 4, is frequently cited without distinction:[^34]

> "The Flower-Gathering Chapter first establishes the chapter of the ten perfections... As for the ten practices: because giving accomplishes joy in self and other, the first is named the Joyful Practice. Because moral discipline accomplishes benefit for self and other, the second is named the Practice of Benefiting..."

Here, "because giving accomplishes joy in self and other, the first is named the Joyful Practice" issues from the first practice of the Ten Practices Chapter (a pre-ground station), not from the first ground of the Ten Bhūmis Chapter (a station upon the grounds).[^35] If one wishes to borrow Zhiyan's phrase "giving accomplishes joy in self and other" as a side-attestation for the *dāna*–*prāmodya* linkage, one must note in a footnote that this is a gloss on the Ten Practices Chapter and a side-attestation by Zhiyan, not a direct gloss on the Ten Bhūmis Chapter — otherwise the textual attribution is misplaced.

Fazang, in T1733 fascicle 9, glosses the name "Joyful Ground" of the Ten Bhūmis Chapter with three meanings:[^36]

> "Hence the commentary says, 'he accomplishes the unsurpassed practice of benefiting self and benefiting others.' First attesting the holy station, much joy arises — hence it is named the Joyful Ground. The explanation has three meanings: first, because the twofold benefit is newly accomplished; second, because the true principle is newly attested; third, because the holy station is newly attained — thereby fulfilling the mind's original aspiration, hence joy arises."

The twofold benefit newly accomplished, the true principle newly attested, the holy station newly attained — Fazang's threefold explanation does not directly establish *dāna* as the foremost cause of the naming of the Joyful Ground. Fazang never said "the Joyful Ground = the giving that accomplishes joy in self and other"[^37] — that saying is Zhiyan's, spoken of the Joyful Practice in the Ten Practices Chapter.

Fazang's treatment of the "four meanings" of entering the ground:[^38]

> "Within the first division, the author of the commentary makes a fourfold division: first, 'depending on what body' — namely, the thickly accumulated roots of goodness as the body depended upon; second, 'for what purpose' — for the sake of seeking the Buddha-fruit; third, 'by what cause' — having great compassion as the cause; fourth, 'with what characteristic' — surpassing the ordinary and attaining the holy as the characteristic."

"Having great compassion as the cause" is the most crucial of the four elements of Fazang's treatment of entry. Great compassion is not a subsidiary condition of entering the ground but the cause of entry — without great compassion, there is no possibility of entering the ground.

Fazang, in T1733 fascicle 11, directly cites the *Liang Compendium of the Mahāyāna* to list the ten names of the ten great vows:[^39]

> "Now, following the *Liang Compendium of the Mahāyāna*, [I] list the ten names in full. It says: first, the vow of offering; second, the vow of upholding; third, the vow of turning the dharma-wheel; fourth, the vow of cultivation; fifth, the vow of accomplishment; sixth, the vow of attending and serving; seventh, the vow of the pure land; eighth, the vow of non-separation; ninth, the vow of benefiting; tenth, the vow of perfect awakening... The explanation says: ... the ten vows of that treatise issue from this sūtra, since there is no text for them anywhere else."

Here Fazang determines the Ten Bhūmis Chapter to be the root-sūtra of the ten great vows.[^40] But this is not equivalent to the Samantabhadra arena of P25 — here it is not explicitly stated to be identical with the Chapter of Samantabhadra's Vows of Practice; that is P25's territory.

### IV.2 The Chengguan Corner: Synthetic Collation

Chengguan, in T1735 fascicle 33, establishes a fourfold-division architecture for the first ground:[^41]

> "Now, following the commentary, [I] divide the prose section into two: first, clarifying the first ground's section of exposition; then, from 'Sons of the Buddha, the bodhisattva abiding herein' onward, the section of comparative superiority... Following the commentary, there are one hundred phrases in all, divided into three sections: the first forty phrases clarify the section of abiding; the second, from 'Sons of the Buddha, the bodhisattva abiding in the Joyful Ground' onward, thirty phrases, the section of explicating the name; the third, from 'Sons of the Buddha, this bodhisattva, through great compassion' onward, thirty phrases, the section of secure abiding. If one also takes in the comparative superiority, this makes the fourfold division of the first ground."

Forty phrases clarifying the section of abiding, thirty phrases the section of explicating the name, thirty phrases the section of secure abiding — Chengguan finely divides the entry passage into three sections, each corresponding to a phase of the bodhisattva's cultivation: abiding (secure establishment), explicating the name (naming), and secure abiding (praxis).

Great compassion as the cause of secure abiding, triangulated with the profound mind and the upright mind:[^42]

> "The third main passage, from 'Sons of the Buddha, this bodhisattva, through great compassion' onward, is the section of secure abiding in the ground... First, great compassion as forerunner is the cause of secure abiding — all that the bodhisattva does is for the sake of sentient beings; compassion is the root of practice, hence it is called the forerunner... Furthermore, these four matters are the three kinds of *bodhicitta*: deeply planting the roots of goodness is the profound mind; next, seeking awakening is the upright mind; third is the mind of great compassion. Possessing these three minds completes the characteristic of the subsequent entry into the ground."

"Possessing these three minds completes the characteristic of the subsequent entry into the ground" — the upright mind, the profound mind, and the mind of great compassion (the three minds named in the *Yogācārabhūmi*) are the complete condition of entering the ground. Here Chengguan expands Fazang's "great compassion as the cause" into the three minds standing side by side, enlarging great compassion from a single pivot into a tripartite structure.

The fully integrated configuration of the ten great vows:[^43]

> "As for 'fully integrated,' it is because [the vows] accord with [their own] nature. Within a single vow all vows are present — this is the entering of layer upon layer, as is regularly distinguished. Six-characteristic full integration lies precisely in this text."

"Within a single vow all vows are present" — this is a forward-pointer toward P25.[^44] But P24 here passes over it with a single sentence and does not unfold it.

### IV.3 The Li Tongxuan Corner: The One-Vehicle Reading of the Layperson's Direct Realization

Li Tongxuan, in T1739 fascicle 22, treats the *dāna* of the first *bhūmi* most economically:[^45]

> "The eighth passage, twenty-one and a half lines of sūtra-text... clarifies that in this station, for the sake of seeking the Buddha's cognition, there is the portion of begrudging nothing of body, life, and wealth. Within this passage the giving is of three kinds: first, the giving of wealth and treasure; second, the giving of elephants, horses, wife, and children; third, the giving of head, eyes, ear, nose, body, and flesh... Taking the perfection of giving as the essence, the remaining nine as its companions."

"Taking the perfection of giving as the essence, the remaining nine as its companions" — these few words position *dāna* among the ten perfections as the "essence" (the root), and the remaining nine perfections as its "companions" (its auxiliaries). This is the most economical single proposition of late-Tang Huayan.

Li Tongxuan, in T1739 fascicle 22, gives a radical elevation of Samantabhadra:[^46]

> "Herein, as for the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha: Vairocana is taken as the Buddha-Jewel, Mañjuśrī as the Dharma-Jewel, and the practice of Samantabhadra as the Saṅgha-Jewel. The Three Jewels of the three vehicles, of gods and humans, and of the six destinies are all gathered within these Three Jewels, because they are all accomplished through the teaching that follows upon Samantabhadra's practice."

"The practice of Samantabhadra as the Saṅgha-Jewel" — this elevates Samantabhadra from a bodhisattva to the status of the Saṅgha-Jewel.[^47] This is a forward-pointer toward P25,[^48] but P24 here does not unfold it.

Li Tongxuan's distinctive register for explicating the first ground is the direct realization of the Buddha of Immovable Wisdom (不動智佛), coupled with the One Vehicle's refutation of the three vehicles' three incalculable eons.[^49] Li Tongxuan's image-and-number cosmology (Samantabhadra correlated with the *mao* position, the East, and wood) does not appear within his commentary on the first ground; this paper does not cite that aspect.[^50]

## V. The Madhyamaka Correction: Three Western Interlocutors

This section treats three Western Huayan scholars, read in a "concede first, then correct" mode.[^51]

### V.1 Hamar: Philology and the Threshold Distinguished

Hamar's core contribution is to read Huayan as a single lineage of transmission — the Sanskrit *Avataṃsaka*, the intermediary translations of Khotan and Central Asia, and the Chinese renderings of Buddhabhadra and Śikṣānanda — constituting a continuous textual continuum, one phase succeeding another.[^52] For the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra*, the Hamar line regards this sūtra as the core of the *Avataṃsaka*'s textual history — the ten-*bhūmi* teaching as generative rather than ornamental.

**Concession:** Hamar's "Sanskrit–Chinese transmission" frame correctly resists the early Western tendency to read Huayan as a purely Chinese sectarian construction. P24 is built precisely upon his point: the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* is the textual-historical core of the *Avataṃsaka* — and this is exactly the scholarly ground on which this paper establishes the entry of the first *bhūmi* as the entry-threshold of the whole sūtra-corpus.

**Push-back:** Hamar's text-critical vantage suspends the practitioner-aspect of entry outside its frame. P24's reading of T1522 and Fazang's T1733 fascicles 9 through 11 is precisely the restoration of this missing soteriological aspect — the first *bhūmi* is not only the textual core but a transformative motivational architecture, opened by acting upon *dāna* with the mind-configuration of the emptiness of the three spheres. Philology brings the sūtra-corpus into view; commentary brings the door of entry into view.[^53]

### V.2 Cook: Cosmology and the Door of Entry Distinguished

Cook's classic monograph remains, to this day, the most widely read introduction to Huayan thought in the English-speaking world. Its core move is to extract Fazang's framework of "dharmadhātu dependent arising / the unobstructed mutual interpenetration of phenomena" and present it, through the metaphor of Indra's net, as a fully integrated cosmology of mutual containment.[^54]

**Concession:** Cook correctly identifies Fazang's framework of the unobstructed mutual interpenetration of phenomena as the doctrinal summit of Huayan — any reading of the first *bhūmi* must, in the end, account for how the entry of the Joyful Ground connects to this summit. P24 grants the accuracy of Cook's description of the cosmology as such.

**Push-back:** Cook's Indra's-net frame inclines toward the end-state — it describes the cosmic vista seen from the higher grounds (even the Buddha-ground), but does not clearly indicate the entrance. In Cook's exposition, the first *bhūmi* becomes a foreshadowing of the cosmic vista rather than a door of entry with its own doctrinal specificity. P24's correction: the first taste of dharmadhātu dependent arising, upon entering the Joyful Ground, is entered by way of the giving of the emptiness of the three spheres, not by way of metaphysical contemplation of Indra's net. Cook's metaphor describes the destination; the first *bhūmi* indicates the door.[^55]

This accords entirely with Chengguan's doctrinal discipline:[^56] Chengguan reserves the configuration of the four dharma-realms and the unobstructed mutual interpenetration of phenomena for the seventh ground and above ("only from the seventh ground onward does cognition become self-victorious," T1735 fascicle 34). The entry of the first *bhūmi* still lies at the level of the wisdom from reflection and the wisdom from cultivation. Dharmadhātu dependent arising is the direction of tendency, not the condition of entry.[^57]

### V.3 Williams: Catalogue and Architecture Distinguished

Williams's textbook treatment of the ten-*bhūmi* system — distributed across the bodhisattva-path chapter and the Huayan chapter — is the most standard introductory synthesis for English readers.[^58] He takes *prasāda* (serene faith, exultation) as the principal aspect of the Joyful Ground.

**Concession:** Williams correctly grounds the first *bhūmi* in *prasāda* — joy is the mind-configuration of entry, not a by-product of cultivation-and-attainment. P24 grants this: anyone who regards the joy of the Joyful Ground as ornamental will miss the affective architecture of this chapter.

**Push-back:** Williams's textbook classification treats *dāna* as the one item among the ten perfections that corresponds to the first *bhūmi*, but does not ask why it is *dāna* that corresponds to the first *bhūmi*. The argument of P24's reading of T1522 and T1733 is this: *dāna* is not merely first in sequence but a constitutive component of the entry-threshold itself — the mind-configuration of the emptiness of the three spheres that opens the first *bhūmi* is itself the mind-configuration of giving, not a precondition lying before giving.[^59] The textbook classification of "one perfection corresponding to one ground" obscures this constitutive function; the Chinese commentarial tradition is precisely what brings it back into the open.

### V.4 The Three Anchors of the Madhyamaka Correction

Synthesizing the three push-backs above, P24's Madhyamaka correction has three anchors:[^60]

1. **The correction of substituting cosmology for entry:** the correction of the Cook line — the door to the Joyful Ground is not the contemplation of dharmadhātu dependent arising but the entry-threshold of great compassion as forerunner and the great giving as praxis. Dharmadhātu dependent arising is a tendency, not a condition.

2. **The correction of substituting the catalogue for the architecture:** the correction of the textbook classification — the *dāna* of the first *bhūmi* is not the first item in a catalogue but the constitutive structure of the entrance itself. *Dāna* is the door itself, not the room behind the door.

3. **The emptiness of the three spheres as the pivot between Madhyamaka and Huayan:** under the frame of the emptiness of the three spheres, *dāna* becomes the pivot between Madhyamaka and Huayan.[^61] This linkage has already received detailed treatment in DĀNA P12–P13 (whose root-text locus is the *Mahāprajñāpāramitāupadeśa*, T1509 fascicle 11).[^62]

As for the oppositional interpretation of "Huayan's supersession of Yogācāra" — this is a modern scholarly construction;[^63] the patriarchs (Zhiyan, Fazang) employed all three systems together: the Dilun, the *Compendium of the Mahāyāna*, and the new translations of Yogācāra. To read Huayan as a supersession of Yogācāra is a modern scholarly frame, not the working posture of the patriarchs themselves.

## VI. Conclusion: The Part VIII Opener Established

The door to the Joyful Ground — great compassion as forerunner, the great giving as praxis, the ten great vows as vow — the triangular structure is established.

Seen from the closing of Part VII's typological arc: P21 (dharma-offering in the Dharma-Teacher Chapter, the substitution-move) → P22 (Sadāparibhūta's *prasāda-dāna*, the axiomatic move) → P23 (Bhaiṣajyarāja's *dehadāna*, the transformation-move) → P24 (the entry-threshold of the Joyful Ground, the architectural move). The typological arc of Part VII is hereby complete; the Huayan opening of Part VIII formally begins.

Why is this threshold the source-structure of the Samantabhadra arena? Because the three elements of the entry-triangle — great compassion (motivation), the great giving (praxis), the ten great vows (vow) — are precisely the three cores of Samantabhadra's practice. Samantabhadra's extensive practice of offering does not arise from nothing; it develops from the seed of this entry-triangle into the temporal dimension of "thought after thought continuing without interruption."[^64]

Chengguan's reading of the three minds of the entry passage — the upright mind, the profound mind, the mind of great compassion — is not unfolded here.[^65] The full integration of the three minds of the Joyful Ground unfolds into the "thought after thought continuing without interruption" of Samantabhadra's ten great vows — and this is the principal arena of the remaining chapters of Part VIII.

The door to the Joyful Ground is not the result of contemplating Indra's net but the entry-threshold of great compassion as forerunner and the great giving as praxis. The key to this door lies not in metaphysical contemplation but in the mind-configuration of giving — begrudging nothing among all things, in order to seek the vast wisdom of the Buddhas.[^66]
