# Samantabhadra's Vow of Vast Offerings

**The Third Vow as Entry-Architecture — From Indian Offering Theory to Huayan Contemplative Offering across Five Centuries**

## Abstract

The third of Samantabhadra's ten great vows, "vast cultivation of offerings" (*guangxiu gongyang*, 廣修供養), is frequently read in Western Huayan scholarship as a devotional appendix — an ornamental catalogue of material offerings appended to a cosmological system. This paper argues instead that the third vow constitutes the entry-architecture of Samantabhadra's central arena, built upon three axes: the inventory of offerings, their cosmic magnitude, and their temporal frame. Far from a sudden ninth-century composition by Prajñā's translation bureau, this architecture is the crystallization of five centuries of Indian and Chinese commentarial accumulation — from Nāgārjuna's threefold compound of offering in the *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*, through Vasubandhu's six greatnesses and nine-element cross-tabulation in the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, Fazang's sevenfold expansion in the *Tanxuan ji* together with his eighth-century precedent for the very term "Samantabhadra's vows and practices," and Chengguan's still-bifurcated reading of practice in form and practice in principle in the *Yanyi chao*, to the systematic coalescence in Chengguan's dedicated commentary on the *Xingyuanpin*. The principal Chinese commentarial wing (Chengguan) collapses material offering, contemplative practice, and dharma-offering into "practice as contemplative offering"; the secondary wing (Zongmi) reframes it as "vastness as the vastness of contemplative practice," grounding this in Fazang's third offering-gate. This entry-architecture lays the doctrinal groundwork for the following paper's deeper treatment of the dharma-offering as supreme among all offerings.

**Keywords:** *Xingyuanpin*, vast cultivation of offerings, the third vow, Samantabhadra's ten great vows, *Bhadracarīpraṇidhāna*, thought-after-thought continuity, contemplative offering, five centuries of accumulation, Chengguan, Zongmi, entry-architecture, Prajñā's translation bureau

## I. Introduction: What Is the Entry-Structure of the Samantabhadra Arena?

In his commentary on the Chapter of Samantabhadra's Vows of Practice, Chengguan lays down a single guiding sentence at the head of the ten great vows:

> "This gate of merit points back to the merit of the Buddha above. From 'what should be cultivated' onward, it directly shows the universal cause. To aspire and incline toward something is vow; to construct and cultivate is practice; borrowing the number of completion, it is expounded as ten."[^1]

What one aspires and inclines toward is vow; what one constructs and cultivates is practice; and the round number is borrowed to expound them as ten — within a single sentence, the three elements of "vow," "practice," and "ten" are already established. To a reader at home in the history of Chinese Buddhist exegesis, this is the most ordinary of analytic openings. But to the contemporary Western student of Huayan, this sentence falls precisely upon a familiar blind spot.

In Western scholarship, Samantabhadra's ten great vows are frequently read as a devotional appendix. The endlessly interpenetrating dharma-realm of Huayan having already been laid out beforehand, the *Xingyuanpin* is then taken as an affective coda of dedication: paying homage to the Buddhas, praising them, making offerings, repenting — a sequence of ritual gestures appended to the end of a metaphysical system. The third vow, "vast cultivation of offerings," is treated this way above all: seven cloud-categories of offering, three types of incense, lamp-wicks like mountains, lamp-oil like the sea — it reads like a grand display of material offering, seemingly nothing more than the rhetoric of religious feeling.

The question this paper raises is this: what kind of structure, in fact, is the entrance to the Samantabhadra arena?

The twenty-fourth paper of this volume has already treated the giving of the first ground, the Joyful Ground, of the Ten Bhūmis Chapter, establishing the threshold-structure of entry onto the bodhisattva grounds — with the arising of the mind as the door, and the first ground's ten great vows as the vow-skeleton of the bodhisattva's vow-power.[^2] That was the *threshold* of entry. The present paper takes up what follows: the second paper of the Huayan arena, the systematic unfolding of Samantabhadra's ten great vows. The third vow, vast cultivation of offerings, is the finest incision-point for testing the question — is Samantabhadra's practice-and-vow an appendix, or an architecture?

The central claim of this paper is that the full countenance of the third vow is constituted by three axes — the inventory axis of material offering, the magnitude axis, and the temporal axis — and that these three form an entry-architecture; and further, that this architecture is neither a passage native to the sixty- or eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, nor a sudden composition by Prajñā's translation bureau at the close of the ninth century, but the crystallization of five centuries of Indian and Chinese commentarial accumulation. Through the layered superimpositions of four generations — Nāgārjuna, Vasubandhu, Fazang, Chengguan — the architecture finally coalesced into a system in the prose of Prajñā's translation bureau. The principal wing of the Chinese commentarial tradition, Chengguan, gathers material offering, contemplative practice, and dharma-offering into "practice as contemplative offering"; the secondary wing, Zongmi, advances a step further, reframing it as "vastness as the vastness of contemplative practice." The entry of the three axes, and the gathering-in of the two wings — this is what the present paper sets out to lay open.

The boundary of this paper must first be drawn. The seven kinds of dharma-offering opened up by the ten-character verse declaring the dharma-offering supreme among all offerings, together with the side-attestation of doctrine-and-Chan unity that it draws in its train, belong to the territory of the next paper; the present paper sets down its brush at the transition verse and does not unfold the seven. With respect to the series of arguments running through Part VII and into Part VIII, the position of the present paper is this: after the "substitution" of the twenty-first paper, the "axiom" of the twenty-second, the "transformation" of the twenty-third, and the "architecture" of the twenty-fourth — what the twenty-fifth paper does is "unfolding": laying open in full the entry-architecture of the Samantabhadra arena, preparing the doctrinal ground for the deeper treatment of the dharma-offering that follows.

## II. Translation Layers and the Systematic Composition of "Vast Cultivation of Offerings"

To answer whether "vast cultivation of offerings" is an architecture, one must first face honestly a documentary fact: as a system, "vast cultivation of offerings" was not present from of old in the transmitted recensions of the *Avataṃsaka*.

The name of Samantabhadra and the practice of Samantabhadra appear early in the Chinese *Avataṃsaka*. Buddhabhadra's sixty-fascicle translation has a "Chapter of the Bodhisattva Samantabhadra's Practice," and Śikṣānanda's eighty-fascicle translation has a "Chapter on Samantabhadra's Practice," both in fascicle forty-nine. Yet a prose passage taking "vast cultivation of offerings" as its heading and the ten great vows as its enumerated skeleton is nowhere to be found in these two recensions — "vast cultivation of offerings" scores zero occurrences in the sixty-fascicle text, and zero occurrences in the eighty-fascicle text.[^3] The systematic ten-vow prose architecture is the unique composition of fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* translated by Prajñā, the "Chapter of Samantabhadra's Vows of Practice on Entering the Inconceivable Liberation-Realm." Here a boundary must be set: the Chapter on Samantabhadra's Practice (in fascicle forty-nine of the sixty- and eighty-fascicle texts) and the Chapter of Samantabhadra's Vows of Practice (in fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle text) are not the same textual stratum; the former contains no systematic ten-vow rubric, and the Samantabhadra-practice discussed in the Chapter on Samantabhadra's Practice must not be conflated into one system with the three-axis architecture of vast cultivation of offerings. The structural seed of the ten great vows finds its embryonic form only in the ten great vows uttered by the first-ground bodhisattva of the Ten Bhūmis Chapter — and that is already the territory of the preceding paper, passed over here with a single sentence.

Does it follow, then, that Prajñā's translation bureau is the inventor of the "vast cultivation of offerings" architecture out of nothing? Pushed to its extreme, this inference is equally inaccurate. The Sanskrit *Bhadracarīpraṇidhāna* does indeed exist, but it is principally a work in verse — the sixty-two verses collected in Watanabe's 1912 critical edition are all in the Rucirā meter, and the earliest Nepalese palm-leaf manuscripts can be traced back to the eleventh century. A verse corresponding to the third vow of vast offerings does have an Indian source. Yet the prose form of the third-vow passage has no parallel in the Sanskrit; the Chinese "by the power of Samantabhadra's vow of practice" corresponds to the Sanskrit *bhadracarī-praṇidhāna-vaśena* (an instrumental, with the Chinese adding a causal particle to make the causal force explicit), and "a mind of vast and profound conviction" corresponds to *adhimukti* (the word "mind" being a Chinese addition). More crucially, the recursive eight-character formula "thought after thought continuing without interruption," together with its three-clause seal "the actions of body, speech, and mind without weariness," has no corresponding fixed recursion in the Sanskrit verses — it is a syntactic creation of the prose of Prajñā's bureau. The same holds for the verse "among all offerings, the dharma-offering is supreme" and the "hundredth-part" comparative formula: their doctrinal content is rooted in the Sanskrit verses, but the explicit hierarchical ordering is unique to the forty-fascicle text.

The translation layers of the third vow of vast offerings must therefore be stated in two layers: the verse form has an Indian inheritance, while the systematic prose form is the redactional composition of an eighth-to-ninth-century Chinese translation bureau. This redaction is not a groundless fabrication. The *Xingyuanpin* had its Sanskrit folios presented to the throne in the eleventh year of the Zhenyuan era; translation opened at Chongfu Monastery in Chang'an in the twelfth year, and was completed in the fourteenth. Among the eleven members of the translation bureau, Yuanzhao took down the text, Zhirou and Zhitong handled the connective phrasing, Daohong and Jianxu polished the diction, and Daozhang collated and verified the meaning — while the office of "settling the final text" was held jointly by Chengguan and Xusui.[^4] In other words, the final redaction of the third-vow passage on vast offerings came about through Chengguan's own hand. The weight of this fact is this: the connection treated in the fifth section of this paper — between Chengguan's commentarial gloss on vast offerings in the *Xingyuanpin shu* and the sūtra-text itself — is not merely a commentator's interpretation of a pre-given text, but a final redactor's looking-back upon a text he himself helped to settle: a continuity at the level of the text, not merely at the level of commentary. This section marks honestly the textual footing of the present argument precisely so that what follows may stand its ground on solid foundations.

## III. The Three Axes of the Entry-Architecture of the Third Vow

To read the third vow not as "a display of material offering" but as "an entry-architecture," one must see how its three axes each perform their distinct office. The sūtra-text opens by establishing the scope of its arena:

> "Furthermore, son of good family! As for what is called the vast cultivation of offerings: throughout the entire realm of dharma and realm of space, in every mote of dust of all the Buddha-fields of the ten directions and three times, in each and every mote there are Buddhas numerous as the motes of dust of all worlds, and at the seat of each Buddha a varied ocean-assembly of bodhisattvas surrounds him; and I, by the power of Samantabhadra's vow of practice, give rise to profound conviction, to knowledge and vision made manifest before me, and with all the most excellent articles of offering I make offering."[^5]

The position of the third vow among the ten is fixed: after paying homage and after praising, before repenting and before rejoicing, it stands third.[^6] The three axes of the entry-architecture then unfold, axis by axis, after the clause "with all the most excellent articles of offering I make offering."

### III.1 The Inventory Axis: The Layered Structure of the Seven Clouds, Three Incenses, and Lamp-Oil

The first axis is the axis of material offering. The sūtra-text lists:

> "Namely: clouds of flowers, clouds of garlands, clouds of celestial music, clouds of celestial parasols and canopies, clouds of celestial garments, celestial incense of every kind, anointing-incense, burning-incense, powdered-incense — clouds such as these, each in magnitude like the king of mountains, Sumeru."[^7]

Flowers, garlands, celestial music, celestial parasols and canopies, celestial garments, and celestial incense of every kind — these six are bound to the word "cloud"; with anointing-incense, burning-incense, and powdered-incense added as three further types, these constitute the seven cloud-categories and three incense-types. There follows a passage on lamp-oil:

> "And I kindle lamps of every kind — butter-lamps, oil-lamps, lamps of every fragrant oil — each lamp-wick like Mount Sumeru, each lamp's oil like the waters of the great ocean; and with offering-articles such as these I make offering unceasingly."[^8]

Butter-lamps, oil-lamps, and lamps of fragrant oil — these three, following on from the prior seven clouds and three incenses, together form a layered inventory of twelve items. The function of the inventory axis lies not in the heaping-up of quantity but in the establishing of a set of offered-characteristics that can be brought under contemplation — it is the first support of entry: the practitioner must first have an object that can be taken as a contemplative referent before contemplative practice has anywhere to take hold.

### III.2 The Magnitude Axis: The Scalar Projection of Mount Sumeru and the Great Ocean

The second axis is the magnitude axis. The seven clouds and three incenses are "each in magnitude like the king of mountains, Sumeru"; the lamp-wicks are "like Mount Sumeru"; the lamp-oil is "like the waters of the great ocean" — the same finite set of material things is projected onto a cosmic scale. This projection is not mere hyperbole. Its function is to turn material offering from the finite volume of "a single censer, a single flower" into a magnitude commensurate with the dharma-realm: since the arena offered to pervades every mote of dust of all the Buddha-fields throughout the realm of dharma and the realm of space, the things by which one offers must likewise take Sumeru and the great ocean as their units of measure, so that the offered and the offering may answer to one another. What the magnitude axis establishes is the scalar correspondence between material offering and the dharma-realm.

### III.3 The Temporal Axis: Thought-after-Thought Continuity and the Recursive Four-Exhaustion Formula

The third axis is the temporal axis, and it is the one among the three most in need of careful discrimination. The third vow closes with a recursive formula:

> "When the realm of space is exhausted, when the realm of sentient beings is exhausted, when the karma of sentient beings is exhausted, when the afflictions of sentient beings are exhausted — then this vast and most excellent offering of mine would be exhausted. But because the realm of space, and so on to the afflictions, cannot be exhausted, this offering of mine likewise is without exhaustion: thought after thought continuing without interruption, the actions of body, speech, and mind without weariness."[^9]

The four — the realm of space, the realm of sentient beings, the karma of sentient beings, the afflictions of sentient beings — "being exhausted," then "my offering would be exhausted": this is the parallelism of the four-exhaustion formula. There follows the reversing clause, "but because [these] cannot be exhausted, this [offering] of mine is without exhaustion," which turns the finite "exhaustion" over into an inexhaustible continuity; and it seals with the three clauses "thought after thought continuing without interruption" and "the actions of body, speech, and mind without weariness." What the temporal axis establishes is the turning of offering from a one-time ritual gesture into a continuous practice unbroken thought after thought.

Here a discrimination must be made: the closed formula "thought after thought continuing without interruption" is not unique to the third vow but is a structure common to all ten great vows. The closing of the first vow, paying homage to all Buddhas, is identical in syntax:

> "When the realm of space is exhausted, my reverence would be exhausted; but because the realm of space cannot be exhausted, this reverence of mine has no end. So too, when the realm of sentient beings, the karma of sentient beings, the afflictions of sentient beings are exhausted, my reverence would be exhausted; but because the realm of sentient beings, and so on to the afflictions, cannot be exhausted, this reverence of mine has no end: thought after thought continuing without interruption, the actions of body, speech, and mind without weariness."[^10]

Plainly, "thought after thought continuing" is a recursive seal shared by all ten vows. To read it as a feature unique to the third vow is a misjudgment at the level of the text; what it truly marks is the temporal dimension of the entire set of ten great vows.

Beyond the three axes, the verse corresponding to the third vow gathers itself upon a single principal-axis statement:

> "With all the most excellent and marvelous flower-garlands, with music, anointing-incense, and parasols, with adornments most excellent such as these, I make offering to all the Tathāgatas. The most excellent garments, the most excellent incense, powdered-incense, burning-incense, and lamps and candles — each like the marvelous-peak heap — all of these I offer to all the Tathāgatas. With a mind of vast and profound conviction, deeply trusting all the Buddhas of the three times, all by the power of Samantabhadra's vow of practice, I make offering universally to all the Tathāgatas."[^11]

The "marvelous-peak heap" is the verse-form epithet for Mount Sumeru, answering to the magnitude axis of the prose. The final verse gathers the whole passage of offering upon two terms — the "mind of vast and profound conviction" (*adhimukti*) and the "power of Samantabhadra's vow of practice": the former is the vast opening-out of the mind that offers, the latter the operative vehicle of the practice that offers. Here one must keep doctrinal precision regarding the clause "by the power of Samantabhadra's vow of practice." The "power of Samantabhadra's vow of practice" is an operative vow-vehicle: it drives the contemplation of "giving rise to profound conviction, to knowledge and vision made manifest before me," so that material offering may reach universally to all the seats of the Buddhas throughout the dharma-realm. It is neither the spurring-on of a personal will, nor the reified "Samantabhadra vow-power" of later popular devotion — that reification is a process of popularization from the Tang and Song onward, and lies outside the doctrinal scope of the present paper. Holding to this measure, the three-axis architecture does not slide into the register of devotion.

The third vow closes here, upon the verse. The transition verse that immediately follows — declaring the dharma-offering supreme among all offerings,[^12] and supplying the comparative formula by which a single thought of dharma-offering surpasses a hundredfold the merit of immeasurable material offering[^13] — together with the sūtra's own gloss on why this is so,[^14] belongs to the territory of the next paper; the present paper does not unfold it.

## IV. Five Centuries of Indian and Early Huayan Accumulation

The three axes of the entry-architecture being clear, their provenance must be answered. The framework of this section is this: the architecture of the third vow of vast offerings is inherited from the twin wings of the Indian treatises, and then, through the crystallizing layer of early Huayan, coalesces into a system in Prajñā's translation bureau. The five-stage inheritance must be strung together by chronology — Nāgārjuna around the close of the third century, Vasubandhu around the fourth, Fazang around the close of the seventh, Chengguan's *Yanyi chao* around the late eighth century, and Chengguan's *Xingyuanpin shu* completing the final coalescence at the turn of the eighth and ninth centuries.

### IV.1 Nāgārjuna's Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā: The Threefold Compound of Offering

The earliest source for the vocabulary lies in the Chapter Explicating the Vows of Nāgārjuna's *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*. In glossing the offering-vow of the first-ground bodhisattva, the treatise breaks "offering" into a threefold compound:

> "I vow to make offerings, to render service, and to revere all Buddhas; I vow to guard and uphold the dharma of all Buddhas... 'Offering' names flowers and incense, necklaces, banners and canopies, lamps and lights, the raising of stūpas and shrines, and the like. 'Rendering service' names garments, bedding, and the things required. 'Reverence' names esteeming, paying homage, welcoming and seeing off, joining the palms and attending closely."[^15]

Offering, rendering service, and reverence — each of the three has its own particulars: offering is bound to flowers, incense, banners, canopies, lamps and lights; rendering service to garments and bedding; reverence to paying homage and attending closely. The vocabulary of the material items in the later first vow of homage and third vow of vast offerings — flower, incense, banner, canopy, lamp — can be found here in word-for-word origin. The Indian treatise of the late third century already possesses this inventory of the material items of offering.

### IV.2 Vasubandhu's Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna: The Six Greatnesses and the Nine-Element Cross-Tabulation

The second wing is Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*. In glossing the first great vow of the first ground, Vasubandhu systematizes offering twofold. The first is the nine-element cross-tabulation:

> "This first great vow, in its 'without remainder,' is of three kinds: first, all Buddhas without remainder; second, all offering without remainder; third, all reverence without remainder. As for all Buddhas, there are three kinds of Buddha: first, the transformation-body Buddha; second, the recompense-body Buddha; third, the dharma-body Buddha. As for all offering, there are three kinds of offering: first, the offering of sustenance — namely garments, bedding, and the like; second, the offering of reverence — namely incense, flowers, banners, canopies, and the like; third, the offering of practice — namely cultivating the practices of faith and moral discipline, and the like. As for all reverence, there are three kinds of reverence: first, the reverence of attendance; second, the reverence of welcoming and seeing off; third, the reverence of practice."[^16]

Three kinds of Buddha, three kinds of offering, three kinds of reverence, crossed into nine elements. Worthy of note is the item "the offering of practice" — "namely cultivating the practices of faith and moral discipline." In Vasubandhu, "practice" has already been listed as one category of offering, set alongside sustenance and reverence. This is the distant source of the later doctrine that gathers offering into "practice as contemplative offering." The second is the six greatnesses:

> "Within this first vow there are six kinds of greatness, named the great vow: first, the greatness of the field of merit...; second, the greatness of the matter offered...; third, the greatness of mind...; fourth, the greatness of the merit gathered...; fifth, the greatness of the cause...; sixth, the greatness of time."[^17]

Field of merit, matter offered, mind, merit gathered, cause, and time — all six are called "great." The offering-vow thereby acquires a systematic analytic skeleton. What Vasubandhu establishes is no longer merely an inventory of items but an architecture that can be analyzed.

### IV.3 Fazang's Tanxuan ji: The Expansion to Seven Greatnesses and the Lexical Precedent for "Samantabhadra's Vows of Practice"

The third is the crystallizing layer of early Huayan: Fazang's *Tanxuan ji*. Glossing the same vow, Fazang expands Vasubandhu's six greatnesses into seven greatnesses:

> "Within this vow, treated comprehensively, there are seven kinds of greatness: first, the greatness of the mind of offering...; second, the greatness of the field of merit...; third, the greatness of the matter offered...; fourth, the greatness of vow — namely, raising a vow in accordance with practice, as the sūtra says, 'he raises such a great vow,' and this is the arousing of the vow...; fifth, the greatness of the merit gathered...; sixth, the greatness of the cause...; seventh, the greatness of time."[^18]

Fazang's expansion is not merely an increase of one in number. The newly added "greatness of vow" — "raising a vow in accordance with practice" — reorganizes Vasubandhu's relatively static architectural analysis into a dynamic frame in which vow and practice mutually drive one another. Offering thereby ceases to be merely an object analyzed and becomes a dynamic process linked to vow and to practice. Moreover, fascicle eighteen of the *Tanxuan ji* carries a key lexical side-attestation:

> "Sixth, one must exhort [the practitioner] to become fully endowed with Samantabhadra's vows and practices — only then is one named an ultimate, true good friend."[^19]

The term "Samantabhadra's vows and practices" is already in use by Fazang in the eighth century. This demonstrates that "Samantabhadra's vows of practice" is not a sudden new coinage of Prajñā's ninth-century translation bureau; the lexical precedent was long since prepared in Fazang.

### IV.4 Chengguan's Yanyi chao: The Still-Unsystematized Bifurcation of Practice in Form and Practice in Principle

The fourth stage is a discontinuity that must be carefully discriminated. Chengguan's *Yanyi chao* is his auto-commentary upon his own *Commentary on the Avataṃsaka-sūtra* (a commentary on the eighty-fascicle text), and it does not overlap with fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle text. In glossing the Chapter on Samantabhadra's Practice, Chengguan's handling of "Samantabhadra's practice" is as follows:

> "Now, Samantabhadra's practice... many sūtras carry its name... The Lotus Sūtra says, 'If there is one who receives and upholds, reads and recites, rightly recollects, understands its meaning and purport, and practices as taught — know that this person performs Samantabhadra's practice.' To explain: here the name is present but the characteristic is still hidden — receiving and upholding, reading and reciting, are practice in form; rightly recollecting penetrates to wisdom, and that is practice in principle. The Sūtra on Contemplating Samantabhadra's Practice — there too, upholding the sūtra, paying homage and repenting, and seated meditation are all its practice."[^20]

Here Chengguan still expresses Samantabhadra's practice through the bifurcation of "practice in form / practice in principle," and refers loosely to its practice through upholding the sūtra, paying homage and repenting, and seated meditation — he has not yet systematized Samantabhadra's practice into the rubric-framework of the ten great vows. In other words, at the stage of the *Yanyi chao*, Chengguan's own ordering of Samantabhadra's practice still rests at the level of a bifurcation. The point at which the systematic ten-vow framework emerges is the *Xingyuanpin shu* that Chengguan composed afterward — a commentary dedicated to fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle text. The five-century accumulation only here reaches its coalescence; and the final hand of that coalescence falls precisely upon the gap between Chengguan's own two works.

## V. The Two Wings of Chinese Commentary, the Madhyamaka Correction, and Dialogue with Western Scholarship

The two dedicated commentaries on fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle text constitute the principal and secondary wings of the Chinese understanding of vast offerings. The principal wing is Chengguan's *Xingyuanpin shu*; the secondary wing is the *Xingyuanpin shuchao*, which Zongmi composed upon Chengguan's separately-circulating commentary — the subcommentary form being itself the second layer of commentary. The boundary of the present paper must first be restated: the item-by-item unfolding of the seven kinds of dharma-offering, and the side-attestation of doctrine-and-Chan unity, both belong to the territory of the next paper; this section sets down its brush at the transition verse.

### V.1 The Principal Wing: Chengguan's Gathering of Material, Contemplative, and Dharma-Offering in the Xingyuanpin shu

Chengguan's gloss on vast offerings first establishes the overall framework of vow-and-practice — the sentence quoted above, "to aspire and incline toward something is vow; to construct and cultivate is practice; borrowing the number of completion, it is expounded as ten," is this.[^1] He then traces the lineage of the ten-vow rubric:

> "Second, from 'what are the ten' onward, it inquires into and enumerates the names and number. Now, this is simply the constant practice of homage and repentance, which the various sūtras and treatises divide and combine differently... the present text gives ten, in order to signify inexhaustibility."[^21]

The various sūtras and treatises "divide and combine differently" the rubrics of homage and repentance — into eight, or seven, or six, or five. Chengguan does not maintain that "ten" is the one correct number; he says, "the present text gives ten, in order to signify inexhaustibility": the forty-fascicle text's adoption of "ten" is rhetorical, taken for its power to signify the inexhaustible. This in itself is a correction of the assumption that "the ten great vows were ten from of old."

Coming to the passage on vast offerings, Chengguan makes a fourfold division:

> "Third, 'furthermore,' it expounds at length the cultivation of offerings. First, it sets out the name. Second, from 'throughout the entire realm of dharma' onward, it explains the characteristic. Within this, two parts: first it directly clarifies the practice of offering, then it weighs and compares to reveal its excellence. Within the former, four: first, the object offered to; second, from 'I, by [the power of] Samantabhadra,' the cause of the one who offers; third, from 'with all,' the listing of the articles offered; fourth, from 'with such as these,' the direct clarification of the offering."[^22]

The object offered to, the cause of the one who offers, the articles offered, the direct clarification of the offering — this division in itself already reads the third vow as a structured practice of offering rather than as an affective display. But Chengguan's true doctrinal center lies in the passage where he cites the "Sanskrit text" to weigh the comparative structure:

> "Hence the Sanskrit text says: 'Furthermore, son of good family! Each and every most excellent offering, and the dharma-offering, and the offering of practice... in a single thought, by each and every offering to the Tathāgata one has already attained the most excellent roots of goodness; set against the augmenting and accumulating of past offerings, those roots of goodness do not amount to a hundredth part,' and so on. The meaning here is this: the contemplative practice mentioned above is named the most excellent offering and the dharma-offering. Of these two categories, the roots of goodness of a single thought are what is weighed, past offerings are that against which it is weighed; for offering without yet cultivating Samantabhadra's vows and practices does not match offering that now cultivates Samantabhadra's contemplative practice. Moreover, the dharma-offering pervades the cultivation of the ten dharma-practices, and the most excellent cultivation pervades the myriad practices: offering grounded in contemplation is precisely cultivation. Hence in the Lotus, the bodhisattva Gladly-Seen's burning of his body is named a dharma-offering; in the Vimalakīrti, the teaching to practice giving — giving equally, all at once, with nothing before and nothing after — is named a dharma-offering. Thus, if material giving can accord with the dharma, all of it is dharma-offering; how much more so profound contemplation — how could that not be dharma-offering? The wise should ponder this."[^23]

The "Sanskrit text" that Chengguan cites here must be carefully discriminated as to its referent: it is a Sanskrit collation copy in the possession of Prajñā's translation bureau in its own time, and it should not be equated with the modern critically reconstructed *Bhadracarīpraṇidhāna*. Chengguan cites this collation copy precisely to show that the structure of the comparison is not a binary opposition of "material offering versus dharma-offering," but rather that "the contemplative practice mentioned above is named the most excellent offering and the dharma-offering" — contemplative practice has gathered into itself all most excellent offering and all dharma-offering. His concluding statement, "if material giving can accord with the dharma, all of it is dharma-offering," gathers the three — material offering, contemplative practice, dharma-offering — into one: so long as material giving can accord with principle, material giving is itself dharma-offering; how much more so profound contemplation. Chengguan's gathering-in is the doctrinal center of the principal Chinese wing — it folds the third vow back, all the way from "the inventory axis of material offering" to "practice as contemplative offering." As for the clause "thought after thought continuing," Chengguan's literal gloss is of the briefest:

> "Second, from 'thought after thought continuing' onward, it makes manifest the absence of interruption. 'Weary' means fatigued; 'averse' means sated. The nine gates below — their inexhaustibility follows this same pattern."[^24]

"The nine gates below — their inexhaustibility follows this same pattern" — Chengguan states plainly that this clause is the general rule for the nine vows that follow, in complete accord with the comparison drawn against the first vow in the third section.

### V.2 The Secondary Wing: Zongmi's Vastness of Contemplative Practice in the Xingyuanpin shuchao

Zongmi's subcommentary advances a further layer upon Chengguan's gathering-in. His approach has three moves. First, in fascicle three, Zongmi cites word for word the third offering-gate of Fazang's *Treatise on Conditioned Arising*:

> "Subcommentary: on 'with such as these... makes offering.' How can one furnish offering-articles as many as these? How can one make offering constantly to Buddhas as many as those above? Following what the venerable Zang sets out in the third offering-gate of his Treatise on Conditioned Arising: whatever the bodhisattva sets forth — down to a single incense, a single flower, a single garment, a single canopy, a single offering-article — none fails to accord with true principle, equal to the realm of space. That is, with a body wholly of dharma he travels to the Buddha-fields, and with things that accord with the true he makes offering to the Buddhas. Therefore the bodhisattva does not practice in vain what he cultivates; he constantly meets the Buddhas, never losing the occasion, and all offering-articles are constantly accomplished in accordance with principle. Why? Because dharma-giving to the Buddhas accords with true principle."[^25]

This passage of Fazang's *Treatise on Conditioned Arising* reconstructs the whole course of material offering as "the dharma-giving that accords with true principle": a single incense, a single flower — none fails to accord with true principle; with a body wholly of dharma one travels to the Buddha-fields, and the things offered are "things that accord with the true." And the reason it is so is precisely that "dharma-giving to the Buddhas accords with true principle." The boundary between material offering and dharma-giving is here run through by the single sense of "according with true principle." Zongmi cites this as warrant, and thereby joins the principal and secondary wings of the Chinese commentarial tradition to the explicit lineage of Fazang.

Second, after the citation Zongmi adds a sentence of his own that folds the matter back:

> "To explain: following this — the saying of 'a single incense, a single flower,' and the saying 'constantly accomplished in accordance with principle' — one knows that what the sūtra lists is all by way of according with principle, the many-within-one and the like. The practitioner need only settle the mind in contemplative practice, and thing upon thing is of itself many, each pervading all Buddhas. Hence the customary saying: 'May this cloud of incense and flowers fill the realms of the ten directions and make offering to all Buddhas.'"[^26]

"The practitioner need only settle the mind in contemplative practice, and thing upon thing is of itself many, each pervading all Buddhas" — the phrase "settle the mind in contemplative practice" turns the subject of offering away from "how many offering-articles one can furnish" and back to the practitioner's own contemplation; so long as the mind is settled in contemplative practice, a single thing of itself reaches universally to all the seats of the Buddhas. The word "settle the mind" implicitly joins to the mind-ground teaching, and this implicit thread of doctrine-and-Chan unity is here touched upon only in passing; its explicit unfolding belongs to the territory of the next paper.

Third, Zongmi reconstructs the "vast" of "vast cultivation of offerings" directly as "the vastness of contemplative practice." His subcommentary, glossing the general summation of inexhaustibility, says:

> "Commentary: third, the general summation of inexhaustibility. 'When this vast and most excellent offering — when the realm of space is exhausted, the realm of sentient beings is exhausted, the karma of sentient beings is exhausted, the afflictions of sentient beings are exhausted — then my offering would be exhausted'... This most excellent offering is the very contemplative practice [spoken of] before. If this most excellent [offering] did not reach as far as the dharma, how could it be inexhaustible?"[^27]

"This most excellent offering is the very contemplative practice before" — the "vast" of "vast cultivation of offerings" is not the vastness of material quantity but the vastness of contemplative practice, the vastness of the subject's contemplation reaching through the dharma-realm layer upon layer without end. Zongmi's conditioned-arising gloss on the "clouds of flowers, clouds of garlands" carries this sense down even into the offering-articles themselves:

> "As for being conditioned arising: these offering-articles — their form and characteristic are evident, yet wisdom discerns them as without own-nature; from the emptiness of dharma-nature, from the unborn dharma, they arise — that which manifests and that which is manifested. That which manifests is the vow-and-practice; that which is manifested is the offering-articles. Utterly without anything to depend upon, they come in response to use; coming, they come from nowhere; their use spent, they depart; departing, they go nowhere. Yet they are able to hold compassion within, to pour down the dharma-rain in moistening flow, to benefit and rescue the myriad things, layer upon layer without end — and therefore there is the image of clouds."[^28]

The offering-articles are bound to the word "cloud" precisely because that which manifests and that which is manifested are both without anything to depend upon: that which manifests is the vow-and-practice, that which is manifested is the offering-articles, and both arise from the emptiness of dharma-nature, from the unborn dharma. The image of the "cloud" is the manifestation, upon the characteristic of material offering, of the vastness of contemplative practice.

### V.3 The Two-Layer Frame of "Thought after Thought Continuing"

The clause "thought after thought continuing without interruption" can now be understood in two layers. The first is the layer of the sūtra-text itself: as discriminated in the third section, it is a closed formula common to all ten great vows, marking the temporal dimension of the entire set of vow-and-practice. The second is the normative reading Zongmi adds. Glossing this clause in the section on paying homage, Zongmi takes it as the general rule for the nine vows that follow:

> "Second, on 'thought after thought continuing... follows this same pattern': if not by the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice, how could one attain thought after thought without interruption? Let the wise carefully ponder this text: do not, with a mind of arising-and-perishing and a homage that grasps at characteristics, take homage as the pattern for the nine gates below; nor, with praise, offering, and even dedication that arise-and-perish and grasp at characteristics, take these as Samantabhadra's vows and practices."[^29]

"If not by the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice, how could one attain thought after thought without interruption?" — Zongmi reads "thought after thought continuing" not as a descriptive formula but as a normative criterion: that which can make offering continue thought after thought without interruption is the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice alone; offering performed with a mind of arising-and-perishing and a homage that grasps at characteristics does not deserve to be called Samantabhadra's vows of practice. The juxtaposition of the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice against the mind of arising-and-perishing that grasps at characteristics is the doctrinal core of this clause. The further unfolding of this juxtaposition belongs to the principal arena of the next paper, and is not elaborated here.

### V.4 The Three Anchors of the Madhyamaka Correction

Three points in the present paper call for correction.

First, to read the third vow as a binary opposition of "material offering versus dharma-offering" is a common misunderstanding. The patriarchs of both the principal and secondary wings doubly negate this reading: Chengguan's "the contemplative practice mentioned above is named the most excellent offering and the dharma-offering" and "if material giving can accord with the dharma, all of it is dharma-offering"; Zongmi's "the practitioner need only settle the mind in contemplative practice" and "this most excellent offering is the very contemplative practice before" — what both masters do is to gather the three, material offering, contemplative practice, and dharma-offering, into one, not to set material offering and dharma-offering in opposition and rank them high and low. The doctrinal move of the third vow is a collapse into one, not a partition. To frame this first correction as a hierarchy of material against dharma offering is to miss the central architectural insight of the paper.

Second, to simplify the architecture of vast offerings into the single-handed achievement of "Chengguan the great synthesizer" is also inaccurate. Chengguan's *Xingyuanpin shu* is indeed the principal-wing commentary on fascicle forty of the forty-fascicle text, but two points must be noted: first, within the passage on vast offerings Chengguan does not cite Fazang by name (although elsewhere in the *Xingyuanpin shu* he does cite Xianshou by name on more than five occasions); second, as seen in the fourth section, Chengguan had still not, at the stage of the *Yanyi chao*, systematized Samantabhadra's practice into the ten-vow framework. The claim of "Chengguan the great synthesizer" must be qualified within measure — the point of emergence of systematization is the *Xingyuanpin shu* itself, which cannot be inferred backward into "Chengguan accomplished it single-handedly," still less projected backward onto the *Yanyi chao*.

Third, to call the architecture of vast offerings "a sudden composition by Prajñā's translation bureau in the ninth century" is the reverse one-sidedness. As the five stages of the fourth section show, this is an accumulation of five centuries. But the balance must run both ways: it is equally one-sided to maintain that "the Sanskrit prose was already thus" — the systematic prose is indeed the redaction of a Tang translation bureau. The accurate statement is two-layered: the verse form has an Indian inheritance; the systematic prose form is the composition of a Chinese translation bureau.

### V.5 Dialogue with Western Scholarship

Anglophone scholarship's handling of Samantabhadra's offering may be charted by three coordinates of dialogue.

Park Sung-bae brought the framework of "the unity of faith, understanding, practice, and verification" from the Korean Hwaŏm tradition — the lineage of Wŏnhyo and Ūisang — into Anglophone Huayan studies, maintaining that the distinctive feature of Huayan lies not in a metaphysical cosmology but in the unity of "faith is itself cultivation, understanding is itself practice." It must be conceded that Park's Korean-Hwaŏm vantage correctly rejects the tendency to read Samantabhadra's vows of practice as a purely devotional appendix, and points toward the inner unity of "vow" and "practice" — and it is precisely upon this foundation that the present paper establishes vast offerings as an entry-architecture rather than a ritual supplement. It should be said that Park's Korean-Hwaŏm vantage is a conscious scholarly choice, with its own disciplinary necessity, an inner reading of the Korean tradition; the present paper's supplement is a disciplinary complement, not a correction. His Korean-inheritance framework enters chiefly through Ūisang's dharma-realm diagram of "one is all"; the architectural detail of the Chinese commentarial lineage — Chengguan's *Xingyuanpin shu* and Zongmi's *Xingyuanpin shuchao*, with their gathering of material offering, contemplative practice, and dharma-offering — it captures more thinly. What the present paper supplies back is precisely this layer of architecture.

Peter N. Gregory's monograph on Zongmi is the standard Anglophone reference on Guifeng Zongmi, its center of gravity resting on Zongmi's doctrine-and-Chan unity and the process of the sinification of Chinese Buddhism. It must be conceded that Gregory correctly positions Zongmi's *Xingyuanpin shuchao* as a conscious extension of the Fazang lineage — Zongmi's word-for-word citation, within the passage on vast offerings, of the third offering-gate of Fazang's *Treatise on Conditioned Arising* confirms exactly this positioning. The present paper builds upon Gregory's lineage-framework, affirming the principal-and-secondary two-wing structure of the Chinese commentarial tradition. Yet Gregory's sinification-framework captures more thinly the doctrinal move proper to the passage on vast offerings — the gathering-in of "material offering → contemplative practice → dharma-offering" that is internal to the commentary; this is a necessary trade-off of his monograph's macroscopic scale. What the present paper supplies back is precisely this layer of architectural movement.

Robert M. Gimello is a senior Anglophone voice on Huayan, and his opening-up of a reading of Li Tongxuan's "practical Huayan" — the preceding paper has already cross-cited Li Tongxuan's reading of "taking Samantabhadra's practice as the Saṅgha-jewel," a reading of lay immediacy — provides a theoretical anchor for the "practice / application" dimension of Western Huayan. It must be conceded that Gimello's "practical Huayan" framework correctly rejects reading Huayan as a purely metaphysical, purely cosmological system, and furnishes a Western scholarly footing for the "architecture is itself practice" reading of vast offerings. Yet Gimello's treatment concentrates chiefly on the dimension of lay immediacy in Li Tongxuan and Zongmi, and dwells more lightly upon the doctrinal centrality of Chengguan's *Xingyuanpin shu*, the commentary dedicated to the *Xingyuanpin*. What the present paper supplies back is this framework centered upon Chengguan's *Xingyuanpin shu*.

## VI. Conclusion: The Completion of the Samantabhadra Unfolding and What Follows

The central findings of this paper may be restated in three sentences. First, the third of Samantabhadra's ten great vows, vast cultivation of offerings, is an entry-architecture constituted by the inventory axis of material offering, the magnitude axis, and the temporal axis — not an affective display of material offering. Second, this architecture is the crystallization of five centuries of Indian and Chinese commentarial accumulation — Nāgārjuna's threefold compound of offering, Vasubandhu's six greatnesses and nine-element cross-tabulation, Fazang's sevenfold expansion and his lexical precedent for "Samantabhadra's vows of practice," Chengguan's still-surviving bifurcation of practice in form and practice in principle in the *Yanyi chao*, finally reaching the systematic coalescence of Chengguan's *Xingyuanpin shu*. Third, the principal Chinese wing, Chengguan, gathers material offering, contemplative practice, and dharma-offering into "practice as contemplative offering," while the secondary wing, Zongmi, reconstructs it as "vastness as the vastness of contemplative practice." With respect to the series of arguments: after the "substitution" of the twenty-first paper, the "axiom" of the twenty-second, the "transformation" of the twenty-third, and the "architecture" of the twenty-fourth, what the present paper completes is the "unfolding" — laying open in full the entry-architecture of the Samantabhadra arena.

For practice, this sense carries a concrete direction. The entrance of the third vow lies not in how many offering-articles one can furnish, nor in how great those articles are; what the gathering-in doctrine of the two wings makes plain is that the place where offering takes hold is the practitioner's own settling of the mind in contemplative practice. The characteristic of material offering is an object that can be taken as a contemplative referent; the greatness of magnitude is the scale commensurate with the dharma-realm; thought-after-thought continuity is the unbroken continuous practice — and what the three axes jointly point toward is that "offering grounded in contemplation is precisely cultivation." That offering is an entry lies precisely in this: it requires the practitioner, at the very first step, to read material offering as contemplative practice, rather than reserving contemplative practice for after material offering.

One question is left for what follows. The two-layer frame of "thought after thought continuing without interruption" — the closed formula of the sūtra-text itself, and Zongmi's normative reading, "if not by the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice, how could one attain thought after thought without interruption" — the present paper touches upon only in passing; the juxtaposition of the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice against the mind of arising-and-perishing that grasps at characteristics is the principal arena of the next paper. The next paper will treat the dharma-offering as supreme among all offerings: the item-by-item unfolding of the seven kinds of dharma-offering, the doctrinal depth of the "hundredth-part" comparative formula, and the entrance to the side-attestation of doctrine-and-Chan unity — that is the third paper of the Samantabhadra arena's unfolding, the deeper treatment of the dharma-offering. Thereafter, the twenty-seventh paper will follow Sudhana's pilgrimage to fifty-three teachers, the transition by which he is received by Mañjuśrī and enters Samantabhadra's arena — and here a discrimination must be made: the terminus of the fifty-three visits is the transition in which Mañjuśrī delivers Sudhana into Samantabhadra's arena, not a climactic close in which Samantabhadra there proclaims the ten great vows; the systematic ten great vows still belong to the redaction of Prajñā's ninth-century translation bureau. The twenty-eighth paper will then close the whole of Part VIII with the Entry into the Dharma-Realm Chapter. That the present paper sets down its brush at the transition verse is precisely to leave the interface open for these three papers.

[^26]: Ibid., *Xingyuanpin shuchao*, fascicle 3: "To explain: following this — the saying of 'a single incense, a single flower'... 'May this cloud of incense and flowers fill the realms of the ten directions and make offering to all Buddhas.'" *Manji 續藏* (X), vol. 5, No. X0229, p. 0271c.

[^27]: Ibid., *Xingyuanpin shuchao*, fascicle 3: "Commentary: third, the general summation of inexhaustibility... This most excellent offering is the very contemplative practice [spoken of] before. If this most excellent [offering] did not reach as far as the dharma, how could it be inexhaustible?" *Manji 續藏* (X), vol. 5, No. X0229, p. 0274b.

[^28]: Ibid., *Xingyuanpin shuchao*, fascicle 3: "As for being conditioned arising: these offering-articles, their form and characteristic are evident... layer upon layer without end — and therefore there is the image of clouds." *Manji 續藏* (X), vol. 5, No. X0229, p. 0271b.

[^29]: Ibid., *Xingyuanpin shuchao*, fascicle 3: "Second, on 'thought after thought continuing... follows this same pattern': if not by the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice, how could one attain thought after thought without interruption?... nor, with praise, offering, and even dedication that arise-and-perish and grasp at characteristics, take these as Samantabhadra's vows and practices." *Manji 續藏* (X), vol. 5, No. X0229, p. 0269b. Zongmi glosses this clause in the section on paying homage, taking it as the general rule for the nine vows that follow.
