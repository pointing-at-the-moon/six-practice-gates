# Entering the Dharmadhātu through Giving

**Dharma-Realm, Infinite Interpenetration, and Samantabhadra's Self-Disclosure**

## Abstract

Western scholarship has tended to read the Huayan *dharmadhātu* as an abstract metaphysical end-point — and giving, as a concrete practice, seems on this picture to be left behind once one ascends to the contemplation of the dharma-realm. This paper asks what the destination of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" (*Gaṇḍavyūha*), the closing chapter of the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* as a whole, in fact is. Taking the autobiographical self-disclosure of *Samantabhadra* in fascicle eighty of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0279, *Śikṣānanda*) as its load-bearing textual anchor, cross-confirmed by the corresponding passage in the sixty-fascicle text (T0278, *Buddhabhadra*, fifth century), and taking as its architectural anchor the gate of "pure-and-mixed both fully endowed" in *Fazang*'s *Huayan yicheng jiaoyi fenqi zhang* (T1866) together with the formula "one act of giving gathers all practices" in his *Jin shizi zhang* (T1881), the paper enters into dialogue with three Western readings — Steve Odin, Brook Ziporyn, and Garma C. C. Chang. Its central finding is that the destination of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is not where giving is superseded but where giving takes its final form. Samantabhadra's sealing line, "in all my ocean of dharma, there is not one letter, not one phrase, that I have not acquired by relinquishing the position of universal monarch," states the equation between dharma and giving in the plainest terms: the dharma-realm is an architecture borne by the act of practice, not a quietist metaphysics, and its structure is precisely "one act of giving IS all practices; all practices ARE this single act of giving."

**Keywords:** Entry into the Dharma-Realm, *dharmadhātu*, infinite interpenetration, *Samantabhadra*, autobiographical self-disclosure, relinquishing-and-giving, pure-and-mixed gate, one act of giving gathers all practices, four dharma-realms, mutual non-obstruction between event and event, ten profound gates, dependent origination, *Avataṃsaka-sūtra*, *Gaṇḍavyūha*, process metaphysics, intersubsumption, destination, *Sudhana*

## I. Introduction: The Dharma-Realm Is Not an Abstract End-Point

> In all my ocean of dharma, there is not one letter, not one phrase, that I have not acquired by relinquishing the position of universal monarch [and giving it away]; nor is there one letter, one phrase, that I have not acquired by giving away all that I possessed.[^1]

This is the voice of *Samantabhadra*, sounding at the close of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" chapter — which is itself the close of the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* as a whole. *Sudhana* the youth has finished his pilgrimage of fifty-three visits; he has arrived. When he enters Samantabhadra's body, and Samantabhadra opens to disclose his own original mark, what is spoken is not an abstract metaphysical pronouncement but this: in this "ocean of dharma" of mine, there is not one letter, not one phrase, that was not acquired through relinquishing-and-giving.

The Western reception of the Huayan dharma-realm tends to read it as the apex of a grand metaphysics — mutual non-obstruction between event and event, Indra's Net, perfect interfusion without end — while giving, this plain practice at the head of the six perfections, that concrete act of offering a single coin or a single meal, seems to belong to a lower, preparatory stage that one leaves behind once the contemplation of the dharma-realm has been ascended to. The implicit picture is a staircase: giving at the base, dharma-realm metaphysics at the summit.

What this paper performs is a mirror move: the destination at the close of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is not where giving is left behind or superseded — it is where giving takes its final form. The *dharmadhātu*, with its infinite interpenetration, is not an abstraction perched above giving; it is the architectural unfolding of giving itself. Samantabhadra's sealing line states the equation in plain terms: dharma IS giving.

This paper is the fifth and final piece in the Part VIII sequence on "Samantabhadra's principal arena" within the present volume. Each of the preceding four papers handled a different angle: P24 took up Indra's Net and the Ground of Joy as the entry-gate of the Ten Grounds; P25 set out the three-axis architecture of Samantabhadra's third vow of vast cultivation of offerings; P26 deep-mined the seven kinds of dharma-offering, anchoring the explicit textual seam between doctrinal teaching and meditative practice; P27 traced the recurring giving-thread through *Sudhana*'s fifty-three visits. P27 followed the trajectory; the present paper, P28, stands at the trajectory's destination and asks what that destination *is*. Its claim is that the answer is given by the sūtra itself, through Samantabhadra's mouth: the road's destination is the road's action, only now fully unfolded.

A brief note on opening posture. P25, P26, and P27 each opened with a commentary's "first taste" — a *Chengguan* or *Fazang* threshold introducing the chapter at hand. P28 does not. It opens with the sūtra's own sealing voice, because P28 is the destination-move: standing already on the destination, the proper voice to speak first is the sūtra's own closing line, not a commentary's portal.

One discipline must be set down at the start (this is also one of the paper's middle-way correctives): the present paper will not take the *dharmadhātu* as an "abstract metaphysics" available for quietist contemplation. The proportion sustained throughout is one held close to "the textual form of giving in the dharma-realm," not allowed to drift into general Huayan metaphysics in the abstract.

## II. The Recensions and the Dharma-Realm as a Huayan-Internal Innovation

### 2.1 The non-uniformity of the three recensions' closings

The "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is preserved in three recension systems whose corresponding closing passages do not agree: the sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0278, translated by *Buddhabhadra*, 418–420 CE), the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0279, translated by *Śikṣānanda*, 695–699 CE), and the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0293, translated by *Prajñā*, 795–798 CE). Only T0293 carries a formal closing in the standard "all rejoiced and received it in faith" form:

> When at that time the World-Honoured One, together with the noble bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, had expounded this inconceivable supreme dharma-gate of the realm of liberation … hearing what the Buddha had spoken, all rejoiced and received it in faith and practiced accordingly.[^2]

T0278 and T0279 both close in verse, culminating in an equation-sealing line — in the eighty-fascicle text, "equal to Samantabhadra, equal to all Buddhas":

> … in due order he attained Samantabhadra's ocean of bodhisattva-practices-and-vows, equal to Samantabhadra, equal to all Buddhas …[^3]

This is a fact worth taking seriously. The "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is itself the close of this entire scriptural corpus — no further chapter follows — and the manner of its closing (an equation-seal rather than a formal "received in faith and practiced" recessional) already discloses that the destination here is not the closing of a book but an equation: Sudhana equal to Samantabhadra, equal to all Buddhas. Because the fascicle-eighty closing of the eighty-fascicle version preserves Samantabhadra's autobiographical disclosure — the paper's load-bearing anchor — the present paper takes the eighty-fascicle text as its base.

### 2.2 "Infinite interpenetration" is a commentary creation, not the sūtra's wording

A negative finding must be stated plainly: the phrase 重重無盡 ("infinite interpenetration," "endless layered interpenetration"), the locution that Western and Chinese readers most readily associate with the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" and with the *Avataṃsaka* corpus, does *not* appear in the sūtra text of the chapter itself. A string-search across the chapter returns zero hits. The phrase is a creation of the commentarial tradition — Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* and *Huayan yicheng jiaoyi fenqi zhang* (henceforth *Wujiao zhang*), Chengguan's *Huayan jing shu* — articulated in order to interpret the sūtra's recurrent prose patterns ("equal to the space-realm," "pervading all the seas of buddha-fields," "without bound, without measure").

Wherever this paper uses the phrase "infinite interpenetration" or "endless interpenetration," it does so as a commentary term of art, not as a sūtra citation. This distinction is not pedantry: it tells us that the *systematic* form of the dharma-realm is itself an achievement of the commentary tradition, built upon the narrative prose of the sūtra. The sūtra does the action; the commentaries name the structure.

### 2.3 The dharma-realm as a Huayan-internal doctrinal innovation

A standard assumption holds that *dharmadhātu* is a concept Huayan inherited from the Prajñā corpus. Cross-canonical philological survey shows the assumption to be mistaken; the doctrinal referent of 法界 across the corpora differs substantially. In the Prajñā literature (the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra*, T0223: three occurrences; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, T1509: five occurrences; the *Vajracchedikā*: zero), 法界 occurs in the eighteen-element classification — "the realm of mental objects" — as one item in the abhidharmic enumeration. The *Lotus Sūtra* (T0262) uses 法界 only once, in a descriptive verse-line. The *Śūraṅgama* (T0945) uses it twenty-six times, split between a *Tathāgatagarbha*-pervading-dharma-realm sense (fascicle four) and a residual eighteen-element use (fascicle three) — a *Tathāgatagarbha* framing, distinct again from Huayan's. By contrast, the Huayan corpus (T0279: numerous; *Li Tongxuan*'s *Xin Huayan jing lun*, T1739: roughly eight hundred and ninety occurrences) deploys 法界 in the architectural-metaphysical sense of *pratītyasamutpāda* and nature-origination.

The Huayan *dharmadhātu*, then, is not the Prajñā *dharmadhātu* with additional ornament. It is a doctrinally internal innovation: the dharma-realm as an architecture of *pratītyasamutpāda* and nature-origination, not an abhidharmic category-slot, nor the *Tathāgatagarbha*'s pervasive enclosure. This is the strongest textual evidence that Huayan stands apart from the Prajñā lineage — and it matters for the present paper because it means: the "final form" of giving unfolds within a specifically Huayan architecture, not within a general Mahāyāna emptiness-space.

### 2.4 The "giving → dharma-realm" linkage is already declared within the sūtra (Ten Dedications)

The decisive point is this: the link from giving to the dharma-realm is neither a commentary imposition nor an innovation that the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" first performs by way of Sudhana's pilgrimage. It is declared in the sūtra's own voice, well before the "Entry" chapter begins, within the Ten Dedications chapter:

> May all sentient beings dwell in pure giving and gather merit equal to the dharma-realm without measure … may all sentient beings perfect the practice of true giving and gather merit equal to the dharma-realm without measure.[^4]

> By dwelling-in-the-dharma-realm immeasurable dedication … by equal-to-the-dharma-realm immeasurable dedication … by pervading-the-dharma-realm immeasurable dedication.[^5]

"Dwelling in the dharma-realm, equal to the dharma-realm, pervading the dharma-realm" — three verbal forms that place the dharma-realm as the destination of pure giving in plain, declarative form. The sūtra's architectural progression therefore runs: Ten Grounds (P24 territory) → Ten Dedications (the explicit "giving → dharma-realm" declaration here) → "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" narrative (P27 territory, Sudhana's dramatized pilgrimage) → "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" closing (the present paper's territory, Samantabhadra's autobiographical disclosure). The Ten Dedications anchor is *internal to the sūtra*, not a commentary articulation; this matters for the textual standing of the paper's thesis.

## III. The Systematic of the Dharma-Realm: Four Dharma-Realms, Ten Profound Gates, Mutual Non-Obstruction Between Event and Event

This section sets up the dharma-realm's *architecture* — its form — so that §IV can place the *content* (giving) within it.

### 3.1 Chengguan's Four Dharma-Realms (not Dushun's)

The Chinese tradition, and a sizeable portion of Western scholarship — partly because of Cleary's translations — habitually attributes the "Four Dharma-Realms" to *Dushun* (557–640). ⚠️ This is a misattribution. Dushun's *Huayan fajie guanmen* (*The Contemplation-Gate of the Huayan Dharma-Realm*) sets out the *three contemplations* — the contemplation of true emptiness, the contemplation of non-obstruction between principle and event, and the contemplation of pervasion-and-containment. The systematic articulation of the Four Dharma-Realms (the event-dharma-realm, the principle-dharma-realm, the dharma-realm of non-obstruction between principle and event, the dharma-realm of mutual non-obstruction between event and event) belongs to *Chengguan*'s *Huayan fajie xuanjing*, fascicle one:

> The dharma-realm spoken of is the deep tenet of this one sūtra … in unity it is the one true dharma-realm … opened out, it makes four gates: first, the event-dharma-realm; second, the principle-dharma-realm; third, the dharma-realm of non-obstruction between principle and event; fourth, the dharma-realm of mutual non-obstruction between event and event.[^6]

Dushun's three contemplations and Chengguan's four dharma-realms are two different systems and must not be conflated. The relevant point for this paper is that Chengguan gathers the whole into "the one true dharma-realm" and opens it into four gates, the fourth of which — the mutual non-obstruction between event and event — is exactly the locus at which a particular event (an instance of giving, for example) is able to enter without obstruction into every other event.

### 3.2 The substance of mutual non-obstruction between event and event: the contemplation of pervasion-and-containment, in ten gates

Mutual non-obstruction between event and event is not an abstract slogan. Its doctrinal substance is Chengguan's contemplation of pervasion-and-containment, unfolded as ten gates:

> The contemplation of pervasion-and-containment … ten gates … first, the gate of principle as event; second, the gate of event as principle; third, the gate of event containing principle and event without obstruction; fourth, the gate of the unobstructed general-and-particular; fifth, the gate of the unobstructed broad-and-narrow; sixth, the gate of unobstructed pervasion-and-containment; seventh, the gate of unobstructed gathering-and-entering; eighth, the gate of unobstructed mutual interaction; ninth, the gate of unobstructed mutual indwelling; tenth, the gate of universal interfusion without obstruction.[^7]

❌ A common error is to read "mutual non-obstruction between event and event" as a permission slip for "any experience whatsoever counts as the dharma-realm" — a quietist mysticism, as though anything sufficiently spacious in feeling would already amount to entry into the realm. Fazang himself closes off this road. After setting out the perfect mutual permeation of the six characteristics and the mutual non-obstruction between event and event in his *Wujiao zhang*, he appends a closing verse:

> This dharma-realm of mutual non-obstruction between event and event … is the domain only of wisdom; it is not what event-cognition can apprehend.[^8]

Mutual non-obstruction between event and event is the domain of "wisdom" (智), not of ordinary "event-cognition" (事識, i.e. everyday perceptual-conceptual cognition). ❌ Not every kind of experience can be re-labelled "dharma-realm." The dharma-realm is an architecture that opens to wisdom — and, as §IV will show, an architecture that opens *by way of action*, not by way of a contemplative stillness.

### 3.3 Fazang's older ten profound gates

Fazang systematized the dharma-realm's dependent-origination architecture in the "ten profound gates." A philological note here: Fazang himself produced two versions — the older ten profound gates in *Wujiao zhang* (T1866) and the revised ten profound gates in *Tanxuan ji* (T1733), the latter reordered. This paper uses the older ten profound gates, marks them as such, and does not conflate the two enumerations.

> First, the gate of the simultaneous correspondence and completion of all dharmas … second, the gate of the unobstructed self-sufficiency of broad and narrow … third, the gate in which one-and-many include each other without becoming identical … fourth, the gate of the perfect self-sufficiency of mutual-identity among dharmas … fifth, the gate in which the hidden and the manifest are jointly accomplished … sixth, the gate of the subtle accommodation and stable establishment [of all phenomena] … seventh, the gate of the realm of Indra's Net … eighth, the gate of giving rise to understanding by way of phenomena disclosing dharma … ninth, the gate of the different becoming of dharmas across the ten worlds … tenth, the gate of the principal-and-companion perfect-bright completion-of-virtues.[^9]

The seventh — "the gate of the realm of Indra's Net," the jewel-net of Indra — is precisely the architecture P24 unfolded in full; here it suffices to note it as one of the ten (Indra's Net unfolding is P24 territory; cross-link in a single line). What matters for §IV are the third and fourth gates — the gate of one-and-many inclusion-without-identity and the gate of perfect mutual-identity among dharmas — *one contains many, one IS all*. These provide the formal slot into which "one act of giving IS all practices" will lodge.

### 3.4 "One IS all": the sūtra's seed, the commentary's system

A careful distinction is needed here too. The formula "one IS all," in its full systematic form, is a commentary achievement; the sūtra-internal *seeds* lie in the middle chapters of the *Avataṃsaka*, not within the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" itself.

> Within the one, understand the immeasurable; within the immeasurable, understand the one. Comprehend their mutual production-and-arising, and one shall attain fearlessness.[^10]

> So also, sons of good family, with the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas … all buddha-fields enter into one buddha-field; one buddha-field enters into all buddha-fields … one IS many, many IS one — *Mañjuśrī*, know that dharmas are such.[^11]

These are within the sūtra — but the fascicle-sixteen passage is situated within the bodhisattva's items of cultivation, not yet stated as a metaphysical system. Restated as a metaphysical system, and given the *pratītyasamutpāda* ground that protects against substantialist reading, this becomes Fazang's work:

> Within the dependently-arisen dharma-gates there is no fixed character … through such a gate one attains "one IS all, all IS one" … what is called the "one" is not a self-natured one, but is constituted dependently.[^12]

Fazang's last clause is the load-bearing safeguard: "what is called the 'one' is not a self-natured one, but is constituted dependently." ❌ This forecloses the substantialist misreading: "one IS all" is not "all are secretly the same single Thing." It is dependent origination — and precisely because it is dependent origination, the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" *performs* the formula (Sudhana's walking, Samantabhadra's autobiography) rather than *asserting* it: a dependently-constituted architecture is shown by being walked through, not by being declared as an identity-claim. The "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is the dramatization of what fascicles thirteen through fifty-three and the Fazang-commentary frame articulate; the chapter itself does not articulate the formula. This paper does not classify the formula as belonging to the textual content of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm."

## IV. The Form of Giving in the Dharma-Realm

This section bears the paper's load.

### 4.1 Samantabhadra's disclosure: the autobiographical content of dharma-realm being is giving

At the close of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm," Sudhana enters Samantabhadra's body, and Samantabhadra opens to disclose his own original mark. This disclosure is dense and first-person — and here is the finding the present paper would draw out — the content is, overwhelmingly, giving:

> Son of good family! Across past kalpas as numerous as the dust-motes of inexpressibly inexpressible buddha-fields, I practiced the bodhisattva-practice in pursuit of all-wisdom … And further, son of good family, I adorned buddha-lands; with the mind of great compassion I rescued and protected sentient beings, taught and matured them, made offerings to all the Buddhas, served spiritual friends; in pursuit of the true dharma, I propagated and protected it, giving up all that was inner and outer … all I relinquished, with not a single grudge.[^13]

The disclosure as a whole runs to more than thirty lines, itemizing in turn the giving of wealth, of cities, of kingship, of wives and children, of eyes, ears, nose, tongue, body, and life, of head and marrow and brain — but the point is not in restating that itemized list. The point is structural: when the sūtra at last permits this "person of the destination" to speak about what he *is*, the content of that autobiography is *relinquishing-and-giving*. Samantabhadra — the figure who *is* dharma-realm being, whose body Sudhana has just entered — describes his own constitution as a continuous history of *relinquishing*. Giving is not Samantabhadra's past, left behind; giving is Samantabhadra's content.

### 4.2 The sealing line: the explicit equation of dharma and giving

This autobiographical disclosure then converges into the line that opened this paper:

> In all my ocean of dharma, there is not one letter, not one phrase, that I have not acquired by relinquishing the position of universal monarch [and giving it away]; nor is there one letter, one phrase, that I have not acquired by giving away all that I possessed.[^1]

This is to be read as an equation, not as a phrase of pious rhetoric. "The ocean of dharma" — the ocean of dharma, that is, the aspect under which the dharma-realm appears as "all that Samantabhadra has realized and spoken" — is said to contain not one letter, not one phrase that was not acquired through relinquishing-and-giving. *Every* letter, *every* phrase. The quantifier is universal. This is the sūtra's own statement of the identity-claim "dharma IS giving": in the dharma-realm, no portion is anything other than a form of an act of giving. The destination of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" does not transcend giving; in Samantabhadra's mouth, it declares that it *is* giving — giving in its final, universal, architectural form.

### 4.3 Cross-recension stability

Might this be a late, Tang-period elaboration — a seventh-century polish in the eighty-fascicle text? It is not. The sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0278, fifth century, Buddhabhadra) carries the same disclosure at the corresponding location:

> Across these many oceans of kalpas, I have always given by way of every kind of giving … all I relinquished, with not a single grudge … in my dharma-store, there is not one letter, not one phrase that is not acquired through relinquishing the position of universal monarch; nor is there one letter not acquired through relinquishing all that I possessed.[^14]

The wording diverges in a few places ("dharma-store" rather than "ocean of dharma"; minor phrase-shifts), but the doctrinal sense is identical; the appearance in a fifth-century translation confirms the line as belonging to the original *Avataṃsaka* (Sanskrit *Gaṇḍavyūha*), not a Tang interpolation. The sealing line of "dharma IS giving" is cross-recension stable. P28's load-bearing anchor therefore stands on the textual base, not on the choice of a single translator.

[A cross-link sentence belongs here: the "not amounting to a hundredth part" comparative — read as a hierarchy metric in P26, and as a containment formula in P27 — operates at a different level; Samantabhadra's disclosure here is not a comparative of magnitude but an equation-sealing.]

### 4.4 Fazang's Chinese-tradition architectural anchor: the pure-and-mixed gate, and "one act of giving gathers all practices"

If the sūtra has stated the equation "dharma IS giving," what Fazang builds is the architecture that displays *how* the equation operates. Two texts.

First, the gate of "pure-and-mixed both fully endowed" (*Wujiao zhang*, T1866):

> Entering a single *samādhi* and practising only giving, in every time, every place, every condition, every event, every kind, every duration … this is called "pure." And entering a single *samādhi* in which giving, precepts, perfections, the saving of beings, and so on [are present together], in every time, every place … this is called "mixed."[^15]

Fazang needs an example with which to articulate how a single practice can be at once *pure* — one practice, exhaustively pervading every time, place, and condition — and at the same time *mixed* — that one practice already containing the giving, the precepts, the perfections, the saving of beings, and so on; containing them all. The example he chooses for the *pure* — is *dāna*. This is doctrinally telling: Fazang did not pick a neutral placeholder. He chose giving, as the worked example of "one IS all" at the level of practice.

Second, the *Jin shizi zhang* (T1881):

> One act of giving gathers all practices … giving IS the nine [other] perfections.[^16]

(Philological note: T1881 contains two layers — Fazang's own main text of roughly six hundred characters, and the later notes of Song Chengqian, roughly 1010–1027 CE, some four hundred years after Fazang; the line "one act of giving gathers all practices" belongs to Fazang's main text, not to Song Chengqian's later notes.) "One act of giving gathers all practices" — the single practice of giving gathers, exhaustively, all the others; "giving IS the nine perfections" — giving simply *is* the other nine perfections. This is "one IS all" applied to action, not to substance: a single act (giving) IS the totality of action.

Set Samantabhadra's sealing line alongside Fazang's two passages and the paper's thesis is complete. The final form of giving is not the dissolution of giving into a dharma-realm metaphysics. It is giving becoming one of the modalities under which the dharma-realm unfolds — "one act of giving IS all practices; all practices ARE this single act of giving." The dharma-realm, in Samantabhadra's autobiographical disclosure and in Fazang's pure-and-mixed reading, is an architecture *borne by* practice; and giving is the worked example the tradition itself selected, by which to display how a single action can BE the whole.

## V. Supporting Wings of Commentary and Dialogue with Western Scholarship

### 5.1 Supporting wings: Li Tongxuan and Zongmi

*Li Tongxuan*'s *Xin Huayan jing lun* (T1739 — and one must note: T1739, *not* X0260; X0260 is an unrelated work of Zongmi's):

> Without departing from a single thought, [one] passes through a lifetime; without departing from a single place, [one] pervades to the ten directions.[^17]

This is Li Tongxuan's "ordinary-and-immediate" register: the whole road is "without departing from a single thought, without departing from a single place" (P27 has already cited this line; cross-link in a single sentence). And:

> Mañjuśrī, Samantabhadra, and *Maitreya* — the three are throughout in one place, the cause and the fruit pervading through.[^18]

This line is the native-Chinese-tradition explicit anchor for the chiastic structure of the fifty-three visits treated in P27 (P27 territory; cross-link in a single sentence). What is genuinely relevant for P28 is the "Maitreya Tower" line:

> Within Maitreya's tower, the three times manifest fully; in a single thought, all is universally beheld.[^19]

The dharma-realm's cosmic content — the three times, all worlds — is compressed into "a single thought universally beheld," one single, total seeing. What Li Tongxuan contributes to P28 is this: he does not allow the dharma-realm to drift away as a distant grandeur — it is "without departing from a single thought, without departing from a single place," available in the immediacy of the present moment of practice.

*Zongmi* (with whom the present series first draws on two further texts — *Huayan yuanren lun* T1886 and *Yuanjue jing dashu* X0243):

> The one true dharma-realm … the threefold dharma-realm … the four kinds of dharma-realm of Huayan are all gathered into the one true dharma-realm.[^20]

Zongmi's distinctive contribution is this two-tier architecture: the "one true dharma-realm" as the single true realm, with the "threefold dharma-realm" and the "four kinds of dharma-realm" as its differential openings — Huayan's four kinds are all gathered under the one true dharma-realm (Zongmi's full five-teachings and five-lineages doctrinal classification belongs in the SĪLA volume; here, cross-link in a single sentence). What this means for P28: Zongmi confirms that the four kinds of dharma-realm are not four separate locations but different *places of entry* into the same dharma-realm — which is why giving, a single "event," can be a complete entry into the whole.

### 5.2 Dialogue with Western scholarship: three stacks in array

A framing note first: the three modern scholars below are arrayed as a *triangle in logical space*, not as a diachronic line of succession. Each receives a "concede-and-push-back" treatment — concede one beat, push back one beat. And a discipline governs the whole section (⚠️): the push-back returns each time to "the action of giving." The present paper does not adjudicate Western metaphysics in general; it asks only what these frames capture, and fail to capture, of "the form of giving in the dharma-realm."

**Stack 1 — Steve Odin, *Process Metaphysics and Hua-yen Buddhism: A Critical Study of Cumulative Penetration vs. Interpenetration* (SUNY 1982).**

Odin's monograph is the sole Western book-length study dedicated to the metaphysics of the Huayan dharma-realm. Its thesis: Whitehead's process philosophy and Huayan's dharma-realm dependent-origination are structurally close but doctrinally distinct — Whitehead being "cumulative penetration" (each actual occasion cumulatively taking up its predecessors), Huayan being "interpenetration" (each contains all).

⚠️ Concede: Odin's distinction of "cumulative" from "interpenetration" is a useful frame and is one of the cornerstones of Western Huayan scholarship; reading mutual non-obstruction between event and event as synchronic interpenetration is compatible with the Chinese commentarial anchors of §III (Fazang's pure-and-mixed, Chengguan's pervasion-and-containment).

⚠️ Push back: Odin's frame foregrounds the *synchronic metaphysical structure* and treats giving, the act, only lightly — the abstract architecture of interpenetration, not the action that fills it. The frame moreover lends itself to overreading: "Huayan dharma-realm = a quietist metaphysics." This paper brings the frame back to the layer of action: Fazang's "one act of giving gathers all practices," "giving IS the nine perfections," and the pure-and-mixed worked example, show that the dharma-realm is an architecture *borne by* practice, not a quiescent one. Odin gives the *form* of the metaphysics; this paper supplies the *content of the action* that the form carries.

**Stack 2 — Brook Ziporyn, *Beyond Oneness and Difference: Li and Coherence in Chinese Buddhist Thought and Its Antecedents* (SUNY 2013).**

Ziporyn's systematic articulation of *li*-coherence works across Chinese Buddhist traditions in their handling of the one-and-many problem. His thesis: Huayan's "one IS all" is neither identity nor mere aggregation of the many, but *intersubsumption* — a logic in which the "one-many" hierarchy dissolves.

⚠️ Concede: Ziporyn's intersubsumption is the most philosophically refined Western reading of "one IS all"; it correctly displays the *impossibility* of a staircase or hierarchical reading — same register as P27's "polyphony rather than hierarchy" and the present paper's "final form of giving."

⚠️ Push back: Ziporyn's frame is primarily ontological — the coherence of *li*; its treatment of "giving as the *coherence of practice* answering to this logic" is lighter. This paper supplies the layer of *action*: Fazang's "one act of giving gathers all practices" is intersubsumption applied to action, not only to ontology — a single practice (giving) subsuming all practices, a coherence at the level of what one *does* and not only of what *is*.

**Stack 3 — Garma C. C. Chang, *The Buddhist Teaching of Totality: The Philosophy of Hwa Yen Buddhism* (Penn State 1971).**

Chang's monograph is the earliest Western book-length introduction to Huayan philosophy, antecedent to Cook (1977); the two together constitute the founding stratum of early Western Huayan scholarship. Chang stabilized a set of introductory English terms for the dharma-realm and "one IS all" — notably the title's rendering "totality" for the dharma-realm.

⚠️ Concede: This paper acknowledges Chang's historical and pedagogical role; his renderings have been widely used in Western introductory contexts (though the present paper prefers "Dharma-Realm" as more doctrinally precise).

⚠️ Push back: Chang's frame is a doctrinal-systematic introduction that largely follows the self-presentation of the Chinese commentaries; its critical-philological work is lighter — understandable for 1971, before Western critical philology had matured. This paper supplies that layer: the attribution-corrections of §II–§III (Four Dharma-Realms to Chengguan rather than Dushun; "infinite interpenetration" as commentary creation rather than sūtra verbatim; the older-versus-revised ten profound gates) are precisely the philological precision a 1971 systematic introduction did not yet carry. Chang's "totality" captures the systematic sweep; this paper supplies the specificity of practice-action that the sweep is built out of.

### 5.3 Summary of the middle-way correctives

Three correctives run through the paper:

❌ The dharma-realm is not an "abstract metaphysics" available for quietist contemplation (Fazang's verse: "the domain only of wisdom"; Fazang's worked example: giving).

❌ "One IS all" is not substantialist monism (Fazang: "not a self-natured one, but constituted dependently").

⚠️ "Infinite interpenetration" and the "four dharma-realms" must be attributed to the commentarial tradition (Chengguan; Fazang and Chengguan), not to the sūtra's literal text, and not to Dushun.

## VI. Conclusion: The Paper-Level Closure of Part VIII, the Forward to the Volume-Level Closure, and the Seam to Śīla

**Concise restatement.** The destination at the close of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is the final form of giving. Samantabhadra's sealing line — "in my ocean of dharma, there is not one letter, not one phrase, that I have not acquired by relinquishing the position of universal monarch" — states the equation "dharma IS giving" in the sūtra's own voice; Fazang's pure-and-mixed gate and "one act of giving gathers all practices" construct the Chinese-tradition architecture: "one act of giving IS all practices; all practices ARE this single act of giving." Giving does not dissolve into a dharma-realm metaphysics; it becomes one modality through which the dharma-realm unfolds.

**Guidance for practice.** The practical settling-place is this: a single, concrete act of giving made in the present moment is not a "lower," "preparatory" practice waiting to be transcended. Within the dharma-realm's architecture — provided it is taken as the domain of wisdom, and not as a quietist felt-spaciousness — a single act of giving already bears the form of the whole road. The practitioner does not need to climb a staircase from "giving" up to "dharma-realm"; no such staircase ever existed. Samantabhadra's autobiography is the model: a dharma-realm being, as to the content of his autobiography, is a history of relinquishing.

**Open question.** But the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is the terminus of this scriptural corpus — no further chapter follows. When this infinitely-interpenetrating architecture meets a modern world that, by the diagnosis to come in Part IX, has transactionalized the act of giving, what happens? That is the question Part IX takes up.

**Part VIII closure.** With the present paper P28, the five-piece sequence of "Samantabhadra's principal arena" — P24 the entry-gate, P25 the architecture of vast cultivation of offerings, P26 the deep-dive into dharma-offering, P27 the trajectory through the fifty-three teachers, P28 the destination of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" — is fully unfolded. ⚠️ A distinction must be plainly preserved: the closure of the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is *paper-level* — P28 closes Part VIII. It is not *volume-level*: the closure of the DĀNA volume as a whole will be completed by Part IX (P29 through P31: the modern diagnosis, the AI coda, and the volume's general close). That the "Entry into the Dharma-Realm" is the terminus of a scriptural corpus is a fact about the *Avataṃsaka*; the DĀNA volume still has its own destination ahead.

**Forward to Part IX.** Part IX (P29–P31) will carry giving out of the scriptural corpus and into the modern diagnosis — a diagnosis adjacent to McMindfulness, of giving as transactionalized — together with the AI coda and the volume's general close.

**Forward to the Śīla volume.** And as the DĀNA volume turns toward its own closure, the seam to Volume 3 *śīla* has already been anchored — the Mahādeva passage from the polyphony of the fifty-three teachers (treated in P27) marks the point where giving hands off to the perfection of precepts; the present paper, the penultimate piece of Volume 2 DĀNA, therefore gestures not only toward this volume's own destination but also across the seam, toward Volume 3.
