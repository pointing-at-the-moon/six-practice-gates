# The Reverse-Giving Hidden in Not-Taking

**The Śūraṅgama Sūtra's Third Pure Precept and Its Doctrine of Dāna-in-Negation**

*中文主題：不偷盜的背面——楞嚴四種清淨明誨之不盜誨與反向施論*

**Abstract**

This paper examines the third of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s Four Pure Precepts (卷六，第三決定清淨明誨)—the precept against not-taking—and argues that its positive completion is itself a doctrine of *dāna* (giving). I call this the "reverse-giving" structure: the negative achievement of "not stealing" contains within it the positive content of "giving." Using CBETA primary sources, the analysis examines: (1) the core anchor "if stealing-mind is not abandoned, liberation from the dust of samsāra is impossible" (T19p0132b02–b03) establishing the ontological priority of abandoning theft over giving; (2) the sūtra's own word "施" in "giving the residue of one's alms to feed starving beings"—the positive act of the third precept is literally giving; (3) Ming-Qing commentary consensus (Jiehuan, Hanshan, Ouyi, Zhenjian) structuring this as four types of giving against four poisons; (4) śāstra precedents in the Mahāyāna treatise tradition (*Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, *Yogācārabhūmi*, *Upāsakaśīla Sūtra*) already grounding dāna on a śīla floor. The *Śūraṅgama* sharpens this logic from external "non-stealing of objects" to internal "absence of stealing-mind."

**Findings:** (1) "If stealing-mind is not abandoned" is the structural hinge of the third precept—the positive completion is giving, not moral exhortation but epistemological condition; (2) "乞食餘分**施**餓眾生" in the sūtra's own text is the bedrock—the positive content of "not-taking" is inscribed as "giving"; (3) Zhenjian's *Zhengmai Shu* codifies "abandoning theft = practicing giving" in his chapter heading; Ouyi's four-giving-four-poison framework maps the positive acts as dāna-pāramitā; (4) Nāgārjuna's "earth as the ground of all wholesome dharmas" + *Upāsakaśīla Sūtra*'s "pure object = non-stolen" already build the logical scaffolding—the *Śūraṅgama* pushes this from external objects to internal intention.

**Core finding:** The deepest meaning of the third precept's "abandoning theft" is not the sharpening of a moral prohibition but the transformation of "not-taking" from possession to donation—using one's own body as mahā-dāna. Reverse-giving is not the *Śūraṅgama*'s creation but its internalization and sharpening of existing Mahāyāna ethical logic.

**Keywords:** reverse-giving (反向施論), precept against not-taking (不盜誨), third decision pure precept (第三決定清淨明誨), stealing-mind (偷心), practicing giving (捨施), four giving against four poisons (四捨對治四毒), Jiehuan, Hanshan, Ouyi, Zhenjian, *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, *Yogācārabhūmi*, *Upāsakaśīla Sūtra*, Mañjuśrī's discriminating wisdom (文殊揀別智), three unattainabilities (三事不可得), pure object (物淨), body-flesh-blood with sentient beings (身肉骨血與眾生共), exploiting Tathāgata (裨販如來), dharma-theft (法盜)

> "Ananda! Furthermore, among the six types of beings in the world, if their minds do not steal, they will not follow the continuum of saṃsāra. You cultivate samādhi precisely to emerge from the dust of affliction. **If stealing-mind is not abandoned, the dust cannot be left.** Even if profound wisdom and meditative absorption appear before you, if stealing is not cut off, you will certainly fall into heterodox paths: at the highest level, spirits who steal the essence of celestial bodies; at the middle level, demons who steal the essence of humans; at the lowest level, evil persons possessed by various spirits. These groups of heterodox beings each have their own followers, each claiming to have attained anuttarā-samyak-sambodhi. In the age after my parinirvāṇa, many such demons will flourish in the world. They conceal fraud, claim to be holy teachers, each declaring themselves to have attained the knowledge of superior persons. They deceive the unseeing, frighten others into losing their minds, and wherever they pass, their hosts are financially ruined. I taught bhikṣus to beg for food in due order, to abandon greed, and to become bodhisattvas. These bhikṣus do not cook their own food; they depend on residual life, sojourning in the three realms, showing a single journey with no return. How can thieves wearing my robes sell the Tathāgata? They commit all manner of deeds and call it dharma, yet they are not monastics who observe the full precepts, leading others toward the hīna path. Through this they mislead countless beings into the avīci hell. If after my parinirvāṇa any bhikṣu resolves to practice samādhi, lighting a lamp before the Tathāgata's image, burning a finger-joint, or offering incense on the body—I say that person's beginningless karmic debts are repaid at once, cutting short worldly existence and forever freeing themselves from all āsravas. Although such a person has not yet realized the anuttarā-margā, their mind is resolved in the dharma. If they do not perform these small acts of self-abandonment, even if they attain an unconditioned state, they will certainly be reborn among humans to repay their debts—as in my own case of eating horse feed. You, teaching the world to practice samādhi, place **cutting off theft afterward**; this is called the Tathāgata's, the Former Buddhas' **third decision pure precept**."[^1]

> "Therefore, Ananda! Those who practice meditation without cutting off stealing are like a person pouring water into a leaky vessel, seeking to fill it—through countless eons, it will never be level. If bhikṣus, after leaving the residue of their robe and bowl without keeping even a fraction, **give the residue of their alms to feed starving beings**, join their palms in reverence before the great assembly, and treat blows and curses as praise—they must abandon both body and mind, **offering flesh and blood together with sentient beings**. They do not twist the Tathāgata's non-final teachings as their own understanding to mislead beginners. The Buddha marks such a person as having attained true samādhi. What I have said is the Buddha's word; what does not match this is Māra's word."[^2]

— *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, ch.6, T19n0945, p0132b01–b28

These two paragraphs constitute the complete portrait of the third decision pure precept. The first establishes the ontological priority of abandoning theft over giving with "if stealing-mind is not abandoned, the dust cannot be left." The second completes the reverse-giving doctrine with "give the residue of alms to feed starving beings." Within these two paragraphs, the sūtra itself embeds a profound paradox: "not-taking" is not the endpoint, but the **precondition for giving**.

## I. Introduction: Why the Precept Against Not-Taking Is the Opening of Reverse-Giving

Part V of *Dāna* unfolded through three papers: the *Diamond Sūtra*'s non-abiding giving (P15[^3]), the *Heart Sūtra*'s silence on giving (P16[^4]), and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra*'s panorama of the six pāramitās (P17). The prajñāpāramitā tradition's discourse on giving has reached a pinnacle: formless giving under the protection of emptiness. But emptiness does not equal the negation of giving. The *Vajracchedikā* says "practice giving without dwelling"[^5]—emptiness language is the protecting condition of giving, not its replacement.

Part VI opens by asking the opposite question: if giving is the bodhisattva's first practice, what must come **before** giving?

The answer hides in a seam long overlooked: the third of the Four Pure Precepts, "cutting off theft afterward."

The positive completion of this precept **is** "giving." Not two separate injunctions side by side—the positive content of "not-stealing" is inscribed in the sūtra's own text as "giving": "乞食餘分**施**餓眾生." The sūtra writes the word "施" itself—this is the bedrock of this paper's thesis-line.

But "not stealing" in Buddhist ethics has always been read as a moral prohibition: do not take what is not given. P18's argument is that in the context of the *Śūraṅgama*, the positive (not the negative) of not-stealing is itself a declaration of giving—transforming "not-taking" from possession to donation.

## II. The Classical Baseline: Not-Taking in Śrāvakayāna Vinaya

To understand the *Śūraṅgama*'s sharpening of not-stealing, one must first establish the classical baseline: the definition and scope of not-stealing in śrāvakayāna Vinaya.

### 2.1 The Fifth Precept, Second: The Standard Formula of adinnādāna

The most stable Chinese formula for not-stealing in the Āgama corpus is the *Madhyama Āgama* formula of "abandoning not-giving, cutting off not-giving, taking only what is given, delighting in giving." In the *Upāsaka Sūtra* (T0026, ch.33), this is the second of the five dharma for the holy lay disciple:[^6]

> "The holy lay disciple abandons not-giving, cuts off not-giving, takes only what is given, delights in taking what is given, constantly practices giving with joy and non-avarice, hoping for no return, not covered by what is stolen, constantly protecting the self."[^7]

Five aspects of this formula deserve attention: (1) double withdrawal of abandon + cut off; (2) take only after given—only what has been given may be taken; (3) delight in giving—the mental orientation flips from grasping to receiving; (4) constantly delighting in giving—the positive pole is giving itself; (5) not covered by theft—an auxiliary mental clause, but framed as concomitant disposition, not a separate offense category.

### 2.2 Dharmaguptaka Pārājika 2: The Six-Condition Structure

The *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* (T1428, ch.1) gives a precise legal definition for the second pārājika. The six-condition analysis requires all six elements to concur:[^8]

> "There are six types of not-giving that constitute pārājika: taking with one's own hand, taking under supervision, sending others to take, heavy objects, stealing-mind, lifting from the original place."[^9]

The six conjuncts structure is: **act of taking** (own hand / supervised / proxy) + **value** (≥ five māsakas) + **stealing-intention** (mens rea) + **displacement from the place** (completed act). Stealing-intention here is the mens rea ingredient bound to a specific overt taking—not a standalone "stealing-mind" offense.

The non-offense clauses (T1428 p575b28) are decisive: if one believes the thing was given, or one's own, or rubbish, or borrowed, or that a close associate would consent—five cognitive framings, none constitute an offense. What determines guilt or innocence is the cognitive framing of the **object**, not the purity of background intention.

### 2.3 The Classical Baseline's Important Silences

The classical baseline explicitly **does not** cover the following:[^10]

(1) **Stealing-mind (偷心)**—coveting another's property without taking action is a kilesa (defilement) issue in the classical baseline, not a sīla (precept) offense. The non-offense clauses are sharp on this: frame of object decides offense, not background mental purity.

(2) **Dharma-theft (法盜)**—using dharma, monastic appearance, or spiritual tableaus to exchange for name and gain; or "claiming partial understanding as complete enlightenment → misleading beginners." None of the six Chinese Vinayas or Pāli Vinaya III has a category for this.

(3) **Reputation-theft (名盜)**—cultivating for the sake of fame. In śrāvakayāna Vinaya this is a kilesa issue, not a śīla offense.

These three silences are not oversights but structural: the classical sīla-system locates ethical regulation at the **body-and-speech boundary**, with *citta* entering only as mens rea bound to a specific overt taking. Mental cultivation is the domain of bhāvanā (meditation), not sīla (precept).

**The seam the *Śūraṅgama* will pry open lies exactly here.**

## III. Core Argument: The Four-Layer Structure of Reverse-Giving

### 3.1 Three Layers of Stealing-Mind: From Coarse Theft to Subtle Theft

The consensus of Ming-Qing commentators divides stealing-mind into three layers, from coarse to subtle:[^11]

**Layer 1: Coarse theft (taking others' property)**—property-theft in the Vinaya sense, directly taking another's things. Zhenjian's *Zhengmai Shu* marks the coarse layer as the starting point with "this stealing is not merely stealing others' property."[^12]

**Layer 2: Subtle theft (taking others' reputation, dharma, or gains)**—Hanshan's *Tongyi* delivers the pivotal verdict: "This stealing is not the stealing of stealing, but stealing-mind!"[^13] Hanshan lists five forms: (1) coveting alms-gains, (2) concealing fraud, (3) falsely claiming virtuous attainment as a holy teacher, (4) hoarding resources, (5) deceiving to take. "Equal to theft in karmic consequence, equal in retribution." Ouyi's *Wenju* further divides into two sub-layers: (a) stealing-mind regarding dependent and environment (external wealth, body-life) + (b) stealing-mind on dharma itself (teaching misappropriation).[^14] Ouyi says: "If one wishes to teach dharma and benefit beings and guide beginners, one must thoroughly investigate the Tathāgata's extensive and abbreviated scriptures, understand the four expedients, and not merely cling to one meaning or one interpretation while declaring oneself correct, thereby misleading beginners."[^15]—this is the Ming-Qing crystallization of "dharma-theft."

**Layer 3: Subtlest theft (the thought of grasping)**—Zhenjian gives the broadest definition: "even a single thought of coveting gains counts."[^16] He adds: "Forget profit, desire, and the mind of greed; let a single thought not arise."[^17] Not only does one not take, but even "the thought of coveting" ceases. This is the core that "if stealing-mind is not abandoned, the dust cannot be left" refers to.

This three-layer structure achieves consensus across the four commentators—Jiehuan, Hanshan, Ouyi, Zhenjian each approach from different angles but point to the same truth.

### 3.2 The Sūtra's Own Text Already Contains "Giving"

The positive paragraph of the third precept is the direct bedrock of reverse-giving:

> "If bhikṣus, after leaving the residue of their robe and bowl without keeping even a fraction, **give the residue of their alms to feed starving beings**, join their palms in reverence before the great assembly, and treat blows and curses as praise—they must abandon both body and mind, **offering flesh and blood together with sentient beings**."[^18]

"Give the residue of alms **施** to feed starving beings"—the sūtra itself completes the positive of abandoning theft with the word "giving." This is not a commentator's retroactive reading but literal inscription in the sūtra's text.

Immediately following: "offering flesh and blood together with sentient beings"—this is not merely "wealth-giving" but **body-giving**. The positive completion of not-stealing is not ordinary dāna but transforming the body from object of possession to object of donation. Zhenjian explains: "One must observe these as bitter, empty, without-self, impermanent, impure—**offering them as buddha-work**."[^19]

### 3.3 Commentary Consensus: Four Givings Against Four Poisons

Ouyi's *Wenju* reads the third precept's practice through four lenses as four types of giving:[^20]

| Practice | Against |
|---|---|
| Leave the residue of robe and bowl, keep nothing | Greed |
| Give the residue of alms to feed starving beings | Avarice (= **giving**) |
| Join palms in reverence before great assembly | Arrogance |
| Treat blows and curses as praise | Aversion |

"Abandoning theft" is simultaneously practicing "giving" (dāna-pāramitā)—and this is not Ouyi's solo reading. Zhenjian's *Zhengmai Shu* codifies it in his chapter heading: "△1 **Relying on the final meaning of practicing giving**"[^21]—directly naming the positive content of the third precept as "practicing giving." Zhenjian concludes: "All of this is to accomplish **the mind without stealing**."[^22]—mind without stealing = practice of giving, a direct equation.

Hanshan's *Tongyi* reads this in three layers of climax:[^23]

> "衣鉢之餘分寸不畜，乞食餘分施餓眾生： **the utmost of non-greed**! Treating blows and curses as praise: **the utmost of non-self**! Mind free from aversion, body without retaliation—this is abandoning both body and mind; and observing one's own flesh and blood as equal with sentient beings, with nothing different between them, let alone external possessions! Only one who has attained **true samādhi** can do this!"[^24]

The threefold "utmost" marks not-stealing→giving as a progressive cultivation path, not two separate matters.

Jiehuan's *Yaojie* gives the ancestral phrase "the hardest to abandon, the most when abandoned":[^25]

> "When the hardest thing to abandon is abandoned, the remaining attachment will certainly be let go."[^26]

### 3.4 Śāstra Precedents: Giving Founded on the Ground of Precept

The *Śūraṅgama*'s sharpening is not creation—the Mahāyāna śāstra tradition already established the logic of giving founded on precept.[^27]

Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* ch.12 gives two causal directions:[^28]

> "How does a bodhisattva's giving give rise to śīla-pāramitā? The bodhisattva reflects: 'Beings who do not give become poor in future lives; through poverty, stealing-mind arises; through stealing, killing follows... But those who practice giving obtain wealth, and with wealth, they do no evil.'... **Furthermore, when a bodhisattva practices giving, they constantly generate a mind of compassion toward the recipient, do not cling to wealth, do not value their own things—how much less would they steal? With compassion toward the recipient, what killing-mind could arise?** Thus giving obstructs precept-breaking (遮破戒); this is giving giving rise to precept."[^29]

"Do not value your own things—how much less would you steal?"—the achievement of giving itself contains not-stealing within it. Nāgārjuna also establishes "the ground of precept bearing giving" through metaphor:[^30]

> "As earth, all visible things depend on to reside; **likewise, precept is the dwelling-place of all wholesome dharmas**."[^31]

Giving is among wholesome dharmas; therefore: giving without precept is like flying without wings.[^32]

The structural evidence of the *Yogācārabhūmi* is even more direct: the Dāna chapter (ch.39) immediately precedes the Śīla chapter (ch.40), two adjacent chapters.[^33] In the three-aggregate precept framework, the moral-precept (含不與取) is the precondition for the collecting-wholesome-dharmas precept (含布施). The Śīla chapter also rules: "If one knows the object is stolen property, refusing to accept it is **not a violation**."[^34]—a bodhisattva must not sit in stealing-karma (盜業) under the name of giving.

The *Upāsakaśīla Sūtra* (T1488 p1044b25) gives three purities for establishing giving:[^35]

> "Three conditions: first **pure object**, second pure mind, third pure field. What is pure object? **Not-stolen property**, not forbidden by the wise, not communal, not Triple-Jewel property... this is pure object."[^36]

The first functional precondition for giving to be valid is "the object is not stolen." T1488's three purities are dāna's functional precondition; T1509's three unattainabilities are wisdom completion—the **bottom and the top** both align with reverse-giving's logic.

The *Brahmajāla Sūtra* (T1484 p1004b21) further intensifies the threshold of not-stealing to "not even a needle or blade of grass" and introduces four layers of analysis: stealing-cause, stealing-condition, stealing-dharma, stealing-karma—the term "stealing-dharma" prefigures the *Śūraṅgama*'s deep definition of stealing-mind.[^37]

## IV. Mañjuśrī's Discriminating Wisdom: A Cognitive Clarity Framework for the Four Pure Precepts

### 4.1 明誨： Seeing First, Then Acting

"Four kinds of pure **instruction**"—all four use "pure instruction" to close lust, killing, theft, false-speech.[^38]

**明** = clarity / discrimination—knowing the difference between defiled and pure. **誨** = instruction—establishing rules of conduct after discriminating. The compound "明誨" itself contains the logic of "see first, then act"—the function of Mañjuśrī's great wisdom.

### 4.2 True Mañjuśrī: The Peak of Discriminating Wisdom Is Transcending Itself

In ch.2, the Buddha takes Mañjuśrī as the reflexive case of discriminating wisdom:[^39]

> "Mañjuśrī! I ask you: like you, Mañjuśrī, is there a Mañjuśrī who is Mañjuśrī? Or is there no Mañjuśrī? 'Thus, World-Honored One. **I am true Mañjuśrī; there is no Mañjuśrī who is Mañjuśrī.** Why? If there were, there would be two Mañjuśrīs. Yet I, today, am not without Mañjuśrī—within this there is truly neither "is" nor "is not."[^40]

The source of discriminating power is "true Mañjuśrī" (non-discriminating root), and on "true Mañjuśrī" one cannot even establish "is Mañjuśrī / is not Mañjuśrī." The peak of discriminating wisdom is discriminating transcending itself.[^41]

### 4.3 Western-Reader Framing: The Four Precepts as Cognitive Clarity, Not Moral Restriction

For Western readers, the Four Pure Precepts can easily be read as ego-driven moral judgment—"the Buddha says don't do this, don't do that." The four precepts are not a moralistic restriction but the natural expression of cognitive clarity.[^42]

The "明" of "明誨" is discrimination—knowing the difference between defiled and pure. This is not the ego-judgment of "I declare you evil" but clarity naturally flowing from the "true Mañjuśrī" level. The discrimination itself has no "I discriminate" attachment.

Mañjuśrī's selection of the twenty-five correct paths in ch.6 and the discrimination of the Four Pure Precepts (defiled-pure: lust, killing, theft, false-speech) are two faces of the same Mañjuśrī great wisdom—one discriminates conduct (precept-body level), the other discriminates entry paths (定慧 level).[^43]

## V. Middle-Path Corrections

### 5.1 The Authenticity Debate

Since Mochizuki Shinkō (1946), mainstream Japanese scholarship has treated the *Śūraṅgama* as an eighth-century Chinese composition. Lü Cheng's *One Hundred Forgeries of the Śūraṅgama* lists 101 points of suspicion. The Chinese Buddhist tradition regards it as authentic Mahāyāna scripture.[^44]

P18's middle path: take the text seriously as practiced scripture, acknowledge the dating debate honestly, and let the doctrine carry the argument. The text's philological provenance (authentic translation vs. Chinese apocrypha) does not affect its function as taught-canon evidence (教證) within the Chinese Mahāyāna tradition.[^45]

### 5.2 "Three Unattainabilities" ≠ "Three Wheels Emptiness"

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* ch.12 uses "**three things unattainable**" (giver, gift, recipient—three things analyzed as unattainable),[^46] and the term "three wheels emptiness" does not appear—this is a later prajñāpāramitā technical term. When citing T1509, one must strictly use "three things unattainable"; if adding a retrospective label, explicitly separate it: "later tradition calls this 'three wheels emptiness.'"[^47]

### 5.3 Two Traps for Western Readers

Trap one: "apocrypha → discount"—if the *Śūraṅgama* is a Chinese composition, does its doctrine still deserve serious engagement? Response: Buswell's (1989) frame is fully applicable—the doctrinal density of a Chinese Mahāyāna synthesis composition is its own warrant, regardless of Sanskrit provenance.[^48]

Trap two: "authentic Mahāyāna → wave away philological critique"—BTTS's (2009) practice-liturgical center and Mochizuki's (1946) philological evidence are both real—they are not contradictory.[^49]

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 The Completion of Reverse-Giving

This paper has argued through three layers:

**Sūtra-text layer:** "Give the residue of alms **施** to feed starving beings" is the third precept's most direct bedrock—the positive of abandoning theft is inscribed in the sūtra itself as giving.[^50] "Flesh and blood with sentient beings"—not merely wealth-giving but body-giving, using one's own body as mahā-dāna.

**Commentary-consensus layer:** Zhenjian's "abandoning theft = practicing giving" chapter heading, Ouyi's "four giving against four poison" framework, Hanshan's "non-greed / non-self / true samādhi three layers," Jiehuan's "the hardest to abandon"—the four commentators independently across the Ming-Qing period but point to the same truth: the positive of abandoning theft is the direct unfolding of dāna-pāramitā.[^51]

**Śāstra-precedent layer:** Nāgārjuna's "do not value your own things—how much less would you steal" + "precept is the ground of all wholesome dharmas," the *Upāsakaśīla Sūtra*'s "first purity = non-stolen object," the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s adjacent Dāna→Śīla chapter structure—the Mahāyāna śāstra tradition already established the logic of giving founded on precept. The *Śūraṅgama* pushes this logic from external "non-stealing of objects" to internal "absence of stealing-mind."[^52]

### 6.2 Paving the Way to P19

The discrimination of the Four Pure Precepts and Mañjuśrī's selection of the twenty-five correct paths in ch.6 are the same great wisdom of the *Śūraṅgama* manifested on two levels: precept (precept foundation) and meditation (meditative absorption). This paper stops at the dāna of precept-body (not-stealing→universal-giving); the next paper P19 will follow this discriminating logic into each of the twenty-five paths, examining how each naturally unfolds its dāna expression.[^53]

After receiving the four precepts, Ananda declares: "**Relying on Mañjuśrī, I attained liberation**."[^54] Precept-instruction (substance) + Mañjuśrī's discrimination (wisdom) + Avalokiteśvara's dharma (practice) form a connected pipeline in the *Śūraṅgama*'s structure. When Mañjuśrī establishes Avalokiteśvara, he already embeds a dāna term:[^55]

> "Excellent indeed, Avalokiteśvara!... **Giving fearlessness to sentient beings**."[^56]

The thread from precept-body flowing to giving-body is planted in Mañjuśrī's verse—this is the complete arc of Part VI's reverse-giving from precept-body to giving-body.
