# Dāna in the Twenty-Five Gateways of Perfect Penetration

**A Map of Meditation-and-Wisdom Giving in the Śūraṅgama Sūtra**

## Abstract

This paper re-examines the twenty-five gateways of perfect penetration (*yuantong* 圓通) in the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s fifth and sixth fascicles through the lens of *dāna* (giving), arguing that the twenty-five paths are not merely twenty-five meditation entry-points but twenty-five expressions of giving. Each sense organ and each element naturally unfolds a specific form of *dāna* — fearlessness-giving, dharma-giving, body-giving, earth-giving, emptiness-giving — forming a comprehensive map of meditation-and-wisdom giving. The analysis proceeds in four layers: (1) Avalokiteśvara's ear-root gateway, with its fourteen fearlessness-bestowals and four unimaginable unconditioned virtues, is the paradigmatic expression of explicit *dāna* — his manifesting bodies is body-giving, his teaching is dharma-giving. (2) Mahāsthāmaprāpta's Buddha-recollection samādhi and Pūrṇa's dharma-teaching represent, respectively, the ultimate of recollection-giving and dharma-giving — Mahāsthāmaprāpta gives through the dual dynamic of mutual recollection, while Pūrṇa gives through the selfless manner of teaching. (3) The *śrāvaka* arhats (Śāriputra, Subhūti, Mahākāśyapa, Upāli, etc.) appear passive, yet each path contains an implicit *dāna* dimension — giving by embodying the dharma, giving through emptiness-teaching, giving via *dhutaṅga*-practice, giving via precept-keeping. (4) A five-skandha and six-dhātu reading of the twenty-five gateways reveals that form, feeling, conception, volition, consciousness, and the six sense organs each serve as a conduit for *dāna*, with the fundamental structure being the reversal of each root's receiving function into a giving function. The thesis: the *Śūraṅgama*'s twenty-five gateways constitute the most complete map of *dāna*'s movement from precept-body to meditation-and-wisdom — every path is a form of giving, every giving leads to perfect penetration.

**Keywords:** twenty-five gateways of perfect penetration, *dāna* of fearlessness, ear-root perfect penetration, Buddha-recollection samādhi, *dhutaṅga*-giving, emptiness-giving, five-skandha giving, six-sense-organs giving, meditation-and-wisdom giving, reverse-giving thesis, Avalokiteśvara, Mahāsthāmaprāpta, Pūrṇa, Mahākāśyapa, Śāriputra, Subhūti, Upāli, *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, *dāna*, perfect penetration

## I. Introduction: Why Look for Giving in the Twenty-Five Gateways?

Paper P18 concluded with the exploration of the third of the Four Pure Precepts — the prohibition against stealing — asserting that the deepest layer of the precept-body is *dāna* itself: the act of "relinquishing greed" is functionally identical to "giving."[^1] That paper established a critical proposition: the positive completion of not-stealing is not found in moral exhortation, but in the actualization of *dāna*. In other words, the positive fulfillment of the precept is the act of giving.

P19 asks a more fundamental question: if the positive aspect of the precept is *dāna*, then what is the positive aspect of meditation (*samādhi*) and wisdom (*prajñā*)?

The answer to this is hidden within the most massive structural component of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s sixth fascicle — the chapter on the Twenty-Five Gateways of Perfect Penetration. Traditional readings treat these twenty-five saints as presenting "twenty-five meditation entry-points," where the selection criterion is simply: which sense-root is most suitable for the practitioners of this particular era?[^2] Such a reading focuses on the convenience of "entry," rather than the altruistic nature of "exit" — that is, the capacity for giving.

The inquiry of this paper is different: if every sense-root and every great element can lead to perfect penetration (*yuantong* 圓通), then what is the specific mode of that penetration? And is this mode, in itself, a form of *dāna*?

This question is vital because it touches upon the deepest dimension of Mahāyāna giving-theory: meditation-and-wisdom giving. Traditional theories of *dāna* — from the Pāli *dāna* to the Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā* — focus almost exclusively on the "act of giving": the donation of wealth, the sharing of dharma, or the bestowal of fearlessness.[^3] However, the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* presents a different dimension: *dāna* is not merely an act, but a state of being. When one attains perfect penetration through the ear-root, through the contemplation of fire, or through the contemplation of emptiness — this state of "reaching liberation through a specific conduit" is, in itself, a form of *dāna* toward all sentient beings.

This is not a metaphor. It is a structure embedded within the text of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* itself.

The core thesis of this paper can be compressed into a single sentence: **the twenty-five gateways of perfect penetration constitute the most complete map of *dāna*'s movement from the precept-body toward meditation and wisdom — every path is a form of giving, and every giving leads to perfect penetration.**

## II. A Map of Giving in the Twenty-Five Gateways: Structural Analysis

The structure of the twenty-five gateways is not random. Traditional commentaries have long categorized them into the five *skandhas* (aggregates), the five great elements, and the six sense-roots — twenty-five in total.[^4] Yet the implications of this classification for *dāna* have never been systematically revealed.

### 2.1 The *Dāna* of the Five Aggregates (*rūpa*, *vedanā*, *saṃjñā*, *saṃskāra*, *vijñāna*)

**The *dāna* of form (*rūpa*):** The saint of the form-aggregate attains perfect penetration through the contemplation of "physical form." Upaniṣad contemplates the impurity of the body, perceiving impermanence within the coarseness of the physical form, thereby achieving total enlightenment.[^5] The *dāna* dimension of this "impurity contemplation" is "impurity-giving" — using the body as a mirror so that the observer may see their own impurity and thus loosen their attachment. This is not "giving" in the traditional sense of providing an object, but rather transforming the body into a *bodhimaṇḍa* where sentient beings naturally let go through observation. In Mahāyāna treatises, the contemplation of impurity is often linked to the Bodhisattva's ability to transform beings by manifesting the abandonment of body-view — those who have deeply realized the impurity of the body naturally become a *dāna* that reveals to others the illusory nature of physical form.[^6] Impurity contemplation is thus a form of dharma-giving — not through language, but through the presentation of existence itself. The depth of form-giving lies in its direct challenge to the most fundamental delusion of sentient beings: the perceived solidity of the physical body. When one sees through the body's illusoriness via form-aggregate perfect penetration, that very existence becomes the most forceful gift toward breaking the view of self-as-body.

**The *dāna* of feeling (*vedanā*):** The saint of the feeling-aggregate attains perfect penetration through the contemplation of "sensations." The essence of feeling is suffering — pleasant feelings are impermanent, painful feelings are oppressive, and neutral feelings are obscured by ignorance. The *dāna* of the feeling-aggregate is "feeling-relinquishing giving" — allowing beings to see that all sensations are unreliable, thereby inspiring the relinquishing of attachment to feeling. In the *Śūraṅgama*, the saint of feeling attains perfect penetration by seeing feeling as illusory. The *dāna* implication is this: when a person is no longer pulled by sensations, their very existence becomes a fearlessness-bestowal to others — beings see in them the "possibility of not being oppressed by suffering." The core of feeling-giving is that when one remains unmoved by circumstances within sensation, their presence provides the greatest comfort to beings floating in the sea of suffering — for the one who has realized feeling-aggregate perfect penetration displays a mode of existence that is not drowned by sensation.

**The *dāna* of perception (*saṃjñā*):** The saint of the perception-aggregate attains perfect penetration through the contemplation of "conceptual memory." The essence of perception is discrimination — naming, classifying, and labeling. The *dāna* of the perception-aggregate is "name-breaking giving" — breaking the attachment of beings to nominal forms, so that names no longer become obstacles. In the *Śūraṅgama*, this is achieved by seeing perception as illusory. When one is no longer bound by concepts, their existence becomes a form of name-breaking giving — beings see in them the possibility of transcending nominal forms.

**The *dāna* of volition (*saṃskāra*):** The saint of the volition-aggregate attains perfect penetration through the contemplation of "volitional formations." The essence of volition is *karma* — construction and flux. The *dāna* of the volition-aggregate is "cessation-giving" — stopping the cycle of ignorant construction. In the *Śūraṅgama*, this is achieved by seeing volition as illusory. When one is no longer driven by volitional impulses, their existence becomes a cessation-giving — beings see in them the possibility of halting ignorant construction.

**The *dāna* of consciousness (*vijñāna*):** The saint of the consciousness-aggregate attains perfect penetration through the contemplation of "cognition itself." The essence of consciousness is discernment — the opposition of subject and object. The *dāna* of the consciousness-aggregate is "non-dual giving" — allowing beings to see equality within the subject-object split of cognition. In the *Śūraṅgama*, this is achieved by seeing consciousness as illusory. When one is no longer bound by the subject-object divide, their existence becomes a form of non-dual giving. The depth of consciousness-giving lies in the fact that it touches the most fundamental premise of *dāna* — the tripartite structure of giver, gift, and receiver is itself a cognitive illusion. When this structure is seen through, giving is no longer "I give to you," but the natural unfolding of existence.

### 2.2 The *Dāna* of the Six Sense-Roots (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind)

The *dāna* of the six roots shares a common structure: each root is simultaneously a "conduit for receiving the external world" and a "conduit for giving to the world."[^7] This is not an analogy, but the internal logic of the *Śūraṅgama* itself.

Avalokiteśvara's ear-root perfect penetration is the most obvious example: the ear-root is originally an organ for "hearing external sounds," but Avalokiteśvara "reverses" this conduit into "hearing the end of hearing" — not hearing external sounds, but hearing the act of hearing itself.[^8] This reversal is simultaneously a reversal of *dāna*: from "passive receiving" to "active giving." The ear-root is no longer just a receiver of sound, but a channel for transmitting compassion.

Mahāsthāmaprāpta's mind-root perfect penetration (Buddha-recollection *samādhi*) follows the same logic: the mind-root is originally an organ for "thinking about external objects," but Mahāsthāmaprāpta reverses the mind-root into the Buddha-recollection gateway — not thinking about the external world, but thinking of the Buddha.[^9] This reversal is also *dāna*: the mind-root becomes a channel for transmitting the Buddha's vows.

The structural formula for the *dāna* of the six roots can be compressed as: **the original function of the root = receiving; the reversed function of the root = giving.**

### 2.3 The *Dāna* of the Five Great Elements (earth, water, fire, wind, space)

The *dāna* of the five elements is more intuitive: earth (Earth-Holding), fire (Ucchuṣma), water (Candraprabha), wind (Vaiḍūryaprabha), and space (Ākāśagarbha) — each corresponds to a form of giving.

**Earth-giving** is the *dāna* of the earth-element — supporting the earth with the body, smoothing the roads. The story of the Earth-Holding Bhikkhu is the most concrete manifestation: he not only physically paved roads but, in his very existence, paved the way for sentient beings.[^10]

**Fire-giving** is the *dāna* of the fire-element — burning away afflictions and transforming them into wisdom. Ucchuṣma's flame-contemplation is not a destructive fire, but a transformative fire — burning the five aggregates into wisdom.[^11]

**Water-giving** is the *dāna* of the water-element — purity and moistening. Candraprabha's purity-contemplation is like moonlight purifying the earth, not selecting objects, but moistening all equally.[^12]

**Wind-giving** is the *dāna* of the wind-element — non-obstruction and freedom. Prince Vaiḍūryaprabha contemplates the rootlessness of the wind-element, awakens the *bodhicitta*, and enters *samādhi* — the observer does not linger on any object, moving like the wind without abiding.[^13]

**Space-giving** is the *dāna* of the space-element — giving everything, withholding nothing. Ākāśagarbha's space-contemplation is the ultimate form of *dāna*: giving all, while remaining unattached to any act of giving.[^14]

## III. Avalokiteśvara's Fourteen Fearlessness-Bestowals: The Paradigm of Explicit *Dāna*

Avalokiteśvara's ear-root gateway is the only one among the twenty-five gateways that is fully expanded in the text.[^15] He describes not only his method of practice but also the detailed fruits of that practice — making his section the most explicit link in the *dāna* chain.

### 3.1 The Thirty-Two Responsive Bodies: Body-Giving

The first fruit of Avalokiteśvara's practice is the "thirty-two responsive bodies entering various lands."[^16] This is not a mythological transformation, but a structural necessity of *dāna*: when one attains perfect penetration through the ear-root, one is no longer limited to a single form of existence but can present different bodies according to the needs of sentient beings.

The text states: "If Bodhisattvas enter *samādhi* and advance in the cultivation of the non-leaking superior understanding to achieve perfection; I manifest the Buddha-body to teach the dharma, allowing them to attain liberation."[^17] Here are two key elements of *dāna*:

First, "manifesting" is *dāna* — not giving an object, but giving oneself. Avalokiteśvara gives not an external item, but the form of his own existence.

Second, "teaching the dharma" is *dāna* — dharma-giving is another form of giving, complementing body-giving.

The thirty-two responsive bodies cover all possible forms of existence, from the Buddha-body to non-human bodies. This is not to demonstrate supernatural power, but to illustrate the ultimate form of *dāna*: the giver is no longer a subject who "gives," but naturally presents the form of existence that is "to be given" according to the conditions of the beings.

### 3.2 The Fourteen Fearlessness-Bestowals: Systematizing Fearlessness-Giving

The second fruit is the "fourteen kinds of fearlessness-virtues."[^18] This is the only section among the twenty-five gateways explicitly named as "giving" — "These are called the fourteen fearlessness-bestowals, providing abundance to sentient beings."[^19] The text explicitly uses the word "give" (*shī* 施).

The structure of the fourteen bestowals progresses layer by layer, dissolving fears from the outside in:

The first four bestowals deal with physical dangers (fire, water, ghosts, blades) — these are the most direct forms of fearlessness-giving, liberating beings from the fear of physical death. Fire cannot burn, water cannot drown, ghosts cannot harm, blades cannot wound — these four correspond to the most primitive fear: the fear of the destructive forces of the physical world. The compassionate power attained through ear-root perfect penetration first manifests as the transcendence of physical danger.

The fifth through seventh bestowals deal with supernatural dangers (darkness, imprisonment, perilous roads) — these are gifts that resolve non-physical fears, liberating beings from the fear of unseen forces. Darkness cannot overwhelm, chains and shackles cannot bind, and robbers cannot plunder on perilous roads — these three correspond to the fear of invisible forces. When one attains perfect penetration, their compassionate power extends into the supernatural realm.

The eighth through eleventh bestowals deal with internal afflictions (lust, anger, ignorance, greed) — these are gifts that resolve psychological fears, liberating beings from the fear of their own afflictions. Those with excessive lust are distanced from greed, those with anger are freed from hatred, and those with dullness and obstruction are freed from the darkness of ignorance — these four correspond to the fear of internal turmoil. When one attains perfect penetration, their very existence becomes the antidote to the internal afflictions of beings.

The twelfth through fourteenth bestowals deal with existential needs (seeking a son, seeking a daughter, collective practice of the name) — these are gifts that resolve existential fears, liberating beings from the fear regarding the purpose of existence. Those seeking a son obtain a son of virtue and wisdom; those seeking a daughter obtain a daughter of beauty, virtue, and gentleness; those who hold the name practice with Dharma-princes without difference in virtue. These three correspond to the fear over existential meaning. When one attains perfect penetration, their very name becomes a tool for beings to realize their existential purpose.

The complete scope of the fourteen bestowals is this: from the coarsest physical danger to the finest existential anxiety, nothing is left ungiven. This progression reveals a deep structure: the further inward and more subtle the giving, the greater its power — because it no longer touches a specific individual fear, but the fundamental fear of all sentient beings.

### 3.3 The Four Unimaginable Unconditioned Virtues: The Zenith of *Dāna*

The third fruit is the "four unimaginable unconditioned virtues."[^20] The fourth virtue is particularly critical:

> "I have attained the Buddha-mind and verified the ultimate; thus, I can offer various precious jewels to the Tathāgatas of the ten directions, and by extension, to all sentient beings in the six realms of the dharma-realm."[^21]

Here are two dimensions of *dāna*: upward offering to the Tathāgatas of the ten directions (the upward dimension of dharma-giving) and extension to the six realms of the dharma-realm (the downward dimension of *dāna*).

The "Buddha-mind" is the foundation of *dāna* — only one who has verified the Buddha-mind can engage in true giving. This is not a moral "should," but an existential "must." For one who has verified the Buddha-mind, giving is not a choice, but their very nature.

The first three virtues demonstrate three facets of *dāna*: the first, "manifesting many wonderful appearances and teaching boundless divine mantras," is a combination of body-giving and mantra-giving; the second, "manifesting one-by-one forms and reciting one-by-one mantras to bestow fearlessness upon beings," is the concretization of fearlessness-giving;[^22] the third, "causing beings to offer their bodies and precious jewels to seek my compassion," is the ultimate form of wealth-giving — where beings voluntarily relinquish their most precious possessions.

Together with the fourteen bestowals, these four virtues form a complete system of *dāna*: from body-giving to dharma-giving, from fearlessness-giving to wealth-giving, and from explicit to implicit giving.

## IV. Recollection-Giving and Dharma-Giving: Mahāsthāmaprāpta and Pūrṇa

### 4.1 The Structure of Recollection-Giving

Mahāsthāmaprāpta's Buddha-recollection *samādhi* is the most frequently overlooked dimension of *dāna* in the twenty-five gateways. His method centers on the dual dynamic of "mutual recollection": "Suppose there are two people, one specializing in remembering and the other in forgetting. If these two meet or do not meet, or see or do not see, and both remember each other with deep recollection, they will remain together, like a shadow, from life to life without deviation."[^22]

The *dāna* implication of the Buddha-recollection *samādhi* lies not in the *content* of the recollection (the Buddha), but in the *structure* of the recollection — the dual dynamic of "mutual recollection." The *Śūraṅgama* deepens this with the analogy of a mother and son: "The Tathāgatas of the ten directions pity and remember sentient beings, like a mother remembering her child; but if the child flees, what use is the remembering! If the child remembers the mother as the mother remembers the child, they will not be far apart through many lives." This "mutual recollection" is the core of *dāna*: giving is not "I give to you," but "I and you are together in mutual recollection."

Mahāsthāmaprāpta's recollection-giving is this: through his own practice of Buddha-recollection, he generates a "drawing-toward" of mutual recollection, guiding sentient beings toward the Buddha almost imperceptibly. This is not active teaching, but a dual resonance of mutual recollection — yet both are *dāna*.

The specificity of recollection-giving lies in its non-intentionality. Avalokiteśvara's fourteen fearlessness-bestowals are intentional gifts — Avalokiteśvara explicitly lists fourteen antidotes. But Mahāsthāmaprāpta's giving is unintentional — his very existence generates a drawing-toward. This makes recollection-giving a more fundamental form of *dāna*: not "I choose to give," but "my existence is giving."

### 4.2 Continuity Between Recollection-Giving and the Precept-Body

The *dāna* of the precept-body (the giving of not-stealing) established in P18 is here deepened: the precept-body is the foundation of *dāna*, and recollection-giving is its sublimation. Mahāsthāmaprāpta does not give by "not doing something" (as in the precept-body), but by "doing something" — through the act of Buddha-recollection itself to draw beings toward liberation.

### 4.3 Cross-Textual Contrast for Buddha-Recollection *Samādhi*

In the Mahāyāna treatise tradition, Buddha-recollection *samādhi* is understood as a gateway through which sentient beings make contact with the Buddhas, rather than only a method for individual self-verification.[^23] This indicates that Buddha-recollection *samādhi* is not only a personal practice but a medium through which beings encounter the Buddhas. This "mediality" is exactly the core of *dāna* — Mahāsthāmaprāpta's recollection is not for his own enlightenment, but to become a bridge through which beings reach the Buddhas.

### 4.4 Pūrṇa's Dharma-Giving: The Self-Combustion of the Teacher

Pūrṇa (富樓那) is the foremost among the Buddha's disciples in "expounding the dharma." His section occupies a unique position among the twenty-five gateways: he is the only saint explicitly marked as a "dharma-expounder."

**Expounding itself is dharma-giving.** Pūrṇa's *dāna*-theory is direct and profound: to expound the dharma is to make a gift of dharma. But the deeper meaning in the *Śūraṅgama* is not in this direct statement — it is in *how* Pūrṇa expounds: through the teaching of "all dharmas as not-self," he directly dissolves sentient beings' attachment to names and forms.

There is a key *dāna*-insight here: Pūrṇa's *manner* of expounding is itself a gift — not through content (the doctrine of not-self), but through manner (the not-self manner). When a person expounds with complete not-self, this "not-self expounding" is the highest *dāna* toward beings.

**The self-combustion of the expounder.** After the Buddha's *parinirvāṇa*, Pūrṇa went west into India to expound the dharma, facing many heterodox teachers and dissenting views. He did not fear these obstacles; rather, he entered them with fearlessness. This very "entering" is *dāna* — not waiting for beings to come and hear the dharma, but walking toward the obstacles where beings already are.

**The paradox of dharma-giving.** Here a paradox of *dāna*-theory must be faced: if expounding is dharma-giving, what is "not-self expounding"? If the expounder is utterly without self, who is expounding?

The Mahāyāna treatise tradition, when handling the bodhisattva's expounding, repeatedly emphasizes its character as "echo to sound — no master, no receiver": the expounder at the very moment of expounding has no self-nature to grasp, and there is no division of expounder-and-expounded.[^24] The expounder's *dāna* does not lie in "what was said," but in "the not-self manner in which it was said." This very manner of expounding is itself a dharma-giving to beings — because it lets beings hear "the not-self voice."

## V. Implicit *Dāna*: The Śrāvakas, the Aggregates, and the Sense-Roots

### 5.1 Mahākāśyapa's *Dhutaṅga*-Giving: The Zenith of Body-Giving

Mahākāśyapa (大迦葉) represents the *dhutaṅga* (austerity) practice in the twenty-five gateways. His practice is the practice of austerities — not asceticism for its own sake, but the loosening of attachment to body and mind through extreme simplicity.

**Dhutaṅga is body-giving.** Mahākāśyapa's *dhutaṅga* is a particular extreme of body-giving: he gives sentient beings not external goods but his own person as testimony — displaying the liberating force of the "not-necessary."[^25] Each of the thirteen *dhutaṅga* practices is a loosening of "necessity" — when one practices austerity, one is simultaneously giving with the body: showing through the body that "few desires" is possible.

The specificity of *dhutaṅga*-giving lies in its non-verbal character. Mahākāśyapa gives not through speech, but through his way of life: his patched robe, his sitting under trees, his dwelling among graves — each of these is a gift to beings, demonstrating that a life entirely undependent on external conditions is possible.

**The paradox of austerity-as-giving.** What does the ascetic give? The answer: a *reverse demonstration* — not telling beings "you should do this," but showing through one's body that "such a life can be lived." This is not imposition but disclosure of possibility.

The Mahāyāna treatise tradition, when discussing *dhutaṅga*, frequently connects it to the effect of "arousing in beings a mind of rarity (希有心)" — the value of austerity is not the austerity itself but the rarity-mind it arouses, which then leads beings to wish to hear the dharma. The key to *dhutaṅga*-giving is not the austerity, but the openness to new possibility that the austerity awakens in those who observe it.

**Mahākāśyapa's historical role of giving.** Mahākāśyapa convened the First Council after the Buddha's *parinirvāṇa* — itself a major *dāna* event. He gave through *dhutaṅga* during life, and through preserving the dharma after death. This *dāna* that crosses the boundary of life-and-death is the highest form of *dhutaṅga*-giving.

### 5.2 Śāriputra's Wisdom-Giving

Śāriputra (舍利弗) is renowned as the foremost in wisdom. His path to perfect penetration is through the contemplation of "form" — not the negation of form, but the realistic seeing of form-as-it-is.

The *dāna* implication for Śāriputra: wisdom itself is *dāna*. When Śāriputra attains perfect penetration through wisdom, his very existence becomes a mirror for sentient beings — allowing them to see "what wisdom looks like."

### 5.3 Subhūti's Emptiness-Giving

Subhūti's (須菩提) contemplation of emptiness is one of the deepest branches among the twenty-five gateways. He attains perfect penetration through the realization of "emptiness" — not as a conceptual void, but as an existential emptiness.

The *dāna* implication for Subhūti: emptiness itself is *dāna*. This does not mean that emptiness "gives" something, but that emptiness is what *makes giving possible* — only through emptiness can one give; only by being unattached can one give everything.

In the Prajñāpāramitā sūtras, Subhūti is often depicted as the representative of "non-stinginess toward the dharma" — his natural outflow of emptiness-realization is precisely the compassion that withholds nothing from any being.[^26] Subhūti's non-stinginess is not a moral choice but the natural overflow of realized emptiness.

### 5.4 Upāli's Precept-Giving

Upāli (優婆離) is renowned as the foremost in precept-keeping. His path to perfect penetration is through the contemplation of dharma — the realistic adherence to the precepts is itself *dāna*.

The *dāna* implication for Upāli: the precept itself is *dāna*. Keeping the precepts is not the passive "not doing" of something, but the active demonstration to beings of a possible mode of life — a life grounded in self-awareness rather than in coercion.

### 5.5 Mahāmaudgalyāyana's Supernatural-Power Giving

Mahāmaudgalyāyana (大目犍連) is renowned as the foremost in supernatural powers. His path to perfect penetration is through the contemplation of "fire" — the transformative nature of flame is itself *dāna*.

The *dāna* implication for Mahāmaudgalyāyana: supernatural power itself is *dāna*. His powers are not for the display of miracles but for letting beings see "the possibility of transcending physical limits." When Mahāmaudgalyāyana rescues beings through supernatural power, he is simultaneously performing body-giving — using his body to display the possibility of liberation.

### 5.6 Aniruddha's Eye-Root Giving

Aniruddha (阿那律) is renowned as the foremost in the divine eye. His path to perfect penetration is through the contemplation of the eye-root — the luminosity of the divine eye is itself *dāna*.

The *dāna* implication for Aniruddha: luminosity itself is *dāna*. When one obtains the power of luminous illumination through perfect penetration, one's very existence becomes a luminosity-giving to beings — letting beings see what they could not otherwise see.

### 5.7 The Structure of *Śrāvaka*-Giving

The *dāna* of the *śrāvaka* saints shares a common structure: **the verification of dharma is itself *dāna*.** They give not through acts but through being.

This is not an over-extension of the concept of *dāna*. It is the internal logic of the *Śūraṅgama* itself: when a saint attains perfect penetration, their realization of dharma naturally affects the world — this is not active giving, but passive radiance.

## VI. Middle-Way Corrections and Conclusion

### 6.1 The Three Layers of *Dāna* in the Twenty-Five Gateways

Before the conclusion, an integrative model is proposed.

**Explicit *dāna*:** Avalokiteśvara's fourteen fearlessness-bestowals are the paradigm of explicit *dāna* — the text itself uses the word "give." His thirty-two responsive bodies are also explicit *dāna* — he explicitly lists the pattern of "I manifest such-and-such a body to expound the dharma, causing them to attain liberation."

**Semi-explicit *dāna*:** Mahāsthāmaprāpta's recollection-giving and Pūrṇa's dharma-giving are semi-explicit — they do not use the word "give" explicitly, but their structure is patently *dāna*. Mahāsthāmaprāpta's "mutual recollection of two persons" is *dāna* in structure (the dual resonance of beings and Buddha); Pūrṇa's expounding is *dāna* in structure (dharma-giving).

**Implicit *dāna*:** Śāriputra's wisdom, Subhūti's emptiness, Mahākāśyapa's *dhutaṅga*, and Upāli's precept-keeping are implicit — they are not even structurally obvious as *dāna*, yet when read through the lens of *dāna*, each is a form of giving.

### 6.2 The Two Basic Modes of *Dāna*

The *dāna* in the twenty-five gateways can be reduced to two basic modes:

**Body-giving:** Avalokiteśvara's thirty-two responsive bodies, Mahākāśyapa's *dhutaṅga*, Mahāmaudgalyāyana's supernatural powers — these give through one's own existence or capacity.

**Dharma-giving:** Pūrṇa's expounding, Śāriputra's wisdom, Subhūti's emptiness — these give through realized dharma or through teaching.

These two modes are not in opposition — they complete each other. Body-giving is the vehicle of dharma-giving; dharma-giving is the meaning of body-giving.

### 6.3 The Unifying Formula of *Dāna*-Theory

The *dāna* of the five aggregates and six sense-roots can be compressed into a single formula:

**The original function of root/element = receiving from objects; the perfect-penetration function of root/element = giving to beings.**

This formula is not an analogy — it is a structural necessity of the *Śūraṅgama*. The process of perfect penetration for every root and every element is a "reversal" — from the receiving of objects to the giving to sentient beings.

### 6.4 Middle-Way Corrections

**Preventing reductionism: perfect penetration is not a "metaphor" for *dāna*.**

This paper's thesis must not be read as: "The twenty-five gateways are metaphors for twenty-five kinds of *dāna*."[^27] This is not metaphor. It is the structure of the *Śūraṅgama* itself — the natural fruit of every perfect penetration is *dāna*. Avalokiteśvara's fourteen fearlessness-bestowals are language the text itself uses; Mahākāśyapa's *dhutaṅga* is read in the commentary tradition as body-giving; Subhūti's emptiness-contemplation is read in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* as non-stinginess in giving.

An important distinction: this paper is not performing a retrospective projection but disclosing the text's own structure. When Avalokiteśvara explicitly uses "give" to describe his fourteen fruits, that is not our interpretation — it is the text's own language. Our argument is that this language is not isolated. The remaining twenty-four gateways, though not using "give" explicitly, follow the same logic in their natural fruits as does Avalokiteśvara's *dāna*.

**Preventing over-extension: not all meditation is *dāna*.**

Not every meditative practice is *dāna*. The critical distinction: a meditative practice is *dāna* when it leads to the loosening of "I-grasping" and naturally flows toward the benefit of beings. If a practice strengthens the perceived solidity of the "I" — whether the "I who can practice" or the "dharma which is practiced" — it is not *dāna*.

The practical significance is this: whether a practice is *dāna* does not depend on its outer form (austerity, expounding, supernatural power can each be *dāna* or not) but on its inner structure — whether it loosens self-grasping and flows naturally to the benefit of others.

**The boundary of *śrāvaka*-giving.**

The *dāna* of the *śrāvaka* saints requires particular care. They are not bodhisattvas; their practice is aimed at personal liberation. But this is precisely where the depth of the *Śūraṅgama* lies: even practice aimed at personal liberation, once perfect penetration is attained, has natural fruits that benefit beings. This is not to say *śrāvaka* "equals" bodhisattva, but that the structure of perfect penetration itself flows naturally toward *dāna*.

**The boundary of cross-textual comparison.**

This paper draws on the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* to compare the *Śūraṅgama*'s giving-theory, but care is required. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* is a Mahāyāna treatise, while the authenticity of the *Śūraṅgama*'s transmission has long been debated (Mochizuki Shinkō and others have argued it is a Chinese composition). Whatever the historical origin, its function in East Asian Buddhism is beyond dispute — it has shaped the *dāna*-understanding of countless practitioners. This paper attends to that function, not to historical origin.

### 6.5 Conclusion: The Twenty-Five Gateways as a Map of *Dāna*

Through four layers of argument, this paper has established a long-overlooked thesis: the twenty-five gateways chapter of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* is not merely a catalogue of meditation entry-points, but a complete map of *dāna*.

**First layer — explicit *dāna*:** Avalokiteśvara's fourteen fearlessness-bestowals are the most explicit manifestation of *dāna*; the text itself uses the word "give."

**Second layer — recollection-giving and dharma-giving:** Mahāsthāmaprāpta's Buddha-recollection *samādhi* shows the zenith of recollection-giving — drawing beings toward liberation through one's own practice; Pūrṇa's expounding shows the zenith of dharma-giving — expounding through the not-self manner.

**Third layer — implicit *dāna*:** The passive practice of the *śrāvaka* saints — Śāriputra's wisdom, Subhūti's emptiness, Mahākāśyapa's *dhutaṅga*, Upāli's precept-keeping — each is a form of giving.

**Fourth layer — structural *dāna*:** The *dāna* formula of the five aggregates and six roots discloses that the perfect-penetration process of every root and every element is a "reversal" — from the receiving of objects to the giving to beings.

**Significance for practice and understanding.** This finding offers a direct inspiration for practice: every meditation entry-point is *dāna*. It is not only "the giving of wealth" or "expounding the dharma" that constitutes *dāna* — when you cultivate perfect penetration through the ear-root, the practice itself is *dāna*; when you cultivate through fire-contemplation, the practice itself is *dāna*.

This transforms the entire understanding of *dāna*: giving is not merely act but state-of-being. When one attains perfect penetration through any conduit, one's state of existence naturally becomes a gift to beings.

**An open question for the next paper.** If all twenty-five gateways are *dāna*, then which gateway of giving is most suited to the beings of this era? Mañjuśrī's selection answers (Avalokiteśvara is supreme), but is this answer itself — "the ear-root is supreme" — also a form of *dāna*?

**Double significance for scholarship and practice.** For scholarship, this finding suggests that research on *dāna* has long been confined to the tripartite framework of wealth-giving, dharma-giving, and fearlessness-giving, whereas the twenty-five gateways supply fifteen or more unique categories of *dāna*. This not only expands the scope of *dāna* research but supplies a framework for re-examining the giving-theory in other Mahāyāna scriptures.

For practitioners, this means that every meditation entry-point is a gift to beings. The practitioner need not wait until they "have the capacity" before giving — present practice is itself *dāna*. This understanding breaks the dichotomy of "self-benefit first, then benefit of others," letting every present moment of practice become a gift to all beings.
