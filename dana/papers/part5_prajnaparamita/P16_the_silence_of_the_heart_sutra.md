# The Silence of the Heart Sūtra

**Giving's Disappearance as Theological Statement**

## Abstract

This paper examines the Heart Sūtra's (T0251) silence on dāna-pāramitā, arguing that giving's absence is not oversight but theological statement. Using CBETA primary sources, systematic comparison across seven Chinese Heart Sūtra translations, Kuiji's *Xinjing Youzan* (T33n1710), and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), alongside the Āgama's giving-wisdom pairing tradition and the *Chiliastikon*'s six pāramitās sequence, I argue that the Heart Sūtra's silence on dāna is not passive omission but active theological choice. Seven doctrinal Heart Sūtra translations all contain zero mentions of dāna--silence is a structural, cross-version feature. Kuiji's "總合說故" explicitly states that the Heart Sūtra's "no cognition and no attainment" comprehensively expresses all six pāramitās, so dāna's absence is subsumption not omission. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* formalizes the six pāramitās into two poles--"the merit group" (功德分: dāna first) and "the wisdom group" (慧明分: prajñā last)--and the Heart Sūtra presents only the wisdom pole. The metaphor "when the king arrives, his retinue follows"--prajñāpāramitā as the guiding principle of all six pāramitās means naming prajñā implicitly includes dāna. "Wealth autonomy is gained through giving"--dāna's achieved presence in the sūtra's framework of Avalokiteśva's ten autonomies. The core finding: the Heart Sūtra's silence on dāna is not passive omission but active theological choice--it provides the condition under which giving becomes pāramitā (perfection), rather than discussing dāna itself.

**Keywords:** absence of giving, comprehensive expression, merit group, wisdom group, adaptation to context, Heart Sūtra, Kuiji's commentary, Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra, king-and-retinue metaphor, wealth autonomy, Avalokiteśva, six pāramitās, giving-wisdom pairing, Chinese-composition hypothesis, liturgical text

> "Avalokiteśvara bodhisattva, while practicing deeply prajñāpāramitā, saw that the five skandhas are empty and thereby liberated from all suffering and anguish."
> "No eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind; no form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or object of mind; no realm of eyes and so on down to no realm of mind-consciousness; no ignorance and no end of ignorance and so on down to no aging-and-death and no end of aging-and-death; no suffering, origination, cessation, or path; no cognition and no attainment."
> -- *Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya Sūtra* (T0251, Xuanzang trans.), CBETA T08n0251 0848c06-18

These twenty characters of the Heart Sūtra's core passage have been chanted by tens of thousands of practitioners in Chinese temples for eight hundred years. It is the most widely recited text in East Asian Buddhism--short enough to recite in two minutes, yet deep enough to contain all interpretation.

But one question has lingered unresolved: **Where is dāna (giving)?**

Not just any "where"--but the fundamental question: In the six pāramitās (perfections) of the bodhisattva path, giving has always been listed first. From the Āgamas' "five perfections" (faith, morality, learning, giving, wisdom) to the Great Prajñāpāramitā's six-pāramitās sequence (giving→morality→concentration→wisdom), from the seven sacred riches' "riches of giving → riches of wisdom" to the *Chiliastikon*'s "Chapter on the Perfection of Giving as First"--the giving-wisdom pairing is a cross-domain consensus from early to middle Buddhism.

But when the Heart Sūtra compresses the bodhisattva path into "no cognition and no attainment," giving doesn't even appear. Not as "no giving"--which would at least be an affirmative negation. It doesn't even deserve negation.

Kuiji (窺基) provided the most direct answer in his *Xinjing Youzan* (*Heart Sūtra Profound Commentary*, T33n1710):

> "For those seeking the bodhisattva path, six pāramitās should be taught; yet [the Heart Sūtra] speaks only of 'no cognition and no attainment,' **because these comprehensively express [all six]**."[^1]

This phrase is the key to P16: the Heart Sūtra's silence on giving is not omission but **subsumption**. Giving is not in the Heart Sūtra's text not because it is unimportant, but because it is "comprehensively" included within the single phrase "no cognition and no attainment"--the entire six pāramitās, including giving, are within this compression.

P16's task is to open this silence into a door, not close it into a wall.

## I. From Non-Abiding to Unsaid: The Compression Trajectory of Giving's Theology

### I.1 The Question: When Giving Disappears

P15 established "non-abiding giving" (無住施) through the *Vajracchedikā-prajñāpāramitā* (Diamond Sūtra) -- the bodhisattva should "practice giving without abiding in anything," and merit is boundless precisely because it is empty. The Diamond Sūtra's attitude toward giving is paradoxical: it doesn't cancel giving, but uses paradoxical language to protect giving's non-grasping nature.

But the Heart Sūtra goes further--it even withdraws the character "giving."

This question is not a Western Buddhist studies invention. Tang dynasty Chinese monks who recited the Heart Sūtra, if they thought of the six pāramitās, would surely ask: "Where is giving?" Kuiji's answer is "comprehensively expressed"--giving is included within. But "comprehensively expressed" itself needs interpretation: Why can it be comprehensive? What does comprehensive expression equal?

P16's core thesis: **The Heart Sūtra comprehensively expresses the six pāramitās through "no cognition and no attainment" (Kuiji's phrase); giving's absence is subsumption, not omission.** This thesis contains three levels:

First, from the Āgamas to the Great Prajñāpāramitā, the pairing of giving and wisdom is a cross-domain consensus--the Heart Sūtra's giving-absence is not a natural occurrence but an intentional choice against this historical depth.

Second, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509) established a "merit group vs. wisdom group" binary classification; the Heart Sūtra's radical choice is to present only the wisdom pole and silence the entire merit group--but this silence is not negation, but merit-group's return-capture from the wisdom pole.

Third, prajñā "when the king arrives, his retinue follows"--naming prajñā implicitly includes all six pāramitās, including giving. The Heart Sūtra's silence is the most radical theology of giving: giving is accomplished *through* prajñā, not replaced by it.

### I.2 The Historical Depth of the Giving-Wisdom Pairing

To understand why the Heart Sūtra's silence is an intentional choice, we must first establish the baseline against which silence operates. This baseline is not a vague traditional impression--but specific, multi-textual, cross-linguistic five sets of giving-wisdom pairing formats.

**First set: The seven sacred riches (七聖財).** The seven sacred riches is one of the most stable giving-wisdom pairing formats in early Buddhism. The *Dirgha-āgama* states: "riches of faith, riches of morality, riches of conscience, riches of shame, riches of learning, **riches of giving**, riches of wisdom--these are the seven riches."[^2] Of the seven riches, the riches of giving and riches of wisdom are fixed adjacent--not randomly arranged, but in the cultivation sequence, giving is the necessary step toward wisdom. The *Madhya-āgama* similarly lists seven riches: "riches of faith, riches of morality, riches of conscience, riches of shame, riches of learning, **riches of giving**, and one who has achieved the riches of wisdom, bhikṣu, should know."[^3] The riches of giving is the prerequisite for the riches of wisdom, the two connected forming a complete cultivation path. The structure of the seven riches is: the first three (faith, morality, conscience) are foundations of personal virtue; the middle three (shame, learning, giving) are the transition from individual to community; the final one (wisdom) is the terminus of liberation. Giving plays a crucial role here--it is the bridge from "personal cultivation" to "liberating wisdom."

**Second set: The five perfections (五具足).** In multiple Āgama texts, the five perfections are listed as "faith, morality, learning, **giving**, **wisdom**"--giving in fourth, wisdom in fifth, adjacent. This sequence appears across T0100, T0099, T0125 and other Āgama recensions, showing it is not the choice of individual translators but the common tradition of early Buddhism. The structural implication of the five perfections is: faith (confidence) → morality (behavioral norm) → learning (knowledge accumulation) → giving (altruistic practice) → wisdom (liberating insight). Note giving's position--it is not the final step, but it is the necessary condition for wisdom. In other words, without giving's practice, wisdom cannot arise.

**Third set: The giving-discourse → morality-discourse → heaven-arising sequence.** This is the Buddha's standard teaching mode for non-believers: first give (the merit of giving), then morality (the benefit of moral conduct), then heaven-arising (the fruit of moral conduct leading to rebirth in heaven), and only then the four noble truths. This sequence appears in over twenty Āgama passages, showing giving is the **first ring** of the Buddha's teaching--not an optional addition, but a necessary first step. The deeper structure is: giving (counteracting avarice) → morality (counteracting evil conduct) → heaven-arising (building confidence) → four noble truths (guiding to liberation). Giving is the starting point of the entire teaching system.

**Fourth set: The Pāli *cāga-paññā* pairing.** In the Pāli tradition, *cāga* (giving) and *paññā* (wisdom) are also paired. AN confirms across seven sections that *saddhā-sīla-suta-cāga-paññā* (faith, morality, learning, giving, wisdom) has cāga adjacent to paññā. This arrangement almost exactly matches the Chinese five perfections (faith, morality, learning, giving, wisdom)--showing this is a cross-linguistic common tradition.

**Fifth set: SN 55.39's structural substitution.** SN 55.39 shows *cāga* can occupy the structural position normally held by *paññā* in the stream-entry factors--giving not only pairs with wisdom, but in certain contexts can substitute for wisdom's role. This is the most radical example of giving-wisdom pairing: giving is not just wisdom's prerequisite, but wisdom's **substitute**.

The consensus of these five formats is clear: **giving and wisdom are inseparable.** Giving is wisdom's prerequisite; wisdom is giving's completion. They are like the wings of a bird. And the Heart Sūtra's silence precisely erases this giving-wisdom pairing consensus that holds across five distinct contextual frameworks in early Buddhism.

### I.3 The *Chiliastikon*: Giving First Among the Six

If the Āgama tradition established the consensus of giving-wisdom pairing, then the *Chiliastikon* (六度集經, T0152, Kang Senghui's translation, Three Kingdoms period) formally organized the bodhisattva path's cultivation framework through the six pāramitās sequence.

> "What are the six? **First, giving**; second, morality; third, patience; fourth, effort; fifth, meditation; sixth, the perfection of wisdom."[^4]

The chapter title is explicit: "Chapter on the Perfection of Giving as **First**" (T03, 0001a06); the opening definition: "What is the perfection of giving? Its principle is this: loving and nurturing beings, compassionate toward all wrongs..." (T03, 0001a14). The six pāramitās begin with giving and end with prajñā ("the perfection of wisdom"). Giving is the starting point of the six pāramitās, and the prerequisite for prajñā.

Xuanzang's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* similarly places giving first among the six pāramitās and establishes the giving→morality→concentration→wisdom causal chain: "First, one should practice the perfection of giving... **Through giving and morality, one attains the concentration-aggregate; through giving, morality, and concentration, one attains the wisdom-aggregate.**"[^5] Giving is the first link of wisdom's causal chain--without giving, there is no wisdom.

Understanding this cross-textual consensus, the Heart Sūtra's silence reveals its depth.

## II. The Map of Absence: From the Great Text to the Heart Sūtra

### II.1 Ten Translation Scan: Silence as Cross-Version Structural Feature

Before interpretation, let us establish facts.

Seven doctrinal recensions (T0250-T0255, T0257) of the Chinese Heart Sūtra translations contain **zero** instances of giving / dāna / pāramitā of giving / dāna-pāramitā / six pāramitās. The absence is comprehensive, cross-version, and cross-translator:

- T0250 (Kumārajīva, Later Qin): No "giving"
- T0251 (Xuanzang, Tang): No "giving"
- T0252 (Facheng, Tubo/Tibetan): No "giving"
- T0253 (Prajñā, Song): No "giving"
- T0254 (Yijing, Tang): No "giving"
- T0255 (Xuanfayi, Sui): No "giving"
- T0257 (Bodhiruci, Tang): No "giving"

The absence is comprehensive, cross-version, and cross-translator--from Kumārajīva (Later Qin) to Yijing (Tang), from Faxiang (Tibetan) to Prajñā (Song), silence is the structural feature of the entire text tradition.

This is not the choice of individual translators. If one translator thought giving was unimportant for the Heart Sūtra, that would be a personal judgment; but seven translators, spanning centuries of independent translation, all similarly excluded giving from the Heart Sūtra--silence is the Heart Sūtra text's own structure.

Further: if the Heart Sūtra originated from the Indian tradition, this silence continues the Indian prajñāpāramitā literature's "unsaid" on giving. If the Heart Sūtra is Chinese-composed (see §IV.1), then this silence is Chinese Buddhism's theological judgment on the essence of prajñā. In either case, silence is structural.

### II.2 Great Text Has Giving, Heart Sūtra Doesn't: Adaptation to Context

Faced with the same core meaning (five skandhas are empty, twelve āyatanas and eighteen dhātus are empty, no cognition and no attainment), why does the Great Text have giving while the Heart Sūtra doesn't?

Fazang (T1712) proposed the principle of "adaptation to context" (廣略隨緣) in his *Heart Sūtra Commentary*: "If detailed account of each matter, words would exceed two hundred thousand verses; if extracting the essence, **truth is contained in fourteen lines**."[^6] The六百卷 Great Prajñāpāramitā and the fourteen-line Heart Sūtra are not contradictory--they are different expansions of the same meaning in different contexts.

"Adaptation to context" means: the Great Text (all six pāramitās present) is the extensive teaching; the Heart Sūtra (pure prajñā) is the compressed teaching. Compressed does not negate extensive--it chooses the most essential part. The Heart Sūtra chose prajñā, not because the other five pāramitās are unimportant, but because prajñā has the capacity of comprehensively including all.

But here is a crucial question: why is it prajñā that is chosen, not giving? If adaptation to context is an equal choice--any pāramitā could serve as the essential pivot--then why did the Heart Sūtra choose prajñā?

The answer may lie in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s "when the king arrives": prajñā is the pivot of the six pāramitās precisely because it is the guiding principle of all six. Only prajñā has the capacity to subsume the six pāramitās. Giving cannot serve as the essential pivot because giving itself cannot subsume the other five pāramitās.

But this answer brings another question: if only prajñā can serve as the essential pivot, then the Heart Sūtra's choice of prajñā implies--**the subsumption of the six pāramitās lies not in any of the six pāramitās, but in the condition that makes the six pāramitās pāramitās.** This condition is prajñā.

Therefore, the Heart Sūtra's choice is not arbitrary--it is the necessary result of prajñāpāramitā tradition's internal logic.

### II.3 Silence Is Not Passive--It Is an Active Choice

Silence has three possibilities: silence from ignorance (not knowing what to say), silence from inability (unable to say), and intentional silence (choosing not to say).

The Heart Sūtra's silence belongs to the third type. The reason is simple: the Heart Sūtra's author (or compiler) has profound mastery of the prajñāpāramitā tradition--if they didn't understand the prajñāpāramitā tradition, they could not have written the Heart Sūtra. So, one who deeply knows the prajñāpāramitā tradition, while writing a compressed version of the prajñāpāramitā sūtra, **intentionally does not write giving**--this intentional non-writing itself is a theological statement.

Like a musician thoroughly versed in music theory who chooses to compose a piece with only three notes--this is not because they cannot write the fourth note, but because they choose not to. Choosing not to write is more powerful than writing.

## III. Theological Interpretation: Comprehensive Expression and the King's Train

### III.1 The Merit Group vs. the Wisdom Group: The Binary Structure of the Six Pāramitās

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509, vol. 27) establishes an extremely important classification:

> "These two things have six parts of practice, named the six pāramitās: **giving, morality, and patience are the merit group; effort, meditation, and wisdom are the wisdom group**."[^7]

The six pāramitās are divided into two axes: the merit axis (led by giving) and the wisdom axis (peaked by prajñā). These two axes each have their function--the merit group accumulates merit resources, the wisdom group develops wisdom resources. Both are indispensable.

The deep structure of this classification is worth noting: the three pāramitās of the merit group (giving, morality, patience) all involve **counteraction**--giving counteracts avarice, morality counteracts evil conduct, patience counteracts anger--all are active practices of counteracting afflictions. The three pāramitās of the wisdom group (effort, meditation, wisdom) lean more toward **development**--effort develops virtuous dharmas, meditation develops one-pointedness of mind, wisdom develops reality-cognition.

The Heart Sūtra's radical choice is to **present only the wisdom pole**. The five skandhas are empty, the twelve āyatanas and eighteen dhātus are empty, no ignorance and no end of ignorance--all belong to the wisdom group's discursive framework. The merit group (giving, morality, patience) is completely silent within this framework.

This silence is not inadvertence. If it were inadvertence, the Heart Sūtra should at least mention "although practicing prajñā, not abandoning giving and so on"--even as a passing reference. But even that is absent.

Notably, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* places "patience" in the merit group rather than the wisdom group--this is an extremely important classificatory decision. Patience involves interaction with others (enduring others' insults) and is the most important altruistic practice within the merit group. The Heart Sūtra completely silences patience--this is not an accidental omission, but an intentional non-engagement with any end of the merit group.

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* states earlier: "Through the causes and conditions of merit and wisdom, all aspirations are fulfilled"--two things equally valued. The Heart Sūtra's silence is an **intentional shift** against this equal valuation.

### III.2 Kuiji's "Comprehensively Expressed" -- Silence as Subsumption

Kuiji's "comprehensively expressed" is the core key to interpreting the Heart Sūtra's theology of giving. His full context is:

> "The school of absolute emptiness (*shengkong*) says: 'Previously there was no hearing-and-near-contemplation for śrāvakas, nor for bodhisattvas here. That which certifies the path is called cognition; that which is certified is called attainment. Where there is certifiable cognition, there can be attainment; where certifying cognition is not-existent, the attained is also empty. As the sūtra says, **for those seeking the bodhisattva path, six pāramitās should be taught; yet [the Heart Sūtra] speaks only of 'no cognition and no attainment,' comprehensively expressed.** If dharmas were not empty, initially there would be practice, and later attainment could follow; but dharmas are not-existent, so initially there is no practice, and how could there be later attainment?'"[^8]

The key is after "as the sūtra says"--Kuiji references some sūtra (possibly a passage from the Great Prajñāpāramitā where the six pāramitās are listed together), saying bodhisattvas should practice six pāramitās, but the Heart Sūtra "speaks only of 'no cognition and no attainment.'" Why only these two? Because these two characters **comprehensively express** all six pāramitās.

"Comprehensively expressed" is not omission. Omission is "it should have been said but wasn't"--like a checklist missing one item. Comprehensive expression is "one term encompasses everything"--like a single sentence containing an entire book.

The crucial distinction: if the Heart Sūtra's attitude toward giving were "omission," then adding giving would suffice--insert "no giving" into the Heart Sūtra. But doing so would destroy the Heart Sūtra's power. Why? Because the power of "comprehensive expression" lies not in how much is said, but in **saying the most with the least**.

Kuiji's logical argument contains two steps:

Step one: "No cognition" encompasses what certifies the path. Each of the six pāramitās (giving, morality, patience, effort, meditation, prajñā) is a manifestation of "cognition"--giving is cognition's manifestation (giving without attainment), morality is cognition's manifestation (holding without attainment), patience is cognition's manifestation (enduring without attainment). Therefore, saying "no cognition" means the cognitive aspect of all six pāramitās is empty.

Step two: "No attainment" encompasses what is certified. Each of the six pāramitās aims at "attainment"--giving attains merit, morality attains heaven-rebirth, patience attains forbearance, effort attains virtuous dharmas, meditation attains concentration, prajñā attains nirvāṇa. Therefore, saying "no attainment" means the fruit aspect of all six pāramitās is empty.

Combined: "no cognition and no attainment" means both the cause (cognition) and the fruit (attainment) of all six pāramitās are empty. This is the logic of "comprehensive expression"--not listing the six pāramitās, but using the negation of their shared condition (cognition and attainment) to encompass the entire six.

This is not literal inclusion. It is **structural inclusion**--the Heart Sūtra's entire framework (non-attainment) is the shared condition of the six pāramitās. For giving to become a true pāramitā (perfection), it must have "non-attainment" as its condition. The Heart Sūtra is not *discussing* giving--the Heart Sūtra is *providing the condition* under which giving becomes giving.

There is a subtle philosophical point here: the Heart Sūtra doesn't "not discuss giving"--it "elevates giving to a level where it doesn't need to be discussed." In the ultimate truth, giving itself is empty--there is no need to specifically point out giving, because giving's inherent nature is already empty. The Heart Sūtra directly says emptiness, which already means that giving (and all other dharmas) has inherent emptiness.

### III.3 When the King Arrives, His Retinue Follows -- Prajñā as Giving's Guide

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* vol. 59 contains an extremely important argument:

> "Previously said: all dharmas have prajñāpāramitā as their head, as their guide; **like a king who arrives with his retinue in tow -- when only the master's name is mentioned, the rest are already all obtained**."[^9]

"when the king arrives, his retinue follows" is P16's core rhetorical framework: prajñā is like the king, the other five pāramitās are like the king's retinue. When the king arrives, the retinue necessarily follows--there is no need to name each retinue member, because "the king arrives" already includes the retinue's presence.

Applied to the Heart Sūtra: the Heart Sūtra names "prajñā" just as it names the king. Giving is not in the Heart Sūtra's text, just as the retinue is not in the king's roll-call--but giving has arrived in prajñā's train.

Nāgārjuna elaborates in the same context:

> "When a bodhisattva practices giving, prajñāpāramitā serves as its guide, **enabling the perfection of dāna-pāramitā**; when practicing morality... patience... effort... meditation... observing dharmas, prajñāpāramitā serves as its guide, enabling the perfection of prajñāpāramitā."[^10]

The key phrases are "serves as guide" and "enabling"--prajñā is not one among the six pāramitās, but the **guide** of all six pāramitās. Six pāramitās without a guide are a scattered set of practices; six pāramitās with prajñā as guide form a complete system.

Here is an extremely important logic: Nāgārjuna doesn't say "prajñā replaces the other five pāramitās," but "prajñā enables the other five to become pāramitās." In other words, giving still needs to exist--but only combined with prajñā does giving transform from "secular giving" to "pāramitā-level giving."

This passage is P16's most important counter-argument: **prajñā does not replace giving, but fulfills giving.** "Enabling the perfection of dāna-pāramitā"--without prajñā's guidance, giving is not a pāramitā; with prajñā's guidance, giving becomes a pāramitā.

The Heart Sūtra's silence thereby produces a profound paradox: the Heart Sūtra doesn't mention giving, but the premise it provides (prajñāpāramitā as guide) is precisely the condition under which giving becomes giving. In other words, the Heart Sūtra is not *discussing* giving--the Heart Sūtra is **creating** the premises under which giving becomes possible.

This is not circular reasoning. This is a fundamental theological insight: **giving's accomplishment lies not within giving itself, but within prajñā.** Giving without prajñā is merely secular giving; giving with prajñā is pāramitā. The Heart Sūtra doesn't discuss giving because it focuses on the fundamental condition under which giving becomes giving--prajñā.

Understanding this insight requires the two characters "明導" (guide). "明導" means "one who illuminates the path." Prajñā illuminates giving's path--transforming giving from blind giving into wise giving. The Heart Sūtra doesn't mention giving, like a guide who doesn't name each traveler--but illuminates each traveler's path.

### III.4 Wealth Autonomy Gained Through Giving -- Giving's Achieved Presence in Silence

The final argument comes from Kuiji's interpretation of "Avalokiteśvara bodhisattva." Avalokiteśva in the Heart Sūtra is not an ordinary bodhisattva--he is the display of the six pāramitās' accomplishment. Kuiji lists the ten autonomies gained through the six pāramitās:

> "Third, wealth autonomy, able to manifest as beings wish, **gained through giving**. Fourth, karma autonomy... gained through morality. Fifth, birth autonomy... gained through patience. Sixth, understanding autonomy... gained through effort. Seventh, aspiration autonomy... gained through meditation. Eighth, power autonomy... gained through wisdom."[^11]

The structure here is: each autonomy has a corresponding pāramitā. Wealth autonomy gained through giving--this means giving is not absent from the Heart Sūtra's realm of accomplishment, but present as **achieved presence**.

Here is an extremely subtle structural correspondence: the Heart Sūtra's speaker is "Avalokiteśvara bodhisattva." Avalokite (observing) is prajñā (wisdom); zizai (autonomy) is the totality of the six pāramitās. But note: among the eight autonomies of "zizai," the third "wealth autonomy" directly stems from giving. In other words, Avalokiteśva's "autonomy"--his capacity for liberated manifestation--has one fundamental source: giving.

Avalokiteśva's Heart Sūtra role is "practicing deeply prajñāpāramitā and seeing the five skandhas as empty"--he displays prajñā's accomplishment. And wealth autonomy (manifesting goods as beings wish) is one of the fruits of the six pāramitās' accomplishment, this fruit stemming from giving. Avalokiteśva's "observing" displays prajñā; his "autonomy" displays the totality of the six pāramitās--where wealth autonomy directly stems from giving.

Therefore, giving's mode of presence in the Heart Sūtra is not "textual presence" but **structural presence**--the Heart Sūtra's choice of Avalokiteśva as the speaker already displays giving's accomplishment at the structural level. Avalokiteśva's "observing" is prajñā, but his "autonomy" is the totality of the six pāramitās, where wealth autonomy directly stems from giving.

This is a reversed argument: giving is not in the Heart Sūtra's text, but the entire identity of the Heart Sūtra's chosen speaker (Avalokiteśva) is the display of giving's accomplishment. Giving's presence is not in speech, but in **manifestation**.

A deeper layer: the Heart Sūtra opens with "Avalokiteśvara bodhisattva"--this is the first noun of the entire sūtra. Avalokiteśva's name is not accidental. Kuiji explains "observing" as "wisdom that understands emptiness and existence," and "autonomy" as "the meaning of freedom, the obtained supreme fruit." But "the obtained supreme fruit" stems from "previously practicing the six pāramitās"--Avalokiteśva is autonomous precisely because he practiced the six pāramitās in the past, among which giving is first. Therefore, giving is an inseparable part of the entire definition of Avalokiteśva's name, the first noun in the Heart Sūtra.

This means: although the Heart Sūtra doesn't mention giving in its body, in the **choice of speaker at the opening**, giving is already included. This is not wordplay--it is the theology of structure. The Heart Sūtra's theology of giving is not hidden in the text, but hidden in **who is speaking**.

## IV. Cross-Textual Dialogue: Three Western Readings

### IV.1 Nattier: The Chinese-Composition Hypothesis -- Silence as Theological Choice

Jan Nattier (1992) proposed an extremely controversial argument: the Heart Sūtra may be a text composed in China (Tang dynasty) in Chinese, not translated from Sanskrit.[^12]

If this argument holds, P16's "giving's absence" gains a deeper interpretive framework: silence is not the natural result of the prajñāpāramitā tradition's internal logic, but an **active theological choice of Tang Chinese Buddhism on the essence of prajñā**. Chinese Buddhism chose to define the essence of the bodhisattva path as "pure prajñā," and giving was intentionally removed from the essence framework.

This means the Heart Sūtra's silence is not passive--it is a choice. The result of this choice: the six pāramitās of the bodhisattva path are compressed into one, and this one is not any of the six pāramitās, but the **shared condition** of all six pāramitās (prajñā).

This carries a deeper consideration: if Nattier is correct, it actually strengthens P16's core thesis. Because if the Heart Sūtra is Chinese-composed, then the silence is not "continuing the Indian tradition"--it is **Chinese people's choice**. An intentional, theologically-directed choice. The result: the essence of the bodhisattva path is defined as prajñā--not because the other five pāramitās are unimportant, but because prajñā is what makes the other five pāramitās pāramitās.

But even if Nattier is correct, P16's analysis remains valid--because even if the Heart Sūtra is Chinese-composed, its compiler uses the vocabulary and structure of the Great Prajñāpāramitā repository. Its structural choice (taking only prajñā, comprehensively expressing the six pāramitās) still reflects the theological judgment on prajñāpāramitā's essence at that time. Whether the Heart Sūtra originates from India or China, **the theology of silence** is the same phenomenon: choosing not to speak is itself speaking.

### IV.2 Sharf: The Heart Sūtra as Liturgical Text -- Giving's Performative Presence

Robert Sharf's recent research emphasizes the Heart Sūtra as a **liturgical text**--the Heart Sūtra is primarily used in ritual recitation, not read as a doctrinal treatise.[^13]

This perspective provides an unexpected supplement to P16: reciting the Heart Sūtra itself is a form of **dharma-dāna** (dharma-giving)--giving is not in the Heart Sūtra's text, but is cashed out in the performative behavior of the text. When a monk chants the Heart Sūtra and dedicates it to all beings, he is practicing giving--not in the text, but in the behavior.

Sharf's position produces a reversal on "giving's absence": giving is not omitted, but transferred to the text's "form of use." What is not said in text is said in behavior.

This reversal's power lies in revealing another dimension of giving: giving is not only giving material goods or teaching dharma, but **using textual performance as giving**. The moment the Heart Sūtra is chanted, giving is already happening--not in the words, but in the chanting.

But this reversal also carries risk: the liturgical reading may hollow out the theology of giving. Chanting the Heart Sūtra as dharma-giving is not the same as practicing giving--the former is symbolic, the latter is real. If one responds to P16's question merely with "chanting equals giving," one may overlook giving's true difficulty--not just speaking of giving, but actually giving.

Further: Sharf's liturgical reading and P16's core thesis (comprehensive expression) form an interesting correspondence: both arrive at the conclusion that "giving is present but unsaid" from different levels. Sharf arrives from the ritual-behavior level; Kuiji arrives from the textual-structure level. The convergence of the two suggests: the Heart Sūtra's theology of giving is not a single interpretive possibility, but a multi-layered presence--absent at the textual level, present at the ritual level, present at the structural level.

### IV.3 Conze / Red Pine: The Traditional Prajñā Reading -- Prajñā Includes the Six Pāramitās

Edward Conze's English translation of the Heart Sūtra (1958) and Red Pine's commentary continue the traditional position: prajñāpāramitā itself contains all pāramitās.[^14] This position is consistent with Kuiji's Tang commentary tradition of "comprehensive expression," providing a cross-cultural comparison between Eastern and Western interpretations for P16.

Conze and Red Pine's position has had profound influence in both East and West--their reading made Western readers naturally accept the "prajñā includes the six pāramitās" interpretive tradition. But the deep logic of this tradition is: prajñā is not a pāramitā that stands "side by side" with the other five, but the **enabling condition** that makes the other five pāramitās pāramitās. Conze and Red Pine may not have explicitly articulated this logic, but their translations and commentaries implicitly assume it.

Conze and Red Pine are indeed correct--from within the prajñā tradition, silence is not a problem. But for readers newly entering Buddhism, silence is not an answer but a question. P16's task is to open this silence into a door, not close it into a wall.

Opening this door means: helping readers see that silence is not mere omission, but **intentional subsumption**--this subsumption's power is greater than direct proclamation because it requires the reader to fill the blank themselves. And in the process of filling the blank, the reader experiences prajñā's operation--not being told "giving is important," but having one's own prajñā awakened while thinking "why is giving not mentioned."

Here is a pedagogical insight: if the Heart Sūtra had explicitly listed the six pāramitās, readers would treat it as a checklist--six items to execute. But the Heart Sūtra chose silence--silence forces the reader to ask "why is giving not here?" In asking this question, the reader begins to experience prajñā's operation. Silence thus becomes a **teaching strategy**--it is not an evasion of giving's discussion, but a more skillful way of helping readers understand giving.

## V. The Middle Way: Correcting Misreadings

### ❌ "The Heart Sūtra Says Giving Is Unimportant"

Source of misreading: From the Heart Sūtra not mentioning giving, concluding the Heart Sūtra thinks giving is unimportant.

Correction: The Heart Sūtra doesn't mention giving not because giving is unimportant, but because giving's importance is assumed. It is like a mathematics professor writing "let x be a real number" on the blackboard--he is not saying x is unimportant; x is the core of the entire problem. The Heart Sūtra's silence is not a negation of giving, but an acknowledgment of giving as a **premise**.

### ❌ "The Heart Sūtra Is Only for the Achieved--Beginners Shouldn't Practice Giving"

Source of misreading: Understanding the Heart Sūtra as a teaching of the "highest realm," thinking beginners are not suitable.

Correction: The Heart Sūtra's intended readers are "those seeking the bodhisattva path" (Kuiji's phrase), not already-achieved bodhisattvas, but bodhisattvas on the way. Those seeking the bodhisattva path still need to practice giving--but giving guided by prajñā. The Heart Sūtra's silence is not excluding beginners, but reminding all bodhisattvas: giving must have prajñā as its condition.

### ❌ "The Heart Sūtra's Non-Attainment Equals Not Practicing Giving"

Source of misreading: Understanding "non-attainment" as doing nothing.

Correction: "Non-attainment" is not not-giving, but giving without grasping. The T0251 says "because of non-attainment, the bodhisattva relies on prajñāpāramitā, the mind has no obstruction"--non-attainment is the condition for liberation from obstruction, not liberation from all behavior. After the mind has no obstruction? "No fear, distant from all inverted dreams, reaching ultimate nirvāṇa"--this is still an active cultivation process, not passive non-action.

### ⚠️ "Using Nattier's Hypothesis to Deny the Heart Sūtra's Theology of Giving"

Correction: Nattier's Chinese-composition hypothesis is a text-history hypothesis; it does not affect the doctrinal analysis of the Heart Sūtra text itself. Even if the Heart Sūtra is Chinese-composed, its textual structure and doctrinal depth still merit analysis--the Heart Sūtra's silence itself is an intentional theological choice.

### ⚠️ "Sharf's Liturgical Reading Hollows Out the Theology of Giving into Performance"

Correction: Sharf revealed giving's "performative presence" (giving is cashed out in chanting behavior), but one cannot conclude from this that "chanting the Heart Sūtra equals completing giving." Giving's practical depth is not in performance but in actual giving. The liturgical reading supplements the theology of giving but cannot replace it.

## VI. Conclusion: Three-Layer Summary and Open Questions

### VI.1 The Essence

The Heart Sūtra's silence on giving is the most radical theology of giving--not canceling giving, but elevating giving to a new level: giving is not discussed in the Heart Sūtra because the Heart Sūtra provides the condition under which giving becomes giving (prajñā). Giving's accomplishment lies not within giving itself, but within prajñā. Giving without prajñā is merely secular giving; giving with prajñā is pāramitā.

### VI.2 Dialogue with P15

P15 established "non-abiding giving" through the *Vajracchedikā-prajñāpāramitā* (T0235, Diamond Sūtra)--using paradoxical language to protect giving's non-grasping. P16 asks: what happens when prajñā language pushes giving to its limit? Answer: even the character "giving" is withdrawn.

From "paradoxical saying" (P15) to "silence" (P16), this is Part V's thesis arc. P15's Diamond Sūtra still spoke of giving--using paradoxical language. P16's Heart Sūtra didn't even speak. But as Kuiji pointed out, silence is subsumption, not negation.

A deeper dialogue: P15's non-abiding giving is "how to embody emptiness in giving"; P16's comprehensive expression is "how emptiness comprehensively includes giving." These two form a complete cycle--P15 moves from giving to emptiness, P16 from emptiness to giving. The cycle's starting point and terminus are both giving: giving is the starting point (cultivation begins with giving) and the terminus (giving is prajñā's accomplishment).

The structure of the two paradoxes is also worth noting: P15's paradox is "because merit is empty, the Tathāgata says great merit is gained" (emptiness → existence); P16's paradox is "because unsaid, all six pāramitās are expressed" (non-existence → existence). Both arrive at positive conclusions from emptiness--this is the deep logic of the prajñāpāramitā tradition: emptiness is not negative negation, but positive possibility.

### VI.3 Open Questions: Toward P17

What is the cost of silence? The Heart Sūtra subsumes the six pāramitās under the name of "prajñā," but is this subsumption intelligible to readers without the six pāramitās' cultivation foundation? When the Heart Sūtra names only "prajñā" and silences the other five, readers lacking background knowledge may mistakenly think prajñā can replace everything--this is precisely Sharf's warned risk.

P17's question is: the Great Prajñāpāramitā (T0220), in six hundred volumes, has both giving's theory (far richer than the Heart Sūtra) and the Heart Sūtra's own emptiness language--how does this "extensive text" handle the co-presence of giving and emptiness? When all six pāramitās are complete, what is the relationship between giving and prajñā? When the six pāramitās are compressed into one, is giving truly subsumed, or drowned out?
