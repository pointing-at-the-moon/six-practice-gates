# Bhaiṣajyarāja's Self-Offering

**A Hermeneutic Crisis**

*中文主題：藥王燃身供養之教義詮釋危機與漢傳註疏之內在防火牆*

*Chinese: 藥王菩薩本事品之身命施：一個詮釋危機*

*Author: 釋慧鏡 (Shi Huijing)*

---

## Abstract

This paper re-examines Chapter 23 (藥王菩薩本事品) of the *Saddharma puṇḍarīka Sūtra* (T0262 vol. 6), arguing that its auto-immolation narrative represents the apex of Mahāyāna *dehadāna* (body-giving) as a doctrinal paradigm, and its true meaning lies not in literal self-immolation but in a double inward turn: Zhiyi's (*智顗*) "inner-verification" reading (burning the fuel of afflictions via inner wisdom-fire) and Zhanran's (*湛然*) "nature-inclusion" reading (suffering itself as full manifestation of *dharmadhātu*). Through tri-version collation (T0262 Kumārajīva 406 CE / T0263 Dharmarakṣa 286 CE / T0264 Jñānagupta 601 CE), the paper demonstrates that the auto-immolation narrative predates Kumārajīva and was already embedded in the Indic Lotus stratum; the four-character compound "真法供養" (*zhēn fǎ gōngyǎng*, true offering of Dharma) is a Kumārajīva coinage that fuses "法供養" (originally from a different chapter, meaning listening-to-and-living-by-the-sūtra) with "身施" (body-giving), creating a doctrinal pivot of dual reversal. The paper further reconstructs the internal hermeneutic firewall of Chinese commentary through Jizang's (*吉藏*) three-karma offering grid (which normalizes rather than transcendentalizes) and Zhanran's double warning (non-inner-verification auto-immolation becomes "useless asceticism"; failure to burn arm/finger makes one not a monastic bodhisattva). In dialogue with Western scholarship, the paper acknowledges Benn's historical density of Chinese self-immolation tradition while noting his methodological bracketing of the doctrinal-exegetical layer; borrows from Ohnuma's Indian *dehadāna* literary genealogy to prove the practice is not Chinese. The axis of middle-way correction is view-based (三事不可得 / *upāya-prajñā*) rather than stage-based (*avaivartika* gate). As the transformation-move of Part VII's three-part typology arc — following P21's substitution-move (dharma-giving replaces material-giving) and P22's axiomatic-move (prasāda-dāna as axiom of dāna) — P23 completes the arc by demonstrating how literal dehadāna, through Chinese commentary's inward turn, becomes a transformed doctrinal paradigm.

**Keywords:** Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter, *dehadāna*, true offering-of-Dharma, tri-version collation, Zhiyi, Jizang, Zhanran, auto-immolation, hermeneutic crisis, Chinese commentary, Benn, Ohnuma, three-events-unobtainable, *upāya-prajñā*, Part VII

## I. Introduction: Auto-Immolation — A Hermeneutic Crisis of Mahāyāna

> "World-Honored One! How does the Bodhisattva Bhaiṣajyarāja dwell in the saṃsāra? World-Honored One! Does this Bhaiṣajyarāja have innumerable hundreds of thousands of manifolds of difficult practices and hardships? Good World-Honored One, please explain briefly."[^1]

The "Capacity-Store Chapter of the Bodhisattva Bhaiṣajyarāja" (藥王菩薩本事品, Chapter 23 of the *Lotus Sūtra*) opens with Nakṣatrarāja-saṃkusumitābhijña's (宿王華菩薩 / Constellation King Bodhisattva) question, and the sūtra itself acknowledges this narrative belongs to the category of "difficult practices and hardships" (難行苦行) — this is not the logic of ordinary offering or practice.[^2] This chapter, positioned after the "Entrustment Chapter," serves as the paradigmatic opening of the widespread-implementation section: the sūtra's own structure implies that the paradigm for upholding this sūtra is the gift of body and life.[^3]

For Western readers confronting this chapter, the first reaction is of the same type as Nakṣatrarāja-saṃkusumitābhijña's "difficult practices and hardships" question: "Does this count as Buddhism?" The question is sharp precisely because the chapter depicts the Bodhisattva Sarvasattvapriyadarśana, before the Buddha Candrasūryavimalaprabhāsaśrī, consuming six varieties of fragrance (sandalwood, kunduruka, turuṣka, prikkā, aguru, gum-resin) and drinking campaka flower-oil for 1,200 years, igniting his own body through the power of samādhi and vow for 1,200 years, reborn into the family of King Vimaladatta (淨德王 / Pure-Virtue King) upon passing, burning his hundred-merit-adorned arm for 72,000 years, and restoring his body as before — the entire narrative, viewed through a modern lens, presents as an extreme image of self-destruction.

However, this very reaction constitutes the two layers of the "hermeneutic crisis" that P23 names: (i) the **lexical layer** — readers misread "真法供養如來" (true offering of Dharma to the Tathāgata) as "the highest of all offerings," conflating it with the ten-character formula from the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*'s Samantabhadra chapter (諸供養中法供養為最), thereby positioning auto-immolation as an extraordinary offering rather than an ontological qualifier;[^4] (ii) the **doctrinal layer** — readers misplace the axis of middle-way correction, reading Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitāupadeśa* firewall (三事不可得 / three-events-unobtainable) as a stage-based gate (未證不退轉位不應妄行 / one who has not attained the *avaivartika* station should not recklessly practice auto-immolation), when Nāgārjuna explicitly defends the right of one who has not attained *avaivartika* to give up his body even for sentient beings.[^5]

Chinese commentary was already addressing this hermeneutic crisis in the sixth century. Zhiyi's *Fahua Wenju* redefined "真法供養" as "內運智觀觀煩惱因果，皆用空慧蕩之" — replacing external conduct with inner wisdom-observation.[^6] Zhanran's *Fahua Wenju Ji* issued a double warning: auto-immolation without inner verification becomes "無益苦行" (useless asceticism), yet the *Brahmajāla Sūtra* states that one who does not burn body, arm, or finger is not a monastic bodhisattva.[^7] Jizang's *Fahua Yishu* normalized auto-immolation as one cell within a three-karma offering grid, rather than a cosmological event.[^8]

This paper's thesis may be compressed into one sentence: the auto-immolation narrative of the Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter is the apex presentation of the Indic Mahāyāna *dehadāna* paradigm within the Lotus Sūtra; its internal Buddhist meaning is Zhiyi's inner-verification reading (burning the firewood of afflictions through the inner wisdom-fire) + Zhanran's nature-inclusion reading (suffering as full manifestation of dharmadhātu) + Jizang's three-karma offering grid (body-karma offering); the axis of middle-way correction is **view-based** (三事不可得 / wisdom-convenience) rather than **stage-based** (avaivartika gate). This constitutes the transformation-move of Part VII's three-part typology arc: P21 substitution (substitutional) → P22 axiom (axiomatical) → P23 transformation (transformative).[^10]

## II. Tri-Version Collation: The Indic-Lotus Stratum of the Auto-Immolation Narrative and the Lexical History of "真法供養"

### II.1 The Auto-Immolation Narrative Predates Kumārajīva: Evidence from Dharmarakṣa's Version

Dharmarakṣa's 286 CE translation, the *Zhengfa Hua Jing* (正法華經) volume 9, "Chapter on the Bodhisattva Xi-jian" (喜見菩薩品), already fully preserves the narrative arc: 服香 → 立誓 → 燃身 → 千二百歲火不滅 → 諸佛讚 → 還生.[^8] Three collation findings are especially crucial.

First, Dharmarakṣa's version preserves a key phrase that Kumārajīva omitted: 「用一心故無有苦患」[^9] — the bodhisattva, in the fire, experiences no suffering due to samādhi-onepointedness. This is the most decisive textual evidence: the Indic-Lotus stratum clearly frames auto-immolation as a pain-free state under samādhi protection, not as agony. To imagine 1,200 years of screaming is to read back into Dharmarakṣa what Kumārajīva chose to remove.

Second, Dharmarakṣa preserves a four-character vow: 「以身為燈，為一切故」[^10] — "making the body a lamp for the sake of all beings." This vow disappears in Kumārajīva's version, replaced by the generalized "以神通力願." This shows that the Indic textual tradition framed the act as the completion of a bodhisattva vow, not as isolated self-violence. The body becomes a lamp — a symbol of illumination (the Dharma shining upon all), not "preparing combustible material."

Third, Dharmarakṣa's 「斷絕五穀專食眾香」[^11] — completely abandoning the five grains and consuming only fragrances — is a cultivation term cognate with Daoist 辟穀 (grain-abstention). Kumārajīva entirely omits this passage. This indicates that the Indic source carried a yogic body-transformation precondition: the body preparation before auto-immolation was transformation into an incense-body (香身), not transformation into combustible material.

### II.2 The Lexical Forge: "真法供養"

The four-character compound "真法供養" is a Kumārajīva 406 CE coinage. In Dharmarakṣa's 286 CE version, the corresponding passage reads 「斯真供養如來經典」[^12] — here *zhēn* (真) modifies *gōngyǎng* (供養), and *rúlái jīngdiǎn* (如來經典) is the object. Kumārajīva fused these into the four-character compound "真法供養如來," simultaneously effecting two doctrinal reversals: (i) shifting the object of offering from "Tathāgata's sūtra" (text) to "Tathāgata" (the personal Buddha);[^13] (ii) reversing the causal arrow: Dharmarakṣa's 「以身施者乃成法施」(body-giving → dharma-giving, ascending arrow) becomes in Kumārajīva's 「以法供養諸如來故」(dharma-offering → body-giving, descending arrow).[^14]

Meanwhile, the ten-character formula 「諸供養中法供養為最」 **does not appear at all** in the Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter.[^15] The corresponding formula in T0262 §23 is 「是名第一之施，於諸施中最尊最上，以法供養諸如來故」[^16] — note that the frame here is 「諸施」(among all gifts/dānas) not 「諸供養」(among all pujās); the former ranks body-life-giving within dāna, the latter ranks pujā categories. The ten-character formula's true source is T0293, the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* volume 40, Samantabhadra chapter.[^17] P23 does not need to cite it at all — it is not in §23.

### II.3 The Indian Genealogy of Auto-Immolation

Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitāupadeśa* volume 12 explicitly cites the Lotus Bhaiṣajyarāja by name: 「眾生喜見菩薩，以身為燈，供養日月光德佛」[^18] — this is the direct cross-canonical doctrinal bridge between T0262 and T1509. Nāgārjuna lists this as the paradigm of supreme dāna. More crucially, Nāgārjuna's orthodox definition of "inner giving" (內布施) is "不惜身命，施諸眾生" (giving up body and life without sparing, to all beings), taking the King-jātaka's 「破肉為燈炷，舉身火燃」 as its exemplar.[^19] In Nāgārjuna's system, Bhaiṣajyarāja's auto-immolation is not an outlier — it is the **superior paradigm** of orthodox Mahāyāna dāna.

Nāgārjuna simultaneously establishes a clear boundary: 「阿羅漢法中，都無此心，當知此為法身菩薩」[^20] — the arhat doctrinal framework structurally lacks the motivational architecture of dehadāna. Body-life-giving is not universal religiosity; it is soteriologically specific to the bodhisattva path.

## III. Three Chinese Commentators: Zhiyi's Inner-Verification, Jizang's Grid, Zhanran's Nature-Inclusion

Chinese commentary on the Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter is formed by a logical triangle of Tiantai's two (Zhiyi, Zhanran) and Sanlun's one (Jizang). This triangle is not a diachronic evolution, but three irreducible hermeneutic positions on the same sūtra text.

### III.1 Zhiyi: Inner-Verification — Burning the Firewood of Afflictions with Inner Wisdom-Fire

Zhiyi's *Fahua Wenju* volume 10B on "真法供養" is the heaviest single sentence in the commentary tradition:

> 「『真法供養』者，當是內運智觀觀煩惱因果，皆用空慧蕩之，故言真法也。」[^21]

Zhiyi reframes the sūtra's "真法供養" (referring to Xi-jian's auto-immolation) as inner wisdom-observation — using empty-wisdom to cleanse the causes and effects of affliction. This hermeneutic move of replacing external conduct with inner observation is the endogenously Chinese firewall.[^22] Zhiyi pushes further in a second explanation:

> 「又觀若身若火、能供所供皆是實相，誰燒誰然？能供所供皆不可得，故名真法也。」[^23]

The dual disappearance of giver (burner) and gift (fire/body) — Madhyamaka's "true nature has no form" applied to the auto-immolation scene. Zhiyi grafts the Bhaiṣajyarāja passage onto Prajñāpāramitā contemplation, raising body-giving from "material act" to "Prajñāpāramitā three-wheel emptiness" exemplar.[^24]

Zhiyi simultaneously positions asceticism as a "vehicle" rather than an end: 「〈藥王〉以苦行乘乘」[^25] — asceticism is the vehicle (乘, *yāna*), not the destination. Doctrinally, this reframes auto-immolation from asceticism itself to the instrument leading to Mahāyāna.

Zhiyi's structural reasoning for positioning the passage:

> 「雖通途囑累，未若示其要術棄身存道，故說〈藥王〉。」[^26]

The Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter after the Entrustment chapter is "showing the essential technique" — demonstrating the core method for upholding the sūtra, whose essence is "abandon body to preserve the Way": the Way's priority over the body.[^27]

### III.2 Jizang: Three-Karma Offering Grid — Normalization Rather Than Transcendentalization

Jizang's *Fahua Yishu* volume 11 presents the Sanlun school's signature style: flattening cosmological fluctuations (起伏), normalizing as an instance within a doctrinal grid.[^28]

Jizang reorganizes the entire Bhaiṣajyarāja passage into a typology of three-karma offering:

> 「前明入三昧雨華香等，謂意業供養；次從定起燒身，謂身業供養也；今說偈，明口業供養。」[^29]

Auto-immolation is demoted from cosmological event to a typological instance — placed alongside mind-karma (rain of flowers), speech-karma (verses). The Sanlun strategy is normalization, not transcendentalization. Jizang further places auto-immolation within the *Bodhisattvabhūmi-śāstra* (《地持論》) ten-types-of-offering grid, concluding 「精撿藥王具足十供養也」[^30] — auto-immolation is not a cosmological act beyond the ten offerings, but a complete exemplar fulfilling all ten.

On the vinaya objection, Jizang directly engages:

> 「問：依《毘尼》結戒，燒身得偷蘭遮，燒臂得突吉羅，此菩薩將不犯罪耶？答：此或可在家菩薩，不預犯戒之例。」[^31]

Jizang's path is "opening-closing" — a lay bodhisattva is not subject to the monastic precept violation framework. The Sanlun school does not take the high Madhyamaka ontological deduction (演繹), but uses vinaya-technical handling.[^32]

### III.3 Zhanran: Nature-Inclusion Reading and the Double Warning

Zhanran's *Fahua Wenju Ji* volume 10B pushes Zhiyi's inner-verification to the height of nature-inclusion. His definition of "真法供養" is:

> 「燒身名真法者，由內觀故。」[^33]

Auto-immolation is true dharma-offering not because of external conduct, but because of inner observation. Zhanran progresses from dharma-emptiness to being-emptiness: 「非但身等皆是實相，身等宰主一切皆無，故名為『誰』。」[^34] Not only are body and the like empty of inherent existence, but even the controller (主宰) of all of them is absent — hence the sūtra says "誰燒" (who burns?).

Zhanran's synthesis is the necessary corollary of the nature-inclusion reading:

> 「境智不二能所斯亡，以不二觀觀不二境，成不二行會不二空，作是觀時苦為法界，見聞者益故曰『乘乘』。」[^35]

Suffering itself is the full manifestation of dharmadhātu — the suffering of body-giving becomes the manifestation of the entire dharmadhātu. This is Zhanran's important advance on Zhiyi's inner-verification: not merely "inner-verification" but "suffering is dharmadhātu-full."[^36]

Zhanran simultaneously provides two warnings. The first is the heaviest anchor — an explicit warning against reading auto-immolation by the external literal:

> 「若不爾者，成無益苦行，佛有誠誡實可先思。所以投巖無招外行之論，赴火不為內眾之譏，良由內有理觀、外曉期心。」[^37]

Without the "non-dual observation" of inner principle, auto-immolation "becomes useless asceticism." The Buddha has already cautioned — jumping off cliffs (投巖), entering fire (赴火) avoid the criticism of external ascetics only because of the dual conditions of "inner principle-observation + external understanding of aspiration."[^38] This is the heaviest anchor of the entire paper: auto-immolation without inner principle-observation is useless asceticism.

The second warning on the vinaya objection is **completely opposite** to Jizang's answer:

> 「律制燒身得蘭，燒指得吉，此中讚燒其事如何？今為答之，大小開制教法不同，小制結過、大制令燒，故《梵網》中若不燒者非出家菩薩，豈獨令俗而不制道？」[^39]

The same vinaya objection: Jizang answers with "lay bodhisattvas are exempt" (disabling the precept); Zhanran answers with "one who does not burn is not a monastic bodhisattva" (reversing the precept).[^40] Two schools, identical question, opposite answers — the most textually load-bearing instance of the three-way dialogue.

### III.4 Asymmetry of the Three-Way Triangle

One point worthy of note: **Zhanran does not name Jizang in his commentary on the Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter** — unlike his treatment of the Sadāparibhūta chapter. Zhanran's critique of Sanlun on the Bhaiṣajyarāja passage is doctrinal-structural rather than vocative.[^41] This does not mean the Sanlun reading is ignored; rather, Zhanran's nature-inclusion "suffering as dharmadhātu" reading structurally advances past Sanlun's grid-typology.[^42]

## IV. Dialogue with Western Scholarship: Benn's Historical Density, Ohnuma's Indian Genealogy, Moerman's Ritual Landscape

### IV.1 James A. Benn: Historical Density and the Interpretive Gap

James A. Benn's *Burning for the Buddha* (2007) is the only Western scholar with monograph-level textual density on the Chinese auto-immolation tradition. Benn explicitly identifies the Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter as the classical founding anchor of the Chinese auto-immolation tradition;[^43] he uses hagiographical materials as his primary evidence, placing individual cases within fourfold contexts: historical, social, cultural, and doctrinal.[^44]

Benn's key methodological choice: in his introduction, he explicitly **brackets** the "doctrinal-exegetical perspective" (i.e., the inner-verification reading of Zhiyi-Zhanran), choosing to approach from local practice instead.[^45] This sentence is precisely the key divergence point between P23 and Benn.

P23's concession to Benn: acknowledging his disciplinary contribution — systematically deconstructing the default reading of "auto-immolation as Western cultural pathology"; establishing the textual-historical genealogy of the Chinese auto-immolation tradition.[^46] Benn is P23's primary Western disciplinary partner.

P23's push-back: Benn's methodological choice (intentionally bracketing the doctrinal-exegetical interpretation) is a legitimate scholarly choice at the historical level, but leaves an **interpretive gap** at the exegetical level — namely, "how the internal commentary tradition built a doctrinal firewall against literal misreading of this chapter," which Benn does not address.[^47] P23's task is precisely to fill this gap that Benn intentionally left: Zhiyi's inner-verification + Jizang's three-karma grid + Zhanran's nature-inclusion + double warning, constituting the intra-doctrinal firewall against literal auto-immolation.[^48]

One-sentence thesis difference: Benn completed the historical-density work; P23 fills in the exegetical-intermediary layer. The two are non-confrontational and complementary.

### IV.2 Reiko Ohnuma: The Indian Body-Giving Literary Genealogy

Reiko Ohnuma's *Head, Eyes, Flesh, and Blood* (2007) is the first Western monograph to systematically treat Indian body-giving literature.[^49] Its home field is the jātaka-Mahāyāna literary genealogy: Prince Sattva giving his body to feed tigers (Six Pāramitā Sūtra T0152 vol. 1 — proto-narrative; *Damamūka-nidāna-sūtra* (賢愚經) T0202 vol. 1 〈Mahāsattva Offering His Body to a Tiger Chapter〉 — naming-stratum; *Suvarṇaprabhāsa-uttamarāja Sūtra* (金光明最勝王經) T0665 vol. 10 〈Self-Sacrifice Chapter〉, Yijing 703 CE — fully-elaborated canonical telling); King Śibi cutting flesh to trade for a dove (T0202 vol. 1); King Candra's head-giving; King Maitrībala (慈力)'s blood-giving — this series of narratives constitutes the Indian Mahāyāna paradigm for "body and life as giving-objects." Ohnuma's disciplinary contribution is removing this genealogy from the "Western cultural anomaly" framework and connecting it to the internal logic of Indian religious literature. Bhaiṣajyarāja's positioning within this genealogy is thus clear: not a Lotus-exclusive innovation, but an inheritance of the jātaka paradigm reaching its apex presentation within the Lotus text.[^50]

P23's concession to Ohnuma is clear: the jātaka literary genealogy provides the inherited substrate for the Bhaiṣajyarāja narrative, enabling P23's §I claim of "Indic-Lotus stratum" textual archaeology to connect to a complete literary tradition; Bhaiṣajyarāja's auto-immolation imagery is not an isolated Lotus innovation, but the peak realization of the Indian Mahāyāna paradigm "body and life can serve as giving-objects."

P23's push-back to Ohnuma is at another level: Ohnuma has a tendency within the Indian literary framework to subsume (收攝) body-giving as a literary theme, implicitly secularizing it — body-giving as narrative device, its soteriological implications diluted by the framework itself. Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitāupadeśa* volumes 11–12 evidence shows: in the Madhyamaka-Prajñā system (體系), body-giving is a fully doctrinal paradigm, not pure literature. Volume 11's orthodox definition of "inner giving" is "不惜身命，施諸眾生," taking the King-jātaka's 「破肉為燈炷，舉身火燃」 as exemplar[^51a] — Nāgārjuna does not take a literary reading of this jātaka, but a doctrinal one: using it as a classical exemplum for the pāramitā category "inner giving." Volume 12 further states 「阿羅漢法中，都無此心」 — the arhat doctrinal framework structurally lacks body-giving's motivational architecture; body-giving is not universal religiosity but soteriologically specific to the bodhisattva path. This boundary cannot be derived at the literary-theme level; it must be established at the soteriological level.[^51]

A textually independent detail: the motif of burning fingers (自燒手指) **does not exist** in T0202 *Xianyu Jing* (賢愚經) jātaka collections[^51b] — the jātaka paradigm of body-giving predominantly uses "whole-body feeding beasts," "cutting flesh," "giving head, eyes, marrow, brain." The specific mode of self-burning / finger-burning is the Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter §23's distinctive advance within the inherited jātaka paradigm. Bhaiṣajyarāja not only inherits the typical jātaka body-giving but advances within it to the more radical mode of inward self-burning / finger-burning — this advance raises the cosmological intensity (1,200 years of unextinguished fire, eighty koṭi-Gaṅgā (八十億恆河沙) world-shaking) to an apex unattained by jātaka literature. Ohnuma's genealogy explains the inheritedness, but cannot explain this specific apex-advance — and Chinese commentary (Zhiyi's inner-verification, Zhanran's nature-inclusion) is precisely the doctrinal response to this apex-advance.

### IV.3 D. Max Moerman: Ritual Landscape and Multiple Intermediaries

D. Max Moerman's *Localizing Paradise* (2005) treats 補陀落渡海 (fudaraku-tokai) — a form of ritualized self-sacrifice in the medieval Japanese Kumano devotional circle: monks secluding themselves in small boats, drifting south toward the Avalokiteśvara pure land of Potalaka, effectively a boat-as-coffin sea-burial self-sacrifice. Moerman counted 24 cases between the ninth and eighteenth centuries, spanning a full millennium.[^52] This work's academic positioning is not individual-case historical study, but a hermeneutic framework of "ritual-regulation + landscape-narrative multiple-intermediary" — body-giving's reception layer in East Asia is not direct-literal practice, but a threefold intermediary: ritual (pilgrimage regulations + boat construction) + landscape (the Avalokiteśvara pure land Potalaka as East Asian cosmological geography) + narrative (the shared textual substrate of Lotus Sūtra chapters on Avalokiteśvara and Bhaiṣajyarāja).

P23 borrows Moerman's key insight at two levels. The first is the asymmetry of frequency and imagination: 24 cases across a millennium is clearly sparse, but Potalaka's weight as cultural imagination far exceeds its actual occurrence rate[^53] — this asymmetry is an important exegetical conclusion of P23 as a whole: the cultural-doctrinal significance of auto-immolation cannot be derived from occurrence frequency, but must be derived from its intermediary position within the literary-commentary-ritual complex. The second is the structural isomorphism of cosmological landscapes: Potalaka as Avalokiteśvara pure land landscape, and the Lotus §23's Candrasūryavimalaprabhāsaśrī Buddha realm (both belonging to "other-world infinite (恒河沙, koṭi-Gaṅgā) worlds") share the same cosmological resource of East Mahāyāna imagination; Bhaiṣajyarāja's eighty billion (八十億) Ganges-sand world-shaking and Potalaka's south-sea landscape are two instances of the same cosmological imagination.

P23's push-back to Moerman is at the scope level. Moerman's ritual-landscape framework applies to historical ritual-practice reception-layer study — the question of "how practitioners enter this imagination"; it has limited applicability to classical doctrinal-layer hermeneutics — the question of "how the text itself frames this practice" lies outside its methodological scope. P23 §三's Zhiyi inner-verification, Zhanran's nature-inclusion, Jizang's three-karma grid — these are doctrinal-interpretive works, not ritual-landscape works. The two scopes are complementary, not competitive: Moerman handles reception-layer; P23 handles doctrinal-hermeneutic-layer.[^54]

---

## V. Middle-Way Correction: Nāgārjuna's View-Based Axis

### V.1 The Doctrinal Axis of Middle-Way Correction: Three-Events-Unobtainable

Nāgārjuna's firewall for body-giving is not "未證不退轉位不應妄行燃身" — this formulation does not exist in Nāgārjuna, and the opposite is true. Volume 96 explicitly defends the body-giving of one who has not attained *avaivartika*:

> 「薩陀波崙未得阿鞞跋致……以深念般若波羅蜜故……不惜身命、有大功德故，亦名菩薩摩訶薩。」[^55]

Nāgārjuna directly addresses the question "can one who has not attained *avaivartika* practice auto-immolation?" — the answer is yes, on the condition of "deep contemplation of the Prajñāpāramitā."[^56] The task of middle-way correction is not to cancel auto-immolation, but to mark its **view-condition**.[^57]

Nāgārjuna's orthodox formula is "三事不可得" (three-events-unobtainable):

> 「檀波羅蜜中，言：『財、施、受者，三事不可得。』」[^58]

Three-wheel emptiness (giver, gift, recipient) is not for a special practice, but the structural definition of the pāramitā — covering all dāna including inner giving.[^59] Nāgārjuna simultaneously closes the quietism misreading: "if three-events are unobtainable, what remains of dāna?" The formula does not negate the act; it negates the giver's attachment.[^60]

The axis of middle-way correction is **view-based** (can one observe three-events-unobtainable) rather than **stage-based** (avaivartika gate). If one has this view, the act is the middle-way; without it, it is self-harm.[^61] The firewall is doctrinal-cognitive, not hierarchical-progressive.[^62]

### V.2 Wisdom-Convenience: Defending Body-Giving Without *Avaivartika*

Nāgārjuna's positive answer to "can a bodhisattva who has not attained *avaivartika* practice body-giving?" rests on the technical definition of wisdom-convenience (*upāya-prajñā*, 慧方便):

> 「慧方便者，所謂三事不可得者。」[^57]

The technical definition of wisdom-convenience is three-events-unobtainable. This means: the legitimacy of body-giving does not rest on the practitioner's attainment-stage, but on their view (whether one can observe three-events-unobtainable).[^63]

## VI. Conclusion: The Part VII Three-Part Typology Arc and the Forward-Anchor to Samantabhadra

Part VII's three-part typology arc is now complete: P21 (Dharma-Teacher Chapter) established the **substitutional** — dharma-giving replacing material-giving, the sūtra's own recitation as the highest dedication to the Tathāgata; P22 (Sadāparibhūta Chapter) established the **axiomatic** — bodily prostration as the axiom of dāna, the ontological affirmation "you all shall become Buddhas" through posture; P23 (Bhaiṣajyarāja Chapter) establishes the **transformative** — literal body-giving, through Chinese commentary's inward turn, transformed from "material act" to "Prajñāpāramitā three-wheel emptiness" instance and the "dual-disappearance of giver-gift" contemplation-field.[^64] All three are the Lotus Sūtra's widespread-implementation section's three paradigmatic treatments of "dāna"; all three point to a common doctrinal center: the essence of giving lies not in the transfer of objects but in the loosening of the mind, not in the intensity of external conduct but in the clarity of inner verification.

P23's transformative move may thus be expressed as: literal body-giving need not be canceled, nor clung to literally; it must be **read into the correct doctrinal framework** — Zhiyi's inner wisdom-observation (burning the firewood of afflictions through inner fire), Zhanran's nature-inclusion reading (suffering as dharmadhātu-full), Jizang's three-karma grid (typological instance of body-karma offering), and Nāgārjuna's three-wheel emptiness (giver-gift-recipient three-events-unobtainable) constitute the four pillars of this framework. The sūtra itself already sets the entry to this frame: 「以神通力願而自然身」 explicitly states that auto-immolation requires the dual yogic precondition of samādhi and vow; 「令無數人發阿耨多羅三藐三菩提心」 explicitly states that the telos of auto-immolation is others' bodhicitta-arousal, not self-dissolution; the sūtra's own sūtra-recitation equivalence formula (「若復有人，以七寶滿三千大千世界供養於佛……不如受持此法華經，乃至一四句偈」) redirects the awe of the entire auto-immolation narrative toward "upholding even a four-line gāthā" as a practicable textual practice.[^65] The sūtra itself already contains the self-correction toward inward-verification; Chinese commentary's inner-verification is not an external rewriting of the sūtra, but the systematic unfolding of the sūtra's own immanent self-correction.[^66]

One point worth mentioning at the chapter's close: 「爾時佛告宿王華菩薩：『於汝意云何？是一切眾生喜見菩薩，豈異人乎？今藥王菩薩是也。』」 — the past-body of Bhaiṣajyarāja is Sarvasattvapriyadarśana; Śākyamuni's self-referential clue here (同型於〈如來壽量品〉之「則我身是」, "it is my own body") suggests that bodhisattva-jātaka narrative structure and the Lotus mythological time constitute the cosmological matrix of East Mahāyāna imagination; this clue falls outside P23's thematic scope and is reserved for Part VIII (Samantabhadra Chapter) and the Avataṃsaka chapter on entering the dharmadhātu.

The forward-anchor to Part VIII, the Avataṃsaka Samantabhadra field, may thus be established: Samantabhadra's ten great vows' "extensive practice of offering" will expand dāna from Bhaiṣajyarāja's single focus of body-giving into the infinite field of "until empty-space is exhausted, until beings are exhausted, until beings' karma is exhausted, until beings' afflictions are exhausted" — the object of offering expands from a single Buddha (Candrasūryavimalaprabhāsaśrī) to infinite dharma-dhātu Buddhas; the time of offering expands from "120,000 years (十二萬歲) of a single body" to "thought after thought without interruption, body-speech-mind karma without weariness."[^67] Bhaiṣajyarāja's finitude (one Buddha, one time, one body) is transcended in Samantabhadra's infinitude. The ten vows and their closing gāthā (where the ten-character formula 「諸供養中法供養為最」 truly originates) systematize "dharma-offering" as the ultimate framework of the bodhisattva path — P23 §I's identified P21/P26 territory is thus returned to its proper position. Bhaiṣajyarāja's auto-immolation is therefore not the endpoint of the body-giving paradigm, but its transition — from the jātaka era's "body as object" concrete offering, transitioning to the Avataṃsaka era's "mind as offering" infinite dharma-offering. This is not the negation of auto-immolation, but its completion.
