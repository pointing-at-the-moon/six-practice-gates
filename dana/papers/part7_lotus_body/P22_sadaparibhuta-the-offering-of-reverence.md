## Abstract

This paper examines the bodily prostration (身禮拜) of the bodhisattva Sadāparibhūta (常不輕菩薩) in Chapter 20 of the *Lotus Sūtra* (T0262, juan 6), arguing that it should be understood as an axiomatic expression of *prasāda-dāna* (the offering of reverential faith) — not as *dehadāna* (gift of body, the P23 territory of Bhaiṣajyarāja's self-immolation), nor as ritual-as-formality, but as a somatic declaration of ontological recognition: "You all shall become Buddhas." What is offered is not body, not substance, not words, but *prasāda* (pure faith) itself. The scholastic *locus classicus* is Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitāupadeśa* (T1509, juan 12), where the triad of *dāna* (generosity) includes "the offering of reverence" (供養恭敬施): "with faith purified, with reverential prostration, with welcoming and escorting, with circumambulation and offering — these and the like are called the offering of reverence." This eight-character formula is the standard Chinese rendering of *prasāda-dāna*.

The name Sadāparibhūta bears a bilateral etymology across the Chinese translations: Dharmarakṣa (286 CE) renders it as "the one always being despised" (常被輕慢), faithful to the Sanskrit passive past participle; Kumārajīva (406 CE) renders it as "Never Disrespectful" (常不輕), capturing the bodhisattva's active conduct. This duality is the paper's load-bearing insight: what is received as "being despised" (受) completes what is enacted as "never despising" (作). The bodily bow is not passive endurance of humiliation but an axiomatic affirmation of Buddha-nature performed publicly.

Three East Asian commentators offer three progressively deeper hermeneutic architectures: Zhiyi locates the chapter within Tiantai's five-fold categorization of predictions, identifying Sadāparibhūta as "prediction based on the primal cause of Buddhahood" (正因記); Jizang constructs a "body-as-worship-of-the-future-Buddha" model grounded in Tathāgatagarbha ontology; Zhanran completes the argument with "subversive edification that penetrates principle" (逆化通理) and "bowing to sentient beings and to Buddhas share the same origin" (禮生禮佛其源不殊). These three commentators trace the gradual unfolding of *prasāda-dāna* typology.

Western interlocutors include Reiko Ohnuma (typological distinction between body-gift and reverence-gift), Gene Reeves (performative-theology reading of the chapter), and Daniel B. Stevenson (Tiantai-contemplative reception). Ohnuma provides the negative boundary (body-as-object-of-transfer vs. body-as-instrument-of-reverence); Reeves the positive axis (bow as performative theology); Stevenson the Eastern reception bridge (bow as contemplative demonstration).

**Keywords:** Sadāparibhūta, bodily prostration, *prasāda-dāna*, offering of reverence, Tathāgatagarbha, Dharmarakṣa, Kumārajīva, Zhiyi, Jizang, Zhanran, Nāgārjuna, *Mahāprajñāpāramitāupadeśa*, *Samantabhadracaryāpraṇidhāna*, *dehadāna*, trace-gate transmission section, six-sense-purification

## I. The Problem: Locating Bodily Prostration in Dāna Typology

> "Mahāsthamaprāpta! For what reason is he named Sadāparibhūta? This bhikṣu, whenever he saw anyone — whether a bhikṣu, bhikṣuṇī, upāsaka, or upāsikā — would prostrate to them, praise them, and say: 'I deeply respect you all and dare not slight you. Why? You all practice the bodhisattva path and shall become Buddhas.' Yet this bhikṣu did not devote himself to reading and reciting the sūtras; he only practiced prostration. Even when he saw the four assemblies from afar, he would go to them and prostrate, praise, and say the same: 'I dare not slight you all; you all shall become Buddhas.'"[^1]

This passage is the key to understanding the Sadāparibhūta chapter. The Buddha asks Mahāsthamaprāpta: "For what reason is this bodhisattva named Sadāparibhūta?" The answer lies not in doctrinal exposition but in a single act: "he would prostrate to them and praise them." The core of Sadāparibhūta's practice is not reading sūtras, not meditation, not asceticism — it is **prostration**.

The phrase "did not devote himself to reading and reciting the sūtras; he only practiced prostration" draws a clear boundary between P21 and P22. P21 examines dharma-offering in the Dharma-Teacher Chapter — the five dharma-teachers center on "reception, carrying, reading, recitation, explanation, copying," which lean toward oral and mental practice. P22 examines bodily prostration in the Sadāparibhūta chapter — "not devoting to reading and reciting, only practicing prostration" — precisely skipping oral and mental practice, grasping only bodily action. This is not a dharma-teacher's practice; it is something else entirely.

But "prostration" is an extremely common behavior in Buddhist practice — circumambulating stūpas, kneeling, prostrating, everywhere visible. Why does the Sadāparibhūta chapter single it out? Why does the Buddha, through Mahāsthamaprāpta's question, have the bodhisattva explain that the core of his practice is "prostration"?

The key question is not about "prostration" itself, but **to whom one prostrates, why one prostrates, and how one prostrates**.

Sadāparibhūta's prostration has three highly unusual structural features. First, the object of prostration is not the Buddha but **ordinary members of the four assemblies** — bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās. Second, the content of the prostration is not praising the Buddha's qualities, but **telling sentient beings "you all shall become Buddhas."** Third, when faced with curses, beatings, and throws of debris, he does not retreat, argue, or retaliate — he "moves away, dwelling at a distance, yet still raises his voice and proclaims."

These three points together constitute an extraordinarily rare pattern of religious practice: **one person prostrating to everyone, not because the others deserve prostration, but because they "shall surely become Buddhas"; the more they resist, curse, and strike, the more loudly he proclaims the same words from a distance.**

This is unlike the religious prostration we typically understand. Ordinary prostration flows downward to upward, ordinary to sacred, believer to Buddha — Sadāparibhūta's prostration inverts this direction entirely. Sacred to ordinary, Buddha to sentient being, predictor to the predicted. This is the defining feature of *prasāda-dāna*: it does not petition upward; it proclaims downward. It does not wait for the other's response; it is a unilateral delivery that must be completed regardless of whether the other accepts it.

### Why *prasāda-dāna*?

Before elaborating the typological analysis, one must answer a more fundamental question: in the Buddhist classification of *dāna* (generosity), what category does "prostration" actually belong to?

Traditionally, *dāna* is divided into material giving (財施) and dharma-giving (法施). But this binary cannot cover Sadāparibhūta's prostration — he gives no money, teaches no doctrine; he merely bows, joins his palms, and says "you shall become a Buddha." This belongs to neither category.

Nāgārjuna provides a third category in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitāupadeśa*: **the offering of reverence** (供養恭敬施, *prasāda-dāna*).[^2]

> "Furthermore, generosity has three types: one is material giving, two is the offering of reverence, three is dharma-giving. Regarding the offering of reverence: with faith purified, with reverential prostration, with welcoming and escorting, with circumambulation and offering — these and the like are called the offering of reverence."[^3]

The eight characters "with faith purified, with reverential prostration" (信心清淨，恭敬禮拜) are the standard Chinese rendering of *prasāda-dāna*. Sadāparibhūta's "he would prostrate to them and praise them" falls precisely within Nāgārjuna's cell of "the offering of reverence." This is not a retrospective academic classification but a generosity category explicitly delineated by Nāgārjuna himself.

Nāgārjuna further explains: **faith-power is foremost; it is *dāna*, morality, meditation, and wisdom that cannot first enter the Buddha-dharma.**[^4] In other words, *prasāda* (pure faith) is not one among the pāramitās; it is the **prerequisite** that makes the pāramitās possible. Without faith, giving is merely material transfer; with faith, the bow itself becomes a complete offering.

This explains why Sadāparibhūta's prostration is so unusual — it is not "basic practice," not "preparation for entering higher offerings," but a **self-sufficient** type within the *dāna* classification system. Nāgārjuna says "faith is like a hand"[^5] — one with a hand enters the mountain of treasures and takes freely; one with faith does the same. Every prostration of Sadāparibhūta is a faithful hand entering the treasure mountain.

### Typological Cut: Not the Gift of Body

Before establishing the positive positioning of *prasāda-dāna*, one negative boundary must first be drawn.

The Sadāparibhūta chapter occupies the twentieth position among the twenty-eight chapters of the *Lotus Sūtra* sequence, with the Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter (chapter 23) separated by three chapters and a trace-manifest boundary line. Both chapters involve "body" and "offering," but their types are entirely different:

- **Sadāparibhūta's bodily prostration**: the body as **posture of prostration**, not consumed, not transferred, not offered as an object of giving. What is offered is the Buddha-nature affirmed by the words "you all shall become Buddhas." This falls within *prasāda-dāna*.
- **Bhaiṣajyarāja's self-immolation**: the body as **object of offering**, consumed, transformed into light. This falls within *dehadāna* (the gift of body).

Ohnuma's distinction between these two is precise: the typical "gift of body" in Indian Buddhist literature refers to the bodhisattva irrevocably transferring the body as an actual *dānavastu* (object of giving) — this is **different** from the bodily devotional behaviors of prostration / circumambulation / service. The former is "body as object-of-giving"; the latter is "body as instrument-of-reverence." Collapsing the two is a typological error.

P22's thesis can thus be stated: **Sadāparibhūta's bodily prostration is not *dehadāna* (the P23 paradigm of Bhaiṣajyarāja's self-immolation), nor is it ritualistic deference, but a fundamental expression of *prasāda-dāna*.** It declares an ontological proposition — "you all shall become Buddhas" — through bodily posture, and this declaration itself is a complete offering.

## II. The Translations and Their Name: Dharmarakṣa's Passive vs. Kumārajīva's Active Reading

The name Sadāparibhūta presents a dual structure across the Chinese translation history. This duality is not a translation error but the load-bearing structure of this bodhisattva's practice.

### II.1 Three Translations Compared

**T0263 (Dharmarakṣa 286 CE, *Zhengfa Hua Jing* 《正法華經》):**

> "There was a bhikṣu named **the one always being despised**, who practiced as a bodhisattva. Why is he called the one always being despised? When the noble one saw a bhikṣu, bhikṣuṇī, trustworthy gentleman, or trustworthy lady, he would always say to them: 'Honorable ones, do not be arrogant and haughty. Why? Your aspirations shall surpass bodhisattvas, Tathāgatas, the Worthy Ones, and the perfectly enlightened — through this expedient, guard your objects of contact.'[^7]"

**T0262 (Kumārajīva 406 CE, *Miaofa Lianhua Jing* 《妙法蓮華經》):**

> "At that time there was a bodhisattva-bhikṣu named **Never Disrespectful**. Mahāsthamaprāpta! For what reason is he named Never Disrespectful? This bhikṣu, whenever he saw anyone — whether a bhikṣu, bhikṣuṇī, upāsaka, or upāsikā — **would prostrate to them and praise them**, saying: 'I deeply respect you all and dare not slight you. Why? You all practice the bodhisattva path and shall become Buddhas.'[^8]"

**T0264 (Jñānagupta + Dharmagupta 601 CE, *Tianpin Miaofa Lianhua Jing* 《添品妙法蓮華經》):**

> "At that time there was a bodhisattva-bhikṣu named Never Disrespectful... This bhikṣu, whenever he saw anyone — whether a bhikṣu, bhikṣuṇī, upāsaka, or upāsikā — **would prostrate to them and praise them**, saying: 'I deeply respect you all and dare not slight you. Why? You all practice the bodhisattva path and shall become Buddhas.'[^9]"

### II.2 The Load-Bearing Weight of Name-Duality

The Skt *Sadāparibhūta* (सदापरिभूत) has the literal structure: *sadā* (ever) + *a-* (negative prefix) + *paribhūta* (past passive participle of *pari√bhū*, "being despised / being oppressed"). The literal etymology is "ever-not-been-despised."

Dharmarakṣa's "the one always being despised" (常被輕慢) matches the **passive literal surface** — he passively receives contempt. Kumārajīva's "Never Disrespectful" (常不輕) matches the **active conduct** — he actively does not slight. Both readings are textually canonical: one matches the Sanskrit passive past participle structure; the other matches the contextual behavior of the bodhisattva.

But these two readings are not mutually exclusive; they are **mutually embedded** — **what is received as "being despised" (受) completes what is enacted as "never despising" (作).**

This is the defining feature of *prasāda-dāna*. *Prasāda* (pure faith) is not "a good feeling" — not calmness, not joy, not tranquility. It is the capacity to say "I deeply respect you all; you all shall become Buddhas" **even in the context of being cursed, beaten, and rejected**.

### II.3 Stratification of Bodily Prostration: Kumārajīva's Hermeneutic Contribution

A highly crucial textual fact: in Dharmarakṣa's 286 CE translation, the bodhisattva **never prostrates**. His behavior is entirely verbal: "he would always say to them," "from afar he would raise his voice and teach." He proclaims; he does not prostrate.

The bodily dimension of "he would prostrate to them and praise them" enters the Chinese translation only through Kumārajīva's 406 CE redaction. T0264 (601 CE) essentially inherits this.

This means: **the bodily prostration of Never Disrespectful as a paradigm of *prasāda-dāna* has its textual anchor in Kumārajīva's 5th-century translation-workshop production, not in an unbroken Indian textual lineage.** We acknowledge this honestly — the bodily dimension of this bodhisattva's practice is a contribution of the Chinese translation tradition.

But this contribution is not a retrospective addition. Dharmarakṣa's text already contains the core elements of *prasāda-dāna* — the affirming words "your aspirations shall surpass bodhisattvas, Tathāgatas, the Worthy Ones, and the perfectly enlightened," and the exhortation "do not practice slighting; cultivate forbearance, arouse bodhicitta." What Kumārajīva did was not to append an external gesture, but to **embody** the affirmation already implicit in Dharmarakṣa's text — transforming verbal proclamation into bodily posture.

### II.4 The Dual Meaning of the Name as the Dual Structure of Bodhisattva Practice

Therefore, the name "Never Disrespectful" carries a dual meaning — what one enacts is "not slighting," what one receives is "always being slighted" — **this dual structure is the load-bearing center of *prasāda-dāna*.**

The bodhisattva's affirmation does not become invalid because of the recipient's opposition. Although you slight me, I still see the cause-ground Buddha (因地佛) in you; therefore I prostrate. What is offered is nothing else, nothing bodily, but the unshakability of affirmation itself.

This is why Nāgārjuna says "faith-power is foremost, it is *dāna*, morality, meditation, and wisdom that cannot first enter the Buddha-dharma" — because the load-bearing of faith-power is the affirmation that can occur even in contexts of opposition. Every prostration of Sadāparibhūta is faith-power manifesting in an oppositional context.

## III. Three East Asian Commentators: The Progressive Hermeneutics of Zhiyi, Jizang, and Zhanran

### III.1 Zhiyi: Primal-Cause Prediction and the Perfect-Teaching Framework of Three Tracks and Three Karmas

Zhiyi's reading of the Sadāparibhūta chapter provides the most systematic framework for understanding Sadāparibhūta's prostration. He makes two highly crucial determinations:

**First, the "prediction based on the primal cause" (正因記) among the five types of prediction.** In the "Explication of the Bestowal of Prophecy Chapter" section of the *Wenju*, Zhiyi made a five-fold classification of predictions:[^10]

> "If by general prophecy, as at the beginning of the Dharma-Teacher Chapter; if by specific bestowal, as after the three circles; **if by primal-cause prediction, as in Sadāparibhūta**; if by auxiliary-cause prediction, as the ten kinds of offering in the Dharma-Teacher Chapter; if by elucidating prediction, as when prophecy is given to the three capacities."

This passage provides a direct canonical basis for the P21-P22 distinction. Zhiyi himself places the Dharma-Teacher Chapter's offerings (P21) and the Sadāparibhūta chapter's prostrations (P22) into different prediction types — the former is "auxiliary-cause prediction" (recognition of conditions and supporting causes for practice); the latter is "primal-cause prediction" (recognition of the primal-cause Buddha-nature inherently possessed by all beings).

The difference between primal-cause prediction and auxiliary-cause prediction: auxiliary-cause is conditional recognition — "you did offering, therefore I recognize this as a supporting cause for Buddhahood." Primal-cause is an axiomatic recognition — "you can be recognized because you inherently possess the primal-cause Buddha-nature." The former is "because you do, therefore you can"; the latter is "because you inherently possess, therefore you can do."

Sadāparibhūta's prostration belongs to the latter — he does not *earn* the qualification for Buddhahood through prostration; he *declares* the Buddha-nature that sentient beings inherently possess.

**Second, the Perfect-Teaching connector of three tracks and three karmas.** Zhiyi directly maps Sadāparibhūta's practice onto the *Dharma-Teacher Chapter*'s three tracks (Tathāgata's room / robe / seat) and the peaceful-practice framework:[^11]

> "Sadāparibhūta's deep respect is the Tathāgata's seat; forbearing cursing and beating is wearing the Tathāgata's robe; maintaining a compassionate heart without ever ceasing is the Tathāgata's room. Furthermore, deep respect is mental karma; the speech of never slighting is oral karma; going to prostrate is bodily karma — these three united with compassion constitute the vow-based peaceful practice."

The subtlety of this determination lies in Zhiyi's self-conscious bridging of the P21 three tracks (room / robe / seat) with the P22 three karmas (mental / oral / bodily). The five dharma-teachers (reception, reading, recitation, explanation, copying) in the Dharma-Teacher Chapter lean toward oral and mental practice, omitting the third track — bodily karma. Sadāparibhūta's bodily prostration **completes this omission**.

Bodily karma = going to prostrate; mental karma = deep respect (primal-cause Buddha-nature); oral karma = the speech of never slighting ("you all shall become Buddhas"). These three karmas united with compassion constitute the vow-based peaceful practice. This is not an ordinary religious act; it is the Perfect-Teaching doctrine-of-inherent-nature's **ritual enactment** — the three karmas of body, speech, and mind simultaneously actualize the ontological proposition that "all beings inherently possess the three Buddha-natures."

**Third, the station of the first-listener.** Zhiyi directly judges the phrase "did not devote himself to reading and reciting the sūtras; he only practiced prostration" as follows: "This is the station of the first-listener of the first chapter of enjoyment."[^12] Sadāparibhūta's practice begins precisely at the initial-listening station (隨喜位) — the first of the five categories of disciples. His "currently attaining six-sense-purification" is the result of many lifetimes, not his initial station.

This is highly significant: Sadāparibhūta's prostration is **not high-level practice**, but the **beginning of entering faith**. Anyone can begin at the initial-listening station — no need for high-level attainment, no need for profound cultivation, only the words "I deeply respect you all; you all shall become Buddhas," and begin from here.

**Fourth, the power of forceful-gift (強毒) and the drum-of-poison metaphor.** When confronted with the question "why does Sadāparibhūta not retreat?" Zhiyi's answer is exceptionally powerful:[^13]

> "Question: Śākya's enlightenment was hesitant and slow in speaking; Sadāparibhūta, upon first sight, speaks abruptly — why?"
> "Answer: Those who already have goodness — Śākya protects them with gentle measures; those who lack goodness, Sadāparibhūta implants through forceful-gift (強毒)."

"Forceful-gift" (強毒) derives from the *Mahāparinirvāṇa Sūtra*'s drum-of-poison metaphor — poison-coated drum sound, those who hear it must die (metaphor: hearing the Buddha-nature name guarantees its effect). This is a positive "forceful-gift" vocabulary: Sadāparibhūta forcibly implants the Buddha-nature name into the auditory consciousness of slanderers, regardless of whether they accept it or desire it.

Those who curse receive both good and bad results — "because of slander they fall into evil; because they hear the Buddha-nature name, the force of the poison-drum, they obtain good results."[^14] The cursing itself is also an indirect delivery of *prasāda-dāna* — hearing the Buddha-nature name from the words "I deeply respect you all" already plants the seed of Buddhahood.

### III.2 Jizang: The Sanlun School's Nirvāṇa-School Turn

Jizang's reading of the Sadāparibhūta chapter presents an unexpected dimension: as the synthesizer of the Sanlun (Three-Treatise) school, in his commentary on this chapter he **abandons the Madhyamaka vocabulary** of the Three-Treatise tradition (two truths, eight negations) and instead adopts the **Nirvāṇa-school's primal-cause / auxiliary-cause framework**.[^15]

Jizang's core argument has three layers:

**First, bodily reverence = revering the future Buddha.** Jizang provides a "three-karma dharma-transmission" model:[^16]

> "This bodhisattva uses three karmas to break afflictions and transmit the dharma: first, bodily karma of prostration; second, oral karma of praise. The reason for bodily reverence is: all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature and are future Buddhas; therefore I prostrate to them to reverence the future Buddha."

The word "future Buddha" (當來佛) is highly crucial — the temporal tension Jizang preserves is "future" (當) rather than "present." He does not reverence the present Buddha but the Buddha that sentient beings will become. This avoids reading Sadāparibhūta as "already enlightened" conflation.

**Second, deep respect = manifesting the primal-cause Buddha-nature.** Jizang's explanation of "deep respect for you all" is:[^17]

> "'Deep respect for you all' means: manifesting that sentient beings possess the primal-cause Buddha-nature. And the reason it is called 'deep' is: since one possesses Buddha-nature, to reverence one as a future Buddha is the utmost of reverence; hence 'deep.' Furthermore, that sentient beings possess Buddha-nature is the profound meaning; hence 'deep respect.'"

The primal-cause Buddha-nature is a Nirvāṇa-school category — inherently possessed by all beings, spanning from beginning to fulfillment. Deep respect is "deep" because its object (the primal-cause Buddha-nature) is the profound meaning.

**Third, Sadāparibhūta fulfills the three matters of the Dharma-Teacher Chapter.** Jizang connects Sadāparibhūta's practice with the *Dharma-Teacher Chapter*'s three matters (Tathāgata's room / robe / seat):[^18]

> "How does Sadāparibhūta's dharma-transmission differ from the three matters of the Dharma-Teacher Chapter? Answer: Sadāparibhūta fulfills all three matters. Having a compassionate heart, one can practice reverence and praise — this is entering the Tathāgata's room; forbearing cursing and beating — this is wearing the Tathāgata's robe; 'deep respect for you all' — this is knowing that all dharmas permanently abide in the stillness of cessation, which is sitting on the Tathāgata's seat."

Jizang's argument that Sadāparibhūta fulfills the three matters forms a cross-school echo with Zhiyi's three-track-three-karma argument — Tiantai and Sanlun reach consensus on the completeness of Sadāparibhūta's practice.

### III.3 Zhanran: Subversive Edification and the Liturgy of Antagonism

Zhanran provides a by-name correction of Jizang's reading:[^19]

> "Jiaxiang's seven meanings are not without one seeing each [locally]; but they do not reach the far destination."

Jiaxiang's (Jizang's) seven meanings each have "local insight" (每一義都有局部見地), but "do not reach the systematic level" demanded by Tiantai. Zhanran replaces them with "six meanings."

The most crucial determination among these is **"subversive edification penetrating principle" (逆化通理)**:[^20]

> "All other sūtras merely describe conventional edification and transmitting doctrine; this chapter: prostrating to the ordinary, subversive edification penetrates principle."

"Subversive Edification" =edification that violates convention — a bhikṣu prostrating to laypersons is not conventional etiquette; Sadāparibhūta's violation of conventional ritual is "subversive edification penetrating principle." Zhanran locates Sadāparibhūta's practice as **antagonistic liturgy** — it breaks the conventional monastic-lay hierarchy and reveres those it ought not conventionally revere.

Zhanran further refutes the objection "a bodhisattva should not prostrate to laypersons" (a bhikṣu not prostrating to laypersons is a vinaya requirement):[^21]

> "Forgetting [precept-]violation for the sake of saving others is precious; what matters is the others' peace. If others are at peace, why distinguish between prohibitive and static precepts? Today I prostrate to the four assemblies to save whom has done what wrong?"

"Forgetting-violation" (忘犯) = forgetting vinaya transgressions for the sake of saving beings. Prostrating to the four assemblies does not violate the precepts — if prostrating to sentient beings can save them, why cling to the framework of prohibitive precepts (non-fundamental transgressions)?

### III.4 Bowing to Sentient Beings and Buddhas: One Origin

Zhanran's most direct formula for "why prostrate to laypersons?" is:[^22]

> "A bodhisattva's transformative occasion has no single standard; it serves only what is beneficial. Thus this form is set: seeing that sentient beings' principle and a Buddha's principle are equal; therefore bowing to sentient beings and bowing to Buddhas share the same origin. This is self-practice."

"Seeing that sentient beings' principle and a Buddha's principle are equal" — the principle-nature (性德) of sentient beings and the principle-nature (果性) of a Buddha are **equal** (equally so). Therefore bowing to sentient beings and bowing to Buddhas share the same origin.

This is the most direct Tiantai formula for *prasāda-dāna* typology: because sentient beings' principle = Buddha's principle, bowing to sentient beings = bowing to Buddhas. What is reverenced is not the ordinary being's present physical form, but the principle-nature inherently possessed by all beings — this aligns with Nāgārjuna's reference to Śāriputra's vision of the dharma-body, the difference lying only in the medium: Śāriputra uses wisdom-eye as the medium; Sadāparibhūta uses bodily prostration as the medium.[^23]

Zhanran adds: "to cause sentient beings to arouse the aspiration toward the fruition" — Sadāparibhūta's prostration is not only self-practice but also transforming-others. By manifesting the Buddha-aspiration of sentient beings, others arouse aspiration toward that fruition.

### III.5 Summary of the Three-Commentator Progression

The three-commentator progression can be summarized as follows:

| Dimension | Zhiyi (early 6th c.) | Jizang (early 7th c.) | Zhanran (mid 8th c.) |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Core framework | Five Buddha-natures + three tracks and three karmas | Primal-cause Buddha-nature + three-karma dharma-transmission | Subversive Edification penetrating principle + principle-fruition equality |
| Sadāparibhūta's positioning | Trace-gate transmission final chapter · primal-cause prediction | Skillful means · secretly teaching Ekayāna | Subversive · manifesting both trace and origin |
| Legitimacy of prostrating to laity | Perfect-practice naturally encompasses | Dharma of greater vehicle permits what dharma of lesser vehicle prohibits | Forgetting-violation for saving beings · breaking prohibitive-precept framework |
| Responding to the audience | Initial-listening station · accessible to ordinary persons | Future Buddha (not present Buddha) | Bowing to sentient beings and Buddhas share the same origin |
| Contribution to *prasāda-dāna* | Establishes theoretical basis for axiomatic recognition and initial-listening station | Provides Nirvāṇa-school model of "body-as-worship-of-the-future-Buddha" | Completes the vinaya-transcending argument of "antagonistic liturgy" |

The logical progression of the three: Zhiyi establishes the theoretical basis (primal-cause prediction, three tracks-three karmas); Jizang supplements the ontology of primal-cause Buddha-nature through Nirvāṇa-school ("body-as-worship-of-the-future-Buddha"); Zhanran ultimately completes the fulfillment of the theory (subversive edification, forgetting-violation-for-saving, bowing-to-sentient-beings-and-Buddhas-share-the-same-origin).

## IV. Western Interlocutors: Ohnuma, Reeves, Stevenson

### IV.1 Reiko Ohnuma: The Typological Boundary Line

Ohnuma's *Head, Eyes, Flesh, and Blood: Giving Away the Body in Indian Buddhist Literature* (2007) establishes the defining paradigm of "gift of body" (*dehadāna* / *ātmabhāvaparityāga*) in Indian Buddhist literature: the bodhisattva irrevocably transfers the body as an actual *dānavastu* (object of giving).[^24]

Ohnuma explicitly distinguishes: the gift of body **differs from** the broader category of bodily devotional behaviors such as prostration / circumambulation / dharma-giving. The boundary line = body-as-instrument-of-reverence vs. body-as-object-of-offering.

This is precisely what P22 needs — she has done the disciplinary boundary-drawing work, separating the gift of body (P23 territory: self-immolation / burning arms / self-burning) from the offering of reverence (P22 territory: bodily prostration). Collapsing the two is a typological error.

But Ohnuma's book is designed with a focus on the gift of body; the prostration-reverence layer receives only passing, set-aside attention. P22 must do the **reverse work**: **positively establish *prasāda-dāna* as an independent type** with its own canonical lineage (Avadāna stūpa-veneration, Lotus Sadāparibhūta), its own object of address (the affirmed Buddha-nature, not the transferred body), and its own emotional core (*prasāda*, not heroic renunciation). Ohnuma provides the *negative* boundary; P22 supplies the *positive* content.

### IV.2 Gene Reeves: The Performative-Theology Reading

Reeves reads Sadāparibhūta's prostration as the most direct socio-ethical teaching of the *Lotus Sūtra*: every person one encounters is a future Buddha, and therefore worthy of reverence in the present moment, regardless of their attitude toward you.[^25] Sadāparibhūta's prostration is a "performative theology" — the work of affirmation completed through bodily gesture, not through discourse or argument.

**Where Reeves is right:** Reeves correctly identifies Sadāparibhūta's practice as "gestural" — the body as the grammar of affirmation, not the materiality of offering. His rejection of purely doctrinal frameworks protects this chapter's character as a practice-narrative.

**Where to push back:** The "inclusive Buddhism / universal dignity" framework flattens the layer of prophecy (*vyākaraṇa*). Sadāparibhūta does not reverence "human dignity"; he reveres the soteriological prediction "you all shall become Buddhas," grounded in Buddha-nature ontology, not democratic anthropology. Reeves's modernization layer has a tendency to re-*prasāda-dāna* as humanistic ethics; P22 affirms his gesture-performative axis but resists sliding from ontology into ethics.

### IV.3 Daniel B. Stevenson: The Tiantai-Contemplative Reception

Stevenson reads Sadāparibhūta as an "embodied parable" — bodily posture verifying the prophecy logic extends to every casual encounter with ordinary beings, not merely to disciples in formal assemblies.[^26] He particularly emphasizes how Tiantai reads Sadāparibhūta as a contemplative model — prostration as contemplative method, not merely a social act.

**Where Stevenson is right:** Stevenson is correct — Sadāparibhūta cannot be read as a purely Indian textual phenomenon when detached from its Chinese reception layer. Zhiyi's Perfect-Teaching reading (Sadāparibhūta's practice as the manifestation of "three thousand realms in a single thought-moment") is already the defining implication of Han-Chinese *Lotus* practice.

**Where to push back:** Stevenson's Tiantai-anchored reading has the risk of back-projecting the Tiantai "inherent-nature" ontology into the Indian textual layer — which is actually closer to the prophecy (*vyākaraṇa*) mechanism + *prasāda* practice. P22 respects the Chinese reception layer but **does not** let "inherent-nature" cover the gesture-performative dimension that Sadāparibhūta shares with early Mahāyāna devotional practice. The prostration was already doing *prasāda* work before the Tiantai elaboration.

### IV.4 Convergence of the Three

The convergence point of Ohnuma, Reeves, and Stevenson:

Ohnuma's typological boundary establishes that *prasāda-dāna* is **not** the gift of body — this is the negative determination. Reeves's performative-theology reading points to what *prasāda-dāna* **is** — the body as the grammar of affirmation. Stevenson's Eastern reception bridge explains how *prasāda-dāna* **is understood** in the East — from Tiantai's contemplative method to Zhiyi's primal-cause prediction.

Together, the three provide the complete support for P22's thesis:

> Sadāparibhūta's bodily prostration — not the gift of body, not ritualistic deference, but a fundamental expression of *prasāda-dāna*: the body as posture, declaring the ontological recognition "you all shall become Buddhas," making the prostration itself a public affirmation of all beings' Buddha-nature.

## V. Samantabhadra and Nāgārjuna: A Cross-Canonical Framework for *prasāda-dāna*

### V.1 The Samantabhadra Chapter: Why Reverence Is First

Among the ten great vows of the *Buddhāvataṃsaka-nāma-mahāvaipulya-sūtra* volume 40, "Chapter on the Contemplation and Practice of Bodhisattva Samantabhadra," the first vow is "reverence to all Buddhas," the second is "praise of the Tathāgatas," and the third is "extensive practice of offering."[^27] The sequence logic is exceptionally clear: **reverence precedes offering.**

The "body-speech-mind" three-karma formula of Samantabhadra's first vow:[^28]

> "With pure body, speech, and mind karma, continually practice reverence... thought after thought, without interruption, body, speech, and mind karma without weariness."

This formula corresponds precisely to the structure of Sadāparibhūta's "I deeply respect you all" (speech) + "seeing the four assemblies from afar, going immediately to prostrate" (body) + "dare not slight" (mind) — the same Samantabhadra-style three-karma binding, appearing on Sadāparibhūta in its most elemental form.

The logic of the Samantabhadra sequence is: reverence is not the "preliminary practice" before offering, but the **ground of the heart** of offering — without the *prasāda* structure of "with deep conviction, as if facing the present," offering becomes empty ritual. Sadāparibhūta's reverence is the most elemental expression of this ground.

### V.2 Nāgārjuna: Faith-Power Is Foremost

Nāgārjuna repeatedly emphasizes in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitāupadeśa*:[^29]

> "The ocean of Buddha-dharma is entered through faith, crossed through wisdom... Faith-power is foremost; faith-power is that which enters, not *dāna*, morality, meditation, wisdom — these cannot first enter the Buddha-dharma."

Faith-power (the power of faith) is the foundation of all Buddha-dharma.*dāna*, morality, meditation, wisdom — the other five pāramitās — are not "those who first enter Buddha-dharma"; only faith is the first gate.

This means: Sadāparibhūta's prostration's *prasāda* is "the first among first-gates," not a low-level stage to be transcended. One might think "prostration is merely basic; offering is what matters" — but Nāgārjuna himself says the other five pāramitās are not first-gates; only faith is the first-gate.

A single arousal of pure faith suffices.[^30] A single evil thought accumulates infinite guilt; a single arousal of pure faith receives human and heavenly happiness, inevitably attaining nirvāṇa's fruit. No accumulation needed; no subsequent action required.

### V.3 Śāriputra's Vision of the Dharma-Body: An Honest Tension

Nāgārjuna also includes an extremely important counter-text:[^31]

> "At that time, the Buddha told the bhikṣuṇī: 'It is not your first prostration; Śāriputra prostrated to me first. Why? Śāriputra, perceiving all dharmas as empty, this is seeing the Buddha-dharma-body; he obtains true offering, the supreme among offerings — not by offering the generated body with reverence.'"

Śāriputra meditating in the cave, perceiving emptiness = "seeing the Buddha-dharma-body, obtaining true offering" — here Nāgārjuna elevates "perceiving the dharma-body" to "true offering" and demotes "revering the generated body" to secondary.

P22's response path: Sadāparibhūta does not prostrate to ordinary beings' **present physical form**, but to "you all shall become Buddhas" — namely, to sentient beings' **future Buddha-body / dharma-body**. The object of his reverence aligns with what Śāriputra meditated upon in the cave; the difference lies in the medium — Śāriputra uses wisdom-eye; Sadāparibhūta uses bodily prostration.

But one must honestly mark: this alignment is P22's hermeneutic direction, not Nāgārjuna's explicit original intent. What is reverenced is the dharma-body, not the generated body — this calibrates the object of Sadāparibhūta's reverence to the future Buddha-body, not the present physical form.

## VI. Conclusion

### VI.1 Core Findings

Through four argumentative lines, this paper establishes Sadāparibhūta's bodily prostration as a typology of *prasāda-dāna*:

First, the sūtra-text layer: the core passage of T0262 volume 6, Sadāparibhūta chapter — "he would prostrate to them and praise them, saying 'you all shall become Buddhas'" — Sadāparibhūta's practice is not reading sūtras, not asceticism, not debate, but **prostration**.

Second, the name-duality layer: Dharmarakṣa's passive ("always being despised") and Kumārajīva's active ("Never Disrespectful") — the dual meaning interpenetrates: what is received as "being despised" completes what is enacted as "never despising." The bodily dimension of Sadāparibhūta is a 406 CE hermeneutic contribution of Kumārajīva, not an unbroken Indian textual lineage.

Third, the three-Han-Chinese-commentators layer: Zhiyi's primal-cause-prediction positioning, Jizang's Nirvāṇa-school body-reverence model, Zhanran's subversive-edification argument — constituting a progressive hermeneutic architecture, each manifesting the paradigm of *prasāda-dāna* from different angles.

Fourth, the Western-interlocutors layer: Ohnuma's typological boundary establishes that *prasāda-dāna* is not the gift of body; Reeves's performative-theology reading points to what it is; Stevenson's Eastern-reception bridge explains how it is understood in the East.

### VI.2 Practice Guidance

The practical inspiration of Sadāparibhūta's prostration lies in this: **anyone can begin at the initial-listening station — no need for high-level attainment, no need for profound cultivation, only the words "I deeply respect you all; you all shall become Buddhas," and begin from here.**

The paradigm of *prasāda-dāna* is not the privilege of religious elites. It is the most basic practice — prostrating to everyone, not because they deserve it, but because they "shall surely become Buddhas." When they resist, curse, and strike more fiercely, proclaiming the same words more loudly from a distance.

This is not moral exhortation; it is the ritual enactment of an ontological proposition.

### VI.3 Open Questions

How is Sadāparibhūta's bodily prostration reinterpreted in the Japanese reception layer (particularly the Ekayāna bodhisattva paradigm of Nichiren schools)? This belongs to a different question-field from the Indian original's *prasāda-dāna* paradigm and awaits separate treatment.

The three-fold typological arc of Dharma-Teacher Chapter substitution-move → Sadāparibhūta axiomatic-move → Bhaiṣajyarāja transformation-move awaits P23.
