# Dharma-Offering in the Lotus Dharma-Teacher Chapter

**Substitution-Move and the Structural Analysis of the Five Dharma-Teachers**

*中文主題：法師品法供養——替代姿態與五法師相之結構分析*

**Author:** 釋慧鏡 (Shi Huijing)
**Date:** May 2026

## Abstract

This paper examines the Dharma-Teacher Chapter's (法師品) distinctive answer within the *Lotus Sūtra* to the question of *dharma-pūjā* (dharma-offering): not by ranking dharma-offering at the apex of a hierarchy, but by an ontological equivalence — "where the sūtra is, there is the Tathāgata's whole body" — that directly absorbs *stūpa*-veneration into *sūtra*-veneration. This substitution-move constitutes the Lotus sūtra's original contribution to the question of the "supreme offering."

The chapter's five lines of dharma-teacher conduct (reception, reading, recitation, explanation, copying) are not five modes of offering to the sūtra, but five incarnation modalities of the *dharmakāya* in the human world. Zhiyi's *Miaofa Lianhua Jing Wenju* (妙法蓮華經文句) reveals that "dharma may serve as a model, yet it cannot propagate itself — it requires a bearer." The five teachers are precisely these five modes of bearing.

Three East Asian commentators offer three logically progressive interpretive architectures for the chapter: Tiantai's unification of five methods and ten offerings, Sanlun's parallel display of praising persons and dharma, and Zhanran's post-period fusion of two into one as the three secret treasures. The fundamental difference between Sanlun and Tiantai lies not in doctrinal superiority, but in two distinct hermeneutic paradigms for how the *dharmakāya* unfolds in the human realm.

**Keywords:** Dharma-Teacher Chapter, *dharma-pūjā*, *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka*, substitution-move, five dharma-teachers, Zhiyi, Jizang, Zhanran, *sūtra*-cult, dharma-body relic

## I. Sūtra-Text Positioning

> "Bhaiṣajyarāja! If there be good men or good women who, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, wish to expound this Lotus Sūtra to the four assemblies, how ought they to expound it?"[^1]

This single question is the key to the entire Dharma-Teacher Chapter. The Buddha addresses not "what is the supreme offering," but "how ought one teach." By entering through the question of *how* to teach, the sūtra naturally leads to the structural discussion of "what makes a dharma-teacher," avoiding the trap of opening with a "dharma-offering vs. material-offering" hierarchy comparison.

The Dharma-Teacher Chapter occupies a pivotal position within the Lotus sūtra's twenty-eight-chapter architecture. It sits in volume four, following the establishment of *upāya-kauśalya* (convenient means) as a Mahāyāna hermeneutic structure in the Skillful Means Chapter (方便品) and the concretization of that framework across the Parable and Bestowal of Prophecy chapters. The Skillful Means Chapter establishes principle; the Dharma-Teacher Chapter turns toward practice.

The chapter occupies a transitional position in the Lotus structure. Volumes one through three establish the theoretical foundation for Ekayāna through the Parable Chapter's fire-house, poor son, and medicinal grass metaphors. Volume four begins answering "how theory becomes practice." The Dharma-Teacher Chapter, as the first chapter of volume four, bridges theory and practice. Before it, the Devadatta Chapter proves Ekayāna's universality through the extreme case of the dragon girl's instant Buddhahood; the Dharma-Teacher Chapter proves how ordinary beings assume Ekayāna's transmission mission through the plain formula of "reception, reading, recitation, explanation, and copying."

The chapter's named interlocutor is Bodhisattva Bhaiṣajyarāja (藥王菩薩). The sūtra opens with "At that time the World-Honored One, through Bodhisattva Bhaiṣajyarāja, addressed the eighty thousand great beings"[^2] and directly addresses "Bhaiṣajyarāja!" at least eleven times throughout. This is not merely rhetorical repetition but a structural fact: the Dharma-Teacher Chapter is, in its architecture, a Bhaiṣajyarāja chapter — the Buddha had already chosen to teach through Bhaiṣajyarāja as the responding bodhisattva before the Bhaiṣajyarāja Chapter (chapter 23) even appears. The chapter's lamp-burning and ointment-offering context foreshadows Bhaiṣajyarāja's own body-burning offering. That the Buddha chose Bhaiṣajyarāja as the recipient of dharma-teacher doctrine establishes a continuous thread of dharma-offering from the start.[^3]

A crucial textual fact about the Dharma-Teacher Chapter's treatment of dharma-offering must be stated upfront: the widely circulated eight-character formula "Of all offerings, dharma-offering is supreme" (諸供養中，法供養最) — **does not appear in the Dharma-Teacher Chapter, nor anywhere in the Lotus sūtra itself.** Its canonical anchor is the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (Buddhāvataṃsaka-nāma-mahāvaipulya-sūtra), volume 40, Chapter on the Contemplation and Practice of Bodhisattva Samantabhadra (普賢行願品), the Broad Practice of Offering section (T10p0845a04–a05).[^4] The Dharma-Teacher Chapter's statement on the supreme offering is not a hierarchical ranking but an ontological equivalence-absorption.

The Chapter's answer to the "supreme offering" question is a **substitution-move** — by the ontological equivalence "this place already contains the Tathāgata's whole body," it directly absorbs **stūpa-veneration** as **sūtra-veneration**.[^5] Where the sūtra scroll resides, the Tathāgata's whole body is present — no further placement of *śarīra* relics is required. "There is no need to place *śarīra* relics. Why? Within this place the Tathāgata's whole body already exists."[^6] This does not rank dharma-offering above material offering but dissolves the distinction between offering-objects entirely: the sūtra scroll *is* the relic, the scripture-bearer *is* the stūpa, the dharma-teacher *is* the offering itself.

Before analyzing the five dharma-teacher lines, one must clarify the semantic layers of "offering" within the chapter. Three strata operate simultaneously: the traditional material offering (flowers, incense, garlands, powdered incense, unguents, burnt incense, silk banners, parasols, robes, music); the implicit semantics of dharma-offering ("one ought to offer with the Tathāgata's own offering"),[^7] viz. treating the scripture-bearer as the Tathāgata to be offered; and the **substitution-move** — the sūtra scroll itself *is* the Tathāgata's whole body, so offering the scroll equals offering the Tathāgata. These three layers are not parallel: material offering is the conventional frame, dharma-offering is the implicit frame, and the substitution-move is the breakthrough frame.

As for the four-character formula *dharma-pūjā* (法供養) itself, its Chinese translation was first established by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護) in his *Zhengfa Hua Jing* (正法華經, 286 CE), one hundred and twenty years before Kumārajīva's version.[^8] In volume 6 of that translation, Dharmarakṣa uses "dharma-offering" (*fǎ zhī gòngyǎng* 法之供養) six times in prose and twice in verse ("法供養最尊無極"/"法供為最").[^9] Crucially, **Kumārajīva's Dharma-Teacher Chapter never uses the literal term "dharma-offering" (法供養) at all.** Instead, it employs descriptive periphrasis: "regard [the sūtra scroll] with reverence as a Buddha and make various offerings,"[^10] and "offer with the Tathāgata's own offering."[^11] This means the claim that "the Lotus sūtra establishes the doctrine of dharma-offering" requires precision: the Chinese formula *dharma-pūjā* derives from Dharmarakṣa, not from the widely circulating Kumārajīva redaction.

## II. Structural Analysis of the Five Dharma-Teacher Lines

### 2.1 The Commentarial Construction of the "Five Types of Dharma-Teachers"

Two overlapping enumerations of action coexist within the Dharma-Teacher Chapter: a four-term list (reception, reading, recitation, explanation, and copying)[^12] and a six-term list (writing, carrying, reading, recitation, offering, and explaining to others).[^13] Both lists coexist within the same chapter with different term counts and orderings, never appearing as a verbatim five-term formula in any single sūtra passage.

The designation "five types of dharma-teachers" (五種法師) — reception, reading, recitation, explanation, and copying — is actually a later commentarial construct from Zhiyi's (智顗) *Miaofa Lianhua Jing Wenju* (妙法蓮華經文句), volume 8B:

> "This chapter's five types of dharma-teachers: first reception, second reading, third recitation, fourth explanation, fifth writing. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (大智度論) specifies six types of dharma-teachers: reception through faith-power, carrying through mindfulness-power, reading from the text, recitation through non-forgetting, explanation through transmission, and interpretation of sacred scriptures requiring explanation because they are difficult. The six types, this sūtra unifies reception and carrying into one, unifies explanation and speaking into one, opens reading and recitation into two, together with writing, makes five."[^14]

Zhiyi merged the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s six types with the Dharma-Teacher Chapter's four-term list, reconstructing the number five through "unify reception and carrying," "unify explanation and speaking," and "open reading and recitation into two." This is not a parallel list from the sūtra text itself but a commentarial construction.

This commentarial nature of the five dharma-teacher lines is critical for understanding the chapter. It means the five teachers are not five "actions toward the sūtra scroll" but five "modes of becoming the dharma-body's extension in the world." Offering is not external to the five methods; it is their **enactment**. Zhiyi's connecting sentence is exceptionally concise:

> "The teacher's marks are precisely the five types of dharma-teachers and the ten kinds of offerings."[^15]

The *xiāng* (相, "mark" or "form") of a dharma-teacher *is* the five types of dharma-teachers, *is* the ten kinds of offerings. The five-teacher persons themselves *are* the doers of the ten offerings. Offering is not external to the five methods; it is the five methods' realization. This breaks the ritualistic frame of offering: offering is not what one burns or presents, but "the five-method persons *themselves* are the offering."

### 2.2 The Substitution-Move: The Sūtra Scroll as the Tathāgata's Whole Body

The chapter's core argument is not a **ranking-move** but a **substitution-move**. The core sentence of this substitution is:

> "Bhaiṣajyarāja! In every place, wherever one expounds, reads, recites, or copies this sūtra, wherever the sūtra scroll resides, one should raise a stūpa of seven jewels, making it extremely tall and broadly adorned, without any need to place *śarīra* relics. Why? Within this place the Tathāgata's whole body already exists."[^16]

This passage establishes an ontological equivalence: where the sūtra scroll resides = where the Tathāgata's whole body resides. No *śarīra* placement is needed because the Tathāgata's whole body is already there. Stūpa-veneration is directly absorbed as sūtra-veneration.

Zhiyi, in the *Wenju*, comments on this passage:

> "Regarding 'no need to place *śarīra*': the *Śāstra* says 'bone fragments are the generated-body relic; sūtra scrolls are the dharma-body relic.' This sūtra *is* the dharma-body relic — there is no need to additionally place a generated-body relic."[^17]

This citation from the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* places the "generated-body relic" (佛陀肉身焚化後的舍利, *śarīra* from physical remains) alongside the "dharma-body relic" (經卷, *dharmadhātu-śarīra* from the sūtra scroll). Both generated body and dharma body have whole and fragment forms, both interpretable. The sūtra scroll *is* the dharma-body relic; placing the scroll *is* placing the dharma-body relic; therefore "no need to additionally place the generated-body relic."

The profundity of the substitution-move lies in this: it does not establish a new offering hierarchy but dissolves the distinction between offering-objects. In traditional Buddhism, offering has three anchors — the Buddha's body (physical), the *śarīra* (post-cremation remains), and the sūtra scroll (dharma text). The Dharma-Teacher Chapter says: where the sūtra scroll resides, the Tathāgata's whole body is present. Three anchors become one.

The dharma-teacher's fourfold protection structure further corroborates this:

> "Bhaiṣajyarāja! Know that after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, those who can write, carry, read, recite, offer, and explain [this sūtra] to others — the Tathāgata will cover them with its robe, and they will be protected and nourished by the Buddhas now present in the other directions. Such persons possess great faith-power, vow-power, and the power of wholesome roots. Know that such persons have dwelt alongside the Tathāgata and will be anointed on the head by the Tathāgata's hand."[^18]

The dharma-teacher receives fourfold protection: (1) covered by the Tathāgata's robe; (2) protected by the Buddhas of other directions; (3) dwelt alongside the Tathāgata; (4) anointed on the head by the Tathāgata's hand. These four are not a hierarchy of merit-rewards but an affirmation of the dharma-teacher's identity: the teacher is not "one who worships the Buddha from outside," but "one whom the Buddha has directly embraced within their **field**."

### 2.3 The Three Ornaments: Kumārajīva's Translation-Workshop Layer

The Dharma-Teacher Chapter's operational triad — "enter the Tathāgata's room, wear the Tathāgata's robe, sit on the Tathāgata's seat" — is key to understanding how the teacher functions as a vehicle:

> "Bhaiṣajyarāja! If there be good men or good women, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, who wish to expound this Lotus Sūtra to the four assemblies — they should enter the Tathāgata's room, wear the Tathāgata's robe, sit on the Tathāgata's seat, and only then should they expound this sūtra broadly to the four assemblies. The Tathāgata's room is the great compassion-heart toward all sentient beings; the Tathāgata's robe is the gentle and forbearing mind; the Tathāgata's seat is the emptiness of all dharmas."[^19]

Each item of the triad has a precise correspondence: room = great compassion, robe = forbearing gentleness, seat = emptiness of all dharmas. This is a set of entry-conditioning: before teaching, the dharma-teacher must first possess these three internal conditions.

Critically, this triadic structure is **Kumārajīva's distinctive expansion**. Dharmarakṣa's *Zhengfa Hua Jing* at the same passage has only a dyad of "robe/seat" — no "great compassion as room" item:

> "Wear the Tathāgata's robe, sit on the World-Honored One's lion-seat, and only then expound this sūtra to the four assemblies. What is 'wearing the Tathāgata's robe'? It is human forbearance, gentleness, peace, and elegance. What is 'the World-Honored One's lion-seat'? It is the understanding that all dharmas are utterly empty and still."[^20]

The "room" semantics is entirely absent from Dharmarakṣa. The triadic structure was expanded at a later stratum of the Sanskrit *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka* text or at Kumārajīva's translation-workshop stratum. The "great compassion as room" item was added at a relatively later stratum.[^21]

Zhiyi, in the *Wenju*, bridges the five methods and the three ornaments:

> "Furthermore, reading, reciting, and writing are outer practice — these are the Tathāgata's robe; reception is inner practice — this is the Tathāgata's seat; explaining for others' benefit is the Tathāgata's room."[^22]

Zhiyi's **mapping** is not mechanical — he extracts from the five methods a dyad of outer practice (reading, reciting, writing) and inner practice (reception), then isolates "explaining for others' benefit" as the room. The final synthesis: "Broadly speaking, this is not so — covering others with compassion, returning benefit to oneself, is called the room; obstructing evil, hiding one's own faults, is called the robe; settling the mind in emptiness enables settling others — settling others and oneself, this is called the seat."[^23]

The structural implication of the triad is: the subject of dharma-offering must first possess these three internal conditions. Emptiness is the prerequisite — "only by settling the mind in emptiness can one settle others."[^24] Prajñā wisdom is not an addendum to dharma-offering; it is its metaphysical precondition.

### 2.4 The Dharma Cannot Propagate Itself: The Bearer as Vehicle

Zhiyi's name-explanation section (釋名段, exegesis of the chapter's name) for the Dharma-Teacher Chapter offers an exceptionally critical insight:

> "Dharma means that which serves as a model; teacher means one who trains craftsmen. Although dharma may serve as a model, its substance cannot propagate itself — it requires a bearer. The five types of passage through the sūtra all qualify as 'teacher.'"[^25]

"Dharma may serve as a model, yet its substance cannot propagate itself" — dharma can function as a norm, but it cannot propagate itself. It requires a person as its vehicle. This sentence is the pivot for understanding the five dharma-teacher lines: the five teachers are not five "things done to dharma" but five "ways dharma comes to exist."

This insight is profoundly significant across Mahāyāna Buddhism. If dharma requires a person to propagate it, then the dharma-teacher is not "a tool for transmitting dharma" but "the condition for dharma's existence." In other words, without dharma-teachers, dharma remains static text; with dharma-teachers, dharma becomes dynamic offering. The teacher-dharma relationship is not "a subject using an instrument" but "a subject *as* the instrument" — this is the substitution-move unfolded at the human level.

> "Broadly speaking, if one models oneself on the five methods, one is a dharma-teacher of self-practice; if one teaches others the five methods, one is a dharma-teacher of transforming others. Through self-modeling, one is generally called a disciple; through transforming others, one is generally called a dharma-teacher. Now, from the broad meaning, this is why the chapter is named 'Dharma-Teacher.'"[^26]

Self-modeling (self-practice of the five methods) and transforming-others (teaching the five methods to others) are not two types of persons but two aspects of the same person. Self-practice *is* transforming-others; transforming-others *is* self-practice. Zhiyi names the chapter from the broad meaning: anyone who broadly models the five methods may be called a dharma-teacher.

## III. Dialogue Among Three East Asian Commentators

### 3.1 Zhiyi: The Tiantai Path of Unification

Zhiyi's interpretation of the Dharma-Teacher Chapter centers on the unification of five methods and ten offerings. He never lists a three-way comparison framework of "dharma-offering vs. material-offering vs. dharma-giving" in his commentary on this chapter — that comparison framework derives from later *Samantabhadra* chapter systems. Tiantai takes another path: it interprets the entire five-methods network as one body of "expounding the sūtra."[^27]

Within Zhiyi's fivefold profound-meaning framework, "dharma-offering" does not appear as an independent level but as a **sub-issue** within the "Third: Function" section. Citing Sengzhao: "From the Buddha-land chapter to the dharma-offering chapter, all indicate the inconceivable."[^28] — from the *Vimalakīrti* "Buddha-land" chapter to the Lotus's "dharma-offering" chapter, all are inconceivable realms. Zhiyi transplanting this structure as a Lotus model: from opening to dharma-offering, "all is wondrous." Dharma-offering falls within "function" — it is the Lotus's reach toward the world (the **dynamic** aspect of expounding), not the Lotus's core. The Lotus's core is *dharmatā* (實相); dharma-offering is the power flowing from it.[^29]

Zhiyi's exegesis on "the sūtra scroll as dharma-body relic" is one of the strongest Han-Chinese doctrinal proofs in this paper. He cites the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s "bone fragments are the generated-body relic; sūtra scrolls are the dharma-body relic" and further **explicates**: "Both generated body and dharma body have whole and fragment forms, both interpretable."[^30] Dharma-offering is precisely where the dharma-body relic is rested — the act of holding the sūtra *itself* completes the supreme offering.

### 3.2 Jizang: The Sanlun Path of Parallel Display

Jizang (吉藏) interprets the Dharma-Teacher Chapter through a "four-gate" structure: first, Purpose-Gate; second, Name-Gate; third, Substance-Gate; fourth, Stage-Gate.[^31] His approach to opening-gate methodology clearly differs from Tiantai's fivefold judging. The Sanlun school prioritizes "purpose" (why this chapter appears) over "substance," reflecting a rational-logical structure rather than a mind-contemplation structure.

Jizang establishes two meanings for "dharma-teacher": "upward, transmitting the great dharma; downward, serving as teacher to beings" + "taking dharma itself as teacher." The crucial point: "only by first taking dharma as teacher can one then become a teacher to others."[^32] This interpretation of "teacher" is fundamentally different from Zhiyi's entry through mind-contemplation. The Sanlun school takes "the Buddhas' teacher is precisely dharma" as its pivot, emphasizing that dharma-giving precedes human-teaching — the ground of dharma-offering lies in "taking dharma as teacher."

Jizang explicitly parallels "human-as-teacher" and "dharma-as-teacher" within the two main-prose sections of the Dharma-Teacher Chapter:

> "Having praised persons, now we praise dharma — through praising persons and dharma, the sūtra's propagation-meaning is completed. The prior section praised persons, showing that persons are dharma-teachers; now praising dharma, taking dharma as dharma-teacher, to explain that the Dharma-Teacher Chapter contains two meanings of dharma-teacher."[^33]

This is the Sanlun school's most fundamental reading of the chapter: dharma-offering is supreme because dharma itself can independently serve as teacher ("the Buddhas' teacher is precisely dharma"). Dharma and person do not merge; they stand in parallel opposition. Jizang's "taking dharma as teacher" does not negate human status — he explicitly says "only by first taking dharma as teacher can one then become a teacher to others" — but he insists that "teacher-of-dharma" is the *precondition* for "teacher-of-person," not its identity. This forms a sharp contrast with Tiantai's unifying path of "self-practice *is* transforming-others."

### 3.3 Zhanran: Post-Period Tiantai's Fusion into the Three Secret Treasures

Zhanran's (湛然) supplement to Zhiyi's five types of dharma-teachers is "progressively reducing many to few" — four reduced to three (body, speech, mind), three reduced to two (self-practice, transforming-others), two reduced to one (the Tathāgata's practice).[^34] This is Zhanran's "perfect integration" logic: the five teachers ultimately converge into one — the Tathāgata's practice, which contains all practices.

Zhanran interprets the Dharma-Teacher Chapter's climax through "the room, robe, and seat as the three secret treasures of the great nirvāṇa":

> "Next, reducing two into one: 'the Tathāgata's practice contains all practices' — this follows the *Mahāparinirvāṇa Sūtra·Saintly Practice Chapter*. There is also one practice that is the Tathāgata's practice, namely the great Mahāparinirvāṇa. Nirvāṇa is precisely the three secret treasures. Therefore, we now use the room, robe, and seat to explain the three treasures. The three treasures are precisely one great nirvāṇa, this great nirvāṇa pervading all places, pervading all dharmas, collectively named one."[^35]

Zhanran directly equates the "enter the Tathāgata's room, wear the Tathāgata's robe, sit on the Tathāgata's seat" at the Dharma-Teacher Chapter's end with the "three secret treasures of the great Mahāparinirvāṇa" from the *Mahāparinirvāṇa Sūtra·Saintly Practice Chapter*. This is a stronger ontological link than Zhiyi's original *Wenju*.

Zhanran explicitly names Jizang (嘉祥) for criticism in the *Wenju-ji*:

> "Jiaxiang also said: 'Opening the two kinds of convenient means, showing the two kinds of truth.' Formerly it was not said that the two are convenient, hence the gate was closed; now it is said that the two kinds are convenient, hence the gate of convenience opens. The opening-meaning is good, but establishing two deviates from the teaching."[^36]

Zhanran's by-name critique of Jizang (嘉祥) is a documented historical exchange, not a posthumous fabrication.[^37] The two schools actually engaged each other at adjacent passages of the Dharma-Teacher Chapter (the praise-of-dharma section), not through later virtual opposition.

### 3.4 Cross-Sūtra Comparison: Dharma-Offering and Dharma-Giving

The final section of the Han-Chinese commentary dialogue requires a cross-sūtra comparison frame. The seven-layer argumentation in volume 11 of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509) provides a Mādhyamika structural base:

> "Why? The fruit of material giving lies in the desire-realm; the fruit of dharma-giving may lie in the three realms or beyond them. Furthermore, the mouth speaks purely, deeply grasping the principle, the mind also grasps it — thus transcending the three realms. Furthermore, material giving is limited; dharma-giving is infinite. Material giving ends; dharma-giving does not end — like adding firewood to a flame, its brightness increases. Furthermore, the fruit of material giving has little purity and much defilement; the fruit of dharma-giving has little defilement and much purity. Furthermore, to make great material offerings, one must await many resources; dharma-giving arises from the mind alone, needing nothing external. Furthermore, material giving causes the four great elements and sense faculties to grow; dharma-giving causes the root of non-outflow, powers, awakenings, and enlightening dharmas to be complete. Furthermore, the dharma of material giving exists with or without Buddhas in the world; but dharma-giving exists only in a Buddha's presence. Therefore, know that dharma-giving is extremely difficult!"[^38]

Nāgārjuna establishes the superiority of dharma-giving over material offering through seven layers of argumentation: (1) fruit-realm comparison; (2) deep mind-grasping vs. mere mouth-speaking; (3) limited/ending vs. infinite/non-ending; (4) little-purity-much-defilement vs. little-defilement-much-purity; (5) needing external resources vs. arising from mind alone; (6) four great elements vs. non-outflow roots; (7) common in the world with or without Buddhas vs. only with a Buddha's appearance.

This seven-layer argumentation forms a meaningful contrast with the Dharma-Teacher Chapter's substitution-move: T1509 establishes dharma-giving's superiority through **hierarchical rhetoric**, while T0293's "of all offerings, dharma-offering is supreme" in eight characters establishes it through **axiomatic ranking** — both follow ranking approaches. The Dharma-Teacher Chapter's distinctive contribution is the substitution-move: not comparing which is higher, but saying the sūtra scroll *is* the stūpa, holding the sūtra *is* the offering, the dharma-teacher *is* the Buddha-body's incarnation.

The eight-character formula from the Samantabhadra chapter (T0293) is the most popular dharma-offering expression in Han-Chinese Buddhism:

> "Good man! Of all offerings, dharma-offering is supreme. Namely: offering of practice according to the teaching, offering of benefiting beings, offering of receiving beings, offering of suffering for beings, offering of diligent wholesome roots, offering of not abandoning bodhisattva practice, offering of not departing from bodhicitta."[^40]

The Samantabhadra chapter establishes a seven-item enumeration of dharma-offering, taking the hierarchical-ranking route to its furthest extent.[^41] But the Dharma-Teacher Chapter does not take this path. It replaces "of all offerings, dharma-offering is supreme" with "this place already contains the Tathāgata's whole body" — not ranking, but *equation*.

## IV. Dialogue with Western Scholarship

### 4.1 Lopez: Sūtra-as-Body — Concession and Push-Back

Donald S. Lopez Jr., in *The Lotus Sūtra: A Biography*, argues that the Lotus sūtra's uniqueness lies in the sūtra itself declaring that it *is* the Buddha-body's presence. Places where the sūtra is received, read, and recited require stūpa-veneration because, within the Lotus's own theory, the sūtra scroll is not "a record about the Buddha" but "another manifestation-form of the Buddha." Lopez further argues that this **sūtra-cult** is not a later folk-deviation from orthodoxy but a core gesture of Mahāyāna orthodoxy — the sūtra scroll is ritually equivalent to *śarīra* relics and Buddha-images, the three constituting Mahāyāna's triad of physical entities.[^42]

Lopez's **sūtra-as-body** framework has exceptionally strong explanatory power for the Dharma-Teacher Chapter's "this place already contains the Tathāgata's whole body" passage. The sūtra scroll *does* demand being read as the Buddha-body's presence rather than as a document-carrier. Lopez's argument for "the sūtra as living Buddha-body" is one of the most explanatorily potent core theses in modern Lotus scholarship.

However, Lopez's framework emphasizes **sūtra-as-object** (the sūtra scroll as the thing-offered), while the five dharma-teacher lines are not "five modes of offering to the sūtra scroll" but "five modes of becoming the dharma-body's extension." The Lotus's core reality is not **cult-of-the-text** but **cult-of-the-text-keeper**.[^43] Lopez misses the dimension of "the bearer as an **incarnation node** of the dharma-body's presence." The Dharma-Teacher Chapter is, in fact, a doctrine *of the text-keeper*, not simply a doctrine of sūtra-veneration.

Returning to this paper's substitution-move framework, Lopez's sūtra-as-body is necessary but insufficient: the sūtra scroll indeed *is* the Buddha-body, but the dharma-teacher *even more so* — the teacher is the fivefold extension of the Buddha-body.

### 4.2 Pye: The Skillful Means Chapter-Order Framework — Concession and Push-Back

Michael Pye, in *Skilful Means: A Concept in Mahāyāna Buddhism*, argues that *upāya-kauśalya* is not a "teaching technique" but the hermeneutic structure of Mahāyāna as a whole. Pye particularly emphasizes that the Lotus's chapter-order constitutes a progressive argument: Skillful Means establishes principle → Parable through Bestowal chapters concretize it → the Dharma-Teacher Chapter turns toward "how the upāya principle is extended through disciples."[^44]

Pye's chapter-order progressive reading holds. The Dharma-Teacher Chapter's question "how ought one teach?" indeed requires framing the act of teaching as an extension of upāya; otherwise, "reception and recitation" would be misread as merit-accumulation.

However, Pye's framework nearly entirely resides at the doctrinal hermeneutics level, handling almost none of the **ritual / somatic** dimension of "offering." The Dharma-Teacher Chapter's lamp-burning, incense, and garlica passages have strong ritual somaticity that a purely hermeneutic approach cannot capture.[^45] One must depart from Pye and supplement with Lopez's **sūtra-as-relic** dimension to achieve completeness.

After combining Pye and Lopez, the full image of the Dharma-Teacher Chapter emerges: the principle of the Skillful Means Chapter (Pye) must be realized through "the sūtra scroll *is* the body" (Lopez) as the fivefold incarnation of the dharma-teacher (this paper's core thesis).

### 4.3 Stone: Practice-Equals-Realization — Concession and Push-Back

Jacqueline I. Stone clarifies the **performative** character of "reception and recitation" — the act of reciting *itself* is the manifestation of awakening, not a means toward it. She calls this **practice-equals-realization** and traces its Han-Chinese source to Tiantai Zhiyi's "six identities" and Zhanran's "inanimate beings possess nature."[^46]

Stone's reading of "reception and recitation" is deeper than Lopez's — Lopez handles "sūtra-as-object," Stone handles **practitioner-as-locus**. Stone's tracing back to Tiantai origins also provides a Western correspondence point for this paper's §III dialogue among Zhiyi, Jizang, and Zhanran.

However, Stone's primary research domain is medieval Japanese Lotus Buddhism (Nichiren lineage and Taimitsu), with little engagement of the Han-Chinese framework.[^47] This paper must be careful not to project Japanese **post-era reception** conclusions back into the Han-Chinese context. Stone's thesis has strong support within the Lotus sūtra itself, but extending it to the T0293 dāna-ranking issue ("of all offerings, dharma-offering is supreme") is outside Stone's direct handling.

Returning to this paper's substitution-move framework, Stone's practice-equals-realization resonates with "dharma may serve as a model, yet it cannot propagate itself": awakening is not outside dharma; dharma is not outside the dharma-teacher — practice *is* realization; holding dharma *is* manifestation. In Stone's Japanese Lotus research, the Nichiren "chanting the title *is* Buddhahood" thesis can be viewed as a later radicalization of this paper's core thesis — but Stone herself maintains distance from this, tracing instead to Tiantai's origins rather than Nichiren's innovation.

The three concessions and push-backs combine rather than substitute. Lopez's sūtra-as-body provides the sūtra's ontological status; Pye's upāya framework provides the hermeneutic background of teacher-practice; Stone's practice-equals-realization provides the performative structure of realization. Their combined limitations point precisely to this paper's core thesis: the Dharma-Teacher Chapter is not about the worship *of* the sūtra, but about the incarnation *through* the bearer.

---

## V. Conclusion

The Dharma-Teacher Chapter's substitution-move — "this place already contains the Tathāgata's whole body" — constitutes the most profound offering-theory breakthrough within Mahāyāna Buddhism. It does not compare offering hierarchies; it dissolves the offering-frame itself. The sūtra scroll *is* the stūpa; holding the sūtra *is* the offering; the dharma-teacher *is* the Buddha-body.

The five dharma-teacher lines (reception, reading, recitation, explanation, copying) are not five actions toward the sūtra scroll but five incarnation modalities of the *dharmakāya* in the human realm. Zhiyi reveals: "dharma may serve as a model, yet its substance cannot propagate itself" — dharma cannot propagate itself; it requires a bearer. These five modes of the bearer's incarnation constitute Part VII's core: how the dharma-body manifests through the bearer.

Part VI closes on the "map of inverted giving" — the Śūraṅgama's fifty skandha-māras as **negative inversion**. Part VII opens on "five modes of the dharma-body's presence through the bearer" — from "one ought not offer thus" toward "how the dharma-body manifests through the bearer."[^48]

The Dharma-Teacher Chapter's closing verse reads: "Whoever nears a dharma-teacher swiftly attains the bodhisattva path; following this teacher's learning, one sees innumerable Buddhas."[^49] Nearing the dharma-teacher *is* swiftly attaining the bodhisattva path — the teacher is not a tool toward the bodhisattva path; the teacher *is* the bodhisattva path's vehicle. The Ever-Disparaging Bodhisattva Chapter's bodily prostration will continue this thread, but in the form of **prasāda-dāna** (信心之供養).[50]

---

## VI. Appendix

As the **opening chapter** of Part VII, the Dharma-Teacher Chapter sets directions for the two subsequent papers. P22 (Ever-Disparaging Bodhisattva Chapter) will treat bodily prostration as a typology of *prasāda-dāna* — "not disparaging" is the **axiomatic affirmation** of all beings' Buddha-nature. P23 (Bhaiṣajyarāja Chapter) will address the hermeneutic crisis of **self-immolation** — is body-burning the ultimate form of true dharma-offering?[^51]

The Dharma-Teacher Chapter, the Ever-Disparaging Chapter, and the Bhaiṣajyarāja Chapter constitute Part VII's three-fold structure: the Dharma-Teacher establishes the bearer's incarnation modalities, the Ever-Disparaging establishes the prostration-posture toward all beings' Buddha-nature, and the Bhaiṣajyarāja establishes the extreme case of body-offering. All three point to the same thesis: how the dharma-body unfolds in the human realm.
