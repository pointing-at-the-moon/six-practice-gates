# *Cāga* as an Object of Recollection
**The Fifth Position of *Cāga* in the Six-*Anussati* Sequence**

*中文主題：捨作為隨念對境——cāga 在六隨念序列中的第五位*

---

## Abstract

For the contemporary English reader immersed in the default grammar of mindfulness — observe present arising, do not judge, do not grasp — returning again and again to one's own past generous acts, holding them as sustained attention, feels suspect on three counts at once, which is why the practice has effectively disappeared from Western Buddhist pedagogy. Yet in the earliest Pāli strata this exact movement has a canonical name, *cāgānussati* (recollection of one's own giving), and sits as the fifth of six *anussati*, receiving stable methodological treatment across *sutta* and commentary alike. This paper argues that what makes *cāga* unique among *anussati* objects is that it is the only recollection whose object is a concrete event-trace of the practitioner's own accomplished action — Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, one's own virtue-formula, and the deva-qualities are each external or normative — and that this object-uniqueness determines the fifth-position placement as the hinge of a three-stage arc (attending-to-other → attending-to-self → self-in-mirror-of-other), forming the functional pair to the *cāga*-as-faculty analysis argued in the previous paper. The evidence proceeds in three layers: first, reconstructing the three-stage sequence via AN 6.25 *Anussatiṭṭhānasutta* with T0099 *sūtra* 931 and *Ekottara Āgama juan* 1–2; second, using Buddhaghosa's two-method distinction in Vism VII.5 and his *dāna* / *saṁvibhāga* gloss to read the dual register built into the *cāgānussati* formula itself; third, using the split-translation 施 / 捨 surfacing within T0099 *sūtra* 931 to refine the previous paper's systematic-softening verdict into a layered one. Read clearly, looking back at what one has given ceases to be a religiously-coded self-affirmation and returns to its canonical place as a stable, accessible, liberation-directed meditative entry point.

**Keywords:** *cāgānussati*, recollection of giving, six *anussati*, *ārammaṇa*, object uniqueness, fifth position, three-stage sequence, *muttacāga*, *yācayogo*, *dānasaṁvibhāgarato*, *upacāra*, *appanā*, *Visuddhimagga* VII, dual method, *dāna* / *saṁvibhāga*, Mahānāma, T0099 dual track, T0125 *Ekottara Āgama*, McMindfulness reverse, faculty/object duality, NIAN-A, middle-way correction

## I. Introduction

Set the scene inside any contemporary Western mindfulness setting — the opening moments of an eight-week MBSR program, the first guided session of a meditation app, an introductory talk in a community sitting group. Before the first breath is counted, three disciplines are already in place. Repeated across countless teachers, books, and app scripts, they have become the default grammar of Western mindfulness pedagogy: *observe present arising* (non-reactive attention to what appears here and now), *non-judgmental awareness* (hold it without evaluation), and *let it pass, don't grasp* (no attachment, no fixation on the object). This configuration, imported into Western mental-health systems on a vast scale through the Kabat-Zinn lineage and its descendants, has become the unexamined starting position of contemporary English-language mindfulness.

Inside this configuration, taking a generous act of one's own from this morning — waving someone through at an intersection, buying a colleague a second coffee, handing the last of dinner to the familiar person at the corner — and *returning to it, holding it as a sustained object of attention, turning it over in meditation* feels immediately suspect. Recalling one's own generosity sounds like self-affirmation. Holding it as an object sounds like grasping. Returning to it repeatedly sounds like fixation on *ārammaṇa*. Each of the three default disciplines is violated. The contemporary reader, almost automatically, files this kind of movement under "religiously coded self-congratulation," loosely analogizes it to the more-accepted "gratitude journaling" of positive psychology, and lets the matter drop.

Yet in the earliest stratum of the Pāli texts, this very movement that contemporary English mindfulness reflexively avoids has its own canonical name: **recollection of letting-go** (*cāgānussati*, 捨隨念). It is one of six *anussati* ("recollections"), and it holds the fifth position. The Buddha teaches it to specific disciples — Mahānāma the Sakyan, Kāḷigodhā the Sakyan laywoman, Mahākaccāna — repeatedly and by name. Buddhaghosa devotes a dedicated section, Vism VII.5, to its meditative method. The Chinese *Ekottara Āgama* (增一阿含經, T0125) lists it as the fifth of ten recollections in *juan* 1 "Chapter on the Ten Recollections" (〈十念品第二〉) and expounds it item by item in *juan* 2 "Chapter of Extended Exposition" (〈廣演品第三〉). It is not a marginal practice. It is a standard coordinate on the early meditative map.

This raises the question the paper pursues: what did early Buddhism know about *returning to one's own generous acts* that contemporary English-language mindfulness vocabulary does not know? Why was a movement that now seems to violate three core disciplines enshrined as one of six stable entry points into recollection?

The answer does not lie in technique — not in some particular operational refinement — but in the *structural uniqueness of cāga as an anussati object*. The previous paper, "*Cāga* as a Faculty of Stream-Entry," handled *cāga* as **a function of the path**: the fourth-limb rotation candidate of *sotāpattiyaṅga*, one of the five virtues perfuming the mind, the direct countermeasure to the root construction of "mine" driving the saṃsāric cycle. That layer is the **faculty side** — *what cāga is*. This paper handles the **object side** — *cāga as a structurally isolable meditation object*, holdable as *ārammaṇa* for recollective practice, playing what role within the six-*anussati* system, why stationed at the fifth position, in what relation to the other five *anussati* objects.

The argument unfolds in three layers. **First,** the six-recollection sequence is internally a three-stage arc — Buddha / Dhamma / Saṅgha (*pasāda*-objects directed toward external paradigms) → *sīla* / *cāga* (objects returning to the practitioner's own conduct and mental configuration) → *deva* (objects seemingly external but functionally serving as a mirror in which the five virtues are reflected back onto the self). *Cāga* at position five is the pivot where the sequence turns from "attending inward to oneself" into "attending to oneself in the mirror of others." **Second,** *cāga* as an object possesses a *singular event-character*: the other five objects are all either external or normative (the Buddha's nine qualities, the Dhamma's six marks, the Saṅgha's nine attributes, the eight marks of one's own virtue, the deva's five virtues); only *cāga*'s object is **a specific action-event that has already occurred** — with time-location, concrete circumstance, recoverable trace. This event-character determines every structural particular of *cāga* as a recollective object: why Buddhaghosa designs two methods of entry rather than one; why the practice stops at access concentration (*upacāra*) and does not ascend to absorption (*appanā*); why, inside *devatānussati*, *cāga* functions as a material for self-reflection. **Third,** *cāga*-as-object and *cāga*-as-faculty form a functional **faculty-object pair**: the faculty (substance) is the inner configuration that makes recollection possible; the object (function) is the actual *ārammaṇa* held during recollective practice. The two are interdependent; neither can be dispensed with.

Read from this direction, the contemporary English reader's discomfort with "returning to one's own good acts" is *half right*: *if* the movement is read as self-affirmation or religiously coded gratitude journaling, it does indeed violate contemporary mindfulness discipline. But this is a misreading of the movement itself. Early Buddhist *cāgānussati* is not a savoring of the self-evaluation "I did a good thing"; it is a sustained concentration on *an event of cāga* as an object. The former takes the self as pivot and clings to it; the latter takes the event as pivot and is drawn by the event's *nimitta* into concentration. Read clearly, *cāgānussati* returns from "a suspect religious movement" to its original place on the early meditation map: a specific, accessible, liberation-directed entry point.

## II. Canonical Foundation

The evidence in this section runs along two axes. **Axis A (discourses)** is organized into four groups, corresponding to the canonical exposition of the six-recollection sequence, its parallel variants, its two-stage architecture, and the Chinese-translation expansion. **Axis B (commentary)** centers on Buddhaghosa's systematic treatment of the six *anussati* in the seventh chapter of the *Visuddhimagga*, particularly its fifth section on *cāgānussati*. In keeping with the NIAN-A dual-track discipline of this volume, every Nikāya reference is paired with its Chinese-translation parallel. *Visuddhimagga* citations follow this vault's licensing metadata of `publication_safe: false` and are *paraphrased only*.

### 2.1 Axis A: Discourses (Pāli with Chinese parallels)

**Group 1: The canonical exposition of the six recollections — AN 6.25 and its Chinese parallel**

*Aṅguttara Nikāya* 6.25, the *Anussatiṭṭhānasutta*[^1], is the representative canonical source in which the six recollections are expounded *as a system*. The sutta opens with the enumeration: "Mendicants, there are these six topics of recollection. What six? Recollection of the Buddha, recollection of the Dhamma, recollection of the Saṅgha, recollection of ethical conduct, **recollection of letting-go**, recollection of the deities." Each of the six is unfolded in turn using an identical formula for the meditative movement — "At such a time the noble disciple's mind is not obsessed by greed, hatred, or delusion; their mind is straight, inclined toward the Buddha / Dhamma / Saṅgha / virtue / letting-go / deities." Each ends with the same causal chain of access: "With the mind thus straight, the noble disciple gains inspiration in the meaning (*atthaveda*), inspiration in the Dhamma (*dhammaveda*), gladness connected with the Dhamma (*dhammūpasaṁhitaṁ pāmojjaṁ*). From gladness arises rapture (*pīti*); then bodily tranquility (*kāyapassaddhi*); then bliss (*sukha*); then concentration (*samādhi*)."

For the fifth position, the object-formula reads: "The noble disciple recollects their own letting-go: 'It is my gain, my great gain, that in a generation consumed by the stain of stinginess (*macchera*), I dwell at home with a mind freed from such stain, **with letting-go loosened** (*muttacāgo*), **with hand washed clean** (*payatapāṇi*), **delighting in relinquishment** (*vossaggarato*), **responsive to those who request** (*yācayogo*), **delighting in giving and sharing** (*dānasaṁvibhāgarato*)." This five-marker formula is **identical** to the fourth-limb *cāga* definition at SN 55.32 cited in the previous paper — the fourth limb of stream-entry and the object-formula for *cāgānussati* share the same five-marker definition. The identity tells us the two layers are mutually defining: the fourth-limb *cāga* is **the configuration of mental factors** (faculty layer); the *cāgānussati* object is **the surfacing of that configuration in a concrete action-event** (object layer). Same formula, different pointing.

The closing section on the sixth recollection, *devatānussati*, then brings the five virtues into view: the noble disciple recollects the deities "by what faith, what virtue, what learning, **what letting-go**, what wisdom" they attained their station, and reflects back "such faith, such virtue, such learning, **such letting-go**, such wisdom are present in me also." These five (*saddhā · sīla · suta · cāga · paññā*) are **not** the Stage-1 pentad on which the six recollections rest (see Group 3 below); they are the *five-virtue mirror pattern* internal to the *devatānussati* section. The distinction is philologically decisive and involves a refinement to the previous paper's framing, which will be addressed in §3.3.

In keeping with the NIAN-A dual-track discipline, the Chinese parallels are cited alongside. *Saṃyukta Āgama* (雜阿含經) *juan* 33, *sūtra* 931[^2], is the lay-track Chinese parallel to AN 6.25. The sutta enumerates "six recollections — recollection of the Tathāgata, recollection of the Dhamma, recollection of the Saṅgha, recollection of ethical conduct, **recollection of the matter of giving** (念施事), recollection of the matter of the deities." The fifth object-term shifts from *cāga* to "the matter of giving" (施事) — the systematic substitution already recorded in §2.1 Group 4 of the previous paper. However, an additional internal detail from *sūtra* 931 deserves attention: within the *devatānussati* section's enumeration of the five virtues, the sūtra uses **two distinct renderings**. In one occurrence it reads "pure ethical conduct, **giving** (施), learning, **letting-go** (捨), wisdom" (施 and 捨 used in parallel within the same list); in another occurrence it is abbreviated to "ethical conduct, giving, learning, wisdom" (a four-virtue variant omitting the fourth position). This internal bifurcation shows that the translation workshop — Guṇabhadra's (劉宋求那跋陀羅) mid-fifth-century Liu Song bureau — **had not lost the distinction** between *cāga*'s outer action-layer (施) and its inner letting-go-layer (捨); it actively used both renderings within a single paragraph to preserve the two-layer meaning, choosing "recollection of the matter of giving" only for the sūtra title and the recollection's nominal object. The previous paper's verdict of "systematic Chinese softening" must therefore be *refined* — see §3.3.

A second set of Chinese parallels, *Ekottara Āgama juan* 1 "Chapter on the Ten Recollections" (〈十念品第二〉)[^3] and *juan* 2 "Chapter of Extended Exposition" (〈廣演品第三〉)[^4], presents the monk-track rendering. The "Ten Recollections" chapter sets out the sequence in summary verse (*uddāna*): "Buddha, Dhamma, noble-Saṅgha-recollection; virtue, giving, and the deva-recollection; rest, in-out-breath-recollection; body, death-recollection last" — the first six preserve the AN 6.25 sequence; beyond six, four additional objects are added (recollection of tranquility, of in-and-out breath, of the body, of death). The "Extended Exposition" chapter unfolds each of the ten in turn, one sūtra per item. For the fifth, "recollection of giving," it uses the monk-track formula: "recollect the surpassing giving of deities and humans… just as I am today able, alone, to give, without stinginess, with a mind of shame and conscience." Notably, across these two *juan* the rendering is **uniformly "recollection of giving" (念施) with no parallel use of 捨** — the monk-track Chinese rendering and the lay-track T0099 *sūtra* 931 diverge on the handling of *cāga*'s inner/outer layers. This inter-track divergence is itself evidence that T0099's translation bureau had not lost the distinction (had the distinction been lost, the lay-track *sūtra* 931 would not have used 施/捨 in deliberate parallel).

**Group 2: Six-recollection parallel variants — AN 6.10 / 6.26 / 6.9**

Beyond the canonical exposition, several parallel variants exist for the six-recollection sequence. The closest to AN 6.25 is *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 6.10, the *Mahānāmasutta*[^5]. This sutta **is the one genuinely addressed to Mahānāma the Sakyan** — its opening reports Mahānāma's visit to the Buddha and his question: "For one who has gained the fruit, understood the teaching, and attained lasting deathlessness, what dhamma should be dwelt in often?" The Buddha answers with the six recollections as a set, each unfolded in a formula nearly identical to AN 6.25; the fifth-position *cāga* formula is the same. The difference from AN 6.25 lies only in framing — AN 6.25 is the Buddha's generalized exposition to the assembly of mendicants; AN 6.10 is personal instruction to Mahānāma. The previous paper's citation of "the five-virtue foundation recorded at AN 6.25" as bearing on the Mahānāma-dialogue context represents an attribution imprecision that should be corrected: for the generalized six-recollection canonical exposition, cite AN 6.25; for Mahānāma-addressed personal instruction, cite AN 6.10; the two may be paired as "AN 6.25 (≈ AN 6.10)" when both frames apply. The present paper observes this distinction strictly.

*Aṅguttara Nikāya* 6.26, the *Mahākaccānasutta*[^6], is a further parallel variant with Mahākaccāna as the expositor. It introduces two additional framing concepts: *gedha* (greedy attachment) and *ākāsasama* ("like space") — the six recollections as a liberation-path for beings entangled in craving, the mind spacious like the sky holding the five virtues. This framing will be appropriated in Part IV's Mahāyāna-turn chapter; the present paper notes its existence without deployment. *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 6.9, the second *Anussatiṭṭhānasutta*[^7], is the barest short-form listing — the names of the six without exposition; a compressed parallel to AN 6.25.

**Group 3: The two-stage architecture of AN 11.11 — six recollections resting on the five faculties**

*Aṅguttara Nikāya* 11.11, the first *Mahānāmasutta*[^8], is Mahānāma's question to the Buddha about *kathaṁbahulavihārī* — "in what mode of dwelling should one abide often?" The Buddha answers with a two-stage architecture: first establish oneself in five qualities, then cultivate six further qualities upon this base. **The Stage-1 pentad is the *pañcindriya*** — *saddhā* (faith-faculty), *vīriya* (energy-faculty), *sati* (mindfulness-faculty), *samādhi* (concentration-faculty), *paññā* (wisdom-faculty). The Stage-2 hexad is the six recollections — Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, letting-go, deities. The bridge between the two is explicit: "Having established yourself in these five qualities, Mahānāma, you should further cultivate six qualities" (*Imesu kho, Mahānāma, pañcasu dhammesu patiṭṭhāya cha dhamme uttari bhāveyyāsi*).

This sutta plays two roles in the argument. First, it supplies the *capacity base* for the six recollections — these are not a tower built from nothing; they are meditative practices performed on the foundation of already-established faculties. *Cāga* as the object of the fifth recollection is a specific-event *ārammaṇa* held by a practitioner in whom the *pañcindriya* have been stabilized. Second, it provides a decisive philological calibration point for the *cāga*-as-faculty / *cāga*-as-object distinction examined in §3.3.

One refinement is required here. The previous paper, "*Cāga* as a Faculty of Stream-Entry," when handling AN 11.11 in §2.1 Group 3, correctly listed the *pañcindriya* five items (faith, energy, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom) but framed them in the surrounding discussion as though they were the same pentad that appears at SN 55.21 and in AN 6.25's *devatānussati* section — the five virtues of *faith, virtue, learning, letting-go, wisdom*. These are in fact **two different pentads**: the *pañcindriya* is a capacity-layer pentad on which meditative practice rests, and it **does not contain *cāga***; the five-virtue formula (*saddhā · sīla · suta · cāga · paññā*) used at SN 55.21 and in AN 6.25's *devatānussati* section is a different enumeration, and it explicitly contains *cāga*. The refinement for this paper is: *cāga* as faculty genuinely appears in two textual positions — as **the fourth limb of stream-entry** (the main stage of P02, §III) and as **the fourth of the five virtues in the *devatānussati* mirror section** (within the six recollections themselves; see §III.3 below) — but it does **not** appear at the faculty-base layer of *pañcindriya*. This refinement does not overturn P02's main argument (which rested on SN 55.31–33 and SN 55.39 and had no reliance on the *pañcindriya*), but it adjusts its framing; on the main thesis of this paper (*cāga*-as-object), it has no material impact.

*Saṃyukta Āgama sūtra* 932[^9] is the Chinese parallel to AN 11.11 — the monastic instruction frames "one should take five qualities (faith, ethical conduct, learning, *giving*, wisdom) as the foundation, and upon this foundation further cultivate six recollections." The Chinese version's Stage-1 pentad is **not** *pañcindriya*; it is the five virtues of SN 55.21 / AN 6.25's *devatānussati* — with *cāga* rendered as 施 at the fourth position. This forms another layer of contrast with the internal split-translation of Group 1's T0099 *sūtra* 931: the Chinese translation bureau here chose to **fold two distinct pentads into one** (substituting the five virtues for the five faculties), which is a structural restructuring of the Pāli source. The motivations and consequences of this choice lie beyond the present paper's scope and are reserved for a later Part IV philological chapter.

**Group 4: The Chinese-translation expansion from six recollections to ten**

The *Ekottara Āgama*'s "Chapter on the Ten Recollections" and "Chapter of Extended Exposition" expand the six recollections to ten by adding four further objects: **recollection of tranquility** (*upasamānussati*, 念休息), **recollection of in-and-out breath** (*ānāpānassati*, 念安般), **recollection of the body** (*kāyagatāsati*, 念身 — *Ekottara Āgama* also renders "recollection of the body's impermanence" 念身非常), and **recollection of death** (*maraṇassati*, 念死). This expansion path has an elegant philological counterpart: Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* treatment of the ten recollections (*dasa anussati*) places the first six (i.e., the AN 6.25 canonical set) in chapter VII and the remaining four in chapter VIII, with the order *maraṇassati · kāyagatāsati · ānāpānassati · upasamānussati* — an order **exactly parallel** to the six-plus-four expansion in *Ekottara Āgama*. The Chinese *Āgama* and the Pāli commentarial tradition independently arrived at structurally near-identical taxonomic treatments of "the four beyond the six." This is a cross-linguistic structural correspondence worth noting in §VI; it will not be unfolded here.

### 2.2 Axis B: Commentary

- **Buddhaghosa, *Visuddhimagga* chapter VII, *Chaanussatiniddesa***[^10], with particular reference to its fifth section, *Cāgānussati-niddesa*. Buddhaghosa's systematic treatment can be organized into four layers, all paraphrased per the vault's licensing discipline. **First, object-definition:** the object of *cāgānussati* is the practitioner's own *cāga* — not an abstract category of "the generous person," nor some exemplary historical figure's *cāga*, but the practitioner's own already-established inclination to give, together with the ongoing giving-action itself. **Second, the six-marker formula:** Buddhaghosa unfolds the object into six internal features — *vigatamalamacchera* (mind freed from the stain of stinginess), *muttacāga* (letting-go loosened), *payatapāṇi* (hand washed clean, ready at any moment to hand over with one's own hand), *vossaggarata* (delighting in the very act of relinquishment), *yācayoga* (responsive to those who request), *dānasaṁvibhāgarata* (delighting in both outward giving [*dāna*] and sharing-from-what-one-uses [*saṁvibhāga*]). **Third, the two methods of cultivation:** Buddhaghosa gives two paths of entry. *Method (a)* suits the practitioner already established in the habit of giving and sharing — sit in solitude, take one's existing giving-nature directly as object. *Method (b)* suits the practitioner in whom this habit is not yet formed — at dawn, make a resolute vow for the day ("I shall not take even one mouthful of food before giving something first"), act on the vow during the day toward one who is worthy to receive, and take the event-trace of that act as *nimitta* for recollection in later solitary sitting. **Fourth, the reason for access-only structure:** Buddhaghosa states explicitly that *cāgānussati*, like each of the six *anussati*, reaches only access concentration (*upacāra*) and does not enter absorption (*appanā*). The reason is identical across all six: the object is a composite of multiple virtue-items (*nānappakāraguṇa*), and the attentive mind must move between the items, unable to stabilize on a single markless object to the point of absorption. This reason is not a *cāga*-specific limitation; it is a **common structural feature** of the entire *anussati* system — *anussati* as a system is designed to come to rest at access concentration. This is functional positioning, not shortcoming.

- **The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra*'s *Śrāvakabhūmi* and *Bodhisattvabhūmi* sections** will be unfolded in Part IV (P11, *From Dāna to Dāna-Pāramitā*, and P14, *The Bodhisattvabhūmi Chapter on Giving*); they are registered here as forward reference only, not as active sources.

## III. Core Argument

Three layers of argument unfold in turn: the three-stage internal structure of the six-recollection sequence (§3.1), *cāga*'s uniqueness as an *anussati* object (§3.2), and the faculty/object duality connecting *cāga*-as-faculty and *cāga*-as-object, together with two refinements to the previous paper's framing (§3.3).

### 3.1 The three-stage structure of the six-recollection sequence — the fifth position as hinge

The order of the six recollections is fixed: Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, letting-go, deities. This order is not arbitrary; it is an internally segmented arc. Examining the object-type of each of the six:

- **Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha:** each object is an **external paradigm or system** — the Buddha's nine qualities (the *itipi so* formula), the Dhamma's six attributes (*svākkhāto* and its fellows), the Saṅgha's nine features (the four-stage *paṭipanna* plus the five *āhuneyyo* etc.). The practitioner's movement is to direct attention outward toward these objects and, through sustained attention on the depth (*gambhīratā*) and breadth (*nānappakāraguṇa*) of these qualities, arouse *pasāda* (confident faith). The internal direction of this segment is **from self outward**.

- **Virtue and letting-go:** each object is **the practitioner's own person** — the object of *sīlānussati* is one's own already-maintained eightfold noble virtue (*akhaṇḍa · acchidda · asabala · akammāsa · bhujissa · viññuppasattha · aparāmaṭṭha · samādhisaṁvattanika*), and the object of *cāgānussati* is one's own already-formed inclination to give and the already-occurred concrete giving-events (the six-marker formula beginning with *muttacāga*). The practitioner's movement turns from outward-projection to **inward return** — attention returns to one's own conduct, one's own configuration of mental factors. The internal direction of this segment is **from outer to inner**.

- **The deities:** the object appears external (the six heavens of the sensuous realm and beyond), but its *mechanism* is a **mirror**. The practitioner recollects the deities "by what faith, virtue, learning, letting-go, wisdom" they were reborn into their stations, then reflects "I too have such faith, virtue, learning, letting-go, wisdom." Attention is nominally projected outward to the deities, but the functional operation is to use the deities as a mirror in which to reflect the self back. The internal direction of this segment is **self folded back through external reflection**.

Connecting the three stages: *from self outward* (the Three Jewels) → *from outer to inner* (virtue, letting-go) → *self folded back through external reflection* (deities). This is a three-stage arc of "attending-to-other → attending-to-self → self-in-mirror-of-other." ***Cāga* at the fifth position is the lower terminus of the second stage and the threshold of the third stage** — it both consummates the "inward return" initiated by *sīlānussati* and supplies the necessary material for "self-in-mirror-of-other" in *devatānussati* (to be able to reflect oneself against the *cāga* possessed by the deities, one must first have learned to return one's attention to one's own *cāga*-events). The fifth position is not a descending rank; it is a structural hinge.

This three-stage reading explains several otherwise puzzling features of the sequence. Why is *sīla* placed before *cāga* rather than after? Because virtue is the **entry point of the inward-return stage** — virtue's marks are more normatively specified and more externally observable (eight definite observable features), making it the natural first step in the transition from "outward-projection" into "inward return." Why is *cāga* placed before *deva* rather than after? Because *cāga*, as a **concrete event that has already occurred**, is the necessary intermediate between *sīla* and *deva*: without *cāga*'s step, the continuity of "one's own action as raw material for recollection" would be broken, and the deva-mirror would have nothing to refract. These structural placements are not Buddhaghosa's retrospective rationalizations — they can be read directly out of the object-formulas of the sutta itself.

### 3.2 The uniqueness of *cāga* as an *anussati* object — the singular event-character

Laid out side by side, the objects of the six recollections reveal a structural fact that leaps out immediately:

| Recollection | Object type |
|---|---|
| Recollection of the Buddha | Nine qualities — a normative list of virtue-items |
| Recollection of the Dhamma | Six marks — a normative list of properties |
| Recollection of the Saṅgha | Nine features — a normative list of status-types |
| Recollection of virtue | Eight marks — a normative list of virtue-maintenance indicators |
| **Recollection of letting-go** | **Concrete event — an already-occurred giving-act of the practitioner** |
| Recollection of the deities | Five virtues — a normative distribution of virtue-items across six heavens (with mirror mechanism) |

Five of the six recollections have **normative enumerations** as their objects — even when the pointing is toward the self (*sīla*), the object remains "the specifications to be upheld," not "the acts that have occurred." Only the fifth recollection, *cāgānussati*, has **a concrete event** as its object: this morning's gesture toward the person on the street corner, last month's sharing with a friend, the many small acts of loosening accumulated across the year. These events share three features: **time-location** (identifiable as having occurred at a particular moment), **concrete circumstance** (with recipient, object, setting), and **recoverable trace** (the event-imprint can be re-presented to awareness).

*Cāgānussati* is the only one of the six recollections whose object carries all three features. This event-character **governs every structural particular of *cāga* as an *anussati* object**.

*First,* it explains why Buddhaghosa designed two methods for *cāgānussati* rather than one. One who has already established giving-nature uses method (a), direct recollection of existing giving. One who has not yet established it uses method (b), a pipeline of same-day vow → giving-act → recollection. The other five *anussati* require no such dual pathway — the Buddha's qualities, Dhamma's qualities, Saṅgha's qualities, virtue's marks, deva's qualities are all normative enumerations *stable in their own right*; the practitioner need only turn attention toward an object already in place, whether or not the practitioner has themselves "established" anything. *Cāga* is different — the object itself must first be **generated** (if the practitioner is not giving-natured and no concrete giving-event has occurred today, there is no object to recollect). A generation-pathway must therefore be built into the methodology — this is the structural necessity of method (b).

*Second,* it explains why the access-concentration limitation of *cāgānussati* is, in Buddhaghosa's hands, *not cāga-specific*. The object of *cāgānussati* is an event, and an event is composed of multiple constituent elements (time, circumstance, recipient, object, inner action, etc.) — this is precisely the concrete manifestation of "the object is a composite of multiple virtue-items, and the attentive mind must move between the items, unable to stabilize on a single markless object." Buddhaghosa makes this reason the chapter's common ground because every one of the six recollection objects is a composite — the virtue-list is itself a composite. *Cāga*'s composite is an **event-composite** (containing space-time elements); the other five are **normative composites** (containing virtue-items). The manifestations of the common structure differ, but the conclusion is identical: all stop at access. This is the integrated design of the *anussati* system, not a limitation of *cāga*.

*Third,* it explains why *cāga* functions as one of the comparable items in the five-virtue mirror of *devatānussati*. The deva's five virtues (faith, virtue, learning, letting-go, wisdom) are not themselves events; but to execute the mirror movement — "the deities possess these five virtues; I too possess these five virtues" — the practitioner must have material for comparison on each item. For faith, virtue, learning, and wisdom, the comparison material is one's own state of confidence, maintenance of virtue, level of learning — all *relatively* normative and directly comparable. For *cāga*, the comparison material can only be one's own already-occurred giving-events. If the practitioner has not previously practiced *cāgānussati* (has not returned to one's own giving-events), then in *devatānussati*'s five-virtue mirror, the *cāga* comparison has no material to work with. *Cāga*'s event-character therefore simultaneously governs its structural dependency relation with *devatānussati*: **the fifth position's letting-go supplies the material for the sixth position's deva-mirror**.

### 3.3 The faculty/object duality — mutual dependence of *cāga*-as-faculty and *cāga*-as-object

Joining §3.1's sequential structure with §3.2's object uniqueness, *cāga*'s form in the early *anussati* system comes fully into view: it is **an event-character object**, seated at the **hinge position** of a **three-stage sequence**, held as *ārammaṇa* via a **dual method** of entry, reaching **access concentration** (*upacāra*), while simultaneously supplying the material for *devatānussati*'s five-virtue mirror. This is the complete structure of *cāga* **as object** (object layer).

How does this object layer interlock with the faculty layer handled in the previous paper? "*Cāga* as a Faculty of Stream-Entry" argued that *cāga* is a faculty — the fourth-limb rotational member of *sotāpattiyaṅga*, one of the five virtues perfuming the mind, the direct countermeasure to the "mine" construction driving saṃsāra. That level's *cāga* is an **inner configuration**: a mental-factor state whose inclination is "non-clinging to what is mine," a dynamic psychological structure describable by *muttacāga*, *vossagga*, and the other five-marker terms. Faculty does not require a specific event in order to exist — a person who possesses this faculty, even when not currently engaged in giving, still has a mind configured in its default toward "letting-go of what is mine."

The object-layer *cāga* is the inverse: it must **attach to a concrete event** in order to exist as an object. One cannot perform *anussati* on the abstraction "my cāga-configuration"; one can only perform *anussati* on the specific event "my giving to that stranger the other day." The event is the faculty's **appearance-form** — each giving is a surfacing of the faculty; each *anussati* is a re-grasping of that surfacing as the object of attention.

This is the faculty/object relation. Faculty (substance) is *anussati*'s **capacity base** — without this faculty, the practitioner could not discern which of their movements are "*cāga*" and which are not, nor stabilize the object during *anussati* (the giving-acts of one lacking the faculty tend to be mixed with transaction, display, or fear as contaminants, and such events are unstable as objects). Object (function) is the faculty's **operational form** — the faculty is **activated, sustained, and deepened** through the occurrence and re-presentation of events. The two are interdependent: without faculty, the object is unstable; without object (without giving-events occurring and being re-presented in *anussati*), the faculty lacks renewal and tends to stagnate.

This faculty/object reading aligns with Buddhaghosa's method-design in *Visuddhimagga* VII.5. Method (a), "recollection of already-established giving-nature," clearly assumes the faculty is already in place; the object is the archive of the faculty's past surfacings. Method (b), "activate a concrete giving-act via the day's vow and take that event as *nimitta*," is in fact a **bidirectional** training for one whose faculty is not yet stabilized — on the one hand, the day's vow and action **activate the faculty**; on the other, they simultaneously **generate the object**. Buddhaghosa's treatment unifies rather than separates the two — his method-design from the beginning understands faculty and object as two sides of the same cultivational structure.

Two philological refinements are called for here. **First**, this paper's §2.1 Group 3 has already made the precise differentiation — the *pañcindriya* (faith, energy, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom) is a capacity-layer pentad that does **not** contain *cāga*; the five-virtue pentad at SN 55.21 and in AN 6.25's *devatānussati* section (faith, virtue, learning, letting-go, wisdom) is a different pentad. *Cāga* as faculty truly appears at **the fourth limb of stream-entry** (P02 §III's main stage) and at **the fourth of the five virtues in the *devatānussati* mirror section** (within the six recollections, this paper's §2.1 Group 1). The previous paper's AN 11.11 citation had folded the framing of these two pentads — this does not overturn the previous paper's main argument (which rested on SN 55.31–33 and SN 55.39 and had no reliance on the *pañcindriya*), but this paper's faculty/object analysis requires the two pentads' positions to be re-distinguished clearly: faculty-layer *cāga* appears in **stream-entry limb** and **deva five-virtue mirror** (two positions), **not** in the five-faculty base; object-layer *cāga* is *cāgānussati* at the fifth recollection. The three positions — faculty/stream-entry-limb, faculty/deva-five-virtue-mirror, object/fifth-recollection — form an intricate internal structure within the early *anussati* system, worth continued tracking in Part IV's Mahāyāna-turn chapter.

**Second**, a refinement concerning the Chinese-translation verdict. The previous paper's §2.1 Group 4 argued for "systematic softening in T0099" on the basis of the whole fascicle 33 showing the *cāga* track swung toward the *dāna* track — visible at the doctrinal-finalization layer (sūtra 935 taking only the *sīla* track), at the five-virtue foundation layer (sūtras 930 and 932 substituting 施 for 捨), and at the *cāgānussati* object-name layer (sūtra 931 calling it "recollection of the matter of giving"). But this paper's §2.1 Group 1 has surfaced that T0099 *sūtra* 931 uses **both 施 and 捨 in parallel** within the *devatānussati* five-virtue enumeration — the translation bureau consciously employed two renderings in the same paragraph to distinguish outer-action from inner-letting-go. This internal dual-track shows: **T0099's translation bureau had not lost the inner/outer differentiation of *cāga*; it simply chose the *sīla* track at the level of structural finalization**. The previous paper's "systematic softening" verdict must therefore be refined — softening **does exist**, but **only at the layer of doctrinal finalization** (the translation bureau chose, at the editorial level, not to retain *cāga* as a complete independent track for stream-entry); at the **object-description layer** (within the *cāgānussati* formula itself, within the five-virtue enumerations), the inner/outer differentiation of *cāga* remains preserved via dual-track rendering. This layered verdict is more precise than the previous paper's whole-fascicle characterization, and it provides a finer philological location for the Western reader's discomfort with "Chinese Buddhism = giving-as-merit": the target of the unease is not the Chinese tradition's understanding of *cāga* as a whole, but specifically the marginalization of the *cāga* track within the doctrinal-finalization layer. The first three papers of this series — at the lexical layer (P01), the faculty layer (P02), and now the object layer (this paper) — have each performed one relocation of *cāga* from that marginalization.

## IV. Commentarial Structure as Analytical Tool

In keeping with the placement convention of the first two papers, this section occupies the §IV slot, but its function deliberately departs from the default form of "cross-disciplinary dialogue." This paper deliberately draws on no contemporary discipline — no psychology, no cognitive science, no positive psychology — for reasons elaborated in §V's middle-way correction. In their place, this section uses **commentarial structure as an analytical tool**, taking the method-design and gloss of Buddhaghosa's Vism VII.5 as a mirror to read back into the sutta itself, to see whether the dual register internal to the *cāgānussati* formula can be made visible through it. This move — using commentary as a tool to recalibrate the reading of sutta — belongs to the same family of moves as §3.3's layered verdict on the Chinese translation: both are **internal structural calibration via commentarial apparatus**, not cross-disciplinary borrowing.

Buddhaghosa's two methods in VII.5 are not structurally two parallel options; they form a **symmetric pair**:

- **Method (a):** recollection of **already-established giving-nature**. Here the object is the archive of the faculty's past surfacings as events; the temporal direction of the object points to the **past** (already-occurred giving-events).
- **Method (b):** taking-as-object the **act activated by the day's vow**. Here the object is the event produced when the faculty is freshly re-activated by present vow; the temporal direction points to the **present** (giving-events occurring or just occurred).

The two methods jointly point at the "event-character object" but from opposite temporal directions — covering two survival-states of the faculty (recollection under stabilized state; present-moment activation under pre-stabilized state). Buddhaghosa's dual-method design is therefore not an arbitrary option-pair but a **bidirectional temporal coverage** tailored to the structural feature that *cāga* is an event-character object.

Equally important is VII.5's distinction between *dāna* and *saṁvibhāga*:

- ***Dāna***: outward giving — handing over what belongs to oneself, without reservation, to another; ownership transferred from self to recipient.
- ***Saṁvibhāga***: sharing — partitioning off a portion of what one is currently using and giving it to another; no complete transfer of ownership, but common use or partial yielding.

Buddhaghosa places the two terms in parallel in the last of the six markers of *cāgānussati*'s object, *dānasaṁvibhāgarata* — the practitioner takes delight in **both** *dāna* **and** *saṁvibhāga*. This is not pleonasm but deliberate second-order gloss — Buddhaghosa signals that the surfacing of *cāga* as faculty is not a single-dimensional "giving" but **contains two distinct registers**: **outward giving** (the relational dimension, involving property transfer and relation to recipient) and **sharing-from-what-one-uses** (the generative dimension, involving loosening the boundary of one's currently-held resources).

Mapping this second-order gloss back onto the *cāgānussati* formula itself surfaces an interesting structure. The formula's six markers are: *vigatamalamacchera · muttacāga · payatapāṇi · vossaggarata · yācayoga · dānasaṁvibhāgarata*. Read through the *dāna*/*saṁvibhāga* second-order gloss, the six markers divide internally into two registers:

- **Inner-state register** (corresponding to *saṁvibhāga*'s generative dimension — inner loosening of boundary): *vigatamalamacchera* (mind freed from the stain of stinginess), *muttacāga* (letting-go loosened), *vossaggarata* (delighting in the very act of relinquishment) — three markers all describing inner mental-factor states.
- **Relational register** (corresponding to *dāna*'s relational dimension — outer realization-in-action): *yācayoga* (responsive to those who request — directed toward the requester), *dānasaṁvibhāgarata* (delighting in both outward giving and sharing-from-use — explicitly relational) — two markers both involving relational movement toward the recipient.
- **Bridging item**: *payatapāṇi* (hand washed clean, ready at any moment to hand over with one's own hand) — the image of the hand simultaneously spans inner readiness-state and outer realization-of-act; it is the hinge between the two registers.

This dual-register reading shows that the *cāgānussati* object-formula **already contains** the *dāna*/*saṁvibhāga* two-dimensional structure that Buddhaghosa unfolds in VII.5. Buddhaghosa is not adding theoretical analysis external to the sutta; he is, through careful reading of the formula, **making visible a structure already there**. This is precisely the function of traditional commentary: not interpretive addition, but structural manifestation.

The tool feeds back into §3.2's argument for object uniqueness: *cāga*'s event-character object is not a single-dimensional "what I did," but a **dual-register event** (inner loosening + relational act + hand-as-bridge). The recollective mind moves across this dual-register event — which is the concrete operation of Buddhaghosa's phrase "attention must move among the multiple virtue-items and cannot stabilize on a single markless object." The dual-register structure therefore further explains why *cāgānussati* stops at access concentration — not because "two registers are too complex to focus," but because the dual-register constitution is itself what gives *cāga* as an *anussati* object its common structural feature of being "a composite of multiple virtue-items."

The form of analysis in this section — using the commentator's method and gloss as a tool to re-read the sutta's own structure — is a **different type of argumentative move** from contemporary cross-disciplinary dialogue. The former is *intra-Buddhist structural calibration*; the latter is *cross-disciplinary conceptual analogy*. The first two papers in this series (P01's scarcity mindset, P02's costly signaling) used the latter type; this paper uses the former. The two types are complementary; both are legitimate argumentative instruments for Part I.

## V. Middle-Way Correction

This section handles three misreadings and one warning.

**❌ Misreading 1: *cāgānussati* = self-congratulation / self-affirmation**

This is the most common projection by contemporary English readers. Once the contemporary psychological context reflexively reads "returning to one's own good acts" as a form of self-affirmation, *cāgānussati* is automatically filed as a religiously-coded self-evaluation exercise. The error lies in this: *cāgānussati*'s object is not the self-evaluation "I am a generous person," but the concrete structure "the manifestation, in a specific past or present event, of the six markers — *muttacāga*, *vossaggarata*, *dānasaṁvibhāgarata*, and so on." The former takes "I" as the pivot and performs a positive self-description; the latter takes "the structure within the event" as the pivot and performs sustained attention on several concrete qualities of the event. The two may look superficially similar but differ entirely in mechanism — self-evaluation **reinforces** the conceptual existence of "I," whereas *cāgānussati*'s attention, by tracking the loosening structures of the object (the loosening of *muttacāga*, the relinquishment of *vossagga*, the departure of *macchera*'s stain), pulls in the **opposite** direction from self-conceptualization. When Buddhaghosa places *vigatamalamacchera* (mind freed from the stain of stinginess) as the first marker of the object-formula, it is not so that the practitioner can take pride in "I have departed from the stain," but so that the practitioner can focus on **the very structure of departure from stain** — the object of concentration is not "I" but "loosening."

**❌ Misreading 2: *cāgānussati* ≈ gratitude journaling**

Contemporary positive psychology's gratitude-intervention research is mature in Western mental-health practice, and readers encountering a cultivation-form of "returning to one's own good acts" are quickest to read *cāgānussati* as a religious version of "recording appreciation for what one has." The two do overlap in certain surface movements — both involve returning to past positive events; both produce observable proximal effects in mood improvement. But there are deep differences at the level of purpose, ontology, and direction. Gratitude's object is **a benefit received from other or from environment** (*what I have received*); *cāgānussati*'s object is **the practitioner's own already-occurred acts of loosening** (*what I have let go of*). The direction is reversed — gratitude points toward *inflow*; *cāgānussati* points toward *outflow*. The purposes also differ — gratitude targets well-being maintenance and positive-affect expansion; *cāgānussati* targets **countermeasure** against the "mine" construction driving saṃsāra, the meditative progress toward *upacāra* and beyond, and ultimately liberation. The radical divergence in directionality and teleology means the two should not be read as simulations of each other. This paper refuses to absorb *cāgānussati* into the gratitude frame in order to preserve the structural integrity of each.

**❌ Misreading 3: "fifth position" means *cāga* is lower in the hierarchy**

This misreading confuses linear order with hierarchy of importance. §3.1 has argued that the six-recollection sequence is a **three-stage structural arc**, not an importance-ordering — the Buddha stands first not because the Buddha is most important and *cāga* least, but because the sequence needs "*pasāda* toward external paradigms" as its starting point and "self-in-mirror-of-other" as its endpoint. *Cāga*'s fifth position is a **structural hinge** (connecting the "inward return" stage and the "self-in-mirror-of-other" stage), not a fifth-place ranking. Reading the fifth position as lower in rank both misconstrues this paper's main argument and can generate errors in practice — for instance, "since recollection of the Buddha is most important, I shall practice only that and skip recollection of letting-go" — ignoring that the six are an **integrated system**, each with its distinct structural function, none substitutable for another.

**⚠️ Warning: The contemporary context-drift of Vism VII.5's Method (b)**

Buddhaghosa's Method (b) — "not to take a mouthful of food before first giving" as a day-vow — requires caution when adopted as a direct practice-manual for modern readers. The vow emerged within the social ecology of Theravāda monastic or devoted lay householder life — the alms-round, the institution of laity supporting *bhikkhus*, the food-offering tradition are its everyday institutional infrastructure, and the method's operation is conditioned on the existence of this infrastructure. Contemporary general readers mechanically imitating it may produce several kinds of context-drift. *First*, the vow may be simplified into a ritualized form of "give something before each meal or you can't eat," losing the essential nature of *cāga* as inner loosening-structure. *Second*, in modern secular settings lacking a *saṅgha* or other *dakkhiṇeyya* (worthy recipient) object, the vow's recipient may default to convenient, objectified, feel-good options (online donations, tips, etc.), so that the *anussati*'s actual object becomes "my performance of virtue signaling" rather than "my realization of the *cāga* structure." *Third*, if the vow itself becomes a compulsory sense of obligation, it can generate reactive *macchera* (resistance, reluctance, resentment) — the exact inverse of *cāga*. A practitioner adopting Method (b) should hold it within Method (a)'s frame (the vow exists to activate the faculty for subsequent *anussati*, not as end in itself) and remain alert to context-drift.

**Supplement: Refinement to the previous paper's Chinese-translation verdict**

Already surfaced in §3.3; briefly restated here. The previous paper's "systematic softening in T0099" verdict holds at the **structural-doctrinal layer** (the stream-entry limbs finalization, the name of the *cāgānussati* object), but at the **object-description detail layer** (the 施/捨 dual-track rendering within *sūtra* 931's *devatānussati* five-virtue enumeration), the Chinese translation bureau did preserve the inner/outer differentiation of *cāga*. This refinement narrows the target of the Western-reader unease: it is not the Chinese tradition's *cāga* understanding as a whole that miscarried, but the marginalization of the *cāga* track at the doctrinal-finalization stage. The three papers in this series have now performed three relocations of *cāga* — at the lexical layer (P01), the faculty layer (P02), and the object layer (the present paper).

## VI. Conclusion

**Recapitulation**: *cāgānussati* is the only one of the six *anussati* whose object is a concrete event of the practitioner's own accomplished giving — Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, and deva all take external or normative objects; only *cāga*'s object is an event. This object-uniqueness governs *cāgānussati*'s structural function at the fifth position of the sequence — the hinge of the three-stage arc "attending-to-other → attending-to-self → self-in-mirror-of-other." It governs the necessity of Buddhaghosa's dual-method design — an event-character object requires bidirectional temporal coverage. It governs the access-only concentration ceiling — which it shares with the other five *anussati* as a common feature of the multi-*guṇa* composite structure. And it governs its faculty/object relation to the *cāga*-as-faculty of the previous paper — faculty is the capacity-base, object is the operational form, and the two are mutually activated through the occurrence and re-presentation of events.

**Practice directive**: for the contemporary English-language reader, recollection of letting-go may be the most accessible of the six *anussati* entry points. You do not need to construct a complex object — the Buddha's nine qualities require extensive scriptural study to stabilize in meditative visualization, the Dhamma's six marks require familiarity with Dhamma classifications, the Saṅgha's nine features require acquaintance with saṅgha structure, virtue's eight marks require actual virtue-practice for raw material, the deva's five virtues require familiarity with deva cosmology — only *cāga*'s object, **you have already done today**. Perhaps only something small: letting someone into a lane ahead of you, sharing a portion of food, receiving a friend's complaint without turning away. Sit quietly, take this event as *ārammaṇa*, and according to Buddhaghosa's six markers (stain-freedom / loosened / hand-ready / delight in relinquishment / responsive to request / delighting in sharing), examine the event's structure one marker at a time, without evaluation, without emphasis, without the contamination of the self-remark "I did a good thing" — concentrating only on the structure of "loosening" within the event. This is the simplest contemporary form of Method (b): no alms-round institutional infrastructure is needed, no religious ritual framework is needed, only a small actual event of loosening that you can take as *nimitta*.

**Open question**: if *cāgānussati* stops at *upacāra*, what *additional conditions* would *appanā* require? This question cannot be answered within Part I — *appanā*'s structural requirement (stable focus on a single markless object) and *anussati*'s multi-*guṇa* object are morphologically incompatible, so the answer cannot be "*cāgānussati* practiced long enough naturally enters *appanā*." The real answer lies in the early Mahāyāna turn: the articulation of Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā* is precisely a systematic response — after both the action-layer and the object-layer of *cāga* have been established — to the open question "how does *cāga* move from *upacāra* toward *appanā*, and ultimately toward liberation itself?" This turn will be taken up in Part IV, *From Dāna to Dāna-Pāramitā*.

**Closing structural observation**: the Chinese-translation expansion noted in §2.1 Group 4 — *Ekottara Āgama*'s "Ten Recollections" and "Extended Exposition" chapters expanding the six to ten by adding *upasamānussati*, *ānāpānassati*, *kāyagatāsati*, and *maraṇassati* — runs in exact parallel to Buddhaghosa's ten-recollection treatment (*Visuddhimagga* chapter VII's six, then chapter VIII's *maraṇassati*, *kāyagatāsati*, *ānāpānassati*, *upasamānussati*). This cross-linguistic philological isomorphism is a structural evidence-point showing that the Chinese *Āgama* and the Pāli commentarial tradition developed independently but converged on similar taxonomic conclusions — worth deeper tracking in the book-phase treatment or in a later Part. The present paper only registers it; Part II, *The Āgama View*, will have further space to handle the expansion path.

The six-recollection teaching genuinely addressed to Mahānāma the Sakyan as interlocutor — the sutta opens with Mahānāma coming to the Buddha and asking: "For one who has gained the fruit, understood the teaching, and attained lasting deathlessness, what dhamma should be dwelt in often?" The content is near-identical to AN 6.25. The present paper observes the attribution distinction strictly: cite AN 6.25 for the generalized six-recollection canonical exposition; cite AN 6.10 for the Mahānāma-addressed personal instruction; the two may be paired as "AN 6.25 (≈ AN 6.10)."

[^6]: AN 6.26 *Mahākaccānasutta*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya*. PTS edition iii.314. A parallel variant with Mahākaccāna as expositor, introducing two further framings: *gedha* (greedy attachment) and *ākāsasama* ("like space") — the six recollections as a liberation-path for beings entangled in craving, the mind spacious like the sky holding the five virtues. This framing will be appropriated in Part IV's Mahāyāna-turn chapter; the present paper registers it without deployment.

[^7]: AN 6.9 *Anussatiṭṭhānasutta*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya*. PTS edition iii.284. The barest short-form listing of the six recollections — names only, without exposition; a compressed parallel to AN 6.25.

[^8]: AN 11.11 *Paṭhamamahānāmasutta*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya*. PTS edition v.328. Mahānāma's question on *kathaṁbahulavihārī* ("in what mode of dwelling should one abide often?"). The Buddha answers with a two-stage architecture: Stage-1 is the *pañcindriya* (*saddhā · vīriya · sati · samādhi · paññā*); Stage-2 is the six recollections. The bridge phrase is explicit — "*Imesu kho, Mahānāma, pañcasu dhammesu patiṭṭhāya cha dhamme uttari bhāveyyāsi*." **Note**: the Stage-1 pentad is the *pañcindriya* (faith, energy, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom); it is **not** the five virtues of SN 55.21 / AN 6.25 *devatānussati* (faith, virtue, learning, letting-go, wisdom). The two pentads occupy different structural positions; the previous paper's §2.1 Group 3 had partially folded their framing, and the present paper's §3.3 issues the philological refinement.

[^9]: *Saṃyukta Āgama* (雜阿含經) *juan* 33, *sūtra* 932; translated by Guṇabhadra (劉宋求那跋陀羅), Liu Song dynasty. Taishō vol. 2, No. 0099. The Chinese parallel to AN 11.11. The Chinese Stage-1 pentad is "faith, ethical conduct, learning, *giving*, wisdom" (i.e., the SN 55.21 / AN 6.25 *devatānussati* five virtues), **not** the Pāli original's *pañcindriya*. The Chinese translation bureau here folded two distinct pentads into one — a structural restructuring of the Pāli source; the philological significance of this choice is reserved for Part IV.

[^10]: Buddhaghosa, *Visuddhimagga* VII.5 *Cāgānussati-niddesa*. PTS edition pp. 223–224. The present paper's engagement with the *Visuddhimagga* continues the paraphrase-only discipline adopted in P01 and P02, to avoid a license conflict between GRETIL's CC-BY-SA and this volume's CC-BY-NC-SA share-alike provisions. Buddhaghosa's six-marker formula for the *cāgānussati* object, the two-method distinction (recollection of already-established giving-nature vs. activation of an event as object via the day's vow), the *dāna*/*saṁvibhāga* second-order gloss, and the chapter-wide reason for access-only structure (the object is a multi-*guṇa* composite, preventing the mind from stabilizing on a single markless object toward absorption) are all rendered according to his argumentative intent, with no direct quotation of Ñāṇamoli's English translation's connected clauses.

## Series Cross-References

- **This volume, P01, *What Dāna Actually Means***: §I, §III, and §V of the present paper draw at several points on P01's three-term semantic field (*dāna* / *cāga* / *yāga*) and its five-marker formula at the fourth position. P01 positions *cāga*'s distinctive semantic location through lexical differentiation; the present paper extends this at the *anussati*-object layer, arguing the structural position of *cāga* as the fifth recollection — an application of the three-term semantic field at the object layer.

- **This volume, P02, *Cāga as a Faculty of Stream-Entry***: the prior side of the faculty/object pair. P02 established *cāga* as faculty (substance) — the fourth-limb triple rotation of stream-entry, the mind-perfuming of the five virtues, the countermeasure to the "mine" construction driving saṃsāra. The present paper establishes *cāga* as object (function) — the object-uniqueness at the fifth recollection, the dual-method entry, the mutual dependence of faculty and object. §3.3 issues two refinements to the previous paper's AN 11.11 pentad framing and the T0099 systematic-softening verdict; neither overturns the previous paper's main argument.

- **This volume, P04, *Eight Motivations for Giving***: §VI's open question and §I's problem-orientation foreshadow it. Having established *cāga*-as-object in this paper, P04 will handle the eight-motivation analysis of AN 8.31 as the triggering-condition spectrum for *cāga*-action — a fine-grained analysis of "under what motivational structures does *cāga* activate." The present paper supplies P04's *anussati* base.

- **This volume, P11, *From Dāna to Dāna-Pāramitā***: the §VI open question points to it explicitly. The present paper treats *cāga* stopping at *upacāra* as a **common design feature of the *anussati* system** rather than a limitation; the structural turn from *upacāra* toward *appanā* and liberation becomes possible only with the articulation of early Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā*. The present paper supplies the Pāli-side structural basis for P11's Part IV turn-argument.

- **This volume, P15, *The Diamond Sūtra on Giving***: the self-congratulation misreading rejected in §V ❌ Misreading 1 has a structural echo in P15's *triple emptiness* (emptiness of giver, gift, and recipient) — the radical completion of *cāga*. The present paper's "concentration on the structure of loosening rather than on 'I did a good thing'" at the *anussati*-object layer provides the Pāli-level support for the *Diamond Sūtra*'s triple emptiness. Full treatment reserved for P15.

- **NIAN Volume, Part I, P03, *The Six Recollections as System***: the same six-recollection material, treated from different entry points in the two volumes. NIAN P03 unfolds the full six-recollection skeleton beginning with "recollection of the Buddha as first" (primarily the *anussati* verb-view); DĀNA P03 unfolds the sequence structure beginning with "recollection of letting-go at the fifth position" (primarily the object-view).
