# What *Dāna* Actually Means
***Dāna*, *Cāga*, *Yāga*, and the Loosening of "Mine"**

*中文主題：施究竟在做什麼——dāna／cāga／yāga 三詞辨義與我所的鬆動*

**Abstract**

Contemporary Western readers translate *dāna* uniformly as "giving" or "generosity," flattening an entire doctrinal field into a single behavioral category. This paper argues that the flattening loses the most important layer. Early Pāli texts deploy a *three-term semantic field* for what English collapses into one word — *dāna* (the giving event), *cāga* (the inner letting-go that makes the event possible), and *yāga* (the ritual-sacrificial register inherited from Vedic culture and reframed). Drawing on AN 6.25, AN 11.11, SN 55.31–32, the three Itivuttaka discourses on giving (It 26, 75, 98), and Buddhaghosa's *Cāgānussati-niddesa* (*Visuddhimagga* VII.5), this paper demonstrates how the three terms divide the labor: *dāna* names what an outside observer can see, *cāga* names what only the practitioner can witness inwardly — the loosening of "mine-making" — and *yāga* names the ritual resonance that early Buddhism deliberately retained from its Vedic environment while replacing the object and the goal. Recognizing this division is the entry ticket for reading the twenty-nine chapters that follow, from the eight grounds for giving in the Aṅguttara, through Nāgārjuna's treatise on *dāna-pāramitā*, through the threefold purity of giver-receiver-gift in the *Diamond Sūtra*, to Samantabhadra's universal worship in the *Avataṃsaka*. Giving in the Buddhist analysis was never the question of "how to give." It was the question of whether what is mine can become *not only mine*.

**Keywords:** *dāna*, *cāga*, *yāga*, *macchera*, generosity, recollection of generosity, four limbs of stream-entry, *Visuddhimagga*

## I. Introduction

You're at the supermarket checkout. The screen prompts: "Donate $1 to children's charity?" Your finger hovers between "yes" and "skip" for a fraction of a second, and there's a small inward contraction. That contraction has a technical name in early Buddhist psychology, with its own developmental mechanism and its own antidote. The name is *macchera* — stinginess.

Its antidote is not "donate more." Its antidote is the cultivation of a counter-faculty — *cāga*.

You think giving is a question of *how much*. The dharma says no. Giving is a question of whether what is *mine* can become *not only mine*. The amount is the surface; the loosening of mine-making is what's underneath. A wealthy donor writing a large check while *macchera* remains untouched, and a poor person sharing the last bite of a meal while *macchera* fully loosens — Pāli texts will rule for the second every time. This is not a moralistic word-game; it is psychology. Early Buddhism uses *three different words* for what English compresses into one — *dāna*, *cāga*, *yāga* — and the three divide the labor of describing how giving actually works. Press them all into a single English "generosity," and the first thing lost is the inner dimension behind that 0.3-second checkout flinch.

This volume — thirty chapters in all — is about that dimension. From the Aṅguttara's eight grounds for giving, through Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* on the perfection of giving, through the *Diamond Sūtra*'s threefold purity, to Samantabhadra's universal worship in the *Avataṃsaka* — the surfaces grow more elaborate, the objects more cosmic, the language more refined, but the underlying engineering is the same: *how to let "mine" loosen*. See the engineering clearly, and the next twenty-nine chapters can be walked through. Miss it, and the whole tradition reads as a baroque history of charitable behavior.

This chapter has one job: to clear the vocabulary. It does not handle the eight grounds (that's chapter 4), the perfection of giving (chapters 11–14), the threefold purity (chapter 15), or universal worship (Part VIII). It only handles the *words* — what *dāna* names, what *cāga* names, what *yāga* names, how the three divide the work, and why the division matters.

The Pāli evidence comes next.

## II. Canonical Foundation

This section runs in two streams: discourses (the *sutta* layer) organized by which of the three terms is centered, and a treatise (the *Visuddhimagga*) for systematic exposition. Each Pāli reference is paired with its Chinese-translation parallel where available, in keeping with the bilingual convention of this volume.

### 2.1 Group A: Discourses (Pāli with Chinese parallels)

**First sub-group: *dāna* as observable giving event**

- **Itivuttaka 26 *Dānasutta***[^1]: "Mendicants, if beings only knew, as I know, the fruit of giving and sharing (*dānasaṁvibhāgassa vipākaṁ*), they would not eat without first giving, and the stain of stinginess would not occupy their minds. Even their last morsel they would not eat without sharing, so long as someone was there to receive it." This opens the field by pairing *dāna* with *saṁvibhāga* (sharing) in a compound, and by setting the *last-bite test* — the proof of real *dāna* is what happens at the limit, not the average.
- **Itivuttaka 75 *Avuṭṭhikasutta***[^2]: the three-types-of-rain-cloud simile — the *rainless cloud* (gives to no one), the *local rain* (gives to some but not others), and the *cloud raining everywhere* (gives to all). *Dāna* is here used as a *character-typology classifier*: how a person distributes giving reveals the level of their character. The recipients listed include ascetics, brahmins, paupers, vagrants, and supplicants; the gifts include food, drink, clothing, vehicles, garlands, lighting, lodging — the breadth of *dāna*'s object-field is itself the discriminator.
- **Itivuttaka 98 *Dānasutta***[^3]: "Mendicants, there are these two gifts: *the gift of material things* (*āmisa-dāna*) and *the gift of the teaching* (*dhamma-dāna*). Of these two, the gift of the teaching is supreme." The doctrine that the dhamma-gift outranks the material gift is laid down here for the first time, to be unfolded in Part VIII. For the present chapter, what matters is that *dāna*'s object-field is not flat; it has internal hierarchy.
- **AN 8.31 *Paṭhamadānasutta***[^4]: eight grounds for giving — from *āsajja* (giving as an insult) and *bhayā* (giving from fear), through *adāsi me ti* (giving in return for past gifts received) and reputation-seeking, to the highest ground, *cittālaṅkāracittaparikkhāratthaṁ* (giving "for the adornment and equipment of the mind"). **The present chapter cites this sutta only for one structural point**: the same verbal phrase *dānaṁ deti* (*to give a gift*) can carry eight radically different inner mechanisms. The full eight-fold analysis belongs to chapter 4.
- **Chinese parallel**: *Ekottarikāgama* (*Zēngyī Āhán*), fascicle 37, "Eight Difficulties" chapter, sūtras 8 and 9[^5]. The Chinese tradition lists *eight kinds of giving* but unfolds two different schemes — sūtra 9 lists eight *qualities* of giving (timely, pure, with one's own hand, with vow, with release, seeking cessation, to a good field, for the benefit of others), and sūtra 8 lists eight *locations* of giving (waterside, monastery, near monastery, on the road, lecture hall, empty places, charnel ground, home). Neither matches the Pāli's eight motivations. This Pāli–Chinese divergence is a substantive editorial difference, not a translation discrepancy, and is addressed in §3.6.

**Second sub-group: *cāga* as inner faculty**

- **AN 6.25 *Anussatiṭṭhānasutta***[^6]: among the six topics for recollection, the fifth is *cāgānussati* — recollection of *one's own* generosity. The standard formula:

  > "Furthermore, mendicants, a noble disciple recollects their own generosity: 'I am so fortunate, so very fortunate. Among people whose minds are full of the stain of stinginess, I live at home with a mind freed from that stain — *with letting-go loosened* (*muttacāga*), *with hand washed clean* (*payatapāṇi*), *delighting in relinquishment* (*vossaggarato*), *responsive to those who request* (*yācayogo*), *delighting in giving and sharing* (*dānasaṁvibhāgarato*).'"

  This formula does not describe a "generous person." It describes a *trained default state of the mind* — what mind looks like when its baseline has been retrained from holding to releasing. The five marker-words read together paint a single profile of inner readiness.

- **AN 11.11 *Mahānāmasutta***[^7]: the formula above is repeated verbatim, embedded now in eleven recollections taught to the Sakyan layman Mahānāma as practices for the householder's settled life. The context-shift matters: AN 11.11 places *cāgānussati* explicitly within the lay practitioner's domain of *sotāpanna* (stream-enterer) cultivation, joining *cāga* directly to fruition.
- **SN 55.31 *Paṭhamapuññābhisandasutta***[^8]: the four "overflows of merit" (*cattāro puññābhisandā*) of a stream-enterer — the first three are unshakable confidence in the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha; the fourth is *sīla*, the noble's ethical conduct.
- **SN 55.32 *Dutiyapuññābhisandasutta***[^9]: the same four overflows of merit, with the same first three — but **the fourth is *cāga***, given in the identical *muttacāga* formula quoted above from AN 6.25.

  **This swap is the strongest textual evidence in the present chapter**. Two adjacent suttas in the same chapter (*Puññābhisandavagga*) substitute *cāga* for *sīla* in the fourth slot of the stream-enterer's standard description, without altering the surrounding judgment. The editorial fact itself is a doctrinal statement: in early Buddhist accounts of the first awakening-fruit, *cāga* is *of equal weight with sīla*. It is not a subset of *sīla*, not a behavioral activity, not an optional virtue. It is an independent faculty of mind — one of the defining qualities of someone who has entered the stream.

- **Chinese parallel**: *Saṃyuktāgama* (*Zá Āhán*), fascicle 30 corresponding section[^10]. The Chinese translators consistently render *cāga* as *shě* (捨, "letting-go") and *dāna* as *bùshī* (布施, "giving"), preserving the distinction with care. Later Chinese tradition gradually allowed the inner-faculty dimension of *shě* to be absorbed into the broader behavioral term *bùshī*, a semantic narrowing that will be tracked in Parts III and IV.

**Third sub-group: *yāga* as ritual-sacrificial resonance**

- **Itivuttaka 98 closing verse**[^11]: "What wise and discerning person would not perform *yajati* (to sacrifice, to make ritual offering) at the proper time?" *Yajati* is the verbal form of *yāga*. The verse praises one who, with confident heart, makes offerings *to the supreme field* (*aggamhi khettamhi*) — the Three Jewels and noble persons. Early Buddhism *retained* the central Vedic verb of sacrifice but redirected its object: from gods to the Buddha and the noble Saṅgha; from cosmic-order maintenance to the planting of seeds in the merit-field.
- **Visuddhimagga VII.5**[^12] gloss on *yācayogo* in the *cāgānussati* formula: Buddhaghosa records two readings preserved by the manuscript tradition — *yācayogo* (yoked to those who request, i.e. responsive to all requests for giving) and *yājayogo* (yoked to *yāja*, ritual offering). Buddhaghosa keeps both readings rather than choosing — a *philological footprint* of the historical merging. Within the same five-word formula that defines the inner faculty *cāga*, the trace of Vedic *yāga* is preserved. Two semantic fields converged here in early texts, and the commentator preserved the convergence rather than tidy it away.

  Chinese translators generally rendered the *yāga* family — *yāga*, *yāja*, *yajati*, *yajña* — variously as *shī* (施), *gòng* (供), *fèng* (奉), *cí* (祠), partially merging the three-term spectrum into a single Chinese conceptual band. This compression contributed to the historical rise of "*gōngyǎng*" (供養, offering-as-worship) as the dominant Chinese term for ritualized giving in later Mahāyāna usage (treated in Part VIII).

A bilingual observation: the three-term distinction is comparatively clean in Pāli (*dāna* for the event, *cāga* for the faculty, *yāga* for the ritual register); in Chinese, the three Pāli terms map onto a different three-Chinese set (*bùshī* / *shě* / *gōngyǎng*) whose internal divisions do not mirror the Pāli ones exactly. This is a feature of the Chinese tradition, not a defect, but readers of the Chinese canon need to know it.

### 2.2 Group B: Treatise

- ***Visuddhimagga* VII.5 *Cāgānussati-niddesa* (Description of the Recollection of Generosity)*[^12]: the central treatise reference for this chapter. Buddhaghosa contributes three technical points:
  1. *Cāga* recollection, as a meditation subject, *reaches access concentration* (*upacāramatta jhāna*) but does not enter absorption (*appanā*) — consistent with the *anussati* boundary established in NIAN's first chapter: *anussati* practices stop at access by structural design, not by limitation.
  2. *Macchera* is defined as "the characteristic of being unable to bear that one's own possessions become shared with others; one of the dark factors that stains the mind's natural luminosity (*pabhassara*)." This definition is unusually precise. *Macchera* is not the emotional discomfort of "not wanting to part with"; it is *the structural refusal that one's own be reconstituted as one's-own-and-also-another's*. It targets the *mine-making* operation itself, not the object.
  3. The *yācayogo / yājayogo* dual reading (noted above in §2.1's third sub-group) functions as a philological signature of the historical convergence between *yāga* and *cāga*.

- **Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra, Śrāvakabhūmi** corresponding sections on *dāna* as a mental factor[^13]: noted here as a forward reference for chapter 14 (*Bodhisattvabhūmi* on giving) and not unpacked in this chapter.

**Note on commentarial structure**: this chapter operates as a *two-voice exposition* — Buddhaghosa as primary, Yogācāra *Śrāvakabhūmi* as supplementary cross-reference — rather than the *three-family dialogue* that is the methodological signature of the Pointing-at-the-Moon project. The reason is documentary: the early Pāli material has only one systematically developed commentarial tradition (Theravāda); the full three-family dialogue (Nāgārjuna / Asaṅga–Vasubandhu / Buddhaghosa) becomes available in Part IV when the early Mahāyāna treatises on giving enter the picture. Honest disclosure of this methodological constraint is itself a commitment of the project: not every paper in this volume can mechanically apply three-family dialogue; each application must follow the actual density of the commentarial tradition at hand. All three papers of Part I share this two-voice structure as a coherent local discipline.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 *Dāna*: The Word for the Visible Event

The root of *dāna* is √dā (*to give*). The root spans the entire Indo-European family — Latin *dare*, Greek *didōmi*, English *donor* and *donate*. At the etymological level, *dāna* and the Western "to give" are cognate. The semantic continuity is real.

Morphologically, *dāna* is an *action noun* — covering both "the act of giving" and "the thing given." When a sutta says *dānaṁ deti* ("gives a gift"), the construction is grammatically redundant by Western standards, but the redundancy carries meaning: the verb (*deti*, "gives") and the event itself (*dānaṁ*, "the gift") are named separately, leaving analytical room for the question *what is happening on the inside of this event*.

Answering that question is the work of *cāga*.

The category named by *dāna* is *the giving event as observable from outside*. It includes the act, the object, the recipient, the timing, the manner — every dimension that can be recorded by a third party. In the four discourses cited in §2.1's first sub-group, *dāna* operates entirely on this observable plane: It 26 lays down the *last-bite test* (externally verifiable), It 75 sorts persons into three rain-cloud types by their pattern of distribution (externally verifiable), It 98 distinguishes material gift from teaching gift by object-type (externally specifiable), and AN 8.31 surveys eight motivations that can drive *the same external event* (and it is precisely the move of distinguishing identical externals from divergent internals that pushes the analysis into *cāga*-territory).

*Dāna* is the word for the behavioral layer. It needs *cāga* to explain its inner conditions.

### 3.2 *Cāga*: The Word for the Inner Faculty

The root of *cāga* is √tyaj (*to release, to relinquish, to let go*). It is cognate with Sanskrit *tyāga* — the same word in Sanskrit literature for "renunciation." √tyaj and √dā are entirely different roots: the first names the gesture of *opening the hand*, the second names the gesture of *extending the hand*. At the etymological level, *cāga* and *dāna* are not synonyms. One describes *no longer gripping*; the other describes *passing across*.

Morphologically, *cāga* is an *abstract noun* — *let-go-ness*, *releasability*, *the disposition that makes giving possible*. It does not name the act; it names the inner configuration that stands behind the act.

In the three suttas cited in §2.1's second sub-group, *cāga* appears within a single five-marker formula. Each marker traces one feature of the trained mind:

- *muttacāga*: "with letting-go *loosened*" — *mutta* is the past participle of *to release*, *to set free*. Not the act of letting go, but the state in which letting-go is *no longer bound* by mine-making.
- *payatapāṇi*: "*hand washed clean*" — *payata* literally means "purified, washed." Buddhaghosa glosses: "with hand always washed, so as to give the appropriate gift respectfully with one's own hand." The image is not cleanliness; it is *standing readiness* — the hand always prepared to extend.
- *vossaggarato*: "*delighting in relinquishment*" — *vossagga* is "release, abandonment," and *rato* is "delighting in, taking pleasure in." The mind's default position has shifted from *retention* to *release*.
- *yācayogo*: "*responsive to those who request*" — open to the requests of others, as the structural inverse of being closed off behind "mine."
- *dānasaṁvibhāgarato*: "*delighting in giving and sharing*" — *dāna* (giving outward) and *saṁvibhāga* (sharing within a group) paired as a single mental orientation.

Read together, the five markers do not describe a "generous person." They describe *a trained default condition of mind* — one in which the resting position has been remade from *holding* into *releasing*. The transformation is not achieved by willpower forcing oneself to be generous. It is achieved by the cultivation of a counter-faculty.

The opposite of *cāga* is *macchera* (stinginess). Buddhaghosa's definition (already cited in §2.2) repays close attention: *macchera* is "the inability to bear that one's possessions be reconstituted as belonging-also-to-others, one of the dark factors that stains the mind's natural luminosity (*pabhassara*)." Two points are crucial.

First, *macchera* is not the emotional discomfort of "not wanting to part with." It is a *structural refusal* — the refusal to allow the category "mine" to expand into the category "mine-and-also-theirs." A person can be effortlessly generous with money but unable to share their chair, because the money does not engage the *mine* structure while the chair does. *Macchera* is triggered by the *mine-making* operation, not by the magnitude of what is at stake.

Second, *macchera* is one of the *stains of the mind's natural luminosity* (*pabhassara*). "The mind's natural luminosity" is a technical concept in Pāli psychology — the mind in its undefiled state is intrinsically bright, and is darkened only by the operation of certain corrupting factors. *Macchera* is named among these. This placement matters enormously. It moves the entire doctrine of giving from *moralism* ("you should be generous") to *psychology* ("remove this stain and the mind brightens"). The mechanism of *cāga* recollection is the systematic reversal of this stain — by recollecting one's own already-arisen *cāga*, the mind builds a counter-faculty that progressively dilutes *macchera*'s grip.

### 3.3 *Yāga*: The Word for the Sacrificial Resonance

The root of *yāga* is √yaj (*to sacrifice, to make ritual offering*). √yaj is the central verb of the Vedic sacrificial tradition — the entire *Ṛgveda* is structured around *yajña*, the ritual of cosmic-order maintenance. √yaj, √dā, and √tyaj are three independent roots: *to sacrifice*, *to give*, *to release* — three distinct gestures.

Early Buddhism *retained* the √yaj family in its Pāli vocabulary — the verb *yajati*, the noun *yāga* / *yāja*, and compounds like *yācayogo* / *yājayogo*. Retention rather than deletion was a *strategic decision*. Early Buddhism arose inside an Indian Vedic culture; deleting the *yāga* vocabulary would have severed the connection to the religious language that lay practitioners already lived inside. Reframing — keeping the verb, replacing the object, rewriting the goal — allowed the *dāna* teaching to penetrate an existing ritual culture rather than competing with it head-on.

The reframing operates in three steps:

First, *the verb yajati was preserved*. The closing verse of It 98 is direct: "*What wise and discerning person would not perform yajati at the proper time?*" *To make ritual offering* is endorsed; the condition is that the offering be made *to the supreme field* (*aggamhi khettamhi*).

Second, *the object was replaced*. Vedic *yāga*'s object was the gods (Indra, Agni, Soma, etc.), and its goal was the maintenance of cosmic order (*ṛta*). Buddhist *yāga*'s object is the *merit-field* (*puññakkhetta*) — the Three Jewels and noble persons — and its goal is the planting of seeds for liberation. The substitution is doctrinal: the energy of ritual offering is rerouted from external deities toward objects whose properties make liberation possible.

Third, *the teleology was rewritten*. Vedic ritual targeted this-worldly benefit (harvest, victory, offspring) or heavenly rebirth. Buddhism preserved the heavenly-rebirth dimension (the *cāgānussati* formula's closing turn — "the deities possess such qualities; I too possess them" — uses exactly this) but moved the principal goal to *awakening*. *Dāna* becomes the first of the six perfections, ultimately oriented toward Buddhahood.

The *Visuddhimagga*'s preservation of *yācayogo / yājayogo* as parallel readings (§2.1) is the *philological footprint* of this entire reframing. In early texts, the two semantic fields actually overlapped — the *yāca-* root (*to request*) and the *yāja-* root (*to make ritual offering*) sounded similar and felt similar; users in different historical layers may have heard them differently, and later commentators, faced with manuscript variants, preserved both rather than choosing. They preserved both *because they pointed to the same inner operation*: take what one has, by some rhythm — whether the ritual rhythm of *yāga* or the responsive openness of *yāca* — and through the inner loosening of *cāga*, give it (*dāna*) outward. Three roots, three resonances, one operation.

*Yāga* is the word for the cultural-ritual layer. It carries ceremonial feeling and group memory, and is absorbed into the inner structure of *cāga*.

### 3.4 The Three-Term Division: A Stereoscopic Picture

Combining the three sub-sections produces the central structural claim of this chapter:

| Term | Root | Layer | What it names | Observable by | Opposite |
|------|------|-------|---------------|---------------|----------|
| *dāna* | √dā (give) | behavioral | the giving event itself | a third party | *adāna* (non-giving) |
| *cāga* | √tyaj (release) | mental-factor | the inner loosening that makes the event possible | the practitioner inwardly | *macchera* (stinginess) |
| *yāga* | √yaj (sacrifice) | cultural-ritual | the ceremonial resonance and group memory of giving | a culture-sharing community | (no single term) |

The three terms operate *simultaneously*. A complete instance of "giving" in the early Pāli sense is a three-layered event: *cāga* (inner loosening) → *dāna* (outward action) → *yāga* (cultural reading). Inner letting-go takes place; behavior follows; the wider community reads the event through the resonance of ritual offering. This is what early texts meant by giving.

Contemporary English translation collapses all three into "generosity" or "giving." *The first thing lost is cāga* — the layer least visible to outside observation. From the outside, a person heavy with *macchera* can still execute a *dāna* (out of fear, out of returned obligation, out of reputation-seeking); the object and amount can be identical; the external event is indistinguishable. But the *cāga* mechanism has not engaged at all. AN 8.31's eight-motivation analysis is precisely the dissection of this gap — *the same dāna event, driven by eight different grades and qualities of cāga* (from zero *cāga* in "giving as an insult" to full *cāga* in "giving for the adornment and equipment of the mind").

Lose the *cāga* layer, and the entire doctrine reads as a behavioral history of charity — a progress narrative about humanity becoming kinder. Early Buddhism's claim is not that narrative. Early Buddhism's claim is: behavioral giving is not enough; the inner faculty must loosen. When it loosens, behavior follows naturally; when it does not, no amount of behavior is anything other than another transcription of *macchera*.

### 3.5 Why the Division Matters: How the Next Twenty-Nine Chapters Lock to It

Every subsequent chapter's central claim depends on this three-term division. A forward map:

- **Chapter 2** — starting from the *cāga ↔ sīla* swap of SN 55.32, argues *cāga*'s status as a defining limb of the stream-enterer.
- **Chapter 3** — places *cāgānussati* back inside the six recollections as a system, examining how it coordinates with the other five (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, *sīla*, *devatā*).
- **Chapter 4** — opens AN 8.31's eight motivations in full: *the same dāna* driven by eight different grades of *cāga*.
- **Chapter 5** — the *Velāma Sutta*'s teaching on the hierarchy of recipients: the merit of *dāna* is not determined by the giver alone; the recipient's level shapes the outcome.
- **Chapters 11–14** — the Mahāyāna turn: Nāgārjuna systematically argues that the *dāna* of *dāna-pāramitā* is *cāga* washed by *prajñā*'s analysis of emptiness.
- **Chapter 15** — the *Diamond Sūtra*'s threefold purity (giver-receiver-gift all empty) is the *radicalization of cāga* — even "I am letting go" is itself let go.
- **Chapters 24–25** — Samantabhadra's universal worship: *pūjā* as the *yāga* layer expanded to the dharma-realm scale.
- **Chapter 28** — online offering: in digital interfaces, the external form of *dāna* is radically compressed (a five-minute click), but does the inner *cāga* mechanism still establish itself? This is the contemporary test.

All twenty-nine remaining chapters can be read back into one or another aspect of the three-term division. *Cāga* is the heart of the doctrine; *dāna* is its hands and feet; *yāga* is its outer garment. See the structure of all three, and the path through the volume becomes walkable.

### 3.6 A Pāli–Chinese Editorial Divergence Worth Noting

The vocabulary work above has been done largely on the Pāli side. One Pāli-Chinese editorial divergence merits explicit acknowledgement here.

AN 8.31 lays out *eight kinds of giving* — eight *motivations* (giving as insult, from fear, in return, anticipating return, out of "it is good to give," out of "I cook and they don't," for reputation, for the adornment of the mind). The *Ekottarikāgama*[^5] also presents *eight kinds of giving* but unfolds *two different schemes that do not match the Pāli*: sūtra 9 lists eight *qualities* of giving (timely, pure, by one's own hand, with vow, with release, seeking cessation, to a good field, for others' benefit), and sūtra 8 lists eight *locations* for giving (waterside, monastery, near monastery, on the road, lecture hall, empty places, charnel ground, home).

This is not a translation discrepancy. It is a *substantive editorial difference* — two traditions filling the structural slot "eight" with different doctrinal content:

- The Pāli runs the *psychological cross-section* (eight reasons *why* one gives).
- The Chinese T0125 fascicle 37 sūtra 9 runs the *practice-theoretical cross-section* (eight ways *to give well*).
- The Chinese T0125 fascicle 37 sūtra 8 runs an *external-conditions cross-section* (eight locations *where* giving takes place).

Three different "eights" track three different analytical levels — and *those levels correspond to the three-term division proposed in this chapter*. The Pāli "eight motivations" maps to *cāga*'s analytical depth (cross-section on inner operation). The Chinese "eight qualities" maps to *dāna*'s analytical depth (cross-section on the quality of the act). The Chinese "eight locations" maps to a fourth, external-conditions dimension that supports both. **The two traditions' differing editorial centers of gravity correspond precisely to different layers in the three-term spectrum** — itself supplementary evidence for the three-term claim. The number "eight" can carry different doctrinal weights in different traditions, because *giving itself is a multi-layered event*.

The full three-eight comparison and its doctrinal analysis belongs to chapter 4. The present chapter records it only as a corroboration of the three-term thesis.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Dialogue: *Macchera* and the Scarcity Mindset

Contemporary behavioral economics has a concept called *scarcity mindset*, articulated by economist Sendhil Mullainathan and psychologist Eldar Shafir in their 2013 work *Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much*. Its central observation: when a resource (time, money, attention) is perceived as insufficient, the brain enters a self-protective mode — attention narrows around the scarce resource, cognitive bandwidth is consumed, and decision-making in unrelated domains degrades. The phenomenology overlaps with *macchera*: the mind is bound to "mine," resists sharing, cannot release.

Three layers of analysis:

**Where they resemble each other**: both describe the *narrowing of mind in the presence of (perceived) finite resource*. Mullainathan and Shafir's experiments show that subjects induced into a scarcity state subsequently exhibit narrower decision horizons and stronger protective bias; Buddhaghosa's description of *macchera* (the inability to bear that one's own become also another's) names the same phenomenological narrowing. Phenomenologically, the two are highly congruent, and the analogy can give a Western reader rapid recognition of *macchera* as a body-felt experience.

**Where they fundamentally differ**: in three dimensions.

First, *teleologically*. Scarcity-mindset research is oriented toward *policy design* — how to reduce scarcity-experience through institutional reform (e.g., financial-service design for the poor). The *macchera* doctrine is oriented toward *inner transformation* — how to loosen the structure of "mine" itself. The first changes external conditions; the second changes internal configuration.

Second, *ontologically*. The scarcity-mindset framework presupposes that resources are *actually* limited; the narrowing is therefore a rational response to external fact. The *macchera* doctrine holds that the *mind's natural luminosity* (*pabhassara*) is obscured by various stains, of which *macchera* is one — that *macchera* is *not* a rational response to external scarcity but an internal pathology of *mine*-grasping. A billionaire can have heavy *macchera*; this is counter-intuitive in the scarcity-mindset frame and entirely natural in the *Visuddhimagga* frame, because *macchera* does not depend on scarcity. It depends on the *mine* structure.

Third, *methodologically*. The intervention for scarcity mindset is *material relief or cognitive restructuring* (provide "bandwidth aid," simplify decision architectures, reduce scarcity cues). The intervention for *cāga* is *recollective training that builds a counter-faculty* — recollecting one's already-arisen *cāga*, letting the mind unfold within itself the formula traced in AN 6.25 / AN 11.11 / SN 55.32, until the *macchera* grip thins. The first is social engineering; the second is psychological-spiritual engineering.

**⚠️ Non-reduction notice**: the two frameworks share phenomenology, diverge in mechanism, and diverge in solution. The analogy can serve as a heuristic entry point — a way for Western readers to locate the body-feel of *macchera*. It cannot serve as theoretical equivalence — *macchera* cannot be eradicated by the policy interventions appropriate to scarcity mindset. To downgrade the *cāga* doctrine into "psychological technique against scarcity mindset" is to lose its standing as the fourth limb of stream-entry (SN 55.32) and to compress religious cultivation into self-management — the move that *McMindfulness* has already performed on *sati*. This volume cannot allow it to be performed again on *cāga*.

The analogy travels in one direction only: from scarcity to *macchera*, for purposes of recognition. Reverse travel does not work — one cannot apply *macchera* doctrine to solve scarcity-mindset's policy problems. The first is in the soteriological domain; the second is in the institutional domain. Conflating their interventions fails on both ends: applying personal cultivation to structural poverty deflects institutional responsibility, and applying policy design to *macchera* misses the inner pathology entirely.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading 1: *dāna* = charity / philanthropy

Western *charity* presupposes *a giver, a recipient, and an objective gap to be filled*. Its theological substrate (Latin *caritas*, Greek *agapē*) carries the Christian frame of *divine love flowing through the giver to the recipient*: the giver is the conduit, the recipient is the endpoint, divine love is the motive force. The narrative axis runs along *gap-filling* and *the flow of love*.

The internal logic of *dāna* runs along neither. It runs along the loosening of *mine*. A wealthy donor writing a major philanthropic check whose *cāga* has not loosened, and a poor person sharing the last bite of a meal whose *cāga* fully loosens — Pāli texts will rule for the second every time. It 26's closing line ("*even their last morsel they would not eat without sharing, so long as someone was there to receive it*") names a marker of full *cāga*, not a marker of "maximized financial impact."

To translate *dāna* as *charity* without further qualification is to bind the entire doctrine to a *material-gap-and-its-filling* social-gospel frame, losing the inner dimension altogether. The contemporary degeneration of English-language Buddhist discourse on giving (from personal-cultivation language into nonprofit-fundraising language) traces partly to this.

### ❌ Misreading 2: *cāga* = generosity-as-personality-trait

Contemporary psychology's "Big Five" personality model includes a generosity sub-facet under Agreeableness, treated as a *stable personality trait* — measured by instruments like the NEO-PI-R, with each subject assigned a generosity score that remains relatively stable across adulthood.

*Cāga* is not a stable personality trait. It is a *trained mental factor* — it can be built, can regress, can be rebuilt. Buddhaghosa's entire treatment of *cāgānussati* is written in the language of *active construction* ("the mendicant who applies himself to this *cāgānussati* becomes *yet more disposed to letting-go*"), not the language of *static description*. To read *cāga* as a personality trait is to shift it from the *cultivation domain* into the *social-psychology domain*, losing its standing as a stream-entry limb (SN 55.32). Awakening-fruit is not a score; it is a qualitative transformation — a stream-enterer's *cāga* is structurally non-regressing (this is the meaning of *aveccappasāda*, "verified confidence"), while a Big Five generosity score can collapse under sufficient pressure. The two operate in different domains.

### ❌ Misreading 3: *yāga* = sacrifice in the Christian/Hebraic atonement sense

The Christian–Hebraic tradition's *sacrifice* carries an *atonement* core — sacrifice transfers culpability or repairs the human-divine relationship. From the Levitical sacrifices to the cross of Christ, the theological axis of *sacrifice* is *propitiatory blood*.

*Yāga* in Vedic and early Buddhist contexts has *no atonement structure*. Vedic *yāga* maintains cosmic order (*ṛta*); Buddhist *yāga* plants seeds in the merit-field (*puññakkhetta*). Neither is the transfer of culpability. To translate *yāga* as *sacrifice* without contextual clarification smuggles Western atonement-theology into Buddhist offering-practice, generating distorted readings such as "offerings can wash away karmic debt." Such distortions are observable in segments of contemporary commercialized Chinese Buddhism (specific institutions promote "karma-eliminating" offering rituals on this model), but this volume does not name particular institutions; it identifies the *mistranslation of yāga* as one root of the distortion. Returning *yāga* to its proper position — as ceremonial resonance, merit-field seeding, and an ingredient absorbed into the inner structure of *cāga* — cuts the distortion at its semantic root.

### ⚠️ Caution 1: the clean three-term division is an analytical model, not a textual partition

In actual Pāli texts, the three terms occasionally cross-substitute — *dāna* sometimes appearing where *cāga* would do equally, *yāja* sometimes interchanged with *dāna* in early verses. The division proposed in this chapter is an *analytical framework*, not a *textual segmentation*. Readers using this framework on subsequent sūtras should expect the historical layering to manifest as cross-usage — the very phenomenon that records *yāga*'s historical absorption into *cāga* (visible in the *Visuddhimagga*'s preservation of *yācayogo / yājayogo*) and *dāna*'s gradual absorption of *cāga*'s inner dimension across the centuries (peaking in the Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā* literature, see chapters 11–14). The framework is a tool for reading; it is not a summary of the texts.

### ⚠️ Caution 2: the unidirectionality of the scarcity-mindset analogy

§4 establishes the analogy in one direction (from *scarcity* to *macchera*, for Western recognition). The reverse does not hold — one cannot apply *macchera* doctrine to scarcity-mindset's policy problems. The first is the cultivation domain; the second is the institutional domain. Mixing the interventions of the two domains fails on both ends: using personal cultivation to address structural poverty deflects institutional responsibility, and using policy design to address *macchera* misses the inner pathology. The two are not in competition; they are different intervention points in different domains, and they require parallel rather than substitutive engagement.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 The Core Thesis Restated

Early Buddhism uses *three words* for what English collapses into one: *dāna* (the giving event), *cāga* (the inner loosening), *yāga* (the ritual resonance). The three divide the labor and operate together. When English compresses them into a single "generosity," the layer first lost is *cāga* — and *cāga* is the heart of the doctrine.

To read giving as *charity* is to demote it to social gospel. To read giving as *generosity-trait* is to demote it to personality psychology. To read offering as *atonement-sacrifice* is to overlay it with propitiatory theology. All three misreadings lose the same thing: *giving in the dharma was never about how to give. It was about whether what is mine can become not only mine.*

The single strongest textual evidence for this thesis is the *cāga ↔ sīla* swap of SN 55.31 / 55.32: two adjacent suttas in the same chapter substitute *cāga* for *sīla* in the fourth slot of the stream-enterer's standard description, without altering the surrounding text. This editorial fact carries a doctrinal verdict: in the early account of stream-entry, *cāga* is *of equal weight with sīla*, an independent faculty defining the awakened, not a subset of *sīla* or a behavioral side-quest.

### 6.2 Practical Orientation

The next time you stand at a checkout and your finger hovers — that 0.3-second inward contraction is *macchera*. Its antidote is not "give more." Its antidote is the cultivation of a counter-faculty, *cāga*. The method is not complex: *recollect your own already-arisen cāga*, even once, even in something small. Let the mind unfold within itself the formula traced in AN 6.25 / AN 11.11 / SN 55.32:

> "I am so fortunate, so very fortunate. Among people whose minds are full of the stain of stinginess, I live with a mind freed from that stain — letting-go loosened, hand washed clean, delighting in relinquishment, responsive to those who request, delighting in giving and sharing."

This is not self-soothing. It is the application of *anussati* engineering to a *cāga* object — the same recollective mechanism described in volume one of the *Four Practice Gates*. Building a counter-faculty does not work by willpower forcing oneself to be generous. It works by training the mind to *notice one more time* a moment in which letting-go has already happened, allowing that moment to accumulate in the mind-stream as an object that can be returned to repeatedly.

From the first time this object is consciously recollected, *cāga* is being built. The next twenty-nine chapters are the unfolding of that path.

### 6.3 Open Questions and Hook to the Next Chapter

If *cāga* is "the inner condition that makes *dāna* possible," and if *cāga* in SN 55.32 stands at the same weight as *sīla* among the four limbs of stream-entry — when and how did *cāga* acquire that fruition status? What is its relation to the other three limbs (the verified confidence in Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha)? Why is *letting-go* — and not some other wholesome mental factor — selected as the inner marker of stream-entry?

The next chapter starts from the fourth limb of stream-entry, examining why *cāga* is not merely "a generous disposition" but the marker of someone who has actually entered the stream — why it matters whether or not a person has *cāga* as a verified inner reality, before they can be said to have entered the path of awakening.
