**Samantabhadra's Vows Four and Five as the Karma Dyad**

> "All the evil deeds I have ever done in the past
> arose from beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion;
> springing from body, speech, and mind —
> all of them I now repent."
> — *Samantabhadra-bodhisattva's Practice-and-Vow Chapter*, T0293, translated by Prajña, fascicle forty (vow four verse)[^1]

## Abstract
This paper continues the previous paper's (NIAN P26) argument that Samantabhadra's first three vows compose a three-karma set, and now treats vows four and five — *repenting karmic obstructions* (*chànhuǐ yèzhàng* 懺悔業障, vow four) and *rejoicing in merit* (*suíxǐ gōngdé* 隨喜功德, vow five). The thesis: these two vows are not the fourth and fifth items in a list of ten meritorious deeds but the **twin wings of karma practice** — a *karma dyad* — vow four operating as the **decrement** wing that addresses one's own past unwholesome action, vow five operating as the **increment** wing that draws into one's own practice the wholesome action of all others. Three pieces of textual evidence support this dyadic reading. First, the four-exhaustion-four-inexhaustion closure formula at the end of vows four and five is fully isomorphic with the closure formula of vows one through three at the textual level of T0293 itself — extending the previous paper's *vows one to three closure-formula isomorphism* finding into a *vows one to five 100% isomorphism* confirmation. Second, the verse-section's thirty-second stanza pairs the two vows internally in a single line — *"rejoicing-along, repenting, and all wholesome roots"* — making the dyadic pairing explicit at the verse-text level of the *sūtra*. Third, in the seven-branch worship lineage (*saptāṅga-pūjā*) — whose earliest Chinese-canonical configuration is fixed by Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509) fascicle sixty-one — vows four and five occupy the third and fourth branches. The argument is developed across three frames: the seven-branch worship genealogy; the Madhyamaka two-truths exposition of karma; and the Yogācāra *two transformations of the basis*. The Chinese ritual-practice closure draws on Zhiyi's ten-section *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Liturgy* (T1941) and the rejoicing segment of the fifth recollection-gateway in Shandao's *Verses for the Six Times of Reverence-Praise* (T1980). The paper engages three Anglophone interlocutors in a one-pushback-plus-two-extensions configuration: vis-à-vis Stevenson 1986/1999 it concedes-and-pushes-back on the modernist internalising reading by retrieving Stevenson's own *tri-register ritual technology* frame; vis-à-vis Heim 2004's *theories of the gift in South Asia* and Sharf 2002/2005's anti-psychologising of Buddhist ritual it concedes-and-extends. Six philological warnings are addressed: the sūtra-layer-versus-treatise-layer drift in *karmic obstruction*; the Tang-Song retrospective character of the *three-modes-of-repentance* labels; the register shift between early-Buddhist *future-oriented future-restraint* and Mahāyāna *retroactive purification* — the cardinal *mitigation, not erasure* slogan — which must be stated in two registers and never collapsed into a single line of development; the four-way distinction *anumodanā / muditā / kataññū / pattidāna*; the terminology drift in *pure precepts*; and the function-rather-than-vocabulary nature of the cross-comparison with the Indo-Tibetan four powers of confession.

**Keywords:** Samantabhadra's vow-practice; *Bhadracarī-praṇidhāna*; repenting karmic obstructions; rejoicing in merit; karma dyad; karma-transformation operator; the twin wings of karma practice; closure-formula isomorphism; seven-branch worship (*saptāṅga-pūjā*); register shift; mitigation, not erasure; *anumodanā*; the three modes of repentance; Zhiyi's *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Liturgy*; Shandao's five recollection-gateways; the two transformations of the basis; Madhyamaka exposition of karma; phenomenal repentance; principial repentance; four powers of confession.

## I. Introduction

Modern readers of the fourth and fifth among Samantabhadra's ten great vows — *repenting karmic obstructions* and *rejoicing in merit* — within the *Practice-and-Vow Chapter of Samantabhadra Entering the Inconceivable Liberation Realm* (henceforth the *Practice-and-Vow Chapter*) tend to read these as the fourth and fifth items in a list of ten meritorious deeds. In Anglophone reception there is a concurrent tendency to interiorise vow four as *"feeling sorry for one's past wrongdoings"* and vow five as *"feeling happy about others' achievements."*

These readings are not wrong, but they fail to register the structural binding the *sūtra* itself imposes between the two vows.

The previous paper (NIAN P26) treated vows one, two, and three as a three-karma set: *body-karma reverencing*, *speech-karma praising*, and *mind-karma offering* — three halves of a single isomorphic triplet at the textual level of T0293, codified in Chinese ritual practice by Shandao's three-karma mapping in his *Verses for the Six Times of Reverence-Praise*.

The present paper extends that argument with a more precise structural reading: **vows four and five are not parallel items at positions 4 and 5 of a ten-item list but a *karma dyad* — the twin wings of karma practice.** Vow four addresses the practitioner's own past unwholesome action (**decrement** of one's own karma — i.e., the dissolving of past unwholesome action); vow five draws into one's own practice the wholesome action of all others (**increment** — the receiving of others' merit as one's own share). Both wings turn together; only then is karma actually transformed.

Three textual evidences support this structural reading.

*First*, the four-exhaustion-four-inexhaustion closure formulas at the end of vows four and five are fully isomorphic with the closure formulas of vows one, two, and three; the only difference is the substitution at the verb position (*reverencing*, *praising*, *offering*, *repenting*, *rejoicing*). The previous paper's vows-one-to-three closure-formula isomorphism finding is now extended into a vows-one-to-five 100% isomorphism confirmation — the second incremental step in establishing the structural argument for the ten great vows as a whole.

*Second*, vow four is the only vow in the ten whose object is the practitioner's *own self*, and vow five is the only vow whose object is the *wholesome action of all others*. This *inward / toward-others* dyad is not a later commentarial imposition: the verse-section of T0293, in its thirty-second stanza, internally pairs the two — *"rejoicing-along, repenting, and all wholesome roots"* — placing both into a single composite verse.

*Third*, Samantabhadra's ten great vows are structurally isomorphic with the Indian *seven-branch worship* (*saptāṅga-pūjā*) lineage. The earliest Chinese-canonical configuration appears in Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle sixty-one: *"There are three modes for the bodhisattva's reverencing the buddhas: first, the mode of confessing transgressions; second, the mode of rejoicing-along and dedicating; third, the mode of inviting the buddhas."*[^2] In the seven-branch framework that develops from this Indic stratum, vows four and five occupy branches three and four: Samantabhadra's ten great vows are the Mahāyāna-Huayan amplification of seven-branch worship.

On the basis of these three evidences, the core argument is developed across five sub-sections (§III.1 closure-formula isomorphism and the dyadic structure; §III.2 vow four; §III.3 vow five; §III.4 the karma dyad in three frames; §III.5 closure on the Chinese ritual-practice side). §IV engages three Anglophone interlocutors in a one-pushback-plus-two-extensions configuration. §V handles six philological cautions. §VI offers cultivation guidance.

## II. Textual Sources and Recensional Configuration

The textual sources for this paper extend across four strata: the canonical-original layer of T0293; the Indo-Buddhist Pāli root layer for early-Buddhist confession and *anumodanā*; the Mahāyāna-canonical extension layer; and the Chinese ritual-practice and treatise layer.

The **canonical-original layer** is T0293 *Mahāvaipulya-buddhāvataṃsaka-sūtra: The Practice-and-Vow Chapter of Samantabhadra Entering the Inconceivable Liberation Realm*, fascicle forty, translated by Prajña (般若, c. 734 – c. 810) at Chang'an in 798 CE. Vow four's main exposition, closure formula, and verse-couplet, vow five's main exposition (across four object-classes — buddhas' fruition-grounds; sentient beings of the six destinies and four births; *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas* with and without further training; bodhisattvas' immeasurable difficult-and-painful practices), closure formula, and verse-couplet, together with the thirty-second stanza of the verse-section that pairs the two vows internally, furnish the principal text used here.[^3]

The **Pāli early-Buddhist root layer** for confession draws on four texts in their Chinese translations: the *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle fourteen *Susīma* chapter (T0099), which fixes the *self-seeing-and-self-knowing → future-restraint* dialogical formula of confession; the *Madhyama-āgama* fascicle five *Śāriputra-correlative* chapter (T0026), which exhibits the saṅgha-as-recipient structure of confession-receiving; the *Dīrgha-āgama Sāmaññaphala* chapter (T0001), which gives the canonical baseline for *mitigation, not erasure* in Ajātaśatru's repentance episode; and the *Pañcavargika-vinaya* fascicle eighteen (T1421), which fixes the formal *uposatha* triple-call (*yāvatatiyaṁ*) of the *prātimokṣa*. The Pāli root layer for *rejoicing-along* draws on two *Aṅguttara-nikāya* texts: AN 5.36 *Kāladānasutta*, the most direct *locus* for *anumodanā* in canonical entry, with the verse *"those who rejoice along [with the offering] … also share in the merit"* (*ye tattha anumodanti / … / tepi puññassa bhāgino*);[^4] and AN 8.36 *Puññakiriyavatthusutta*, which gives the canonical three-base list (*dāna* / *sīla* / *bhāvanā*) of the meritorious-action-bases — a list later expanded by the commentarial layer (*Dīgha-aṭṭhakathā* and parallels) into the *dasa-puññakiriyavatthu* (ten meritorious-action-bases), with *anumodanā* added as the fifth.[^5]

The **Mahāyāna-canonical extension layer** consists of four texts. The *Sūtra on the Visualisation of Samantabhadra Bodhisattva's Practice-Method* (T0277, translated by Dharmamitra, c. 424–442 CE) is the *locus classicus* for the three modes of repentance — though, as §V.2 below establishes, the three labels (*marks-grasping repentance*, *ritual repentance*, *no-arising repentance*) do not appear in the *sūtra* itself but are Tang-Song retrospective summaries. The *Sūtra of Golden Light: Chapter on Repentance* (T0663, translated by Dharmakṣema, 414 CE) — *"with single-mindedness, in one act of repentance, the gravest unwholesome karma accumulated through a thousand kalpas is wholly extinguished"*[^6] — is the principal canonical witness to the Mahāyāna *retroactive-purification* register that §V.3 below treats. The *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-sūtra: Chapter on the Merit of Rejoicing-Along* (T0262, translated by Kumārajīva in 406 CE) gives the *fifty-fold transmission* passage that establishes the *minute-action, vast-merit* structure of *rejoicing-along*. The *Avataṃsaka* itself supplies the host system for the ten great vows.

The **treatise and Chinese-ritual-practice layer** consists of three Indic-treatise sources, two Tang sub-commentaries, and four Chinese ritual sources. On the Indic-treatise side: Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra: Chapter on the Exposition of Rejoicing-Along and Dedication* fascicle sixty-one (T1509, translated by Kumārajīva), which yields the seven-branch worship's earliest Chinese-canonical configuration and the doctrinal exposition of *rejoicing-along and dedication*; Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā: Chapter on the Examination of Karma* fascicle three (T1564, also translated by Kumārajīva), which deploys the *"empty yet not annihilated, existent yet not eternal, the fruition of action is not lost"* verse to handle karma at the level of the two truths; and the *Cheng weishi lun* (*Vijñaptimātratā-siddhi*) fascicle nine of Dharmapāla and others (T1585, translated by Xuanzang), which supplies the four-fold definition of the *two transformations of the basis* (*āśraya-parāvṛtti*) as the Yogācāra coordinate within which karma-seeds and the work of repentance are situated. On the Tang sub-commentary side: Chengguan's (澄觀, 738–839) *Practice-and-Vow Chapter Commentary* (X05n0227), with its philological gloss of *kṣama* (the Sanskrit etymon of the Chinese *chàn* 懺), its three-section schema for vow four, and its *"reverse the obstruction of jealousy → arouse equal-and-impartial wholesome action"* exposition for vow five; and Zongmi's (宗密, 780–841) *Detailed Sub-Commentary on the Practice-and-Vow Chapter* (X05n0229), which draws on Chengguan and elaborates the *suppress-karma / transform-karma / extinguish-karma* three-mode schema, the *enemy-of-equal-strength* doctrine, the *seven preparatory minds for repentance*, the citation of the *Vimalakīrti-sūtra*'s *Upāli* chapter on the empty nature of transgression, and the *"Tiantai says: there are three bodhisattva-dharmas — the gateway of precepts, the gateway of merit, the gateway of no-arising"* passage (X05n0229 line 4751-4756) that supplies the original Tiantai vocabulary against which §V.2 below corrects the conventional three-modes labels. On the Chinese ritual side: Zhiyi's (538–597) *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Liturgy* (T1941), the standard mother-text for the ten-section repentance liturgy in Chinese Buddhism; Zhiyi's *Mohe zhiguan* (*Great Calmness and Insight*) fascicle two (T1911, recorded by Guanding), which contains the *"contemplating the empty nature of transgression"* passage that defines *no-arising repentance* at its core; Shandao's *Verses for the Six Times of Reverence-Praise* fascicle one (T1980), whose fifth recollection-gateway segment on dedication contains the rejoicing locus that anchors vow five on the Chinese Pure Land side; and the Yuanzhao (元照) sub-commentary *Sifenlü xingshi chao zichi ji* (T1805) on Daoxuan's *Karma Treatise* with its five conditions for *phenomenal repentance* in the Vinaya tradition (used in §V.6 below for the Indo-Tibetan four-powers cross-comparison).

Jizang's *Madhyamaka-śāstra Sub-Commentary* (T1824 / T1827) fascicle one cites the *Vimalakīrti-sūtra*'s *Upāli* chapter — *"directly extinguish"* alongside the doubled *"the nature of transgression and merit is empty / the marks of transgression and merit are clear and full"* — supplying the Sanlun-school complement to §III.4's two-register treatment.

## III. Core Argument

### III.1 Closure-Formula Isomorphism and the Dyadic Structure of the Karma Dyad

The closure formula at the end of vow four reads:

> "Just as space is exhausted, the realms of sentient beings are exhausted, the karmic deeds of sentient beings are exhausted, the afflictions of sentient beings are exhausted — *then my repentance is exhausted*. But since space — and likewise the afflictions of sentient beings — cannot be exhausted, this repentance of mine is also without end, moment-by-moment continuous, without interruption, body and speech and mind never wearying."[^7]

The closure formula at the end of vow five reads:

> "Just as space is exhausted, the realms of sentient beings are exhausted, the karmic deeds of sentient beings are exhausted, the afflictions of sentient beings are exhausted — *then this rejoicing-along of mine is exhausted*. But since space — and likewise the afflictions of sentient beings — cannot be exhausted, this rejoicing-along of mine is also without end, moment-by-moment continuous, without interruption, body and speech and mind never wearying."[^8]

The two passages are 100% parallel at the four-exhaustion-four-inexhaustion closure-formula structure. The only difference is the substitution at the verb position: *repenting* / *rejoicing-along*. Aligning the closure formulas of vows one through five yields the following table:

| Vow | Verb | Object | Closure |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | reverencing | all the buddhas | *my reverencing is without end* |
| 2 | praising | the tathāgatas | *my praising is without end* |
| 3 | offering | all the buddhas (extensively making dharma-offerings) | *my offering is without end* |
| 4 | repenting | one's own past karma | *my repenting is without end* |
| 5 | rejoicing-along | the wholesome action of all others | *my rejoicing-along is without end* |

The five closure formulas are fully isomorphic; the only difference is the verb at the verb-position. The methodological consequence is critical: **the closure formula is the *form* of the practice-vow, not its *content*.** What makes the ten great vows *practice-vows* is precisely the four-exhaustion-four-inexhaustion non-exhaustibility structure — not any particular content. The content fills the verb-slot. The previous paper's vows-one-to-three isomorphism finding is now extended into a vows-one-to-five 100% isomorphism confirmation; vows six through ten exhibit the same closure-formula structure (treated in P28-P29).

Within the ten vows as a whole, **vow four is the only vow whose object is the practitioner's *own self*** — *"the bodhisattva reflects: in the boundless past kalpas, … I have committed unwholesome deeds without measure or limit"* — and **vow five is the only vow whose object is the *wholesome action of all others*** — *"all … the buddhas and tathāgatas … all their wholesome roots, in all of these I rejoice along."* The remaining eight vows have an *I-toward-the-buddhas* or *I-toward-sentient-beings* outward-facing structure.

Vow four and vow five thus constitute the **karma dyad** — the twin wings of karma practice: **decrement** (one's own past karma being dissolved through vow four) and **increment** (the wholesome action of others being received as one's own share through vow five). Both wings turn together; only then is karma actually transformed.

The dyadic pairing is not a later commentarial imposition. The verse-section's thirty-second stanza pairs the two internally:

> "All the merit from reverencing, praising, and making offerings —
> requesting the buddhas to abide and to turn the dharma-wheel —
> *rejoicing-along, repenting, and all wholesome roots* —
> I dedicate to all sentient beings and to the buddha's path."[^9]

The verse-text of the *sūtra* itself binds the two vows internally within a single composite stanza. This same stanza further reveals the seven-branch worship's Chinese configuration in *embryonic* form (reverencing, praising, offering, requesting-abiding, requesting-turning-the-wheel, rejoicing-along-and-repenting, dedicating); it also confirms that **Samantabhadra's ten great vows are the Mahāyāna-Huayan amplification of the seven-branch worship lineage**. Vows four and five are the third and fourth branches. §III.4 below will trace this lineage back through Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle sixty-one as the Indic-treatise root.

### III.2 Vow Four: Repenting Karmic Obstructions

Vow four's main exposition in T0293 fascicle forty develops in a three-tier causal chain, beginning from *the bodhisattva's reflection on unwholesome deeds done since beginningless kalpas*:

> "Furthermore, son of good family! As to the meaning of *extinguishing-and-repenting karmic obstructions*: the bodhisattva reflects: in the boundless past kalpas, by reason of greed, hatred, and delusion, I have given rise to body, speech, and mind that have committed unwholesome deeds without measure or limit. **If these unwholesome deeds had a body and form, the boundless reaches of space could not contain them.**"[^10]

The three-tier causal chain is: the *root* (the three poisons of greed, hatred, and delusion) → the *path* (the three karmas of body, speech, and mind) → the *act* (unwholesome deeds without measure or limit). The line *"if these unwholesome deeds had a body and form, the boundless reaches of space could not contain them"* quantifies the *whole* of past karma through reverse-image hyperbole.

The exposition then sets out the active formula of repentance:

> "I now, by means of *purified body, speech, and mind*, in the presence of all the buddhas and bodhisattvas in the dust-mote-like buddha-fields throughout the dharma-realm, repent with sincerity, vowing not to commit such deeds again, *abiding eternally in the merit of pure precepts.*"[^11]

The passage sets out three terms in parallel: (1) *by means of purified body, speech, and mind* — the three karmas as the **medium** through which repentance operates; (2) *repent with sincerity, vowing not to commit such deeds again* — the **two-track** structure: *repenting* applies to deeds already committed (an inner-mental turn-from-past), *not committing such deeds again* applies to deeds not yet committed (a future-oriented future-restraint, *saṁvarāya*); (3) *abiding eternally in the merit of pure precepts* — the **outcome**: the ultimate horizon of repentance is the establishment of precept-merit. **It is worth noting that, in the *Practice-and-Vow Chapter*, repentance is not an isolated verb but is interlocked with the establishment of pure precepts.** This interlock means that repentance is not *retrospective regret* but the **starting device of future-discipline** — the active mechanism of karma-transformation, the concrete textual articulation of what the present paper terms the *karma-transformation operator*.

The vow four verse (T10.0848a) is the *locus classicus* for the Chinese-Buddhist verse of repentance — the daily-recitation, *mengshankou-shi* (Pure Hungry Ghost-feeding), and Water-Land ritual all anchor on this four-line formula:

> "All the evil deeds I have ever done in the past
> arose from beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion;
> springing from body, speech, and mind —
> all of them I now repent."[^12]

Zongmi's *Detailed Sub-Commentary on the Practice-and-Vow Chapter* divides the verse into a four-fold analytical schema: *fruit* (the karma being repented) → *cause* (the cause of karma-arising) → *path* (the instrument of karma-arising) → *act* (the formal repenting itself).[^13] The four-line structure is tightly composed in reverse-rhymed order — a condensation of the three-tier causal chain of the main exposition into the verse register.

Chengguan's *Practice-and-Vow Chapter Commentary* offers the Sanskrit gloss of *repentance-as-such* under the term *kṣama*:

> "*Chàn*: in Sanskrit fully *kṣama*, here *huǐguò* (repenting transgression). Distinguishing them more precisely: *chàn* names the *self-disclosing-of-prior-transgression*; *huǐ* names *amending-from-the-past, cultivating-toward-the-future*. **Extinguishing the obstruction of unwholesome karma; bringing about the wholesome of pure precepts.** Thus the text names this only *repenting karma*, but in fact the three obstructions and the four obstructions are all alike repented."[^14]

Chengguan's two-fold gloss — *self-disclosing-of-prior-transgression* / *amending-from-the-past, cultivating-toward-the-future* — corresponds exactly to the main exposition's two-track *repenting with sincerity / not committing such deeds again*. The doubled outcome — *extinguishing the obstruction of unwholesome karma / bringing about the wholesome of pure precepts* — likewise echoes the main exposition's *abiding eternally in the merit of pure precepts*.

Zongmi, on the front-end of repentance, sets out the structure of the *seven preparatory minds for repentance*:

> "First arouse the seven minds, as the means of approach: **first, shame and repugnance; second, fear; third, world-renouncing aversion; fourth, arousing the awakening-mind; fifth, equal-and-impartial regard for those who have wronged you and those dear to you; sixth, recollection of the buddhas' kindness; seventh, contemplating the empty nature of transgression.**"[^15]

The unfolding of the seven minds is: (1) *shame and repugnance* — self-judgement on what has been done; (2) *fear* — recognition of the consequence of karma; (3) *world-renouncing aversion* — a turning of attitude toward saṃsāra; (4) *arousing the awakening-mind* — a locking-in of will toward future practice; (5) *equal-and-impartial regard* — the adjustment of mental disposition toward those one has wronged; (6) *recollection of the buddhas' kindness* — recognition of the cooperating conditions; (7) *contemplating the empty nature of transgression* — the ultimate ontological judgement of karma. The seven-mind structure already provides a complete protocol of repentance as a *bodily-speech-and-mental* karma-transformation operator (not a purely inner repenting): emotional (1, 2), cognitive (3, 7), and volitional (4, 5, 6) — three registers operating jointly. §IV below will use this directly as a frame-anchor for engagement with Stevenson.

A philological detail must be flagged here. T0293 fascicle forty's chapter list has *chànhuǐ yèzhàng* 懺悔業障 (*repenting karmic obstructions*), but vow four's main exposition opening reads *yán chàn-chú yèzhàng zhě* 言懺除業障者 (*as to the meaning of extinguishing-and-repenting karmic obstructions*). The same translation (Prajña, 798 CE), single fascicle, the translator's deliberate dual usage: *huǐ* names the inner-mental turn (*repent with sincerity, not committing such deeds again*), *chú* names the function (*the obstruction of karma being extinguished*). The CBETA XML and the *Taishō* base text agree here; this is not a textual-critical anomaly. The doubled use of *repenting* and *extinguishing* will be picked up again in §V.1 below as part of the sūtra-layer-versus-treatise-layer terminology drift around *karmic obstruction*.

### III.3 Vow Five: Rejoicing in Merit

Before turning to vow five's main exposition, a philological hard-anchor must be set. The Pāli equivalent of *rejoicing in merit* is *anumodanā* (*anu-mud-*, *rejoicing-along*) — and not *muditā* (sympathetic joy, the third of the four immeasurables), and not *kataññū* (gratitude / recognition of kindness), and not *pattidāna* (giving the merit one has gained). The four terms have distinct etymologies and distinct doctrinal-canonical loci:

| Pāli term | Literal | Principal *locus* | Chinese rendering |
|---|---|---|---|
| *anumodanā* | rejoicing-along | AN 5.36 *Kāladānasutta*; commentarial *dasa-puññakiriya*, position 5 | *suíxǐ* 隨喜 |
| *muditā* | gladness | the four immeasurables (loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity) | *xǐ* 喜 (the third immeasurable) |
| *kataññū* | knowing-gratitude | AN 2.32 ff. and the *Recognition of Kindness* discourses | *zhī'ēn* 知恩 / *bào'ēn* 報恩 |
| *pattidāna* | giving-the-attained | commentarial *dasa-puññakiriya*, position 7 | *huíxiàng* 迴向 (reverse-direction structure) |

Anglophone secondary literature — including portions of P. Williams, Heim, and Schopen — sometimes uses the loose translation *sympathetic joy* to cover all four; this is the philological pitfall that §V.4 below addresses. Samantabhadra's vow five's early anchor is *anumodanā*, and the *Aṅguttara-nikāya* 5.36 *Kāladānasutta* verse is its most direct canonical *locus*:

> "*Those who rejoice along [with the offering], or render assistance, do not thereby diminish the offering; they too share in the merit.*"[^16]

A layered editorial history must also be flagged. The canonical-original *puññakiriyavatthu* (meritorious-action-bases) has three positions — *dāna* (giving), *sīla* (precept-keeping), *bhāvanā* (cultivation) — with AN 8.36 as its principal canonical formulation;[^17] *anumodanā* is **not** in the canonical list. *Anumodanā*'s position as the fifth position is the result of the commentarial layer (the *Dīgha-aṭṭhakathā* and parallels) expanding the three-base list into the *dasa-puññakiriyavatthu* (ten meritorious-action-bases). Samantabhadra's *rejoicing in merit* is thus the third tier of this elevation: the canonical three-base list → the commentarial ten-base list (*anumodanā* at position five) → the Mahāyāna ten great vows (*anumodanā* now the principal verb of vow five). Three successive elevations — from a canonical-list position to a Mahāyāna bodhisattva-path core verb.

Returning to T0293 vow five's main exposition. The *sūtra* unfolds rejoicing-along across four classes of objects:

**§1 The buddhas' fruition-grounds (all wholesome roots from initial mind-arousal through final *parinirvāṇa*):**

> "As to the meaning of *rejoicing in merit*: all the dust-mote-like buddhas-and-tathāgatas in the buddha-fields throughout the dharma-realm and space-realm in the ten directions of past, present, and future — from their initial mind-arousal in pursuit of all-knowing wisdom, through their diligent cultivation of merit, not sparing body or life, throughout buddha-field-dust-mote-like kalpas beyond expression … through their attainment of the buddhas' supreme awakening and *parinirvāṇa*, their distribution of relics — *all their wholesome roots, in all of them I rejoice along*."[^18]

**§2 The wholesome roots of sentient beings of the six destinies and four births:**

> "And of all kinds of beings of the six destinies and four births in all worlds of the ten directions, *down to one mote-of-dust, in all of them I rejoice along*."[^19]

The phrase *down to one mote-of-dust* sets the **lowest threshold** at the level of the everyday wholesome action; the object of rejoicing-along is not selectively chosen.

**§§3–4 *Śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas* with and without further training, plus the bodhisattvas' immeasurable difficult-and-painful practices:**

> "Of all *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas* with and without further training in the ten directions of past, present, and future — all their merit, in all of it I rejoice along. The immeasurable difficult-and-painful practices of all bodhisattvas — vast merit aspiring to supreme perfect awakening — in all of them I rejoice along."[^20]

The four classes — buddhas' fruition-grounds, sentient beings of the six destinies, *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas*, bodhisattvas' immeasurable practices — are an exhaustive enumeration of every possible *source of wholesome roots within the Buddhist system*. Vow five's verse compresses the four classes into four lines (in reverse-rhymed order):

> "All sentient beings of the ten directions —
> *śrāvakas* with and without further training —
> all the tathāgatas and the bodhisattvas —
> in all their merit I rejoice along."[^21]

Chengguan's exposition of vow five gives the doubled structure of *reversing the obstruction of jealousy*:

> "Because in the past one did not delight in others' wholesome action, now this rejoicing-along celebrates them: *extinguishing the obstruction of jealousy, arousing equal-and-impartial wholesome action.* … Now I will explain that *rejoicing-along is a sub-meaning of repentance*, since their force-and-form are mutually opposed, so it is set out alongside it: **equal-and-impartial — in the equal-and-impartial vastness one is fit to receive the dharma.**"[^22]

Two key clauses are decisive. *First*, *"rejoicing-along is a sub-meaning of repentance"* — Chengguan reads vow five directly as a *sub-meaning of repentance*, because the functional logic of rejoicing-along is *extinguishing the obstruction of jealousy / arousing equal-and-impartial wholesome action*, structurally isomorphic with vow four's *extinguishing the obstruction of karma / bringing about the wholesome of pure precepts* (*extinguish a certain obstruction → bring about a certain wholesome*). *Second*, *"equal-and-impartial — in the equal-and-impartial vastness one is fit to receive the dharma"* — the prior mental disposition of rejoicing-along is the establishment of self-and-others-equality.

Zongmi extends Chengguan's reading and gives a more compact judgement at the term *vast*:

> "As to *the sub-meaning of repentance*: it extinguishes the obstruction of jealousy. **As to *vast*: self and others are not two.**"[^23]

The phrase *self and others are not two* breaks open vow five's deeper doctrinal layer: the reason rejoicing-along can extend *down to one mote-of-dust* is precisely that, at the dharma-realm level, self and others are not actually distinct — others' wholesome action is *one's own share* of one's own wholesome. This connection prepares the entrance to the Madhyamaka and Yogācāra frames in §III.4.

A final source-stratum observation must be added: the dyadic pairing of vow five with vow four — *rejoicing-along* paired with *repenting* — is not a Huayan-school innovation. The *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-sūtra*'s *Chapter on the Merit of Rejoicing-Along* already exhibits the *minute-action, vast-merit* structure of rejoicing-along (the fifty-fold transmission passage: a single moment of rejoicing-along, transmitted through fifty intermediaries, yet the merit of the fiftieth recipient remains incalculable); Nāgārjuna's *Chapter on the Exposition of Rejoicing-Along and Dedication* in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle sixty-one supplies the seven-branch worship's earliest configuration *and* the doctrinal exposition of rejoicing-along — the chapter's title (*Rejoicing-Along and Dedication*) itself signals rejoicing-along's systematic position in the Mahāyāna treatise layer. Samantabhadra's vow five is the fully-formed apex of this lineage.

### III.4 The Karma Dyad in Three Frames (the Argument's Pivot)

The argument's pivot can now be stated: **vows four and five are not two isolated items in a list of ten meritorious deeds but an *isomorphic dyad* — the twin wings of karma practice — vow four operating as the *decrement-mechanism* on one's own karma (decrement of one's own karma); vow five operating as the *increment-mechanism* on the karma of others (increment of others' wholesome action); only when both wings turn together is karma actually transformed (*karma-transformation*).** This dyadic reading lands in three doctrinal frames.

**Frame one: the seven-branch worship lineage (*saptāṅga-pūjā*).** The earliest Chinese-canonical configuration of seven-branch worship appears in Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle sixty-one:

> "There are three modes for the bodhisattva's reverencing the buddhas: *first, the mode of confessing transgressions; second, the mode of rejoicing-along and dedicating; third, the mode of inviting the buddhas.* Spoken at length, they are immeasurable and limitless."[^24]

Nāgārjuna's three modes already explicitly place *confessing transgressions* and *rejoicing-along and dedicating* as the first two modes of the bodhisattva's reverencing-the-buddhas. Later esoteric ritual texts (T0866, T0897 et al.) expand this into a fixed five-to-six-position sequence (*repenting → rejoicing-along → taking refuge → arousing the awakening-mind → vow-making → dedicating*); Śāntideva's *Bodhicaryāvatāra* chapter two (*the chapter on confessing transgression*) consolidates the late-Indic seven-branch structure (*reverencing / offering / repenting / rejoicing-along / requesting-the-turning-of-the-dharma-wheel / requesting-the-buddhas-to-abide / dedicating*). Samantabhadra's ten great vows are the apex of this lineage: the Mahāyāna-Huayan amplification of seven-branch worship — vows 1+2+3 = branches 1-2 (reverencing, offering); vows 4+5 = branches 3-4 (repenting, rejoicing-along); vows 6+7 = branches 5-6 (the *requesting* dyad); vows 8+9+10 = the expansion of branch 7 (dedicating) into a closing triad (treated in P28-P29).

**Frame two: the Madhyamaka two-truths exposition.** Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā: Chapter on the Examination of Karma* handles karma's ontological status in a single verse:

> "*Empty yet not annihilated, existent yet not eternal, the fruition of action is not lost — this is what the buddha taught.*"[^25]

The doctrinal logic: at the level of ultimate truth, karma's nature is empty (*ultimately empty*, *quiescent*, *natureless*); at the level of conventional truth, karma's fruition is not lost (*deeds are not lost, even after a hundred-thousand kalpas; when conditions converge, the fruition returns to one's own self*). The two truths must be stated together; they cannot be collapsed onto a single side. Within this frame, vow four's repentance can be read in two ways: at the ultimate level, *the nature of transgression and merit is empty* (*repenting is no-repenting; the repenter and what is repented are alike unattainable*); at the conventional level, *the karma of action is not lost* (the *ritual act* of repentance has a real action on future-direction). Vow five's rejoicing-along has the same doubled reading: at the ultimate level, *self and others are not two* (the rejoicer and what is rejoiced-in are alike effaced); at the conventional level, *down to one mote-of-dust, in all I rejoice along* (the *ritual* has a real referent). **The two truths stated together is the karma dyad's coherent treatment in the Madhyamaka frame.**

**Frame three: the Yogācāra *two transformations of the basis*.** The *Cheng weishi lun* fascicle nine sets out the core definition of the *two transformations of the basis* (*āśraya-parāvṛtti*):

> "By transforming the afflictions one obtains great *nirvāṇa*; by transforming the cognitive-obstacle one realises supreme awakening. … By repeated cultivation of the wisdom of non-discrimination, one severs the gross seed of the two obstacles in the root-consciousness; thus one is able to *transform-and-extinguish-the-basis-as-saṃsāra* and *transform-and-realise-the-basis-as-nirvāṇa*. **Although the suchness is by nature pure but its marks are mixed-and-defiled, when it is freed from defilement we provisionally speak of a new purity; this new purity is just what is called the transformation of the basis.**"[^26]

Within the Yogācāra system, the extinguishing of karmic obstruction is understood as the *transformation of karma-impressions* (*karma-vāsanā*) within the *ālayavijñāna* (the storehouse-consciousness) — not the *erasure* of past karma into nothing, but the severing of its grossness and the turning of its basis. The line *"although the suchness is by nature pure but its marks are mixed-and-defiled, when it is freed from defilement we provisionally speak of a new purity"* is decisive: *karmic obstruction extinguished* operates at the level of marks; the level of nature is intrinsically pure. Repentance's functional logic is the severing of grossness, not the extinction of the nature-substance. Vow five's rejoicing-along has the same Yogācāra reading: others' wholesome roots, at the shared-aspect level of the storehouse-consciousness, are *pure-seeds* whose perfuming-increase one's own karma can receive through the act of rejoicing along — this is the meaning of *sharing in the merit*. This doctrinal layer underpins the mechanism of *down to one mote-of-dust, in all I rejoice along*: rejoicing-along is not idle words but a concrete verb of perfuming-increase upon karma-impressions.

The three frames cannot be collapsed onto a single side — and this is the prelude to §V.3's *register-shift* warning. The seven-branch worship frame is the *ritual-genealogical* level; Madhyamaka two truths is the *ontological* level; Yogācāra *two transformations of the basis* is the *karma-impression mechanism* level. Each of the three has its own field of operation; the habits of two-truths-stated-together cannot be transposed onto the ritual-genealogical inquiry, and *vice versa*.

But all three frames converge on a single conclusion: **repenting and rejoicing-along are the karma dyad's two-faced active treatment of karma — *the decrement of one's own karma* and *the receiving-in of others' wholesome action* are the twin wings of karma-transformation.** This is the argument's pivot, and the concrete articulation of the term *karma-transformation operator*.

### III.5 Closure on the Chinese Ritual-Practice Side

The previous paper (P26) developed Shandao's three-karma-five-recollection-gateways correspondence as the daily six-period ritual schedule; the present paper does not re-deploy the three-karma mapping here, but instead takes the *fifth recollection-gateway*'s rejoicing segment alone as the Chinese-Pure-Land anchor for vow five, and then proceeds to the further Chinese-patriarchal articulations of the repentance-rejoicing dyad.

The standard mother-text for the configuration of Chinese repentance liturgy is the *ten-section* structure of Zhiyi's *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Liturgy*:

> "When the practitioner first enters the ritual space, he must equip himself with ten dharmas: (1) purifying the ritual space; (2) purifying the body; (3) the three-karma offering; (4) inviting the Three Jewels; (5) praising the Three Jewels; (6) prostrating to the buddhas; **(7) repentance**; (8) walking the ritual path in circumambulation; (9) reciting the *Lotus Sūtra*; (10) contemplating the one true reality. … Within the three-times-seven-day period, day and night six times, beginning from the first entry into the ritual space, all ten dharmas are to be cultivated together within each session."[^27]

The internal structure of the ten sections is suggestive: 1-2 (purifying space and body) are *preliminaries*; 3-6 (offering, inviting, praising, prostrating) are *invocation* (i.e., seven-branch worship's first two branches); 7 (repentance) is the *decrement of one's own karma*; 8 (circumambulation) is a *body-karmic transition*; 9 (sūtra-recitation) is the *internalisation of the dharma*; 10 (contemplating one true reality) is the *contemplative closure*. **Repentance (the seventh section) sits at the middle of the ten and forms the axis of *self-orientation* following the invocation.** Although rejoicing-along is not enumerated as an independent section, it is already contained within *contemplating the one true reality* (i.e., the contemplation of the empty nature of transgression and merit, of self-and-others-not-two).

Zhiyi's core definition of *no-arising repentance* (*Mohe zhiguan* fascicle two) reads:

> "Contemplating the empty nature of transgression: realising that the minds of greed, hatred, and delusion are all gateways of stillness. … *Penetrating deeply into the marks of transgression and merit, illuminating in all ten directions.* This empty wisdom, being matched with the mind, is *like the morning dew at sunrise that vanishes all at once*. … This is the ten-fold repentance: along the path of *nirvāṇa*, against the stream of saṃsāra, capable of extinguishing the four major and five inverted transgressions."[^28]

The line *"like the morning dew at sunrise that vanishes all at once"* is the classical metaphor of *no-arising repentance*, and bears on §V.3's register-shift caution (the *extinguishment-of-transgression* metaphor must be read as a re-judgement of the ontological status of karma, not as the physical erasure of a physical event).

Shandao's *Verses for the Six Times of Reverence-Praise* fascicle one, on the fifth recollection-gateway segment, reads:

> "Fifth: the gateway of dedication. That is, with single-mindedness, *whether toward the wholesome roots one has oneself made, or toward all the wholesome roots made by all the saintly-and-ordinary of the three vehicles and five destinies, in each and every one, deeply give rise to rejoicing-along.* As all the buddhas and bodhisattvas perform rejoicing-along, so do I likewise rejoice along. With this rejoicing-along's wholesome roots, together with my own wholesome roots, all are shared with sentient beings and dedicated toward the Land of Bliss."[^29]

The parallel placement of *the wholesome roots one has oneself made* and *all the wholesome roots made by all the saintly-and-ordinary of the three vehicles and five destinies* is precisely vow five's *one's-own-karma + others'-karma* dyadic structure on the Chinese Pure-Land side; rejoicing-along, in the five-recollection-gateway scheme, is not an isolated segment but the active mechanism of dedication. Shandao has already concretised the three-fold structure *receiving-in-one's-own-karma + receiving-in-others'-karma → shared dedication* into a daily six-period ritual programme. Yongming Yanshou's *Records of the Source Mirror* and Ouyi Zhixu's *Essential Commentary on the Amitābha-sūtra* further compress this structure into the doctrinal simplification of *name-recollection's encompassing of Samantabhadra's myriad practices* (Ouyi's *Essential Commentary* chapter-by-chapter exposition is reserved for the closing paper of NIAN's Part VIII).

A final philological anchor must be supplied. The early-Buddhist dialogical formula of confession is fixed in the *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle fourteen *Susīma* chapter:

> "*If an ordinary person commits a transgression, and self-sees, self-knows, and confesses-the-transgression*, then in the world to come the discipline-of-restraint will be established, merit will increase, never to retrogress or diminish. … The Buddha said to Susīma: *'I receive your repenting-of-transgression. You should fully say: I was foolish, unwholesome, and without wisdom; I went forth surreptitiously into the True Dharma and Discipline; today I confess, self-seeing the transgression, self-knowing the transgression. In the world to come the discipline-of-restraint will be established, merit will increase, never to retrogress or diminish.*'"[^30]

This passage realises in full the four anchors of early-Buddhist confession: *community-positioning* (declaring before the Buddha) + *dialogical exchange* (the offender speaks → the recipient receives, *I receive your repenting-of-transgression*) + *formulaic structure* (*self-seeing transgression, self-knowing transgression → discipline-of-restraint established*) + *future-orientation* (the discipline-of-restraint's object is *the world to come*). **Early-Buddhist confession is not a transgression-extinguishing mechanism but the starting device of future-discipline** — this is the textual root of §V.3's *mitigation, not erasure*.

The Chinese ritual-practice trajectory is clear: early-Āgama dialogical formula → sectarian-Vinaya triple-call *uposatha* → Mahāyāna three modes of repentance → Zhiyi's ten-section liturgy → Shandao's five recollection-gateways → Yongming and Ouyi's name-recollection-encompassing. Each stage is a more refined ritual-protocol articulation of the karma dyad; Samantabhadra's vows four-and-five are the doctrinal apex of this lineage, while the Chinese patriarchs concretise it on the ritual-curricular side into the actual practice of daily six-period observance.

A single response to Cook 1977's reading of Huayan as a metaphysical system (and not a concrete practice) can be offered here: the concrete ritual configuration of the *karma-transformation operator* is the hard evidence on Huayan's practice side — Huayan is not metaphysics-only. But this is only a glancing remark on the background target; the foreground engagement will be picked up in §IV by Stevenson.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Engagement

This paper's §IV configuration matches the previous paper's: one pushback ally plus two extensions, the principal interlocutors being the Anglophone Huayan and Chinese-Buddhism interpretive tendencies. Cook 1977 remains the background target, glanced at the close of §III.5, and is not foregrounded in §IV.

**Stevenson 1986 / 1999 (primary pushback ally · *tri-register ritual technology*).** Daniel Stevenson, in *The Four Kinds of Samādhi in Early T'ien-t'ai Buddhism* (Gregory ed., *Traditions of Meditation in Chinese Buddhism*, University of Hawaii Press, 1986) and *"Protocols of Power: Tz'u-yün Tsun-shih"* (Gregory and Getz eds., *Buddhism in the Sung*, University of Hawaii Press, 1999), establishes that the Tiantai repentance liturgy is not pure inner repenting-of-transgressions but a **bodily / verbal / mental tri-register ritual technology** — somatic (prostration, circumambulation, perambulation), verbal (sūtra-recitation, name-calling, praising), and contemplative (contemplating the empty nature of transgression, contemplating the one true reality) all activated together as a ritual technology.[^31] The present paper extends this frame: **vow four's repenting is itself a karma-transformation operator and not pure inner repenting** — the main exposition's *"by means of purified body, speech, and mind, repent with sincerity"* internally binds repentance to the *medium* of the three karmas (§III.2); Zongmi's seven preparatory minds for repentance (shame and repugnance, fear, world-renouncing aversion, arousing the awakening-mind, equal-and-impartial regard, recollection of the buddhas' kindness, contemplating the empty nature of transgression) supply a complete protocol with all three of emotional, cognitive, and volitional registers operating jointly (§III.2); Zhiyi's *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Liturgy*'s ten-section structure embeds repentance still more concretely into a three-times-seven-day six-period bodily curriculum (§III.5). Stevenson's tri-register frame is the strongest available ally for pushing back against the modernist *interiorise-repentance-as-private-regret* reading.

**Heim 2004 (concede-and-extend · *merit-pool economics*).** Maria Heim, in *Theories of the Gift in South Asia: Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain Reflections on Dāna* (Routledge, 2004), demonstrates that the South Asian *dāna* system embeds a structure of *merit-pool economics* — giver, recipient, and rejoicer-along all share in merit within a single giving event, distributed not by proportion of material contribution.[^32] **The concede-and-extend move:** the present paper *concedes* that vow five's rejoicing-along has, at the *dāna*-economics level, the merit-pool mechanism Heim identifies (the AN 5.36 *Kāladānasutta* line *"they too share in the merit"* is precisely the canonical-layer textual evidence for this mechanism); and *extends* the frame to the Mahāyāna's super-large scale of three times and ten directions — vow five's four classes of objects of rejoicing-along (the buddhas' fruition-grounds, sentient beings of the six destinies and four births, *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas* with and without further training, bodhisattvas' immeasurable practices) far exceed the localised South Asian *dāna*-ritual economy and constitute a *cosmological extension* of the merit-pool structure. Heim's frame is a *secondary articulation* of vow five rather than its principal thesis, but as an Anglophone-academic frame-anchor, it serves dialogue effectively.

**Sharf 2002 / 2005 (concede-and-extend · P26 carry-over · anti-psychologising extension).** Robert Sharf's methodological essays — particularly *Coming to Terms with Chinese Buddhism: A Reading of the Treasure Store Treatise* (University of Hawaii Press, 2002) and the *"Ritual"* entry in *Critical Terms for the Study of Buddhism* (Lopez ed., University of Chicago Press, 2005) — argue that two modern Western interpretive tendencies in reading Chinese-Buddhist liturgy both miss the phenomenon: (a) reading liturgy as superstitious residue requiring apologetic defence, or (b) reducing liturgy to a phenomenological inner-experiential mechanism. Sharf argues that, within the Chinese-Buddhist context, liturgy is a *constitutive social-and-cosmological action* whose *real* effect on the karmic cosmos is not subject either to subjective-psychological analysis or to superstition-charge.[^33] The previous paper (P26) used this frame on the *offering-as-cosmological-action* side; the present paper extends it to the *repentance-and-rejoicing-along* dyad — to resist the modernist drift that would interiorise repentance as *feeling sorry for past wrongs* and rejoicing-along as *feeling happy about others' achievements*. Sharf's anti-psychologising is the strongest support for the present paper's dyadic structure; the present paper's thesis — the karma-transformation operator's twin wings — is the concrete articulation of Sharf's frame within the Huayan textual context.

The three-interlocutor configuration forms a 1+2 cadence: Stevenson supplies the foreground pushback frame (*ritual technology, not inner repenting*); Heim supplies the secondary articulation on the vow-five side (*merit-pool economics*); Sharf carries forward the previous paper's conceptual guard (extending the *constitutive-action* frame from offering to the dyad). Cook 1977's metaphysics-only frame is no longer foregrounded; the concrete ritual-curricular instantiation of the karma-transformation operator is itself the sharper pushback lever against any reading-as-metaphysics-only.

## V. Middle-Way Cautions

This paper's philological cautions are six. Four are addressed in full (C.1–C.4); the remaining two (C.5 the pure-precepts terminology drift; C.6 the Indo-Tibetan four-powers cross-comparison) are flagged in single-sentence footnote glosses.

**❌ C.1: The treatise-layer concept of *karmic obstruction* must not be retroactively imposed on the *sūtra* layer.** In T0293 fascicle forty as a whole, the term *karmic obstruction* (*yèzhàng* 業障) appears only three times in the surface text (the chapter list, vow four's main exposition heading, and the vow-king's general-summary-of-merit passage). **The *sūtra* itself offers no definition.** The sūtra's reference for *karmic obstruction* is restricted to the *extensional* description *unwholesome deeds committed by body, speech, and mind through the three poisons of greed, hatred, and delusion since beginningless kalpas*. The later treatises and sub-commentaries — Zongmi citing the *Sūtra on the Names of the Buddhas* (*Foming jing*) for the three obstructions (*affliction-obstruction / karmic-obstruction / fruition-obstruction*); the *Chapter on the Excellent Marks* for the four obstructions (*karma / affliction / fruition / view*); the Yogācāra two-obstruction schema (*affliction-obstruction / cognitive-obstruction*) — represent a layered framework expansion. **The *sūtra*-layer *karmic obstruction* is not the treatise-layer *karmic obstruction*** — the former is extensional, indicating unwholesome deeds done through the three poisons and three karmas; the latter is the formal taxonomic distinction within a three / four / two-obstruction structure. §III.2 has already noted the dual-track translation fact (*repenting* / *extinguishing*); here the principle is reaffirmed: the framework-distinctions of the treatise layer cannot be retroactively imposed on the *sūtra* layer; the two layers must be allowed to stand together.

**⚠️ C.2: The *three-modes-of-repentance* labels (*marks-grasping / ritual / no-arising*) are Tang-Song retrospective summaries.** The *Sūtra on the Visualisation of Samantabhadra Bodhisattva's Practice-Method* (T0277) is the *locus classicus* of the three modes of repentance (it does set out three classes of repentance practice), but **the *sūtra* itself does not deploy the three labels** *marks-grasping repentance / ritual repentance / no-arising repentance* as a parallel triplet. Zhiyi's *Mohe zhiguan* (T1911) employs the *phenomenal repentance / principial repentance* binary plus the *ten minds / twenty minds* framework, not the three-label triplet. Zongmi's *Detailed Sub-Commentary on the Practice-and-Vow Chapter* cites *"Tiantai says: there are three bodhisattva-dharmas — *the gateway of precepts, the gateway of merit, the gateway of no-arising*"* (X05n0229 line 4751-4756) — this is the formal landing of Zhiyi's three-mode schema in the Huayan context. **Note: the actual term is not *marks-grasping / ritual / no-arising* but *precepts / merit / no-arising*.** The triplet *marks-grasping repentance / ritual repentance / no-arising repentance*, in its parallel form, is most likely a later abbreviation; direct grep on X05n0227 + X05n0229 yields no occurrence. The present paper, when it deploys the three-modes label, supplies a footnote noting *"a later abbreviation; the original Tiantai vocabulary is *precepts / merit / no-arising* in three gateways"*.

**❌ C.3: Early-Buddhist confession is not a transgression-extinguishing mechanism; the Mahāyāna three modes of repentance are not a continuation of early-Buddhist confession — *register shift* must be stated in two registers and never collapsed into a single line of development.** Early-Buddhist confession's outcome is *"the discipline-of-restraint will be established; merit will increase, never to retrogress or diminish"* (the *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle fourteen *Susīma* chapter and the *Madhyama-āgama* fascicle five *Śāriputra* chapter, verbatim); confession **generates** future-discipline, **does not erase** the past act. The *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle thirty-two *Self-Repentance Chapter* states explicitly: *"although it cannot make that act not have been done, by virtue of this self-repentance, in the world to come one will be free from killing."*[^34] The *Sāmaññaphala-sutta* episode of King Ajātaśatru is the canonical baseline: *mitigation, not erasure* — the act of patricide cannot, ontologically, be made not to have been done, but, by reason of repentance, the future-orientated discipline-of-being-free-from-killing is established. By contrast, the Mahāyāna *Sūtra of Golden Light: Chapter on Repentance* — *"with single-mindedness, in one act of repentance, the gravest unwholesome karma accumulated through a thousand kalpas is wholly extinguished"* (T16n0663, 0337b29-c05)[^35] — represents a **register shift**: the early-Buddhist *future-discipline* register has shifted into the Mahāyāna *retroactive-purification* register.

The textual evidence for both registers is original, both are *sūtra*-layer, neither can be set aside — the two must be stated in two registers. **They cannot be collapsed into a single line of development** (one cannot say either that the Mahāyāna register is a reasonable development of the early register, or that it is a deviation or hyperbole). Zhiyi's *"like the morning dew at sunrise that vanishes all at once"* metaphor (§III.5) is the Mahāyāna *retroactive-purification* register's reading of the *re-judgement of karma's ontological status* (transgression's nature is intrinsically empty; *phenomenal repentance* is able to halt its perfuming, *principial repentance* is able to alter its basis-of-support). This reading should not be drawn into either modernist apology (as a *symbolic, magical-thinking* picture) or maximalist literalism (as *physical erasure*). **The correct treatment is the two-register textual statement**: the early register and the Mahāyāna register are two distinct doctrinal articulations, belonging to two distinct normative frameworks.

**⚠️ C.4: *anumodanā* ≠ *muditā* ≠ *kataññū* ≠ *pattidāna* — the four-way pitfall.** See §III.3 opening. Anglophone secondary literature — including portions of P. Williams, Heim, and Schopen — sometimes employs the loose translation *sympathetic joy* to cover all four. This loose rendering collapses four terms with distinct doctrinal-canonical loci into a single cover term, and is a hard philological error. Samantabhadra's vow five's early anchor is *anumodanā* (*rejoicing-along*, AN 5.36) — not *muditā* (the third of the four immeasurables); not *kataññū* (gratitude / recognition of kindness); not *pattidāna* (the reverse-direction structure of giving the merit one has gained). The present paper's usage: the Chinese *suíxǐ* 隨喜 always corresponds to *anumodanā*; the Chinese *xǐ* 喜 (third immeasurable) always corresponds to *muditā*.

**⚠️ C.5 (one-sentence footnote gloss):** T0293 deploys only *"pure precepts"* (a single category) in its surface text; it has neither the *triple-grouping pure precepts* taxonomy (*restraint-precepts / wholesome-action-precepts / sentient-being-benefiting-precepts*) nor the *precept-substance* category. The development into the *triple-grouping / precept-substance* system is a Tiantai, Huayan, and Vinaya-school sub-commentarial elaboration. Vow four's main exposition phrase *"abiding eternally in the merit of pure precepts"* must not be tacitly upgraded to *triple-grouping pure precepts*; the *sūtra* layer must be distinguished from the treatise layer.[^36]

**⚠️ C.6 (one-sentence footnote gloss):** The Indo-Tibetan *four powers of confession* (*śaraṇa* the power of reliance, *pratipakṣa* the power of antidote, *vidūṣaṇā* the power of regret, *saṃvara* the power of restraint) has no direct vocabulary parallel in the Chinese Vinaya canon. The closest cognate-structures are Daoxuan's *Karma Treatise* five conditions for *phenomenal repentance* (inviting buddhas and bodhisattvas as witness / reciting sūtras and dhāraṇī / declaring the name of the transgression / making the vow / following the teaching's verification) + Chengguan's *ten minds against the stream of saṃsāra* + Zongmi's seven preparatory minds for repentance from the *Sūtra on the Names of the Buddhas*. The cross-comparison must be conducted at the level of *function-correspondence* and not *vocabulary-correspondence*; Patrul Rinpoche / Tsongkhapa serve as comparative reference, but Indo-Tibetan vocabulary must not be applied directly to Chinese sūtra-and-treatise materials.[^37]

## VI. Closing

### Essence Restated

Samantabhadra's vows four and five are not the fourth and fifth items in a list of ten meritorious deeds. They are the **karma dyad** — the twin wings of karma practice: vow four addresses the practitioner's own past unwholesome action (the *decrement* wing — the dissolving of one's own past unwholesome action); vow five draws into one's own practice the wholesome action of all others (the *increment* wing — the receiving of others' merit as one's own share). Both wings turn together; only then is karma actually transformed. This dyadic structure is supported by three pieces of textual evidence: the closure-formula isomorphism (vows one through five 100% isomorphic); the verse-section's internal pairing (the thirty-second stanza's *"rejoicing-along, repenting, and all wholesome roots"*); and the seven-branch worship lineage's branches three and four (the Indic *locus classicus* is Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle sixty-one). The argument lands in three doctrinal frames — the seven-branch worship lineage, the Madhyamaka two-truths, and the Yogācāra *two transformations of the basis* — each with its own field of operation; all three converge on a single conclusion: the *karma-transformation operator* is a *dyad*, not a pair of *isolated items*.

### Cultivation Guidance

For modern Anglophone practitioners, the present paper recommends two Chinese ritual-canonical texts as concrete starting-points.

*First*, Zhiyi's *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Liturgy*, the three-times-seven-day six-period ten-section curriculum: *purifying the ritual space → purifying the body → three-karma offering → inviting the Three Jewels → praising the Three Jewels → prostrating to the buddhas → repentance → walking the ritual path in circumambulation → reciting the Lotus Sūtra → contemplating the one true reality.* The ten sections embed repentance (the seventh) within a three-times-seven-day six-period bodily curriculum, with *contemplating the one true reality* (the contemplation of the empty nature of transgression and merit, of self-and-others-not-two) as the contemplative closure — the historical prototype of Stevenson's tri-register ritual technology.

*Second*, Shandao's *Verses for the Six Times of Reverence-Praise*, the six-period reverence-and-praise pairing. Each period contains the complete seven-branch structure of *prostration* (body-karmic) + *praising* (speech-karmic) + *name-recollection* (mind-karmic) + *repentance* + *rejoicing-along* + *dedication*. For the busy modern practitioner, a single-period daily form (morning or evening) can be drawn off: (1) three prostrations to the buddhas; (2) recite vow four's verse (*"All the evil deeds I have ever done in the past …"*) — repentance; (3) recite vow five's verse (*"All sentient beings of the ten directions …"*) — rejoicing-along; (4) a passage of dedication to all sentient beings and the buddhas' path. The single-period daily form takes only five to ten minutes but already carries the structural skeleton of seven-branch worship.

The shared point of both starting-points: repentance and rejoicing-along are not isolated private events but the body-speech-and-mind ritual protocol embedded within the ritual curriculum. *Abide in the body, abide in the schedule, abide in the dyad.*

### Open Question

Vows four and five's dyadic structure handles the *self-karma / others'-karma* dyad; the next paper (NIAN P28) will handle vows six and seven's *requesting* dyad — *requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel* and *requesting the buddhas to abide* — whose doctrinal *locus classicus* is *the long-abiding of the dharma* (*Dharma-cira-sthiti*) rather than karma-transformation: another isomorphic dyad. The present paper does not pre-empt that content.

### Closing Verses

To close, the central verses of vow four and vow five read together as the paper's epigraph-of-conclusion:

> "All the evil deeds I have ever done in the past
> arose from beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion;
> springing from body, speech, and mind —
> all of them I now repent."
> — Vow Four
>
> "All sentient beings of the ten directions —
> *śrāvakas* with and without further training —
> all the tathāgatas and the bodhisattvas —
> in all their merit I rejoice along."
> — Vow Five

Repentance and rejoicing-along are the *karma dyad* — one wing toward one's own past (*decrement*), the other toward the wholesome action of all others (*increment*); both wings turning together, *karma* is at last transformed.
