## Abstract
Anglophone readers meet the relation between Chinese buddha-recollection (*niàn-fó* 念佛) and Chan (禪) almost entirely through the "Zen vs Pure Land" binary consolidated by Suzuki Daisetsu (1870–1966) in his mid-twentieth-century English writings — Chan as self-power, sudden awakening, anti-image; Pure Land as other-power, name-invocation, ritual devotion. That binary is a twentieth-century Japanese sectarian projection carried into English, not a description of the Tang–Song Chinese scene. Building on P20's documentation of Tanluan's (曇鸞, 476–542) invention of the *self-power/other-power* vocabulary and the anchoring of the *fánfū* 凡夫 as the Pure Land soteriological subject, this paper traces the Chan tradition's three-layer response to that same vocabulary and subject. The first response is Daoxin's (*Dàoxìn* 道信, 580–651): in his *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* (*Rù-dào ānxīn yào fāngbiàn fǎmén* 入道安心要方便法門), preserved at T85n2837, Daoxin takes the *eka-vyūha-samādhi* (*yī-xíng sānmèi* 一行三昧, one-practice samādhi) passage from the *Mañjuśrī-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra* (T0232, the P19 lodestone) as his charter and reads "recollection of one buddha" as "recollection of one mind," completing the semantic turn in the fourteen characters 「念佛即是念心，求心即是求佛」 — *recollecting the buddha is recollecting the mind, seeking the mind is seeking the buddha*. The second is Huineng's (*Huìnéng* 慧能, 638–713) direct handling, in the *Inquiry* chapter of the *Platform Sūtra* (T48n2008), of a lay official's question about name-invocation for rebirth in the West: the four Pure Land names — Guanyin, Mahāsthāmaprāpta, Śākyamuni, Amitābha — are *preserved* (not abolished), but re-anchored to inward virtues in the hinge formula 「平直即彌陀」 (*the upright-and-direct is Amitābha*). The third is Zongmi's (*Zōngmì* 宗密, 780–841) absorption through gradient: in the *Sub-commentary on the Practices and Vows Chapter Commentary* (*Huáyán jīng xíngyuàn pǐn shū chāo* 華嚴經行願品疏鈔, X05n0229), Zongmi builds a four-kinds-of-buddha-recollection typology (name-invocation 稱名 / image-contemplation 觀像 / visualization 觀想 / reality-contemplation 實相) arranged as an insight-depth ladder, and thereby replaces Tanluan–Daochuo's *self-power/other-power* axis with a gradient of root-capacity and view-depth — accommodating name-invocation as the legitimate entry-stage. All three responses share one scriptural charter (T0232) and one soteriological subject (the *fánfū*) with P20, but move that shared material in the opposite direction. The paper stages Bernard Faure, John McRae, and Robert Sharf as structural interlocutors — handling the rhetoric/institution distinction, Chan's plurality, and chronological hygiene respectively. §V supplies three middle-way corrections centered on the anti-caricature thesis of *redirection, not rejection* (再定位而非廢除), documented through the *niàn-fó* office in the *Chǐxiū Bǎizhàng qīngguī* (敕修百丈清規, T2025), Hongren's (*Hóngrěn* 弘忍, 601–674) value-hierarchical "surpasses" grammar in the *Treatise on the Supreme Vehicle* (*Zuìshàngshèng lùn* 最上乘論, T2011), and Daoxin's own acknowledgment that "the buddha directs dull-faculty beings toward the West." The earliest attested fixed-string occurrence of 「念佛是誰」 (*who is recollecting the buddha?*) is Lianchi Zhuhong's (*Liánchí Zhūhóng* 蓮池袾宏, 1535–1615) 1599 preface to Gaofeng's Discourse Record, a matter belonging to P24's late-Ming synthesis; the present paper's §3.4 sketches only the Song–Yuan arc (Dahui's seed-citation, Zhongfeng's *who*-pivot installation, Gaofeng's non-engagement with *niàn-fó*) with forward hand-off to P24. Part VI's structural symmetry lies precisely here: P20 records the other-power stream reading T0232's "binding the mind to one buddha, solely invoking the name" as warrant for turning toward the other; P21 records the Chan stream reading the *same* T0232 passage's "dharmadhātu of a single mark, binding to the dharmadhātu" as warrant for turning toward the mind. Same words, same passage, two directions — this is structural isomorphism *within* the Chinese tradition, not a retrospective projection of later sectarian opposition.

## I. Problem-Statement

When Anglophone Buddhist readers first meet the relation between Chan (*Chán* 禪) and Pure Land (*jìngtǔ* 淨土), most already carry a ready-made frame: Chan is self-power (*jiriki* 自力), sudden, iconoclastic, anti-image; Pure Land is other-power (*tariki* 他力), name-invocating, faith-based, ritually dense. The two are described as one of the sharpest internal fault-lines in East Asian Buddhism. The origin of this frame is not Chinese but Japanese — more precisely, it is the mid-twentieth-century product of Suzuki Daisetsu's (鈴木大拙, 1870–1966) English mediation of Japanese Kamakura-period Buddhism. Suzuki's books on "Zen" shaped the Buddhist imagination of an entire generation of English readers; Shinran's (親鸞, 1173–1263) Jōdo Shinshū (*Jōdo Shinshū* 淨土真宗) other-power doctrine reached Western audiences through scholars of the same generation, presented in parallel rhetoric. Once the Suzuki-era English mediation was complete, Anglophone readers reading back into Tang–Song Chinese masters and patriarchs would regularly, and unconsciously, read the Kamakura-period sectarian positions *back into* seventh- and eighth-century Chinese ground — in the same way that Tanluan and Daochuo, as P20 documented, get read through the Shinshū frame as immature precursors of Shinran. The reception of Chan suffers the same retrojection: Daoxin (580–651), Hongren (601–674), Huineng (638–713), and Zongmi (780–841) are portrayed as *already holding* a Kamakura-style Linji iconoclasm and, on the strength of that iconoclasm, as rejecting Pure Land other-power soteriology.

Bernard Faure, in *The Rhetoric of Immediacy* (1991) and *Chan Insights and Oversights* (1993), argued that Chan's self-presentation — "direct pointing at the mind, no reliance on scripture, a separate transmission outside the teachings" — is a *rhetorical construction*, not an institutional description. John McRae, in *Seeing Through Zen* (2003), stated his second methodological rule more bluntly still — "lineage assertions are as wrong as they are strong" — and argued that there is no single "Chan" at all; Chan is a plurality of local, sub-lineage, and period-specific formations. The present paper stands at the intersection of both warnings. Once one stops projecting the Suzuki-era frame, a different question becomes visible: when Tang–Song Chinese Chan masters meet P20's other-power thesis — Tanluan's invention of the *self-power/other-power* binary at the opening of the *Wangsheng lun zhu* (*Wǎngshēng lùn zhù* 往生論註, T1819), Daochuo's anchoring of the *fánfū* in an empirical *mofǎ* (末法) time frame grounded in the 574 North Zhou Emperor Wu persecution in the *Anle Ji* (*Ānlè Jí* 安樂集, T1958) — *what do they actually do?*

This paper's answer unfolds in three layers: the Chan response is not rejection but *redirection*. First, Daoxin, in the *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* (preserved in Jìngjué's ca.-716 *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkāvatāra*, T85n2837), opens his chapter by naming two co-foundations: 「我此法要，依《楞伽》諸佛心第一，又依《文殊說般若》一行三昧」 (*the essential methodology I teach rests on the Laṅkāvatāra's "the mind of all buddhas is foremost" and on the Mañjuśrī-prajñā one-practice samādhi*; translation mine). The *eka-vyūha-samādhi* passage of T0232 — originally a Mañjuśrī-devotion prajñāpāramitā samādhi (the principal subject of P19) — is thereby inscribed as formal methodological program of the East Mountain Teaching's lineage. A few lines later, Daoxin completes the inward turn: 「念佛即是念心，求心即是求佛」 (*recollecting the buddha is recollecting the mind, seeking the mind is seeking the buddha*; translation mine). The *act* of *niàn-fó* is preserved; the *object* of the recollection shifts from an other-buddha's appearance to the mind that recollects. Second, Huineng in *Platform Sūtra* *Inquiry* chapter 3 (T48n2008; Yuan-dynasty Zongbao recension) meets directly the question of the Shaozhou prefect Wei Qu — "monks and laity are everywhere reciting Amitābha, aspiring to be reborn in the West" — not by abolishing name-invocation but by a *reductio* ("if those in the East commit evil and recite the buddha-name to be reborn in the West, those in the West who commit evil will recite the buddha-name to be reborn *where?*") aimed at the directional fixation of *aspiring-east, aspiring-west*; the move completes in the inward re-anchoring of the four names — 「慈悲即是觀音，喜捨名為勢至，能淨即釋迦，平直即彌陀」 (*compassion is Guanyin, giving-and-renunciation is Mahāsthāmaprāpta, purifying-capacity is Śākyamuni, the upright-and-direct is Amitābha*; translation mine). Third, Zongmi in his sub-commentary on Chengguan's (澄觀, 738–839) *Separate Commentary on the Practices and Vows Chapter* (X05n0229) builds a four-stage gradient — name-invocation, image-contemplation, visualization, reality-contemplation — in which name-invocation is the *legitimate entry stage* and reality-contemplation the apex. The gradient is insight-depth; it *replaces* Tanluan–Daochuo's self-power/other-power axis with an orthogonal one. That Zongmi, across his full corpus (T48n2015 + X05n0229), cites Tanluan, Daochuo, and Shandao *zero times* and never uses the *self-power/other-power* lexicon is not coincidence.

All three responses share one scriptural charter (T0232) and one soteriological subject (the *fánfū*). P20 already displayed Tanluan's 劣夫跨驢 (*liè-fū kuà-lǚ*, cripple-rider-on-donkey) and Daochuo's 置草引牛 (*zhì-cǎo yǐn-niú*, tethering-ox-to-grass) anchoring the *fánfū* at the receiving end of other-power. P21 records the Chan masters operating under the same admission of the *fánfū* — Huineng's "the foolish and the ordinary do not recognize their own nature, do not know the Pure Land within the body, and so aspire east and aspire west" still takes the *foolish-and-ordinary* as description, not as object of rejection — while reversing the direction of the recollection's object. The *same* T0232 under Tanluan's reading grounds name-invoking other-power ("binding the mind to one buddha, solely invoking the name"); under Daoxin's and Zongmi's readings, the same text's "dharmadhātu of a single mark, binding to the dharmadhātu" grounds recollection-of-mind and reality-contemplation. Same words, same passage, two directions — this is structural isomorphism *within* the Chinese tradition, not post-hoc projection of two opposed sects.

Yet stopping here, without a middle-way correction, would mean falling into the rhetorical trap of Chan texts themselves. The tonal register of Chan passages easily leads an untrained reader to conclude that "Chan abolished *niàn-fó*." In fact Chan never abolished *niàn-fó* institutionally. The *Chǐxiū Bǎizhàng qīngguī* (敕修百丈清規, T48n2025), fascicle five, installs a formal *niàn-fó* office (*niàn-fó zhí* 念佛職) as standard monastic position; the liturgy for sending off a deceased monk uses *niàn-fó* as its vehicle; the weinuo (維那, rector) leads *niàn-fó* at mealtimes with joined palms; before-the-hall recitation prescribes a single *niàn-fó* call as instituted ritual. Hongren's *Treatise on the Supreme Vehicle* (T48n2011) states 「守本真心勝念他佛」 (*guarding one's own original true mind surpasses recollecting the other-buddha*; translation mine) — "surpasses" is a value-comparison, not a prohibition; outward *niàn-fó* is placed *lower*, not *abolished*. Daoxin himself says: 「佛為鈍根眾生令向西方；不為利根人說也」 (*the buddha directs dull-faculty beings toward the West; this teaching is not spoken for sharp-faculty beings*; translation mine) — a direct acknowledgment of the legitimate soteriological function of the Western Pure Land for those of dull faculty. The three witnesses jointly show: redirection is not abolition. §V will develop the full correction on these three load-bearing evidences.

The paper proceeds as follows. §II grounds seven primary texts — T85n2837 (Daoxin) + T48n2011 (Hongren) + T48n2008 (*Platform Sūtra*) + X05n0229 (Zongmi's sub-commentary) + T48n2015 (Zongmi's *Chan Prolegomenon*) + T47n1998A (Dahui) + X70n1402 (Zhongfeng) + T48n2025 (*Baizhang qīngguī*) — and handles stratification, document status, and recension warnings for each. §III mounts the core argument in four parts: 3.1 Daoxin's absorption of *yī-xíng sānmèi* and the inward turn of the object; 3.2 the *Platform Sūtra* *Inquiry* chapter's redirection of outward *niàn-fó*; 3.3 Zongmi's four-kinds gradient and the axis-substitution; 3.4 the Song–Yuan historical arc from Dahui (1089–1163) to Lianchi (1535–1615), strictly bounded at Lianchi's 1599 earliest attestation, with late-Ming confluence reserved for P24. §IV engages Faure, McRae, and Sharf by a concede-and-push-back structure. §V delivers three middle-way corrections — ❌ Chan did not abolish *niàn-fó*, ❌ self-nature Amitābha is not subjective idealism, ⚠️ 「念佛是誰」 is not Tang-era Chan's own technique — establishing the guardrails for *redirection-not-rejection*. §VI concludes and hands off to P22 on Shandao's institutionalization. This paper is Part VI's second of five: P20 grounds the other-power stream; P21 grounds the Chan interpretive counter-thesis; P22–P24 will unfold the institutional, methodological, and confluence developments on this symmetrical frame.

## II. Textual Grounds

This section lays the textual ground of the argument. The seven primary texts are treated in the order of their structural role in the argument, not strictly in chronology: first the Daoxin–Hongren East Mountain core (T2837 and T2011); next Huineng's *Platform Sūtra* (T2008); then Zongmi's four-kinds locus (X0229) and his Chan–teaching meta-framework (T2015); last the §3.4 materials of Dahui (T1998A) and Zhongfeng (X70n1402), plus the institutional witness of the *Baizhang qīngguī* (T2025). What each subsection aims to display is what is — and is not — present at that textual stage; a philological genealogy of ideas rather than a biography of patriarchs. §III's argument will rest on the joints and gaps made visible here.

Throughout this paper, X-series references are prefixed "Wànxù cáng (卍續藏)" on first use to distinguish them from the Taishō; subsequent references take the short form (e.g., X05n0229, 0280c05). Taishō references follow the standard format T48n2008, 0352a21.

### 2.1 T85n2837: The Daoxin Chapter of the *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkāvatāra* — *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind*

The *Léngqié shīzī jì* was compiled by the Tang monk Jìngjué (淨覺, ca. 683–750+) around 711 CE (Jǐngyún 景雲 2) or shortly thereafter, based on the now-lost *Léngqié rénfǎ zhì* (楞伽人法志) of his teacher Xuánzé (玄賾, ?–before 716), and presents an eight-generation lineage (Guṇabhadra → Bodhidharma → Huike → Sengcan → Daoxin → Hongren → Shenxiu, Xuanze, Lao'an). It survives today as a reassembly of Dunhuang manuscript fragments (the Taishō uses Stein S.2054 as its base), collected at Taishō vol. 85, No. 2837. The **Daoxin chapter** is the longest section of the work (T85.1286c19–1289b10); it contains the chapter-head statement, the near-complete transcription of the *yī-xíng sānmèi* passage, the pivot sentence "recollecting the buddha is recollecting the mind," the seated-meditation protocol, the five-gate outline, and the concrete method of *shǒu-yī bù-yí*.[^1]

Two layers of document-status must be declared explicitly in the main text. First, T2837 is not a Daoxin autograph; it is Jìngjué's compilation of the teachings Xuanze recorded of Daoxin. When citing, this paper uses wording such as "the teachings of Daoxin as preserved in T2837" and "as recorded in the Daoxin chapter of the *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkāvatāra*," avoiding any implication of autograph. Second, the Daoxin chapter itself is internally stratified. The *yī-xíng sānmèi* transcription (§2 at T85.1286c24–1287a07) and the pivot paragraph 「念佛即是念心，求心即是求佛」 (§3 at T85.1287a07–18) are regarded by scholarship (Yanagida Seizan, McRae, Faure) as reliable East Mountain Teaching core material; the "neither recollecting the buddha nor grasping the mind nor watching the mind" spontaneity passage (T85.1287b17–23), though its central vocabulary is consistent across manuscripts, uses *zhí rèn yùn* (直任運, direct spontaneous functioning) language that is close to the later Hongzhou–Mazu "ordinary mind is the Way" stratum; injection of later idiom during Jìngjué's transcription cannot be ruled out. §3.1 takes the first two passages as load-bearing evidence; §V cites the spontaneity passage with a stratification warning attached.

### 2.2 T48n2011: Hongren's *Treatise on the Supreme Vehicle*

The *Zuìshàngshèng lùn* (also known as *Xiūxīn yàolùn* 修心要論), traditionally attributed to Hongren, is collected at Taishō vol. 48, No. 2011. Its authorship is traditional; manuscript witnesses stem from the Song; scholarship (Yanagida, McRae) regards it as an East Mountain Teaching compilation reflecting Hongren's teaching or his direct disciples' transmission, not necessarily a Hongren autograph. This paper, on first citation, attaches the note "attributed to Hongren."

The core passage is T48.0377b17–21: 「問曰：何名自心勝念彼佛？答曰：常念彼佛不免生死，守我本心則到彼岸。《金剛經》云：若以色見我，以音聲求我，是人行邪道不能見如來。故云：守本真心勝念他佛。」 (*"Question: Why is it said that the mind of oneself surpasses recollecting the other-buddha? Answer: Constantly recollecting that buddha does not exempt one from birth-and-death; guarding my original mind is what reaches the other shore. The Vajracchedikā says: 'One who seeks me through form and sound walks a wrong path and cannot see the Tathāgata.' Therefore it is said: guarding the original true mind surpasses recollecting the other-buddha."*; translation mine.) This is §3.1's clearest value-hierarchical formulation — "surpasses" is comparison, not prohibition; the anti-image proof-text from the *Vajracchedikā* argues that mind-guarding is *above* name-invocation, but issues no ban against invocation itself. Hongren elsewhere (T48.0377a21) writes 「守自本心乃勝念十方諸佛」 — the "surpasses" grammar is iterated throughout the text, forming a consistent gradient-schema. §V uses this passage as the second witness for the ❌ "Chan abolished *niàn-fó*" correction.

### 2.3 T48n2008: Huineng's *Platform Sūtra*

The *Liùzǔ dàshī fǎbǎo tánjīng* (六祖大師法寶壇經) as received is the one-fascicle Yuan edition compiled by the monk Zongbao. The Dunhuang recension (T48n2007) is briefer, with a shorter *Inquiry* chapter; the "self-nature Amitābha" line develops more fully in the Zongbao recension. This paper cites primarily the Zongbao recension; where §3.2 discussion touches recensional variance, footnotes register it.[^2]

The *Inquiry* chapter 3 (T48.0352a07–c07) is the entire sūtra's only direct treatment of *niàn-fó* practitioners. Its structure: Wei Qu's question about the efficacy of name-invocation for Western rebirth → Huineng's "if speaking by form" faculty-sorting → the "Easterners commit evil" *reductio* → the re-cartography of body-and-mind (compassion = Guanyin, giving = Mahāsthāmaprāpta, purifying = Śākyamuni, upright = Amitābha) → the *Formless Verses* closure. §3.2 takes this chapter as the central locus; §3.3 and §V attach supplementary citations from the *Repentance* and *Concentration-and-Wisdom* chapters.

**Two philological cautions** must be stated here. First, the four-character string 「自性彌陀」 (*self-nature Amitābha*) does *not* appear anywhere in the *Platform Sūtra*; a full-text search of the Zongbao recension yields no hits for this sequence. The closest strings are 「平直即彌陀」 (T48.0352b13), 「自心是佛」 (T48.0362a02), and 「真如自性是真佛」 (T48.0362a08).[^15] "Self-nature Amitābha" is a later crystallization — chiefly through Yongming Yanshou's (904–975) *Wanshan tonggui ji* (*Wànshàn tóngguī jí* 萬善同歸集) and Yunqi Zhuhong's *Amitābha Sūtra Commentary* (*Ēmítuó jīng shūchāo* 阿彌陀經疏鈔) with its phenomenal-vs-principial (*shì-chí / lǐ-chí* 事持／理持) distinction, all of which belong to P24. When citing the *Platform Sūtra* directly, this paper holds strictly to the actual attested wording. Second, the four-character string 「即心即佛」 (*the mind itself is the buddha*) belongs to Mazu Daoyi (馬祖道一, 709–788), attested at *Jingde Record of the Transmission of the Lamp* (*Jǐngdé chuándēng lù* 景德傳燈錄, T51n2076) fascicle six, T51.0246a04 ("outside the mind there is no other buddha; outside the buddha there is no other mind") and T51.0246a21 (the "mind-is-buddha / not-mind-not-buddha" pairing) — *not* to Huineng. The *Platform Sūtra* uses 「自心是佛」 and "if self-nature is awakened, the living being is the buddha." §3.2 does not attribute 「即心即佛」 to Huineng.

### 2.4 X05n0229: *Sub-commentary on the Huayan Practices and Vows Chapter Commentary*

The *Huáyán jīng xíngyuàn pǐn bié-xíng shū chāo* (六 fascicles; Wànxù cáng X05n0229) is the combined "Chengguan's Separate Commentary + Zongmi's Sub-commentary." Chengguan (澄觀, 738–839) was the fourth patriarch of Huayan; the *Separate Commentary* is his independent exegesis of the *Samantabhadra Practices and Vows Chapter* (T0293) — the closing fascicle of the Forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* translated by Prajñā. Zongmi was concurrently fifth patriarch of Huayan and fifth patriarch of the Heze lineage of Chan; he composed a sub-commentary on Chengguan's *Separate Commentary*.

The *four kinds of buddha-recollection* taxonomy is located in Zongmi's sub-commentary layer at **X05n0229, 0280c05–0281a16**, embedded in Zongmi's expansion of Chengguan's "recollecting the buddhas of the ten directions" gloss (the ninth of the ten counter-flow mind-states in the *Samantabhadra* confession-of-obstructions passage).[^3] A forward philological correction must be issued here: early drafts of this volume's plan listed "T2015 Zongmi's four-kinds-of-*niàn-fó*" — that attribution is philologically incorrect. T48n2015, the *Chányuán zhūquánjí dūxù* (*Chan Prolegomenon*), contains Zongmi's five-tier dhyāna classification (T48.0399b07–19), his three-lineages-three-teachings doxography (T48.0402b), and the sudden-awakening-with-gradual-cultivation framework, together with one brief bridging clause at T48.0399b10 — 「至於念佛求生淨土，亦須修十六觀禪，及念佛三昧、般舟三昧」 (*as for recollecting the buddha to seek rebirth in the Pure Land, one must also cultivate the sixteen-contemplation dhyāna, as well as the buddha-recollection samādhi and the pratyutpanna samādhi*; translation mine) — **but contains no four-kinds typology at all**. The canonical locus of the four-kinds is X05n0229, not T2015. §3.3 cites X05n0229 as primary locus and T2015 as secondary witness for the Chan–teaching meta-framework.[^4]

### 2.5 T48n2015: Zongmi's *Chan Prolegomenon*

The *Chányuán zhūquánjí dūxù* (*Chan Prolegomenon*), two fascicles, Taishō vol. 48, No. 2015, is Zongmi's general preface to his (now lost) *Chányuán zhūquánjí*. It survives in Song recensions. Two core passages are needed here for §3.3: first, T48.0399b07–19 sets out the five-tier dhyāna classification (non-Buddhist / ordinary being / Hīnayāna / Mahāyāna / Supreme — this last equated with pure tathāgata dhyāna, with *yī-xíng sānmèi*, with *zhēnrú sānmèi*, and with Bodhidharma dhyāna); second, T48.0407c07–11 articulates the "interpenetration of Pure and impure lands" in Huayan ten-mystery-gates (*shí-xuán mén* 十玄門) terms. The first passage establishes Zongmi's consistent use of the *root-capacity gradient* as his organizing doxographic axis — the same axis running through both the four kinds of buddha-recollection and the five-tier dhyāna, two taxonomies applying one interpretive approach across two domains. The second is §V's load-bearing citation for the ❌ "self-nature Amitābha = subjective idealism" correction: Zongmi does not abolish the Pure Land cosmologically; he presents pure-and-impure as mutually interpenetrating realities under the Huayan ten-mystery-gate schema.[^5]

### 2.6 T47n1998A: *Discourse Record of Chan Master Dahui Pujue*

The *Dàhuì Pǔjué chánshī yǔlù* (thirty fascicles, Taishō vol. 47, No. 1998A) is the compiled discourse record of Dahui Zonggao (大慧宗杲, 1089–1163), assembled by his disciples after his death. Dahui is the decisive figure of the Linji Yangqi sub-lineage and the systematizer of *kànhuà* Chan. Two core passages are cited in §3.4: first, T47.0824a06–10, Dahui's sermon citing the Zhaozhou (從諗, 778–897) *gōng'àn* "who is the one recollecting?" — a structural precursor to the 「念佛是誰」 string, but not a *huàtóu* assigned by Dahui; second, T47.0899a13–17, the instructions to Lay Adherent Miaozheng on contemplating Zhaozhou's "No" — the paradigm of Dahui's *kànhuà* mechanism, cited in §3.4 to show that Dahui's critical-phrase technique was *not* applied specifically to *niàn-fó* and that 「念佛是誰」 as fixed expression is not Dahui's creation.

### 2.7 X70n1402: *Extensive Discourse Record of Master Zhongfeng of Tianmu*

The *Tiānmù Zhōngfēng héshang guǎnglù* (thirty fascicles, Wànxù cáng X70n1402) is the collected discourses, verses, sermons, and letters of Zhongfeng Mingben (1263–1323). Zhongfeng was the disciple of Gaofeng Yuanmiao (高峰原妙, 1238–1295) and the decisive Yuan-dynasty figure of combined Chan and Pure Land practice. §3.4 cites four core passages: X70.0718c22–24's "discard the very thought of reciting itself" (*hé gè niàn-tóu dōu yáng què* 和箇念頭都颺却); X70.0719b15–17's four *who*-questions posed to the Goryeo king ("*before parents were born, who am I? When a breath does not come, who am I?*"); X70.0747a24–b01's Yuan-era unity thesis "*Amitābha dwells in the West, the patriarch came from the West — recollecting the buddha and investigating Chan share one body-and-pattern*"; and X70.0718b13–c16's *Exhortation to Recollect Amitābha* with its "recollecting-the-buddha is recollecting-the-mind, recollecting-the-mind is recollecting-the-buddha" mutual-interpenetration passage. Of these, the second evidences the *who*-pivot as a mature Chan technical term by Yuan times, and the third is the clearest Yuan-era textual statement of combined Chan–Pure Land practice.

A contrastive note is essential here. Zhongfeng's master Gaofeng Yuanmiao's *Chan Essentials* (*Chányào* 禪要, Wànxù cáng X70n1401) and his own extant writings (X70n1400) — excluding Lianchi's 1599 preface — engage the topic of *niàn-fó* **zero times**. Gaofeng belongs to a strictly *kànhuà* lineage; the popular later attribution of 「念佛是誰」 to Gaofeng is philologically traceable not to Gaofeng but to Lianchi Zhuhong's 1599 CE (Wanli 27) preface to Gaofeng's *Discourse Record* (X70.0677a12–22). This is a significant philological correction in the history of Chinese Chan attributions, and both §3.4 and §V will treat it.

### 2.8 T48n2025: *Chǐxiū Bǎizhàng qīngguī*

The *Chǐxiū Bǎizhàng qīngguī* (*Imperial Revision of Baizhang's Pure Rules*, eight fascicles, Taishō vol. 48, No. 2025) is the Chan monastic code revised by Dōngyáng Déhuī under imperial commission from the Yuan Emperor Shundi around 1341 (Zhizheng era). Its ancestor was the now-lost *Gu-qingguī* (古清規) attributed to Baizhang Huaihai (720–814), but the Yuan revision reflects Yuan-era institutional elaborations and liturgical norms; the *niàn-fó* office (T48.1132a04) and the deceased-monk *niàn-fó* liturgies belong to the Yuan stratum.[^6]

This Yuan-stratification caveat matters for §V's ❌ "Chan abolished *niàn-fó*" correction: the attestation period of the formal *niàn-fó* office is Yuan, not Tang. But Yuan-era Chan is still Chan, and an institutional *niàn-fó* office operating *within* Yuan-era Chan is enough to establish that Chan *as an institution* never abolished *niàn-fó* — whether the Tang period already had equivalent institutionalization is a separate question, but its answer does not affect the force of this correction. §V uses this evidence with the stratification caveat declared openly.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Daoxin's Absorption of *Yī-xíng Sānmèi*: From Recollecting One Buddha to Recollecting One Mind

The Daoxin chapter opens by laying a dual-scripture foundation for his methodology: 「我此法要，依《楞伽》諸佛心第一，又依《文殊說般若》一行三昧，即念佛心是佛，妄念是凡夫」 (*the essential methodology I teach rests on the Laṅkāvatāra's "the mind of all buddhas is foremost" and on the Mañjuśrī-prajñā one-practice samādhi: that the mind recollecting the buddha is the buddha, while deluded thought is the ordinary being*; translation mine).[^7] The philological significance of this opening in the history of Chinese Chan is substantial: Daoxin is the first to inscribe the *eka-vyūha-samādhi* passage of the *Mañjuśrī-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra* (T0232) explicitly into a Chan lineage's methodological program. The preceding Guṇabhadra–Bodhidharma–Huike–Sengcan Laṅkāvatāra mind-lineage relied primarily on the *Laṅkāvatāra* (T0670) and made no recorded use of T0232; Daoxin welds the two lines together — adding the methodological samādhi-apparatus of the Mañjuśrī-prajñā onto the mind-nature substrate of the *Laṅkāvatāra*. Immediately following this dual-scripture declaration, Daoxin inserts a near-complete transcription of the *eka-vyūha-samādhi* chapter of T0232 (T85.1286c24–1287a07), preserving the original sūtra's entire outward-directed, name-invoking, directionally-fixed vocabulary — "*binding the mind to one buddha, solely invoking the name, facing the direction of that buddha, keeping the body upright, continuing moment-to-moment recollection of that one buddha*" — without alteration.[^8]

After this near-complete transcription, Daoxin enters his own exegetical voice, and here the inward turn of the object is completed:

> 「即念佛心名無所念。離心無別有佛，離佛無別有心。念佛即是念心，求心即是求佛。所以者何？識無形，佛無形，佛無相貌。若也知此道理，即是安心。常憶念佛，攀緣不起，則泯然無相，平等不二。」
>
> The very mind that recollects the buddha is named "no-object-of-recollection." Apart from mind there is no separate buddha; apart from buddha there is no separate mind. Recollecting the buddha is recollecting the mind; seeking the mind is seeking the buddha. Why is this so? Consciousness has no form, the buddha has no form, the buddha has no appearance. If one knows this principle, this is pacifying the mind. With constant recollection of the buddha, grasping does not arise; then one vanishes into formlessness, equal and non-dual. (translation mine.)[^9]

This is the single clearest textual support for this paper's thesis. The fourteen-character line 「念佛即是念心，求心即是求佛」 completes a semantic reversal: the lexical shell of T0232's "binding the mind to one buddha, solely invoking the name" has just been preserved intact in the preceding paragraph, and this paragraph now re-reads "binding the mind to one" as *binding-the-mind-itself*, and "seeking the buddha" as *seeking-the-mind* — with the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra*'s (T0223) apophatic proof-text 「無所念者，是名念佛」 (*the one with no object of recollection is properly called "recollecting the buddha"*; translation mine) as warrant. Daoxin's move is not word-replacement — he does not rewrite T0232; it is *gloss-superposition* — he sets a new interpretive layer alongside T0232. The sūtra's lexicon is fully preserved; the referent silently rotates.

This paragraph deserves direct mirror-comparison with P20's citation of Tanluan at T40.827a13 — 「念無礙光如來，心心相續，無他想間雜」 (*recollecting the Tathāgata of Unimpeded Light, mind continuing upon mind, no other thought intermixing*; translation mine). Both passages describe the continuity of *niàn-fó* with "mind continuing upon mind," but the two direct the continuity in exactly opposite directions. Tanluan's *mind-upon-mind* grows more solidified as it continues: the recollected Tathāgata of Unimpeded Light becomes sharper, more the object on which the practitioner's attention crystallizes. Daoxin's *mind-upon-mind* — "the mind recollecting the buddha is continuous mind upon mind; suddenly it becomes clear and quiet, with nothing further as object of recollection" — grows more *dissolved* as it continues: the recollected finally vanishes into formlessness, equal and non-dual. Same words, two directions. The textual mirror here is Part VI's most refined evidence of the two-streams structural symmetry: Chinese *niàn-fó* did not develop within two sealed traditions on disjoint vocabularies; it bifurcated *on the same vocabulary*.

Daoxin does not stop at the pivot. The chapter's middle section (T85.1288a15–23) lays out the *Five Gates outline*: (1) knowing the substance of mind, (2) knowing the function of mind, (3) constant awareness without pause, (4) constant contemplation of body as empty and still, (5) *guarding-the-one-unmoving*.[^10] The first four gates lean on view (epistemic verbs); the fifth is the method-core. This method-term is attributed by Daoxin explicitly to Fu Dashi (*Fù Xī* 傅翕, 497–569), but integrating it into a five-gate architecture is Daoxin's or the East Mountain Teaching's own contribution. The concrete protocol of *guarding-the-one-unmoving* is developed at T85.1288b16–20: "*fix the eye with pure seeing, settle the attention on one object; day and night, whenever, concentrate with one-pointed unwavering; if the mind rushes off, quickly bring it back; like tying a bird's foot with a string — if it tries to fly, pull it back; watching all day without cease, it vanishes of itself into composure*" (translation mine).

The relation of this technique to T0232's "binding the mind to one buddha, solely invoking the name" is philologically rich. Both are techniques of *sustained focus*; but what is bound differs decisively. T0232's "one" is a specific *one-buddha* (direct *buddhānusmṛti* object); Daoxin's "one" is an abstract *one-object / one-mind* — left unnamed, which can be the buddha, or the mind, or any object of attention. This abstraction opens the technical passageway from *niàn-fó* to *mind-watching* — what is bound finally dissolves into the binding itself, and the energy of binding *is* samādhi. Daoxin does not abolish T0232's shell; he abstracts the content of the shell so that later practitioners can execute the same technique even in the absence of a specified buddha-name.

This absorption is not Daoxin's private view. In the Shenxiu chapter near the end of T2837, Empress Wu Zetian summons Shenxiu (神秀, 606–706) to ask his teaching's foundation. Shenxiu answers: "*I inherit the East Mountain Teaching of Qizhou*." The Empress asks which scripture he relies on. Shenxiu answers: "*I rely on the Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra's one-practice samādhi*." The Empress concludes: "*For cultivation of the Way, nothing surpasses the East Mountain Teaching*" (translation mine).[^11] This is an *early-eighth-century imperial confirmation*: the East Mountain Teaching's own declared scriptural foundation is T0232, publicly avowed at the most institutionally visible moment of the Tang dynasty. This internal self-avowal is not a retrospective Song-era construction by Chan historiographers (Fayan Wenyi, Yuanwu Keqin); it is spoken in their own voices by Daoxin → Hongren → Shenxiu.

Hongren's *Treatise on the Supreme Vehicle* inherits Daoxin's absorption and crystallizes it further: Daoxin's five gates are collapsed in Hongren's hand into a single methodological term, "guarding the mind" (*shǒu-xīn* 守心). The core passage, cited above in §2.2, is T48.0377b17–21: "*Question: Why is it said that the mind of oneself surpasses recollecting the other-buddha? Answer: Constantly recollecting that buddha does not exempt one from birth-and-death; guarding my original mind is what reaches the other shore.*" This is the clearest value-hierarchical formulation for this paper's thesis. "Surpasses" is comparison, not prohibition; Hongren argues *mind-guarding over name-invocation* by appeal to the *Vajracchedikā*'s "one who seeks me through form and sound walks a wrong path" anti-image proof-text — but he issues no ban against name-invocation itself. Elsewhere in the same treatise Hongren writes 「守自本心乃勝念十方諸佛」 (*guarding one's own original mind surpasses recollecting the buddhas of the ten directions*; translation mine). The iterated "surpasses" forms a consistent gradient logic: outward *niàn-fó* is not abolished; it is placed *beneath* the guarding of one's original true mind. This is the East Mountain Teaching's completed form of the Chan inward-turn.

### 3.2 The *Platform Sūtra* *Inquiry* Chapter: Redirection of Outward *Niàn-fó*

Huineng's *Inquiry* chapter 3 in the *Platform Sūtra* (T48.0352a07–c07) is the only direct treatment of *niàn-fó* practitioners in the whole sūtra. The questioner is Wei Qu (韋璩), prefect of Shaozhou, a layman in office: "*Your disciple commonly sees monks and laity reciting Amitābha, aspiring to be reborn in the West. Master, please explain whether they can be reborn there, so as to resolve this doubt*" (translation mine).[^12] The phrasing "commonly sees monks and laity" is first-hand evidence that by the late seventh or early eighth century, *niàn-fó* was already a widespread monastic-and-lay practice in Chinese territory. Huineng does not deny the reality of the question, nor does he give a flat yes-or-no answer; he redirects: *how should this practice be understood?*

Huineng's first move is to acknowledge scriptural authority through a faculty-based layering of "far speech" and "near speech": "*The World-Honored One in Śrāvastī spoke of the West to guide beings. The sūtra-text is clear: it is not far from here. If one speaks in terms of form, the distance is 108,000 miles — which is the ten evils and the eight wrong ways within the body, called "far speech." Far speech is spoken for the dull; near speech is spoken for the superior*" (translation mine). The 108,000 miles of spatial distance is deconstructed into the ethical distance of ten evils and eight wrongs. This is the first layer of spatial internalization: the Western Pure Land is not abolished, but re-read as the Pure Land within the mind.

Huineng's second move is the famous *reductio*:

> 「迷人念佛求生於彼，悟人自淨其心。所以佛言：隨其心淨即佛土淨。使君東方人，但心淨即無罪。雖西方人，心不淨亦有愆。**東方人造罪，念佛求生西方。西方人造罪，念佛求生何國？**凡愚不了自性，不識身中淨土，願東願西。悟人在處一般。」
>
> The deluded recollect the buddha seeking rebirth there; the awakened purify their own mind. As the Buddha said: *"When the mind is pure, the buddha-land is pure."* Prefect, for an Easterner, if the mind is pure there is no transgression. Even for a Westerner, if the mind is not pure there is still offense. **If Easterners commit evil and recollect the buddha to be reborn in the West, where do Westerners who commit evil recollect the buddha to be reborn?** The foolish and ordinary do not recognize their self-nature, do not know the Pure Land within the body, and so aspire eastward and aspire westward. The awakened are the same wherever they stand. (translation mine.)[^13]

The sharpness of this *reductio* **is not directed at *niàn-fó* itself**, but at the directional fixation of aspiring-east-and-west. If the Pure Land is read as geographical space, then any *rebirth there* is merely an infinite regress — where do the evil-doing Westerners *niàn-fó* to be reborn next? Huineng's counter-question forces out the structural blind-spot of the geographical reading of Pure Land doctrine. But note: Huineng's phrasing is "*the foolish and ordinary do not recognize their self-nature, do not know the Pure Land within the body, and so aspire east and aspire west*" — he does *not* deny the existence of foolish-and-ordinary beings, nor does he reject name-invocating practitioners. What he criticizes is the *simultaneous invocation and insistence on it as geographical rebirth*.

Huineng's third move is the complete re-cartography of body-and-mind:

> 「世人自色身是城，眼耳鼻舌是門，外有五門，內有意門。心是地，性是王。……佛向性中作，莫向身外求。自性迷即是眾生，自性覺即是佛。慈悲即是觀音，喜捨名為勢至，能淨即釋迦，**平直即彌陀**。」
>
> The body of worldly people is a city; the eyes, ears, nose, and tongue are gates; outside there are five gates, inside there is the gate of mind. The mind is the ground, the nature is the king. … The buddha is made from within the nature; do not seek outside the body. Self-nature deluded — this is the living being; self-nature awakened — this is the buddha. Compassion itself is Guanyin; giving-and-renunciation is called Mahāsthāmaprāpta; purifying-capacity is Śākyamuni; **the upright-and-direct is Amitābha**. (translation mine.)[^14]

This is the *Platform Sūtra*'s most refined treatment of the *niàn-fó* topic: **the names are preserved, their referents are rotated inward**. The four names of the Pure Land saints — Guanyin, Mahāsthāmaprāpta, Śākyamuni, Amitābha — are kept entirely in Huineng's re-cartography; their referents shift from *the buddhas and bodhisattvas of another cosmic region* to *inward virtues*: the act of compassion is what "Guanyin" refers to; the act of giving-and-renunciation is what "Mahāsthāmaprāpta" refers to; the act of purifying-capacity is what "Śākyamuni" refers to; the act of uprightness-and-directness is what "Amitābha" refers to. This is not an erasure of names; it is a **re-anchoring of what the names point to** — from outward other-buddhas to the virtues of one's own mind. It is the most refined textual support for this paper's thesis — *turning the name inward* is *not turning away from the name*.

Two philological clarifications are necessary at this point. First, the four-character string 「自性彌陀」 does *not* occur anywhere in the *Platform Sūtra*; a full-text search of the Zongbao recension yields no hits for this sequence. The closest attested phrasings are the present "the upright-and-direct is Amitābha" (T48.0352b13), the *Repentance* chapter's "to take refuge in one's own mind and in the self-nature is to take refuge in the true buddha" (T48.0354c01–03), and the closing *Self-nature True Buddha Verse* of the *Instructions* chapter, "suchness-self-nature is the true buddha" (T48.0362a08).[^15] "Self-nature Amitābha" is a later generalization — mature as a term chiefly with Yongming Yanshou's *Wanshan tonggui ji* (T48n2017) Chan–Pure Land confluence, Yunqi Zhuhong's *Amitābha Sūtra Commentary* with its phenomenal/principial distinction, and early-Qing confluence writers (all within P24's scope). When citing the *Platform Sūtra* directly, this paper holds strictly to the attested wording; when the term "self-nature Amitābha orientation" appears, it is understood as later reception language, not the *Platform Sūtra*'s own usage. Second, the four-character string 「即心即佛」 belongs to Mazu Daoyi (709–788), attested at *Jingde chuándēng lù* (T51n2076) fascicle six, T51.0246a04 ("outside the mind there is no other buddha; outside the buddha there is no other mind") and T51.0246a21 ("mind-is-buddha / not-mind-not-buddha" pairing). The *Platform Sūtra* uses 「自心是佛」, "if self-nature is awakened, the living being is the buddha," "suchness-self-nature is the true buddha," and similar phrasings, but not "the mind itself is the buddha" as a fixed string. §3.2 does not attribute 「即心即佛」 to Huineng. Both clarifications are declared here as a matter of discipline — not retrojecting late-Ming-era crystallizations into Tang source texts.

Huineng closes with the *Formless Verses*: "*Prefect, if your heart-ground holds nothing unwholesome, the West is not distant from here. If you harbor an unwholesome mind, *niàn-fó*'s rebirth is hard to reach. I now exhort you: first remove the ten evils — thus walking the ten thousand miles; next remove the eight wrongs — thus passing the eight thousand. Moment by moment seeing the nature, constantly practicing uprightness, and one arrives in the snap of a finger, seeing Amitābha. Prefect, simply practice the ten wholesome ways — why aspire further to rebirth? Without breaking the mind of the ten evils, what buddha will come to welcome you? If one awakens the sudden teaching of non-arising, seeing the West is only in an instant*" (translation mine).[^16] The tone of *why aspire further* is strong, but two layers of preparation have already been laid: the 108,000 miles reduced to ten evils and eight wrongs; *moment-to-moment seeing the nature, constantly practicing uprightness* as the condition of arrival. The refinement of this closing lies in handling spatial internalization (outside → inside) and temporal internalization (future → present) in parallel: the Western geography is compressed into the present moment of seeing-the-nature, and the future of the Pure Land is contracted into the now within the sudden teaching of non-arising.

Huineng in the *Concentration-and-Wisdom* chapter (T48.0353a17–22) establishes the theoretical ground for the internalization through his "no-thought as foundation" (*wú-niàn wéi zōng* 無念為宗) doctrine:

> 「無者無二相，無諸塵勞之心。念者念真如本性。真如即是念之體，念即是真如之用。真如自性起念，非眼耳鼻舌能念。……真如自性起念，六根雖有見聞覺知，不染萬境，而真性常自在。」
>
> "No-" means being without the two characteristics, without the mind of defilements-and-toils. "Thought" means recollecting the original suchness-nature. Suchness is the substance of thought; thought is the function of suchness. Suchness-self-nature gives rise to thought — not the eyes, ears, nose, or tongue. … When suchness-self-nature gives rise to thought, though the six faculties have seeing, hearing, knowing, and perceiving, they are not defiled by the ten thousand objects, and the true nature remains constantly free. (translation mine.)[^17]

The importance of this passage is that within the *Platform Sūtra* itself, Huineng supplies an ontological ground for the *subject-predicate structure of niàn-fó*. The true agent of "thought" is not the six faculties (not the ordinary being's senses or consciousness), but *suchness-self-nature*; the substance of "thought" is suchness; thought is the function of suchness. Under this theoretical framework, the activity of "recollecting the buddha" is not the act of an ordinary subject reaching out through the six faculties toward an external object (such as the name Amitābha); it is suchness-self-nature's self-manifestation. This is Chan's theoretical reversal of the grammar of *niàn-fó*: from subject-predicate structure "*ordinary being (subject) → buddha-name (object)*" to the self-manifestation structure "*suchness (subject) → suchness (object)*." The outward form of name-invocation *niàn-fó* may remain unchanged, but the subject and object both are relocated into suchness-self-nature. This ontological reversal is the deepest version of the redirection — it reaches not only spatial and temporal internalization but the very subject-predicate grammar.

### 3.3 Zongmi's Absorption through Gradient: From Name-Invocation to Reality-Contemplation

Zongmi's four-kinds-of-*niàn-fó* taxonomy is located in the *Sub-commentary on the Practices and Vows Chapter Commentary* (X05n0229), in Zongmi's sub-commentary layer at 0280c05–0281a16, embedded in his elaboration of Chengguan's gloss on "recollecting the buddhas of the ten directions." Chengguan's commentary lists "recollecting the buddhas of the ten directions" as the ninth of the ten counter-flow mind-states (*shí-xīn* 十心) of confession-of-obstructions in the *Samantabhadra* chapter; Zongmi's sub-commentary asks at this juncture: *what exactly is the meaning of buddha-recollection?* — and answers with the four-kinds taxonomy.[^18]

Zongmi's framing opening is philologically crucial to the argument:

> 「故須念佛。然念佛一門，修行之要津、攝心之關鍵，因此略明念佛之義。言念者，明記不忘為義，體即是慧。今名念者，即隣近彰名也。」
>
> Therefore one must recollect the buddha. Yet "buddha-recollection" is a ford for cultivation, the hinge of mind-collection; for this reason I shall briefly clarify the meaning of "recollecting the buddha." As for *niàn* (recollection): its meaning is *clearly remembering without forgetting*; its substance is *prajñā*. What is here called *niàn* is named by proximity. (translation mine.)

Two philological levers in this framing are decisive. First, *niàn* (念) is defined in Abhidharma terms as *smṛti* (clear-remembering-without-forgetting), and the substance of *smṛti* is *prajñā*. This definition forecloses in advance the reading "*niàn-fó = oral recitation*": oral recitation is *chēng* (*chēng* 稱, calling-the-name); *niàn* is the mental operation of clearly-remembering-without-forgetting, with *prajñā* as its substantive nature. When Zongmi's subsequent four-kinds schema places "name-invocation *niàn*" as first, he marks "name-invocation" explicitly as *a proximate concept to "niàn"*, not *niàn* itself. Second, Zongmi frames *niàn-fó* under the heading "hinge of mind-collection" (*shè-xīn zhī guānjiàn* 攝心之關鍵) — locating *niàn-fó* as a mind-collection technique, not as a salvific technique. This framing has already pulled *niàn-fó* wholly into the orbit of meditative-prajñā.

Zongmi then unfolds the four kinds. The canonical references can be displayed in tabular form:

| Kind | Core description | Scriptural basis |
|------|------------------|------------------|
| Name-invocation (*chēng-míng niàn* 稱名念) | Binding the mind to one buddha, solely invoking the name, moment-to-moment continuous | *Mañjuśrī-prajñā* (T0232) *yī-xíng sānmèi* chapter |
| Image-contemplation (*guān-xiàng niàn* 觀像念) | Contemplating the sculpted or painted image of the Tathāgata | *Mahāratnakūṭa* (T0310) — the Bodhisattva Mahāvīrya carrying a painted image into the mountains |
| Visualization (*guān-xiǎng niàn* 觀想念) | Contemplating one of the thirty-two marks, or the whole buddha-body in space | *Guānfó sānmèi hǎi jīng* (T0643) white-urna contemplation; *Zuòchán sānmèi jīng* (T0614) buddha-body recollection |
| Reality-contemplation (*shí-xiàng niàn* 實相念) | Contemplating the true self-nature of body and all dharmas | *Mañjuśrī-prajñā* (T0232) "*neither arising nor ceasing, neither coming nor going, neither name nor form*"; *Dà-zhìdù lùn* (T1509) "*not contemplated through physical body or marks*"; *Zhānchá jīng* (T0839); *Avataṃsaka* (T0293) "*all buddha-bodies are but one dharma-body*" |

Zongmi concludes the four-kinds with: 「此之四等，各隨根器，從淺至深，最後為妙。以是濟要，是故略明」 (*these four are arranged each according to root-capacity, from shallow to deep, with the last as the supreme. As these are essential for salvation, I have briefly outlined them*; translation mine).[^19] This concluding sentence carries three structural features that are decisive for this argument.

First, the four-kinds are a *gradient*, not a set of mutually exclusive alternatives. Name-invocation is the legitimate entry-stage and is *not* rejected; reality-contemplation is the apex, but the existence of an apex does not abolish the legitimacy of the lower stages. The logic of the classification is "*each according to root-capacity, from shallow to deep*" — a progressive *upāya* (skillful means) schema, not an absolute truth-claim. The four-kinds gradient is homologous with Zongmi's same-work five-tier dhyāna (T48.0399b07–19) and three-lineages-three-teachings doxography; all are applications of the same interpretive approach — a consistent depth-gradient exegesis — across different domains.

Second, **the same T0232 serves both poles**. Name-invocation's scriptural basis is T0232's "binding the mind to one buddha, solely invoking the name"; reality-contemplation's scriptural basis is T0232's "*dharmadhātu* of a single mark, binding to the *dharmadhātu*" and "neither arising nor ceasing, neither coming nor going, neither name nor form." One sūtra, one technical term (*yī-xíng sānmèi*), serves simultaneously the lowest and highest tier of Zongmi's taxonomy — not by accident, but as structural evidence of *one charter, divergent readings*. Under Tanluan's reading in P20, T0232 grounds other-power name-invocation; under Zongmi's reading in P21, T0232 grounds *name-invocation and reality-contemplation as two poles of one substance, differently placed*. This is Part VI's most precise textual evidence for the two-streams structural symmetry.

Third, Zongmi's **zero citations of Tanluan, Daochuo, and Shandao** across his full surviving corpus is a further reverse-finding. Across T48n2015 (both fascicles) and X05n0229 (all six fascicles), Zongmi cites Tanluan, Daochuo, and Shandao zero times, and uses the *self-power/other-power* lexicon zero times. His scriptural genealogy of evidences (T0232, T0310, T0643, T0614, T0839, T1509, T0279/T0293) altogether excludes the three-sūtras-one-treatise corpus (T0360, T0365, T0366, T1524). Zongmi's selection-and-omission in constructing the four-kinds-*niàn-fó* taxonomy is itself philological evidence for the axis-substitution: he does not argue with the Tanluan–Daochuo axis, he operates on a different axis with a root-capacity gradient. This is a crucial clarification of this paper's thesis: Zongmi's absorption is neither endorsement of the Tanluan–Daochuo other-power thesis nor its abolition, but a re-mapping of *niàn-fó* in its entirety onto the orthogonal meditative-prajñā axis. Name-invocation, *on Zongmi's axis*, is a legitimate entry-stage; reality-contemplation is the apex — but the axis itself has been rotated from *self-power/other-power* to *shallow/deep*.

Zongmi elsewhere in his corpus makes the cosmological extension of this axis explicit. At T48n2015 T48.0407c07–11, the pure-and-impure interpenetration passage reads:

> 「諸法是全一心之證法。一心是全諸法之一心。性相圓融，一多自在。故諸佛與眾生交徹，淨土與穢土融通，法法皆彼此互收，塵塵悉包含世界，相入相即無礙鎔融，具十玄門重重無盡。」
>
> All dharmas are the complete witness of one mind; one mind is the complete one-mind of all dharmas. Nature and form are perfectly fused; one and many are free. Therefore all buddhas and all living beings interpenetrate each other; pure lands and impure lands fuse and penetrate each other; dharma upon dharma mutually contains; particle upon particle contains worlds; entering-and-identifying without obstacle, fused; possessing the ten mystery-gates, repeatedly without end. (translation mine.)[^20]

The significance of this passage will be taken up fully in §V's middle-way correction. Zongmi does not, at the cosmological layer, abolish the Pure Land; pure-and-impure lands are presented under the Huayan ten-mystery-gates architecture as mutually interpenetrating. This is Zongmi's position at its most refined: *niàn-fó* is relocated, at the interpretive layer, onto a root-capacity gradient, but the Pure Land is *not* reduced, at the cosmological layer, to psychological projection. §V uses this passage as the anchor for the ❌ "self-nature Amitābha = subjective idealism" correction.

Finally, one confusion-risk must be flagged here. Zongmi's four-kinds-*niàn-fó* (ninth century, a shallow-to-deep insight-gradient) and Zhiyi's (智顗, 538–597) four-kinds-of-samādhi (sixth century, classified by posture-and-temporality: constant-sitting, constant-walking, half-walking-half-sitting, neither-walking-nor-sitting) are *two different* classifications that share the heading "four kinds" but are substantively distinct. Zhiyi's classificatory axis is posture-temporal combination, not depth; his *constant-walking samādhi* (*pratyutpanna-samādhi*, per the *Banzhou sanmei jing* T0418), though involving buddha-names (invoking Amitābha's name for ninety days continuously), belongs to method-typology, not depth-gradient. Zhiyi's four samādhis belong to P23 (*The Tiantai Four Kinds of Buddha-Recollection*), which the present paper does not develop. §3.3 flags the non-interchangeability of these two same-named-different-subject taxonomies as a matter of philological discipline within the NIAN volume.

### 3.4 From Dahui to Lianchi: The Historical Arc from *Kànhuà* to "Who is Recollecting the Buddha?"

This subsection traces the historical formation of the fixed-string 「念佛是誰」, strictly bounded at Lianchi Zhuhong's 1599 preface to Gaofeng's *Discourse Record* — the earliest CBETA attestation. The fully developed teaching form and the institutional confluence of Chan and Pure Land lie in P24's scope. What this subsection displays is the Song–Yuan technical arc: Dahui (twelfth century) builds the *kànhuà* mechanism and transmits Zhaozhou's seed-citation; Zhongfeng (early fourteenth century) installs the *who*-pivot and declares Chan–Pure Land as of one body-and-pattern; Lianchi (1599) first crystallizes the fixed string.

Dahui Zonggao (1089–1163) is the systematizer of *kànhuà* Chan. His *Discourse Record* (T47n1998A) displays the core mechanism of *kànhuà*: one *huàtóu* (*huà* 話's *tóu* 頭, *the head/onset of speech*) is taken as single object of concentration, lifted continually; the accumulation of "doubt-mass" (*yíqíng* 疑情) reaches "a sudden cutting-off of information" — a sudden interruption of concept-generation. The paradigm case is Zhaozhou's 「無」 ("No"): "*the huàtóu of whether a dog has buddha-nature — as soon as one lifts this single word, the mundane flow of attention settles of itself. … Suddenly on the No, life and death are cut off*" (translation mine).[^21] Dahui's innovation is not the use of public-case language (*gōng'àn* language had long circulated in Chan), but the *sustained, non-intellectual, doubt-filled* gaze at a single word — the *huàtóu* is a *content-free fulcrum*, not a proposition awaiting solution.

On *niàn-fó*, Dahui cites a near-match phrasing in one passage only. At T47.0824a06–10, his platform-sermon cites:

> 「上堂舉：趙州示眾云：不得閑過。念佛、念法、念僧。便問：如何是學人自己念？州云：**念者是誰**？僧云：無伴。州叱云：這驢。」
>
> The master ascended the hall and cited: Zhaozhou instructed his assembly: *"Do not pass time idly. Recollect the buddha, recollect the dharma, recollect the saṅgha."* Someone asked: *"What is the student's own recollection?"* Zhaozhou said: *"Who is the one recollecting?"* The monk said: *"There is no companion."* Zhaozhou shouted: *"You ass!"* (translation mine.)[^22]

This is the phrasing in Dahui's discourse record closest to 「念佛是誰」. But three philological precisions must be noted. First, 「念者是誰」 (*who is the one recollecting?*) is Zhaozhou (Congshen, 778–897)'s saying, not Dahui's creation; Dahui merely cites it on the platform. Second, this citation is, in context, a *narrative gōng'àn*, not a *prescribed huàtóu* — Dahui does not tell *niàn-fó* practitioners, "you should take this 'who is the one recollecting?' as your *huàtóu*." Third, its object is the generic recollection of the three jewels (*buddha, dharma, and saṅgha*), not Amitābha-specific name-invocation. The structural precursor exists as a seed within Dahui's discourse record, but it has not crystallized into a *niàn-fó*-specific *huàtóu*. The union of *kànhuà* mechanism with *niàn-fó* practice has not yet taken shape in Dahui's time.

Zhongfeng Mingben (1263–1323) of the Yuan dynasty is the decisive figure in this union. His *Guǎnglù* (X70n1402) contains twenty-four references to *niàn-fó* dispersed across his sermons, verses, and letters. Zhongfeng does not use the fixed string 「念佛是誰」 (this four-character sequence yields no hits across his thirty-fascicle *Guǎnglù*), but the substance of *turning the name inward* has become fully articulate. Four passages are central.

First, X70.0718b13–c16, the *Exhortation to Recollect Amitābha*, with its "recollecting-the-buddha and recollecting-the-mind" mutual-containment: "*'This mind is the buddha; this mind makes the buddha.' … The World-Honored Śākyamuni opened the teaching of recollecting-the-buddha; Amitābha's vow welcomes those who recollect. … Recollecting-the-buddha is recollecting-the-mind; recollecting-the-mind is recollecting-the-buddha. The mouth constantly recollects the buddha; the mind constantly reveres the buddha. … Moment by moment without dimness, who is not buddha?*" (translation mine). This passage cites the *Contemplation Sūtra*'s "*this mind makes the buddha*" as proof-text, but unfolds it with the Chan mutual-containment of "recollecting-the-buddha is recollecting-the-mind" — name-invocation and mind-contemplation are not opposed in Yuan-dynasty Chan; they define each other. "*Who is not buddha?*" already installs **who** as a grammatical pivot at the verse's close.[^23]

Second, X70.0718c22–24, the *Cherishing the Pure Land* verse: 「終朝合掌念彌陀，舉念之間蹉過多。**和箇念頭都颺却**，全機獨脫苦娑婆」 (*"All day long with joined palms recollecting Amitābha, yet in each raising of thought one often misses. Discard even the thought-of-recollecting itself — then whole functioning slips free and escapes the suffering Sahā world"*; translation mine).[^24] This is the most concentrated Zhongfeng formulation of this paper's thesis — not *stop recollecting*, but *discard the very thought of recollection itself*. The form of name-invocation is preserved ("*all day long with joined palms recollecting Amitābha*"), but the reification of "recollection" as thought-object is dissolved. This is the Yuan-dynasty completion of the inward turn *within* the context of combined Chan–Pure Land cultivation.

Third, X70.0719b15–17, the instruction to the Goryeo king: "*Human life is like an illusion within an illusion; in the dusty world meeting-in-passing, who is who?* **Before parents were born, who am I? When a breath does not come, who am I?**" (translation mine).[^25] Four successive **who** questions, addressed to the Goryeo king (an elite lay patron). Among them, "*before parents were born, who am I?*" and "*when a breath does not come, who am I?*" are precursors of the later "original face" *huàtóu*-type, and their structural kinship with 「念佛是誰」 is direct: same *who*-pivot, same reflexive interrogation of subject-hood. This is the clearest evidence in the extant CBETA corpus that the **who-pivot had become a formally adopted Chan technical term by the Yuan**. The critical technical term of *kànhuà* has migrated — from Song Dahui's "No" to Yuan Zhongfeng's "who."

Fourth, X70.0747a24–b01, the couplet: 「彌陀西住祖西來，**念佛參禪共體裁**。積劫疑團如打破，心花同是一般開」 (*"Amitābha dwells in the West, the patriarch came from the West; recollecting the buddha and investigating Chan share one body-and-pattern. When the accumulated-aeons of the doubt-mass is broken through, the mind-flowers of both bloom alike"*; translation mine).[^26] This is the clearest Yuan-dynasty textual declaration of combined Chan–Pure Land practice: *niàn-fó* and *cān-chán* (Chan investigation) are declared as "sharing one body-and-pattern" (共體裁, *same substance and textual-pattern*); doubt-mass and mind-flowers are descriptions common to both. At this point, *niàn-fó* has attained formal declaration as a Chan technical form; the fixed-string 「念佛是誰」 is one step away, completed by Zhongfeng's grand-master's descendants and by early-Ming Chan masters — but the philological witness for that fixed string must wait until late-Ming Lianchi.

One scholarly clarification is necessary here. Zhongfeng's master Gaofeng Yuanmiao (1238–1295) — popularly and mistakenly credited in later attributions as the founder of 「念佛是誰」 — engages the topic of *niàn-fó* **zero times** in his own *Chan Essentials* (X70n1401) and *Discourse Record* (X70n1400, excluding Lianchi's 1599 preface). Gaofeng belongs to a strictly *kànhuà* lineage ("No" / "the ten thousand dharmas return to one"), and did not apply *kànhuà* to *niàn-fó*. The later "Gaofeng's 「念佛是誰」 teaching" traces philologically to Lianchi Zhuhong's 1599 CE (Wanli 27) preface to Gaofeng's *Discourse Record*, which contains the sentence:

> 「但了念佛是誰，不必問一歸何處。」
>
> Simply realize "*who is recollecting the buddha*" — there is no need to ask "the one returns to where?" (translation mine.)[^27]

This is the earliest CBETA attestation of 「念佛是誰」 as a fixed string. The source is Lianchi's preface, not Gaofeng's own words. In this preface, Lianchi — answering a contemporary challenge (*"If the Lotus Society practitioners are satisfied with invoking Amitābha, why do they need to take up huàtóu like 'the ten thousand dharmas return to one'?"*) — pairs 「念佛是誰」 with the older 「一歸何處」 as functionally equivalent *huàtóu*. The very pairing presupposes **「念佛是誰」 is already a term known to his readers**; Lianchi is citing, not coining. The full teaching form is elaborated at *Yunqi jìngtǔ huìyǔ* (X62.0007c22–0008a03): "*Venerable elders of old taught people to contemplate the* huàtóu*, raising doubt to await great awakening. … To take a comparison: 'the ten thousand dharmas return to one, the one returns to where?' resembles closely 'who is recollecting the buddha?' … Recollect the buddha several times, then turn the light back and inspect: who is the one recollecting the buddha? Using the mind in this way, without forgetting and without forcing, in time one will of oneself attain realization. The ancients said: 'a* niàn-fó *practitioner who wishes to investigate Chan need not take up a different* huàtóu*.' This is precisely what they meant*" (translation mine).[^28] This is the earliest textual evidence of 「念佛是誰」 as a fully taught technique.

The temporal precision here has decisive scoping consequences for this paper's argument. 「念佛是誰」 *technically* was complete by the Yuan (Dahui's *kànhuà* mechanism + Zhongfeng's *who*-pivot + "recollecting-the-buddha and investigating Chan share one body-and-pattern"); but *as fixed string* it crystallizes only in the late Ming — and that crystallization, along with Lianchi's institutionalized Chan–Pure Land confluence, is P24's scope. §3.4 stops here and does not develop the post-1599 late-Ming confluence. This subsection's thesis: from Daoxin's "recollecting-the-buddha is recollecting-the-mind" to Zongmi's "reality-contemplation," the Chan hermeneutic reversal was already complete in the Tang; one concrete technical form of that reversal (contemplating-the-buddha-recollection as *huàtóu*), from the Song seed-citation through the Yuan *who*-pivot, crystallizes as fixed string only in the late Ming — its historical arc is three-staged, and the late-Ming crystallization cannot be projected back into Tang or Song. This temporal precision is also the in-paper operationalization of §IV's chronological-hygiene caution from Sharf.

## IV. In Dialogue with Western Scholarship: Faure, McRae, Sharf

For this paper's thesis of *redirection-not-rejection* to hold under Western-scholarly critical pressure, it must be tested in turn against three structural interlocutors: Bernard Faure's rhetoric/institution distinction, John McRae's no-single-Chan, and Robert Sharf's chronological hygiene. Each interlocutor receives a *concede–push-back* structure: conceding the specific force of the critique, then indicating with precision why this paper's thesis survives under that critique.

### 4.1 Faure: Rhetoric and Institution

Bernard Faure, in *The Rhetoric of Immediacy* (Princeton UP, 1991), *Chan Insights and Oversights* (Princeton UP, 1993), and *The Will to Orthodoxy* (Stanford UP, 1997), consistently develops one structural argument: that Chan's self-presentation — "direct pointing at the mind, no reliance on scripture, a separate transmission outside the teachings, seeing the nature to become buddha" — is a *rhetorical construction*, not a description of institutional reality. Faure's "rhetoric" is not pejorative; he is not arguing that Chan is a fraud. He argues that a systematic gap exists between Chan's self-image and its institutional life. At the institutional level, Chan was deeply involved in ritual, portrait-and-relic veneration, lineage genealogies, and (by the Song) fully integrated Pure Land liturgy. Any scholarship that treats Chan's self-rhetoric as an institutional description is *reproducing* Chan's rhetoric rather than studying it.

**Concede**: Faure's warning has specific force on this argument. If this paper's thesis of "turning the name inward" were to be read as "Chan as institution abolished *niàn-fó*," it would exemplify the typical error Faure identifies. The counter-evidence invoked in §V — the *Baizhang qīngguī* witnesses (the *niàn-fó* office, the deceased-monk *niàn-fó* liturgy, weinuo-led *niàn-fó*) — bears direct institutional witness: Chan as institution never abolished *niàn-fó*. Faure's warning about Chan self-rhetoric is thus not merely acknowledged; it is adopted as methodological foundation for this paper's middle-way correction.

**Push back**: But this paper's thesis operates at a level other than "institution-as-monolith" — it operates on **hermeneutics**, the method of reading texts. Daoxin in T2837 reading T0232's "binding the mind to one buddha" as "recollecting-the-buddha is recollecting-the-mind"; Huineng in the *Platform Sūtra* reading "Amitābha" as "the upright-and-direct"; Zongmi in X0229 arranging the four kinds of *niàn-fó* as an insight-depth gradient — all three actions are *hermeneutic acts*, acts of re-assigning the meaning of canonical vocabulary. The existence of these acts does not cancel Chan's institutional liturgy (both are simultaneously true), nor does it cancel Chan's rhetorical construction (the two coexist). This paper's thesis claims only this: at the *hermeneutic* layer of Chan, the direction of *niàn-fó*'s object is reversed; at the *institutional* layer, the *niàn-fó* liturgy is preserved. Faure's rhetoric-institution distinction does not overturn this thesis but *scopes* it — what is recorded here is (a) Chan's hermeneutic act, not (b) Chan's self-image, and not (c) Chan's institutional liturgy. All three layers can historically coexist without contradiction, and do, as recorded in this paper. Faure's significant contribution is internalized as guardrail: §V's correction takes Faure's warning as its explicit frame.

### 4.2 McRae: No Single Chan

John McRae, in *The Northern School and the Formation of Early Ch'an Buddhism* (U. Hawai'i Press, 1986) and *Seeing Through Zen* (U. California Press, 2003), consistently argues that the "Southern sudden / Northern gradual" narrative of Chan history is a polemical construction by the eighth-century Shenhui (684–758), later canonized via the *Platform Sūtra* and the Song-era *transmission-of-the-lamp* system. The actual eighth-century Chan landscape had Northern Chan (the Shenxiu lineage) in a dominant position at court and in major monasteries; Shenxiu himself served as imperial teacher, and is not the gradualist straw-figure described by later Southern Chan. McRae also formulates four methodological rules ("McRae's Rules"): (1) It's not true, and therefore it's more important; (2) Lineage assertions are as wrong as they are strong; (3) Precision implies inaccuracy; (4) Romanticism breeds cynicism. Each rule is a warning against the "Chan's position" style of argument.

**Concede**: McRae's warning carries marked force on this argument. If this paper were to assert "Chan's position on *niàn-fó*" without scoping, it would fall into McRae's typical error — treating "Chan" as a single subject. McRae's second rule, "lineage assertions are as wrong as they are strong," is especially to be watched here: if this paper's thesis rests on the rhetorical force of the "Daoxin → Hongren → Huineng → Zongmi" lineage, it would be structurally homologous to Song-era lamp-history construction and fall under McRae's critique. This concession is not verbal; §3.1 already noted that the Shenxiu–Empress-Wu passage indicates this lineage's *self-avowed* scriptural foundation in the early eighth century, not a later Song construction — this very noticing is a methodological internalization of McRae.

**Push back**: But this paper's scope is strictly narrowed to *one specific interpretive sub-lineage* (Daoxin → Hongren → Huineng → Zongmi acting consecutively along one *yī-xíng sānmèi*-reading axis), *not* a claim about "Chan as a whole." This sub-lineage is textually confirmable: T2837's Daoxin chapter explicitly cites T0232 as foundation; T2011's Hongren inherits it; T2008's *Platform Sūtra* *Inquiry* chapter treats it directly; X0229's Zongmi builds the four-kinds with T0232 as the basis for both name-invocation and reality-contemplation poles. Four nodes, in different texts, different periods, different geographies, *self-referentially*. This sub-lineage is not a Song-era lamp-history construction. Shenhui's Northern-Southern split and this paper's P20 → P21 Pure Land–Chan axis-rotation belong to *different axes*: the former is internal Chan historiography of sub-lineage disputes, the latter is an interpretive cross-point of two doctrinal axes (Pure Land and Chan). McRae's warning is absorbed as scope-limitation — this paper's topic is not "Chan's position on *niàn-fó*" but "the actions of one specific Chan interpretive sub-lineage along the *yī-xíng sānmèi*-reading axis." This precise scope-delimitation allows the thesis to stand under McRae's critique.

One further note is required. McRae's warning also points to one latent risk in this paper: Shenxiu's *Guānxīn lùn* (*Treatise on Contemplating the Mind*, T2833, attributed), a dominant court teaching in the eighth century, whose doctrine "one method of contemplating the mind subsumes all practices" gives positive role to *niàn-fó* and is not the gradualist straw-figure of later Southern Chan. The present paper does not include Shenxiu in the §3.1 sub-lineage, because the paper's scope focuses on the interpretive absorption along the *yī-xíng sānmèi* axis (T0232 → T2837 → T2011), not Northern–Southern split. But this paper acknowledges here that the Northern lineage of Shenxiu exists with its own distinctive stance on *niàn-fó* — a layer outside this paper's scope but worth flagging.

### 4.3 Sharf: Chronological Hygiene (Backup Interlocutor)

Robert Sharf, in *Coming to Terms with Chinese Buddhism* (U. Hawai'i Press, 2002), "The Zen of Japanese Nationalism" (*History of Religions* 33.1, 1993), and his several studies of *gōng'àn/*kōan* practice (including the chapter in Heine & Wright, eds., *The Kōan*, Oxford UP, 2000), consistently advocates a methodological principle: **chronological hygiene** — later practices must not be projected back onto earlier texts. In P20, Sharf appeared as the primary interlocutor, resisting the Shinshū-projection onto seventh-century Tanluan–Daochuo. In P21, Sharf's role is against a different retrojection: the projection of Song-era Chan onto Tang. *Kànhuà* Chan as a prescribed meditational method is the twelfth-century institutionalization of Dahui Zonggao; Tang-era Chan (Daoxin, Huineng, Mazu, and indeed Linji in the ninth century) did not practice "*kànhuà* applied to *niàn-fó*," and the crystallization of that specific form (*niàn-fó shì shéi*) belongs to the late Ming.

**Concede**: Sharf's warning applies directly in §3.4. Were this paper to project 「念佛是誰」 back onto Tang Chan masters (Daoxin, Huineng, or even Tang-era Zhaozhou), it would fall into Sharf's identified projection error. The concession here is precise, not merely rhetorical: the scope-limitation structure of §3.4 in its entirety is a methodological operationalization of Sharf's principle — Dahui (twelfth century) is the seed-citation provider, not the author of a *niàn-fó*-specific *huàtóu*; Zhongfeng (early fourteenth century) installs the *who*-pivot, not the fixed string; Lianchi (1599) is the earliest attestation of the fixed string, and this belongs to P24, not to this paper's Tang-to-Song hermeneutic axis. The three-layer temporal division is Sharf's principle operationalized.

**Push back**: But on §§3.1, 3.2, and 3.3's Tang-era materials, Sharf's warning does not constitute a rebuttal to this paper — it only serves as a scope-reminder. The materials in §3.1 are all within T2837 — a Dunhuang manuscript compiled by Jìngjué ca. 716 CE, an early-eighth-century document, not a Song-era construction; its citation of T0232 matches (save for critical-apparatus marks) what is found in the Taishō T0232 at T08.731a; the T0232 reading of the early Tang rests on its own Tang-era textual ground. The §3.2 materials rely mainly on the Yuan Zongbao recension of the *Platform Sūtra*, but the Dunhuang recension (T48n2007), though briefer, already contains the core motifs (the East-vs-West *reductio*, the upright-is-Amitābha identification, suchness-self-nature); the Zongbao expansions are Yuan-era textual polishing, but the doctrinal core is a late-Tang to early-Song Chan consensus, not a Yuan creation. The §3.3 materials on Zongmi are ninth-century late-Tang first-hand sources, temporally three centuries before the Song *kànhuà*. Each of these sections argues from within its own temporal stratum, and does not use later Song-era *huàtóu* techniques as retroactive legitimation of Tang hermeneutic acts. Sharf's warning is absorbed by this paper as strict scope-limitation for §3.4, not as rebuttal of §§3.1–3.3's Tang-era materials.

The concede–push-back structure of all three interlocutors jointly anchors this paper's thesis within a sustainable precise scope: (a) at the hermeneutic layer rather than the institutional (against Faure); (b) within a specific sub-lineage rather than "Chan as a whole" (against McRae); (c) arguing from within each material's own temporal stratum rather than across-time projection (against Sharf). The thesis remains standing after this threefold testing.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### 5.1 ❌ Misreading One: "Chan Abolished *Niàn-fó* Institutionally"

**Content of the misreading**: If Chan's hermeneutic of redirection reverses the direction of *niàn-fó*'s object (§§3.1–3.3), does this hermeneutic act amount to an institutional or practice abolition of *niàn-fó*? Western readers, under the influence of Chan's characteristic rhetoric ("meeting the buddha, kill the buddha; meeting the patriarch, kill the patriarch"), often draw this inference.

**Correction**: Three layers of institutional, textual, and doctrinal evidence jointly show that Chan **never** at any layer abolished *niàn-fó*. First, at the institutional layer, the *Chǐxiū Bǎizhàng qīngguī* (T48n2025), fascicles 5–6, installs a formal **niàn-fó office** (T48.1132a04: "*at the completion of the niàn-fó office, one is in line for the rank of inner attendant in one's home mountain*"). Its duties include the full liturgy of sending off a deceased monk (T48.1112b10 table of contents: "*the deceased monk's registering of robes and bowl, inviting buddha-rituals, appraisal of robes, the great-night recitation, the sending-off, and the cremation* — *recitation* and *sending-off* use *niàn-fó* as vehicle"); the weinuo leading *niàn-fó* with joined palms at mealtimes (T48.1145a08: "*the weinuo recollects the buddha with palms joined; the fingers shall not be uneven*"); and before-the-hall recitation with one prescribed *niàn-fó* call (T48.1155c05: "*at before-the-hall recitation, recollect the buddha once, lightly strike once*"). The *qīngguī* is an imperially revised Yuan text; the attestation period of the institutional *niàn-fó* office is Yuan, not Tang. But Yuan-era Chan is Chan; the institutional existence of the *niàn-fó* office suffices to establish that **Chan never institutionally abolished *niàn-fó*** — whether the Tang had equivalent institutionalization is a separate independent question, and its answer does not affect this correction's force. Second, at the textual layer, Hongren's *Treatise on the Supreme Vehicle* T48.0377b17–21's "guarding the original true mind surpasses recollecting the other-buddha" — "*surpasses*" is the grammar of value-comparison, not prohibition; "*constantly recollecting that buddha does not exempt one from birth-and-death*" is a limitation on name-invocation as *sole salvific path*, not an abolition of name-invocation itself. Outward *niàn-fó* is placed *beneath*, not banned. Third, at the doctrinal layer, Daoxin in T85.1287c07–12, answering the question "*should one face the West?*", says clearly that "*if one knows the mind is originally unborn and unceasing, ultimately pure, this is the pure buddha-land; no further need to face westward*" is a teaching for sharp faculties, but adds: "**the buddha directs dull-faculty beings toward the West; this is not spoken for sharp-faculty beings**" — Daoxin explicitly acknowledges the legitimate salvific function of the Western Pure Land for dull-faculty practitioners. Of the three witnesses, the third (Daoxin's own words) is earliest (seventh century) and most decisive: it establishes Chan's *inclusive-gradient* stance on *niàn-fó* — *niàn-fó* is not abolished but *placed within a gradient structure*, coexisting with mind-watching and *guarding-the-one-unmoving*. The "inward" in this paper's thesis is an *interpretive direction*, not a practical abolition.

### 5.2 ❌ Misreading Two: "Self-Nature Amitābha = Subjective Idealism"

**Content of the misreading**: If Chan internalizes *niàn-fó* as "the upright-is-Amitābha," "suchness-self-nature is the true buddha," and "reality-contemplation *niàn-fó*," are the Pure Land and Amitābha reduced to psychological projections? Under the influence of the Cartesian mind–matter binary, Western readers easily read "internalization" as "subjectification" and "turning inward" as "abolition of external objective existence."

**Correction**: This misreading misreads Chan's underlying ontology. Chan's *self-nature* is not the subjective mind in the Cartesian sense (*res cogitans*); it is a substantive ontological category equivalent to buddha-nature / *tathāgatagarbha* function. The *Platform Sūtra*'s *Conditions* chapter at T48.349a19–21 — 「何期自性：本自清淨、本不生滅、本自具足、本無動搖、能生萬法」 (*"How unexpected that self-nature: originally pure; originally unborn and unceasing; originally complete; originally unmoving; capable of generating the ten thousand dharmas"*; translation mine) — is Huineng's exclamation at the moment of sudden awakening upon hearing the *Vajracchedikā*'s "abide in no place and yet arise in mind"; it is the *Platform Sūtra*'s foundational definition of the term "self-nature." The fifth of the five specifications — "*capable of generating the ten thousand dharmas*" — is especially important: self-nature is not a passive subjective consciousness, but the generative root of all dharmas. This is connected to P18's discussion of the *Ghanavyūha* ālaya-consciousness's *āśraya-parāvṛtti* axis, and to P17's Laṅkāvatāra thesis of "the mind of all buddhas is foremost" — all are ontological descriptions of *tathāgatagarbha* / buddha-nature, not psychological reductions. "Self-nature awakened is the buddha" (T48.352b12) has the grammar of an ontological identity, not a metaphor.

More importantly, the guardrail is explicit in Zongmi's corpus. At T48.0407c07–11, the pure-and-impure interpenetration passage: "*All dharmas are the complete witness of one mind; one mind is the complete one-mind of all dharmas. … Pure lands and impure lands fuse and penetrate each other; dharma upon dharma mutually contains … possessing the ten mystery-gates, repeatedly without end*." Zongmi, through the Huayan ten-mystery-gates architecture, presents pure-and-impure as mutually interpenetrating realities — *the Pure Land is not abolished at the cosmological layer, nor is it reified as an absolutely isolated other-realm*; the two penetrate each other within the interconnected structure of the *dharmadhātu*. This is Zongmi's position at its most refined: at the hermeneutic layer, *niàn-fó* is relocated onto the root-capacity gradient (name-invocation to reality-contemplation, four stages), but the Pure Land at the cosmological layer is *not* reduced to psychological projection. Chan's act is *not* "abolishing the Pure Land as cosmological fact"; it is "dismantling the reading of the Pure Land as *geographical other-region*" — the two are philosophically distinct steps. Subjective idealism would say: the Pure Land exists only in the mind, not outside. Zongmi says: pure-and-impure lands mutually penetrate within the *dharmadhātu*; neither only-in-mind nor only-in-the-other-realm, but both simultaneously true within the mutually-containing architecture of dharma-on-dharma. This level of ontological refinement cannot be compressed into the Cartesian mind-matter binary.

### 5.3 ⚠️ Misreading Three: "「念佛是誰」 is a Native Tang-Era Chan Technique"

**Content of the misreading**: Contemporary Chan retreat *gōng'àn* handbooks, popular Chan–Pure Land confluence lectures, and even certain scholarly introductions habitually trace 「念佛是誰」 as a unified Chan technique back to Zhaozhou, Dahui, or Gaofeng — or generally to "Tang-era Chan." This attribution is philologically imprecise and requires correction.

**Correction**: Three temporal layers must be rigorously distinguished. **(1) Technical seed** (Song Dahui, twelfth century): T47.0824a06–10 cites Zhaozhou's "who is the one recollecting?" as a platform anecdote. This is the structural precursor, but not the *huàtóu* form prescribed by Dahui; Dahui's own *kànhuà* paradigm is "No" (T47.0899a13, T47.0903c01, et al.), not 「念佛是誰」. **(2) Installation of the *who*-pivot** (Yuan Zhongfeng, early fourteenth century): X70.0719b15–17's four *who*-questions to the Goryeo king witness "*who*" having become a formally adopted Chan technical term by Yuan times; X70.0747a24's "recollecting the buddha and investigating Chan share one body-and-pattern" is the clearest Yuan-era declaration of combined Chan–Pure Land cultivation. At this stage Zhongfeng completes the technical layer of the inward turn — yet the four-character 「念佛是誰」 as fixed string does **not** appear anywhere in the thirty fascicles of the *Zhongfeng Guǎnglù* (verified by CBETA full-text search across X70n1402). **(3) Fixed-string crystallization** (late-Ming Lianchi, 1599 CE): at X70.0677a12–22, Lianchi, in his Wanli-27 preface to Gaofeng's *Discourse Record*, writes "*simply realize who is recollecting the buddha — there is no need to ask the one returns to where*"; at X62.0007c22–0008a03, the *Yunqi jìngtǔ huìyǔ* unfolds the full teaching form "*recollect the buddha several times, turn the light back and inspect: who is the one recollecting the buddha?*" This is the earliest extant CBETA attestation of 「念佛是誰」 as fixed string.

The precise temporal division has direct scoping consequence for §3.4 and for P24. The present paper's §3.4 is restricted to the Song–Yuan arc; the full development of the late-Ming confluence (Lianchi's 1599 teaching form, Yongming Yanshou's Chan–Pure Land foundation in *Wànshàn tóngguī jí*, Ouyi Zhixu's phenomenal-vs-principial distinction) belongs to P24. Popular handbooks commonly mis-attribute 「念佛是誰」 to Gaofeng — yet Gaofeng's own *Chan Essentials* (X70n1401) and *Discourse Record* (X70n1400, excluding the 1599 Lianchi preface) contain **zero** engagement with *niàn-fó*; the later "Gaofeng 「念佛是誰」 teaching" traces philologically to Lianchi's 1599 preface to Gaofeng's record. This mis-attribution is a widely circulated folk-level error in Chinese Chan historiography, and this ⚠️ correction clarifies it philologically. The marker here is ⚠️ rather than ❌: the technical continuity between Song and Yuan is real (Dahui's *kànhuà* mechanism applied in Zhongfeng to *niàn-fó*, crystallized at Lianchi as fixed string), not philological fiction; the misreading lies in temporal blurring and retrojective string-attribution, not in the existence of the technique itself.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essence restated**: This paper advances a three-layer thesis. On the foundation laid by P20's documentation of the other-power lexical invention (Tanluan's act in T1819) and the anchoring of the *fánfū* subject (the joint work of Tanluan, Daochuo, and the three-sūtras-one-treatise corpus), this paper tracks the Chan response to that vocabulary and subject as *redirection, not rejection*. First, Daoxin, in the *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* (T85n2837), takes the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā* T0232's *yī-xíng sānmèi* as his charter, reads "recollection of one buddha" as "recollection of one mind," and completes the inward turn in 「念佛即是念心，求心即是求佛」; Hongren's *Treatise on the Supreme Vehicle* (T48n2011) inherits and extends this with "guarding the original true mind surpasses recollecting the other-buddha" as value-hierarchical formulation. Second, Huineng's *Platform Sūtra* *Inquiry* chapter (T48n2008), meeting directly the prefect Wei Qu's question, uses the *reductio* "*if Easterners commit evil and niàn-fó to be reborn in the West, where do Westerners who commit evil niàn-fó to be reborn?*" to criticize *aspiring-east, aspiring-west*'s directional fixation, and re-anchors the four Pure Land names in inward virtues with "the upright-and-direct is Amitābha." Third, Zongmi in the *Sub-commentary on the Huayan Practices and Vows Chapter Commentary* (X05n0229) builds, in his sub-commentary layer, a four-kinds-of-*niàn-fó* gradient — name-invocation / image-contemplation / visualization / reality-contemplation — arranged as an insight-depth ladder, accommodating name-invocation as the legitimate entry-stage, and thereby replacing Tanluan–Daochuo's self-power/other-power axis with another axis (shallow-to-deep). The *same* T0232 grounds, in P20's reading, other-power name-invocation; in P21's reading, both name-invocation and reality-contemplation poles — *same words, same passage, two directions*. This is Part VI Two Streams' most refined textual evidence: Chinese *niàn-fó* did not develop in two traditions on disjoint vocabularies, but bifurcated *on the same charter layer*. The fixed string 「念佛是誰」 is earliest-attested at Lianchi's 1599 (X70.0677a18), belonging to P24's scope; §3.4 of this paper displays only the Song–Yuan technical arc (Dahui → Zhongfeng → not-yet-Lianchi). The three Western interlocutors' warnings (Faure, McRae, Sharf) — rhetoric/institution distinction, no single Chan, chronological hygiene — are internalized as scope-limitations: (a) the thesis operates at the hermeneutic, not institutional, layer; (b) the scope is a specific sub-lineage, not Chan as a whole; (c) each passage argues from its own temporal stratum. The three middle-way corrections of §V (Chan did not abolish *niàn-fó*, self-nature Amitābha is not subjective idealism, 「念佛是誰」 is not native to Tang Chan) guard the thesis against common misreadings by attaching specific documentary anchors.

**Cultivation guidance**: For the contemporary practitioner — particularly one who has read P20 and grasps that *other-power*, in its Tang-era origin, is a dependent-arising collaborative structure (the practitioner's five-recollection-gate practice as self-contribution; *pūrva-praṇidhāna-bala* as *adhipati-pratyaya* contributory condition rather than sole-sufficient cause) — this paper offers a direct implication for practice. The Chan and Pure Land axes in the Chinese tradition have never been two doctrines in competitive opposition; they are two readings of one shared charter (T0232's *yī-xíng sānmèi*). A practitioner of name-invocation need not abandon *chēng-míng* and shift to 「念佛是誰」 to enter Chan's field of vision — Zongmi's four-kinds already accommodate name-invocation as legitimate entry-stage, without disparagement. For a practitioner whose emphasis is mind-watching, there is equally no need to treat name-invocation as a *prohibited step* to exclude from practice — the *Platform Sūtra*'s *Concentration-and-Wisdom* chapter's "*suchness-self-nature gives rise to thought*" makes explicit that the subject of thought is suchness-self-nature, not the six faculties; if name-invocation takes suchness-self-nature as subject, it is already the practice of "*recollecting-the-buddha is recollecting-the-mind*." The daily meaning of *redirection* is this: when invoking the name, do not fix the name on a region outside the body; when watching the mind, do not expel the name from the field of awareness. The boundary between these two is not the sectarian boundary of Chan and Pure Land; it is the boundary of one subject's different skillful means at different faculty-stages. For Anglophone readers — especially those shaped by the Suzuki-era "Zen vs Pure Land" binary — this paper's correction is this: the binary is not the original Chinese picture; it is the English transplantation of twentieth-century Japanese sectarian positions. Once that transplantation is set down, the internal structure of the Chinese tradition — two streams on one charter — becomes visible.

**Open question**: The structural symmetry of Part VI's Two Streams is now established between P20 and P21; the remaining three papers will unfold subsequent developments on this symmetrical frame. P22 (*The Pure Land Patriarchs II: Shandao and the Instituting of the Name*) will inherit P20's account of the Tanluan–Daochuo tradition and address how Shandao (613–681) institutionalized the other-power and *fánfū*-enter-the-reward-land theses — the *fánfū-enter-the-reward-land* thesis, the standardization of name-holding, and the architecture of the four-fascicle *Guānjīng Shū* — bringing the vocabulary and subject established here into complete institutional form on the Pure Land line. P23 (*The Tiantai Four Kinds of Buddha-Recollection*) will address Zhiyi (538–597)'s name-invocation / image-contemplation / visualization / reality-contemplation fourfold — a typology that shares a name with Zongmi's four kinds but must not be conflated (Zhiyi, sixth century, by posture-and-time typology; Zongmi, ninth century, by insight-depth gradient), as flagged at the close of §3.3; Zhiyi's architecture can be read as a method-structural bridge between P20's Tanluan–Daochuo *fánfū* subject and this paper's Chan mind-recollection. P24 (*From Yongming to the Late-Ming Confluence*) will address Yongming Yanshou's (904–975) *Wanshan tonggui ji* and *Zongjing lu*, Yunqi Zhuhong's (1535–1615) phenomenal/principial distinction in his *Amitābha Sūtra Commentary*, and the Chan–Pure Land confluence of Zhongfeng Mingben and Ouyi Zhixu — where Lianchi's 1599 earliest-attestation of 「念佛是誰」 will be unfolded in its complete teaching form. The Song–Yuan arc this paper's §3.4 has bounded will there extend into the late Ming. Once Part VI's five papers are read together, the interpretive symmetry this paper and P20 have established will receive full filling at institutional, methodological, and confluence layers.

One open question is left to the reader: if Part VI's Two Streams have, throughout Chinese history, never been truly opposed but have always bifurcated along a single charter, is the current Chinese-and-English Chan–Pure Land opposition frame — whether phrased as "self-power vs. other-power," "sudden vs. gradual," or "self-nature Amitābha vs. other-realm Pure Land" — a heritage that can be set down? This paper does not answer it; it leaves it to the reader's own practice and reading experience.

[^12]: *Liùzǔ dàshī fǎbǎo tánjīng*, T48n2008, fascicle 1, *Inquiry* chapter 3, T48.0352a13–14. Wei Qu's (prefect of Shaozhou) question. The phrase "*commonly sees monks and laity reciting Amitābha, aspiring to be reborn in the West*" is first-hand evidence that by the late seventh to early eighth century, *niàn-fó* was already a widespread monastic-and-lay practice in Chinese territory.

[^13]: *Ibid.*, T48.0352a21–25. The East-vs-West *reductio* passage. Cites the *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa* (T14n0475) "*when one's mind is pure, the buddha-land is pure*" as proof-text, then uses "*Easterners who commit evil niàn-fó to be reborn in the West; Westerners who commit evil niàn-fó to be reborn where?*" to force out the structural blind-spot of the geographical reading of Pure Land doctrine.

[^14]: *Ibid.*, T48.0352b09–14. The re-cartography-of-body-and-mind passage. "*The upright-and-direct is Amitābha*" is the *Platform Sūtra*'s most refined treatment of the *niàn-fó* topic — the four names (Guanyin, Mahāsthāmaprāpta, Śākyamuni, Amitābha) are preserved while their referents are rotated to inward virtues.

[^15]: *Ibid.*, T48.0349a19–21 (*Conditions* chapter, the "five 'how unexpected'" self-nature definition); T48.0354c01–03 (*Repentance* chapter, "*to take refuge in one's own mind and in the self-nature is to take refuge in the true buddha*"); T48.0362a08 (*Instructions* chapter, *Self-nature True Buddha Verse*'s "*suchness-self-nature is the true buddha*"). These three passages are the *Platform Sūtra*'s foundational articulations of the self-nature position. The philological fact that "self-nature Amitābha" as a four-character fixed string does not appear in the *Platform Sūtra* is verified by grep across the full Zongbao recension (T48n2008) and by cross-reference with Mazu Daoyi's *yǔlù* in T51n2076 fascicle 6, where "mind-is-buddha" is the attested phrasing.

[^16]: *Ibid.*, T48.0352b02–07. The *Formless Verses* preface's doubled temporal-and-spatial internalization. The 108,000 miles are reduced to the ten evils and eight wrongs (*ethical distance*); "*in an instant seeing the West*" completes the internalization of time.

[^17]: *Ibid.*, T48.0353a17–22, *Concentration-and-Wisdom* chapter 4. The "suchness-self-nature gives rise to thought" passage — the *Platform Sūtra*'s ontological ground for *niàn* (thought/recollection): the true subject of thought is suchness-self-nature, not the six faculties. This is the theoretical basis for §3.2's discussion of Chan's grammatical reversal of *niàn-fó*'s subject-predicate structure.

[^18]: *Huáyán jīng xíngyuàn pǐn shū chāo*, X05n0229, fascicle 4, 0280c05–0281a16. The *locus classicus* of the four-kinds-*niàn-fó* taxonomy, embedded in Zongmi's expansion of Chengguan's gloss on "recollecting the buddhas of the ten directions" (the ninth of the ten counter-flow mind-states in the confession-of-obstructions passage of the *Samantabhadra Practices and Vows Chapter*).

[^19]: *Ibid.*, X05n0229, 0281a14–16. Concluding sentence of the four-kinds-*niàn-fó*: "*These four are arranged each according to root-capacity, from shallow to deep, with the last as the supreme. As these are essential for salvation, I have briefly outlined them.*" This is Zongmi's most concise statement of the root-capacity gradient.

[^20]: *Chányuán zhūquánjí dūxù*, T48n2015, T48.0407c07–11. Pure-and-impure interpenetration passage.

[^21]: *Dàhuì Pǔjué chánshī yǔlù*, Dahui Zonggao's discourses recorded by Yúnwén, Taishō vol. 47, No. 1998A, T47.0899a13–17, instructions to Lay Adherent Miaozheng. The paradigm case of the *kànhuà* apparatus.

[^22]: *Ibid.*, T47.0824a06–10, platform citation. Dahui's citation of Zhaozhou's "who is the one recollecting?" *gōng'àn*. This appears here as a narrative citation during a platform sermon, not as a prescribed *huàtóu* for *niàn-fó* practitioners.

[^23]: *Tiānmù Zhōngfēng héshang guǎnglù*, Zhongfeng Mingben's records compiled by Cíjì, Wànxù cáng X70n1402, X70.0718b13–c16, *Exhortation to Recollect Amitābha* verses.

[^24]: *Ibid.*, X70.0718c22–24, *Cherishing the Pure Land* seven-character quatrain. "*Discard even the thought-of-recollecting itself*" is this paper's §3.4 most concise Zhongfeng formulation of "turning inward" — not *stop recollecting the buddha*, but dissolve the reification of "thought-of-recollecting."

[^25]: *Ibid.*, X70.0719b15–17, instruction to the Goryeo king. Four successive *who* questions; evidence that the *who*-pivot had become a formally adopted Chan technical term by the Yuan.

[^26]: *Ibid.*, X70.0747a24–b01, seven-character quatrain. "*Recollecting the buddha and investigating Chan share one body-and-pattern*" — the clearest Yuan-era textual statement of combined Chan–Pure Land practice: *niàn-fó* and *cān-chán* are declared to share one body-and-pattern.

[^27]: Lianchi Zhuhong, *Preface to the Discourse Record of Master Gaofeng* (1599 CE), collected in *Gāofēng dàshī yǔlù*, Wànxù cáng X70n1400, X70.0677a12–22. "*Simply realize 'who is recollecting the buddha' — there is no need to ask 'the one returns to where.'*" The earliest attestation in the extant CBETA corpus of 「念佛是誰」 as a fixed string.

[^28]: Lianchi Zhuhong, *Yunqi jìngtǔ huìyǔ*, collected in *Yúnqī fǎhuì*, Wànxù cáng X62n1170, X62.0007c22–0008a03. The complete teaching form: "*Venerable elders of old taught people to contemplate the* huàtóu*, raising doubt to await great awakening. … To take a comparison: 'the ten thousand dharmas return to one, the one returns to where?' resembles closely 'who is recollecting the buddha?' … Recollect the buddha several times, then turn the light back and inspect: who is the one recollecting the buddha? Using the mind in this way, without forgetting and without forcing, in time one will of oneself attain realization. The ancients said: 'a niàn-fó practitioner who wishes to investigate Chan need not take up a different huàtóu.' This is precisely what they meant.*" Full development and the institutional confluence of the late-Ming Chan–Pure Land confluence belong to P24.

### V. Cross-Reference to Series Papers

**Backward references within the NIAN volume:**

- **P17 (*The Laṅkāvatāra's "Mind of All Buddhas is Foremost"*):** P17's discussion of the *Laṅkāvatāra* thesis reappears in this paper's §3.1 in Daoxin's dual-scripture declaration — "*relying on the Laṅkāvatāra's 'the mind of all buddhas is foremost,' and on the Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra's one-practice samādhi*." Daoxin welds the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s mind-nature doctrine with the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā*'s samādhi methodology; this welding concretizes the P17 Chan doctrine of "the mind of all buddhas is foremost" onto the methodological platform of T0232's *yī-xíng sānmèi*. The inward turn of the object in §3.1 ("recollecting the buddha is recollecting the mind") can be read as P17's "mind of all buddhas is foremost" precisely developed on the *niàn-fó* topic.
- **P18 (*The Ghanavyūha: Buddha-Recollection as Ālaya-Parāvṛtti*):** P18's discussion of the *āśraya-parāvṛtti* axis in the *Ghanavyūha* aligns with the self-nature ontology underlying §5.2 of this paper. Self-nature (the *Platform Sūtra*'s five specifications) and ālaya-parāvṛtti (P18's Yogācāra axis) together supply the ontological underpinning for Chan's "subject of *niàn-fó*." The two are not the same thesis but both take the full *tathāgatagarbha* / buddha-nature functional category as the theoretical ground of *niàn-fó*, not a psychological reduction.
- **P19 (*The Mañjuśrī Prajñā Sūtra and One-Practice Samādhi*):** P19's main subject is the Mañjuśrī-devotion reading of T0232's *yī-xíng sānmèi*; this paper's §3.1 displays Daoxin's absorption of T0232 into Chan lineage and the completion of the object-turn in T2837. P19's Mañjuśrī-devotion reading and P21's Chan inward-turn reading are two readings of the same sūtra passage, not two opposed positions — Daoxin's near-complete transcription of T0232 at the chapter-head (T85.1286c24–1287a07) itself evidences that the Chan reading unfolds *on* the Mañjuśrī-devotion textual wrapper, not as a separate sūtra.
- **P20 (*The Pure Land Patriarchs I: Tanluan and Daochuo*):** This paper is P20's counter-thesis, forming a structural symmetry with P20 in Part VI Two Streams. P20 documents the lexical invention of *other-power* and the anchoring of the *fánfū* subject; this paper documents the Chan redirection response to that vocabulary and subject. The same T0232 grounds, under P20's reading, other-power name-invocation; under this paper's reading, both name-invocation and reality-contemplation poles — same words, same passage, two directions — the clearest textual evidence for Part VI's structural symmetry. This paper's §I, the close of §3.1, and §VI all explicitly cite P20 as counterpoint.

**Forward references within the NIAN volume:**

- **P22 (*The Pure Land Patriarchs II: Shandao and the Instituting of the Name*):** P22 will inherit P20's account of the Tanluan–Daochuo tradition and address how Shandao (613–681) institutionalized the other-power and *fánfū*-enter-the-reward-land theses — the *fánfū*-enter-the-reward-land thesis, the standardization of name-holding, and the architecture of the four-fascicle *Guānjīng Shū*. The vocabulary and subject established in this paper will there receive their full institutional form on the Pure Land line, set alongside this paper's Chan hermeneutic redirection as the mid-Part-VI diptych.
- **P23 (*The Tiantai Four Kinds of Buddha-Recollection*):** P23 will address Zhiyi's (538–597) name-invocation / image-contemplation / visualization / reality-contemplation fourfold *samādhi* — a name-sharing typology not interchangeable with Zongmi's four-kinds-*niàn-fó* (the close of §3.3 of this paper already declares the difference: sixth century vs ninth century, method-time typology vs insight-depth gradient). Tiantai's architecture can be read as a method-structural bridge between P20's *fánfū* subject and this paper's Chan mind-recollection.
- **P24 (*From Yongming to the Late-Ming Confluence*):** P24 will address Yongming Yanshou's (904–975) *Wanshan tonggui ji* (T2017) and *Zongjing lu*, Yunqi Zhuhong's (1535–1615) phenomenal-vs-principial distinction in his *Amitābha Sūtra Commentary*, and the Chan–Pure Land confluence of Zhongfeng Mingben and Ouyi Zhixu — where Lianchi's 1599 earliest-attestation of 「念佛是誰」 will be unfolded in its complete teaching form. The Song–Yuan arc this paper's §3.4 bounds will there extend into the late Ming. This paper's three-layer division of 「念佛是誰」 formation (Song seed / Yuan pivot / late-Ming crystallization) is P24's pre-reading.

**Cross-volume references across the eight-series corpus:**

- **S2 (*Heart Sūtra*):** The semantic turn in §3.1 of this paper — Daoxin's "recollecting the buddha is recollecting the mind, seeking the mind is seeking the buddha" — shares an axis with S2's *Heart Sūtra*'s "*while practicing the deep perfection of wisdom, saw that the five aggregates are all empty*": both are Chinese Buddhism's ontological relocation of "the subject of thought/contemplation."
- **S5 (*Laṅkāvatāra*):** The S5 series reserves the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "mind-as-juggler" passage as material for a sixth-consciousness *kōan* treatment; this paper's §3.1 strictly avoids that passage (see the P17-vs-S5 *Laṅkāvatāra* demarcation in NIAN_PLAN §VI), using only the "mind of all buddhas is foremost" passage as the textual root for Daoxin's dual-scripture declaration, without intruding into the reserved S5 ground.
