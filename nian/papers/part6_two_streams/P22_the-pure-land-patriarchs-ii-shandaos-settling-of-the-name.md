## Abstract
Anglophone introductions to Pure Land Buddhism routinely attribute the doctrinal proposition that *ordinary unliberated beings enter the recompense land* (*fánfū rù bàotǔ* 凡夫入報土) to the Japanese Kamakura tradition — above all to Hōnen's (法然, 1133–1212) *Senchakushū* (1198) and Shinran's (親鸞, 1173–1263) absolute-other-power philosophy in the *Kyōgyōshinshō*. This reception-history retrojection has, for over a century of Anglophone scholarship, displaced the seventh-century Tang anchoring at its scriptural source. The present paper takes Shandao's (善導, 613–681) *Guanjing Sitie Shu* (觀無量壽佛經疏, T1753) — specifically its first-fascicle *Xuányì fēn* (玄義分, profound-meaning section) — as the documentary skeleton for arguing that Shandao's doctrinal moves on *fánfū rù bàotǔ* and *yīxiàng zhuānchēng* (一向專稱, single-hearted recitation) rest upon the *doctrinal warrant* established by Tanluan (曇鸞, 476–542) and Daochuo (562–645), but complete a *discontinuity-not-development* by means of two further moves: *exclusivization* of name-recitation as the singular axis, and the lifting of the *samādhi ceiling* that had still bounded Daochuo's seven-fold *niàn-fó* menu. Shandao's discontinuity is institutional, not doctrinal rupture; it is also not the radicalization that Kamakura Pure Land would later inherit. The paper's three conclusions: Shandao's act is institutionalization, not bare commentary; his relation to Tanluan–Daochuo is *inheritance of warrant + completion of exclusivization*, not linear progression; his relation to the later Japanese tradition is that of the scriptural anchor, not the indirect ancestor of a Kamakura invention. Three Anglophone scholars are staged as structurally integrated interlocutors: Julian Pas (1995, anti-rupture-claim), Charles Jones (2019, integrated-matrix), and Kenneth Tanaka (1990, pre-Shandao baseline). The productive tension among them sustains the paper's third position: doctrinal grammar is continuous, the practice matrix is integrated, but the institutional center has shifted.

## I. Introduction: The Anglophone Reader's Retrojection Bias

The Anglophone reader's first encounter with the Chinese Pure Land proposition that *ordinary unliberated beings enter the recompense land* (*fánfū rù bàotǔ* 凡夫入報土) typically arrives through a specific historical channel: first Hōnen's (法然, 1133–1212) radical reorganization of the three Pure Land sūtras in the *Senchaku hongan nenbutsu shū* (選擇本願念佛集, 1198); then Shinran's (親鸞, 1173–1263) elevation of that proposition to the philosophical apex of *absolute other-power* in the *Kyōgyōshinshō* (教行信証); and finally Suzuki Daisetsu's (鈴木大拙, 1870–1966) mid-twentieth-century English mediation, after which Jōdo Shinshū's overseas mission established *bombu nyū hōdo* as a signature term of Anglophone Pure Land vocabulary. This reception-history pathway carries a structural consequence: when an Anglophone reader opens an introductory volume on Chinese Pure Land Buddhism, they tend to misread *fánfū rù bàotǔ* as a Kamakura doctrinal invention rather than as a scriptural anchoring already laid down five centuries earlier in Tang Chang'an.

At the textual layer, however, the proposition is anchored five hundred years before Hōnen and Shinran — its documentary terminus lies not in twelfth- or thirteenth-century Kamakura but in seventh-century Chang'an. Shandao (善導, 613–681), in fascicle one of the *Guanjing Sitie Shu* (觀無量壽佛經疏, T1753), in the sixth section of the *Xuányì fēn* (玄義分, the introductory profound-meaning section), under the heading *huì tōng èr-shèng zhǒng bù-shēng yì* (會通二乘種不生義, "reconciling the meaning of 'no rebirth from the seed of the two vehicles'"), states the proposition explicitly. Shandao there judges Amitābha's land to be *recompense, not transformation* (*shì bào fēi huà* 是報非化); and he then poses, and answers, the difficulty of how a *defilement-obstructed ordinary being* (*gòu-zhàng fánfū* 垢障凡夫) might gain entry — answering with the warrant of *all five vehicles equally entering* (*wǔ-shèng qí rù* 五乘齊入). This is the scriptural anchoring of *fánfū rù bàotǔ*. Hōnen and Shinran, working seven and eight centuries later, are inheritors and radicalizers of that anchoring, not its inventors.[^1]

The correction of this retrojection is not merely a historical detail; it unsettles a doctrinal genealogy long in circulation in Anglophone Pure Land literature. If Shandao is the scriptural anchor, then Chinese Pure Land already carried, in the Tang dynasty, a complete three-layer system of doctrine, practice, and institution; the Kamakura radicalization is a seven-centuries-later re-reading of that system in another linguistic and institutional setting, not its source. The present paper performs that re-anchoring by taking Shandao's *Guanjing Sitie Shu* together with his ritual-praise compilations (T1980 *Wǎngshēng lǐzàn jié* / 往生禮讚偈; T1979 *Zhuǎnjīng xíngdào yuàn wǎngshēng jìngtǔ fǎshì zàn* / 轉經行道願往生淨土法事讚; T1981 *Yī Guānjīng děng míng bānzhōu sānmèi xíngdào wǎngshēng zàn* / 依觀經等明般舟三昧行道往生讚; T1959 *Guānniàn Ēmítuó fó xiānghǎi sānmèi gōngdé fǎmén* / 觀念阿彌陀佛相海三昧功德法門) as its documentary skeleton, and reconstructs the *discontinuity-not-development* that Shandao completes upon the doctrinal warrant of Tanluan (曇鸞, 476–542) and Daochuo (562–645). Shandao's relation to his predecessors is *inheritance of warrant + completion of exclusivization* (一向專一化) and *lifting of the samādhi ceiling*; his relation to the later Japanese tradition is that of the scriptural anchor, not the indirect ancestor of a Kamakura invention.

This re-anchoring continues, in symmetry, the moves of the two prior papers in Part VI's *Two Streams* arc. P20 reconstructed the Tang vocabulary of *other-power* (*tariki* 他力) as a *dependent-arising co-operation* structure (*adhipati-pratyaya*-co-operation), tracing the *tariki / jiriki* binary back to Suzuki's Kamakura lens in order to recover its original textual face as *contributory condition*. P21 reconstructed Chan's *inward redirection* of the same Pure Land vocabulary, treating *self-nature Amitābha* (自性彌陀) and *who is recollecting the buddha* (念佛是誰) as readings of *the same scriptural source under two directions* — Chan's act being *redirection, not rejection* (再定位而非廢除). The present P22, in this triadic unfolding, occupies the symmetric counterposition: Shandao's act is *discontinuity* — not a fundamental rupture from the Sacred Path gate (the Pas position will receive careful treatment in §IV), but an exclusivizing pivot, internal to Tanluan–Daochuo's doctrinal warrant, that turns *name-holding* from one item on a menu of recollection-practices into the *default institutional axis*. Together, the three moves — *dependent-arising co-operation*, *inward redirection*, and *discontinuity-pivot* — constitute Part VI's threefold motif, and form the shared groundwork for §VI's open question pointing forward to P23 (Tiantai's four samādhis as bridge) and P24 (the late-Ming confluence).

## II. Textual Foundation: The Six Sources of T1753 Architecture

Shandao's *Guanjing Sitie Shu* (T1753) is a four-fascicle commentary on the *Guan Wuliangshou fo jing* (觀無量壽佛經, T0365, translated by Kālayāśas in the Liu Song). The work is organized in four large parts: the *Xuányì fēn* (fascicle one, doctrinal classification), the *Xùfēn yì* (序分義, fascicle two, exposition of the prologue), the *Dìngshàn yì* (定善義, fascicle three, exposition of meditative-good practice), and the *Sànshàn yì* (散善義, fascicle four, exposition of distributed-good practice). The narrative arc moves from doctrinal classification down to practice-architecture, and from contemplative method down to the liturgy of the name. The passages central to this paper are concentrated in fascicle one (doctrinal anchoring) and fascicle four (practice architecture and institutional pivot), supplemented by the *observation-to-recitation* pivot passage in T1980 *Wǎngshēng lǐzàn jié*, the *time-near-and-time-far inversion* in T1958 *Ānlè jí*, and the *easy-practice* and *ten-recollections* passages in T1819 *Wǎngshēng lùn zhù*.[^2] Together these constitute P22's six-source documentary spine.

In the *Xuányì fēn* fascicle one, sixth section, *huì tōng èr-shèng zhǒng bù-shēng yì*, Shandao adjudicates the ontological status of Amitābha's land:

> 「彌陀淨國為當是報、是化也？」答曰：「是報非化。云何得知？……一一願言：『若我得佛，十方眾生稱我名號，願生我國，下至十念，若不生者不取正覺。』今既成佛，即是酬因之身也。」[^3]
>
> *("Is Amitābha's pure realm a recompense-land or a transformation-land?" Answer: "It is recompense, not transformation. How is this known? … Each [of the forty-eight] vows says: 'If I attain buddhahood, may sentient beings of the ten directions who call my name, vowing to be born in my land, even down to ten recollections — if they should not be born there, may I not attain perfect awakening.' Now that he has indeed attained buddhahood, his is precisely the body that is the fruition of [vow-]causes."*; translation mine.)

This is the textual anchor for *Amitābha's land is recompense, not transformation*. The argumentative chain is clean: the forty-eight vows constitute Dharmākara's causal vow-power; now that he has attained buddhahood, he manifests his recompense body (*saṃbhogakāya*); the land that this body manifests is therefore a recompense-land (*saṃbhogakāya-kṣetra*), not a transformation-land (*nirmāṇakāya-kṣetra*). The judgment itself is not isolated within Tang commentarial literature — Jingying Huiyuan (淨影慧遠, 523–592), Zhiyi (智顗, 538–597), and Jizang (吉藏, 549–623) had each addressed the ontological status of Amitābha's land in their own *Guanjing* commentaries. What is distinctive in Shandao's hand, however, comes in the very next paragraph:

> 問曰：「彼佛及土既言報者。報法高妙，小聖難階。垢障凡夫云何得入？」答曰：「若論眾生垢障，實難欣趣，正由託佛願以作強緣，致使五乘齊入。」[^4]
>
> *(Question: "Since that Buddha and his land are said to be of the recompense kind — the recompense Dharma is lofty and subtle, hard to ascend even for the lesser sages. How then can a defilement-obstructed ordinary being gain entry?" Answer: "If we speak in terms of beings' defilements and obstructions, it is indeed hard for them to be drawn there. It is precisely by their reliance upon the Buddha's vow as a *strong condition* that the five vehicles equally enter."*; translation mine.)

This is the doctrinal anchoring of *fánfū rù bàotǔ*. The reasoning proceeds: the recompense-land is lofty, the ordinary being's obstructions are real and grave, but the *strong condition* (*qiáng yuán* 強緣 = *adhipati-pratyaya* 增上緣) of Amitābha's fundamental vow operates such that all *five vehicles* (the *śrāvaka*, *pratyekabuddha*, bodhisattva, human, and deva) enter the recompense-land equally. The key technical term *qiáng yuán* belongs to the same Tang vocabulary that P20 reconstructed for *tariki* — not a binary-toggle of self-power-versus-other-power but a structure of *co-operation*: the practitioner's vowing-and-reciting activity is the practitioner's own act, and the original vow-power is the dominant condition that supplies that act with a powerful *receiving end*, thereby allowing the act to bear its fruit in the recompense-land. Within this frame, *fánfū rù bàotǔ* is not paradox but co-operative result: the original vow does not displace the ordinary being's act; it provides the *strong condition* under which that act becomes effective.

In the same fascicle one, fifth section *dìng wèi fánfū bù wèi shèngrén kēwén* (定為凡夫不為聖人科文, "the section adjudicating that the [text] is for ordinary beings, not for sages"), Shandao adjudicates the *Guanjing*'s pedagogical addressee:

> 又看此《觀經》定善，及三輩上下文意，總是佛去世後，五濁凡夫，但以遇緣有異，致令九品差別。何者？上品三人，是遇大凡夫。中品三人，是遇小凡夫。下品三人，是遇惡凡夫。[^5]

This is the documentary anchoring of *jiǔ-pǐn quán fán* (九品全凡, "all nine grades are ordinary beings") — a more radical doctrinal move than the pre-Shandao commentarial baseline that Tanaka (1990) reconstructs (in which Jingying Huiyuan, Zhiyi, and Jizang read the nine grades as a *gradient of Sacred-Path cultivation*, with the lowest grades figured as exception rather than paradigm). Shandao reassigns the entire nine-grade taxonomy to the category of the ordinary being, and explains the differentiation via *yù-yuán yǒu yì* (遇緣有異, "encountering differing conditions") — those ordinary beings who encounter great-vehicle good are upper-grade, those who encounter lesser-vehicle good are middle-grade, those who encounter evil are lower-grade. The result is that *fánfū*, hitherto a marginal category in the Sacred-Path taxonomy, is elevated to the *paradigm* of the scripture. *Jiǔ-pǐn quán fán* is the practice-side correlate of *fánfū rù bàotǔ*: the ordinary being who enters the recompense-land doctrinally is also the same ordinary being whom the nine grades stratify scripturally — the two anchorings are mutually supporting and constitute Shandao's *fánfū*-centering twofold textual peg.

In fascicle four *Sànshàn yì*, Shandao's documentary move shifts from doctrinal adjudication to practice-architecture. The deep-mind passage of the three-minds section yields a third key textual locus:

> 二者深心，言深心者，即是深信之心也。亦有二種。一者決定深信，自身現是罪惡生死凡夫，曠劫已來常沒常流轉，無有出離之緣。二者決定深信，彼阿彌陀佛四十八願攝受眾生，無疑無慮，乘彼願力定得往生。[^6]

This is the *jīfǎ èrzhǒng shēnxìn* (機法二種深信, "the twofold deep faith in capacity and in dharma") — the most doctrinally dense paragraph within Shandao's three-minds structure (sincere mind, deep mind, mind dedicating-merit-and-vowing-rebirth). Its structure is *twofold deep faith*: deep faith *in one's own condition* (信機, *xìn jī*) — that one is in fact a sinful samsaric ordinary being long submerged through eonic transmigration with no condition for liberation; and deep faith *in the dharma* (信法, *xìn fǎ*) — that Amitābha's forty-eight vows fully receive sentient beings, that, riding their vow-power, one will assuredly attain rebirth. The two deep faiths bear two doctrinal consequences: *first*, they translate the *Xuányì fēn*'s abstract anchoring of *fánfū rù bàotǔ* into the *internal mental disposition* of fascicle four's practice-architecture; *second*, they relocate the grammatical bind of *self-power versus other-power* from the *external act-attribution* layer to the *internal faith-constitution* layer. Faith-in-capacity is *not* a self-power affirmation (it is the precise *negation* of the self's competence); faith-in-dharma is *not* a unidirectional projection of other-power (it is the practitioner's own act of receiving, in faith, the vow-power's reach). Both co-operate within the same act of deep faith, constituting the complete internal condition for the receiving of the original vow.

The three-minds passage is followed by the *èrhé báidào* (二河白道, "two rivers and the white path") parable, which renders this internal structure visually:

> 言中間白道四五寸者，即喻眾生貪瞋煩惱中能生清淨願往生心也。乃由貪瞋強故，即喻如水火。善心微故，喻如白道。[^7]

The parable's narrative architecture (east-bank fire-river / west-bank water-river / mid-stream four-or-five-inch white path / Śākyamuni urging from behind / Amitābha calling from before) encodes, in pictorial form, the internal mental disposition of *jīfǎ èrzhǒng shēnxìn*: the water-river is greed, the fire-river is wrath, the white path is the *pure mind of vowing rebirth* that an ordinary being can yet generate amid greed and wrath. *Shàn xīn wēi gù, yù rú báidào* — "because the wholesome mind is faint, it is figured as the white path" — is the kernel sentence: the white path's narrowness and frailty is *not* a defect of the doctrine but the actual face of the ordinary being's wholesome mind across eonic transmigration. The parable's reception-history reach — into Song-Yuan painting, drama, and lay devotional literature — is one of Shandao's most culturally durable moves; but for the doctrinal-and-institutional argument here, its function is to render the abstract twofold deep faith concretely operable as an everyday mental disposition under the conditions of greed and wrath.

At this point Shandao's documentary work has secured the first two layers of the four-layer move: the *doctrinal anchoring* of *Xuányì fēn* and the *practice architecture* of *Sànshàn yì*'s three minds and *èrhé báidào*. §III now takes up the third layer (the institutional pivot) and the fourth (the lineage-discontinuity vis-à-vis Tanluan and Daochuo).

## III. Core Argument: Four Layers of Discontinuity-Not-Development

This section unfolds Shandao's move in four layers: §3.1 the distinctiveness of the *Xuányì fēn* doctrinal anchoring; §3.2 the internalizing move of the *Sànshàn yì* practice architecture; §3.3 the documentary hinge of the institutional pivot; §3.4 the lineage-discontinuity with Tanluan and Daochuo. The four layers jointly argue a single thesis: Shandao is neither the linear extension of a Tanluan–Daochuo trajectory nor a precursor of Kamakura radicalization. He is, rather, the agent of a distinctive *discontinuity-not-development* internal to Tang Chinese Pure Land — *inheriting* the doctrinal warrant of his predecessors while *completing* an exclusivization and a removal of the samādhi ceiling, by means of which name-holding moves from being one item on a menu of recollection-practices to being the *default institutional axis*.

### 3.1 Doctrinal Anchor: The Distinctiveness of the *Xuányì fēn*

Shandao's adjudication in *Xuányì fēn* sixth section — that Amitābha's land is *recompense, not transformation*, that the warrant for the ordinary being's entry is *all-five-vehicles-equally-entering* (B1) — together with his adjudication in the fifth section that the *Guanjing* addresses ordinary beings throughout its nine grades (B2), occupies a particular textual position within the Tang commentarial scene.

Tanaka (1990), in *The Dawn of Chinese Pure Land Buddhist Doctrine*, devotes a chapter-length reconstruction to the pre-Shandao *Guanjing* commentarial scene. Jingying Huiyuan, in his *Guan wuliangshou jing yishu*, reads the *Guanjing* through the Sacred-Path frame: the sixteen meditative contemplations are read as the gradient contemplative practice of bodhisattva cultivation, and the nine grades are read as bodhisattva-stage gradients — upper-grade as those already in the ten-stages (*daśabhūmi*), middle-grade as those in the three-worthies stage, lower-grade as exception rather than paradigm. Zhiyi, in his *Guan wuliangshou fojing shu*, reads the *Guanjing* through the Tiantai *five profundities*, treating the three-grade-nine-pin schema as a gradient of self-cultivation-and-other-conversion; the lower grades, again, as accommodative skillful means rather than paradigm. Jizang, in his *Guan wuliangshou jing yishu*, reads the *Guanjing* through the Three-Treatise *establishment-and-refutation* frame, classifying Amitābha's land as one accommodative manifestation of buddha-activity rather than as ultimate. Across all three pre-Shandao commentaries, the shared assumption is that the *Guanjing*'s pedagogical addressee is *the gradient of cultivation-capable beings*, with the ordinary being as exception. Tanaka's contribution is the lucid display of that pre-Shandao consensus; his subsequent essays then position Shandao's *Guanjing Sitie Shu* as the *deliberate inversion* of that consensus — the lowest grade becoming the scripture's *paradigm*, and the entire ordinary-being category becoming doctrinally central.

This is the distinctiveness of Shandao's *Xuányì fēn*: not a fine adjustment internal to the commentarial register but a *systematic inversion* of the early-Tang commentarial consensus. The recompense-land is the destination of the ordinary being; the ordinary being is the addressee of the *Guanjing*; the nine grades are the gradient of the ordinary being. The three adjudications jointly compose a *fánfū-centered doctrinal network*.

### 3.2 Practice Architecture: The Internalizing Move of the *Sànshàn yì*

Descending from the doctrinal anchoring of the *Xuányì fēn* into the practice-architecture of the *Sànshàn yì*, Shandao's move shifts from abstract doctrinal positing to the description of internal mental disposition. The *jīfǎ èrzhǒng shēnxìn* (B3) is the kernel of the practice-architecture: faith-in-capacity (deep faith that one is a sinful samsaric ordinary being) and faith-in-dharma (deep faith that Amitābha's fundamental vows fully receive sentient beings) are *two faces of a single deep faith*, established together within the same practitioner at the same moment within the same mind.

The significance of this move lies in its relocation of the *self-power / other-power* grammatical bind from the *external act-attribution* layer to the *internal faith-constitution* layer. The classical Sacred-Path concern — *if all is borne by other-power, then the practitioner has no act of his own* — and the classical Pure Land concern — *if the practitioner's act is emphasized, then the warrant of other-power is undercut* — receive an elegant reconstrual within the twofold deep faith: the practitioner's *act* is precisely the establishment of the twofold deep faith — an act in his own mind that no other can perform on his behalf — yet the *content* of that act is the *negation of self-competence* and the *reception of vow-power*. The practitioner's act (self-power, in the structural sense) and the act's content (other-power, in the doctrinal sense) co-operate within a single deep faith, rather than dividing into a binary choice.

The *èrhé báidào* parable (B4) renders this internal architecture pictorially. *Shàn xīn wēi gù, yù rú báidào* carries great doctrinal weight: the white path's narrowness and frailty is not a doctrinal flaw but the actual condition of the ordinary being's wholesome mind. The narrow, frail white path can yet conduct the practitioner to the western bank — not by virtue of the white path's strength, but by virtue of the *bilateral strong-condition field* generated by Śākyamuni's urging from the eastern bank and Amitābha's calling from the western. The parable's narrative architecture maps point-for-point onto the doctrinal architecture of the three minds: the strength of fire and water corresponds to the actual condition of greed and wrath (faith-in-capacity); the bilateral urging and calling correspond to Amitābha's fundamental vow's full reception (faith-in-dharma); the white path's capacity to conduct corresponds to the internal establishment of twofold deep faith.

The documentary completeness of the *Sànshàn yì*'s practice-architecture is now in place. Together with the *Xuányì fēn*'s doctrinal anchoring, it forms a mutually-supporting two-layer structure: the *Xuányì fēn* establishes the doctrinal warrant of *fánfū rù bàotǔ*; the *Sànshàn yì* establishes the *interior mental disposition under which the ordinary being concretely enters the recompense land amid daily defilements*. §3.3 now takes up the third layer: the institutionalization of that mental disposition into a name-holding axis.

### 3.3 Institutional Pivot: The *Wénshū-prajñā* Hinge of T1980

The documentary hinge of Shandao's third move — the institutional pivot — is located *not* in the body of T1753 but in the preface of his ritual-praise compilation T1980 *Wǎngshēng lǐzàn jié*. The passage there constitutes the most explicit articulation of the *observation-to-recitation* pivot in the entire Shandao corpus:

> 又如文殊般若云。明一行三昧。唯勸。獨處空閑。捨諸亂意。係心一佛。不觀相貌。專稱名字。即於念中。得見彼阿彌陀佛及一切佛等。問曰。何故不令作觀。直遣專稱名字者。有何意也。答曰。乃由眾生障重。境細心麁。識颺神飛。觀難成就也。[^8]
>
> *(Again, as the* Wénshū-prajñā [-pāramitā-sūtra]*says — clarifying the* eka-vyūha-samādhi *— it solely urges: dwell alone in stillness, set aside all distracting thoughts, bind the mind to a single buddha, do not contemplate the appearance, but concentrate on reciting the name; then within recollection one obtains the vision of that Amitābha-buddha and of all buddhas alike. Question: Why is one not directed to contemplate, and only sent forth to concentrate on reciting the name? What is the intent? Answer: It is because beings' obstructions are heavy, the [contemplative] objects are subtle, the mind is coarse, consciousness is scattered, and the spirit takes flight — contemplation is hard to bring to completion.*; translation mine.)

This passage carries three documentary significances. *First*, Shandao cites the *eka-vyūha-samādhi* (*yī-xíng sānmèi* 一行三昧) of the *Mañjuśrī-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra* (T0232, the same scripture P21 reconstructed as the *common scriptural source under two readings*: Chan reading it as "do-not-contemplate-appearances" inward turn, Pure Land reading it as "concentrate-on-reciting-the-name" outward orientation). Shandao's act in this T1980 preface is precisely the explicit display of the Pure Land reading on that same scriptural ground: the parallelism *do not contemplate the appearance / concentrate on reciting the name* relocates the original textual material of one-practice samādhi onto the name-holding axis. *Second*, Shandao completes the *observation-to-recitation* pivot by means of *self-posed question and answer*: "Why is one not directed to contemplate, and only sent forth to concentrate on reciting the name?" The framing of this question presupposes a hearer — a traditional Pure Land practitioner familiar with contemplative method, who finds the redirection toward sole name-recitation surprising — and the answer is direct: not a defect in contemplative method itself, but the psychological condition of the ordinary being in the present age, for whom *contemplation is hard to bring to completion*. The institutional inference is that name-recitation should be the default entry. *Third*, this passage *does not appear* in the body of T1753, nor in the *Xùfēn yì* or *Dìngshàn yì*; it appears, rather, in the *preface* of a ritual-praise compilation. Its location signals that Shandao's institutionalizing move occupies a particular institutional venue: not the commentarial venue of fine doctrinal adjudication, but the venue of *daily liturgical practice*.

Within the body of T1753 fascicle four, this institutionalizing move has three corresponding loci:

> 又就此正中，復有二種：一者一心專念彌陀名號，行住坐臥不問時節久近念念不捨者，是名正定之業，順彼佛願故。若依禮誦等，即名為助業。除此正助二行已外，自餘諸善悉名雜行。[^9]

This is the *taxonomic upgrade* of *wǔ zhèngxíng / zhèngdìng yè / zhùyè / záxíng* — the *five right practices* (recitation-of-the-three-sūtras, contemplation-of-the-pure-recompense, ritual-prostration, name-recitation, praise-and-offering) further subdivided by a *primary-act-of-right-concentration / assisting-acts* binary that elevates name-recitation: name-recitation is the *primary act of right concentration*, ritual-prostration and recitation of the sūtras are *assisting acts*, and any wholesome practice outside the five right practices (the six perfections of the Sacred Path, the precepts and meditative practices, etc.) is reclassified as *miscellaneous practice*. The institutional consequence of this taxonomy is that name-recitation, hitherto *one item among seven* in Daochuo's *seven-fold niàn-fó menu* (T1958 fascicle one), is elevated to the *singular primary act of right concentration*, with all other practices reclassified as either *assisting* or *miscellaneous*.

In the same fascicle four, in the *wàng fó yuàn yì* (望佛願意, "regarding the intent of the Buddha's vow") passage:

> 然望佛願意者，唯勸正念稱名往生……處廣歎，勸令稱名，將為要益也。應知。[^10]

This is the documentary anchor of *wàng fó yuàn yì wéi quàn zhèngniàn chēngmíng* ("regarding the intent of the Buddha's vow, [the scripture] solely urges right-mindful name-recitation"). The grammar of *wàng fó yuàn yì* repays attention: *wàng* means *facing toward* or *seen from*, and *fó yuàn yì* means *the intent of Amitābha's fundamental vow*. The clause does not say "what the Buddha desires is"; it says "*viewed from the perspective of Amitābha's fundamental vow, the intent is …*." The framing thereby relocates the status of name-recitation from *the practitioner's elective choice* to *the very intent of the original vow*, conferring upon name-holding a binding doctrinal force.

In the closing *liútōng fēn* (流通分, transmission section) of fascicle four, Shandao secures the entire work's documentary terminus with the phrase *yīxiàng zhuānchēng*:

> 益，望佛本願，意在眾生一向專稱彌陀佛……[^11]

This is the documentary terminus of all fourteen name-holding-related passages distributed across Shandao's commentary. The institutional weight of *yīxiàng zhuānchēng* — *single-direction, sole-recitation* — is considerable; the phrase would later be cited by Hōnen's *Senchakushū* as a kernel sentence and radicalized by Kamakura Pure Land into the *shè-bì-gé-pāo* (捨閉閣抛, "abandon, close, set aside, cast off") declaration of *exclusive name-recitation* (the four-character formula prescribing the abandonment of Sacred-Path teachings, the closure of remaining practices, the setting aside of assisting acts, and the casting off of miscellaneous practices). Within Shandao's T1753 textual context, however, *yīxiàng zhuānchēng* does *not* abolish the assisting acts (ritual-prostration and the rest remain within the right-practice category as auxiliary), nor does it abolish the miscellaneous practices (wholesome acts remain wholesome); its act is institutional *re-centering* rather than doctrinal abolition. This fine distinction is the foremost item of the middle-way correction in §V.

The *observation-to-recitation* hinge of T1980, the *five-right-practice* taxonomic upgrade in T1753, the *wàng fó yuàn yì* framing, and the *yīxiàng zhuānchēng* documentary terminus — together with the seven-day ritual selection of T1979 and the seven-day-altar liturgy of T1959 — compose Shandao's institutional pivot. The result is the Tang Chang'an liturgical infrastructure of a name-holding-centered practice system. This is the concrete face, in Shandao's hand, of what Charles Jones (2019) describes as the *integrated contemplation-and-practice matrix*: the matrix is integrated, but its *center of gravity* has been reorganized around name-recitation.

### 3.4 Lineage: Tanluan–Daochuo *Warrant* vs Shandao *Exclusivization*

Shandao's lineage relation with Tanluan and Daochuo is read, in both Anglophone and Sinophone scholarship, as a *gradient construction of name-recitation doctrine* — Tanluan setting up the easy-practice-path, Daochuo establishing the *time-far-name-is-primary* inversion, Shandao completing the systematization. This narrative is succinct but inaccurate. Close reading of the texts shows: Tanluan and Daochuo provide the *doctrinal warrant* (*jiàoyì bǎozhèng* 教義保證); Shandao completes the *exclusivization*. The relation is *inheritance of warrant + completion of exclusivization*, not linear progression.

In Tanluan's *Wǎngshēng lùn zhù* (T1819) fascicle one, the *easy-practice-path* passage:

> 易行道者，謂但以信佛因緣願生淨土，乘佛願力便得往生彼清淨土。佛力住持，即入大乘正定之聚……[^12]

The textual content of this passage repays close attention. Tanluan defines the *easy-practice-path* by three components: faith-in-Buddha-as-condition, vowing-rebirth-in-the-pure-land, and riding-the-Buddha's-vow-power. *Within these three, name-recitation is not listed as a kernel component.* In the same fascicle, his ten-recollections definition (T1819 0834c13) reads "*recollecting Amitābha's two-fold adornment-of-recompense, without any other thought or mixed mentation*"; name-recitation is appended only as an *also like this* secondary inclusion, with the kernel still being recollection-and-contemplation.[^13] Tanluan's act is to establish the *doctrinal warrant of other-power* — vow-power as the *strong condition* (*adhipati-pratyaya*) by which the ordinary being enters the pure land; and this warrant is the textual basis of Shandao's later move. But Tanluan himself does *not* place name-recitation as the singular entry of the easy-practice-path; name-holding within Tanluan's textual scene remains *one item* on the seven-fold *niàn-fó* menu.

In Daochuo's *Ānlè jí* (T1958) fascicle one, the *time-near-and-time-far inversion*:

> 又若去聖近，即前者修定、修慧是其正學，後者是兼；如去聖已遠，則後者稱名是正，前者是兼。[^14]

This passage establishes the *mòfǎ-name-is-primary* doctrinal inversion. The argumentative chain: *near-the-time-of-the-sage*, concentration-and-wisdom cultivation is primary practice, name-recitation is auxiliary; in the *mòfǎ* age, *far from the time of the sage*, the inversion places name-recitation as primary and concentration-and-wisdom as auxiliary. The inversion carries doctrinal weight: it adjusts, in institutional terms, the priority of name-recitation within the practice menu by reference to the temporal-conditional factor of *mòfǎ*. Yet Daochuo's act preserves *two important reservations*. *First*, the seven-fold *niàn-fó* menu (recollection of the dharma-body, of supernormal power, of wisdom, of the white-tuft mark, of marks-and-features, of the original vow, and of the name) remains *intact and parallel*, with name-recollection as one item among seven. *Second*, the *samādhi ceiling* persists — Daochuo's recollection-practice in the *Ānlè jí* still treats *entry into name-recitation samādhi* as its cultivative ceiling, with name-recitation as the means of entry into that samādhi rather than as the supplanting of samādhi as the practice's terminus.

Shandao's distinctive lineage acts are two. *First*, *exclusivization*: by means of the *five-right-practices / primary-act / assisting-acts / miscellaneous-practices* taxonomic upgrade, name-recitation moves from being *one of seven recollection-practices* to being the *sole primary act of right concentration*; the other six recollection forms are either absorbed into the assisting acts (*observation*, *prostration*, etc.) or reclassified as miscellaneous (the recollection forms aligned with Sacred-Path cultivation). The menu structure is dissolved; the *singular axis* is established. *Second*, *the lifting of the samādhi ceiling*: Shandao's name-holding does not posit *entry into name-recitation samādhi* as its ceiling — the description *"walking, standing, sitting, lying, without distinction of duration of time, every recollection unceasingly maintained"* describes a continuous everyday practice rather than the apex of a samādhi ascent; name-holding *itself is the primary act of right concentration*, not a means of entry into samādhi. The two acts together institutionalize Tanluan's other-power warrant and Daochuo's *mòfǎ* inversion as a *new institutional system with name-holding as the everyday default axis*.

This is the lineage face of Shandao's *discontinuity*. His relation to Tanluan and Daochuo is not the *linear gradient* of "seed → sprout → maturation"; the textual evidence shows the three patriarchs occupying *different doctrinal positions*. Tanluan establishes the warrant but does not center name-recitation; Daochuo establishes the inversion but preserves the menu and the ceiling; Shandao completes the exclusivization and the ceiling-removal that together institutionalize name-holding as the system's center of gravity. The relation is *inheritance of warrant + completion of exclusivization*, not linear progression. *Discontinuity-not-development* receives, in this lineage close-reading, its clearest documentary support.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Interlocutors: Three Anglophone Voices

This section stages three Anglophone scholars as structurally integrated interlocutors, addressing the three principal positions on Shandao within Anglophone scholarship. The three positions stand in *productive tension* with one another: Pas (1995) cautions against reading Shandao through a Kamakura lens, holding that he must be understood internally to the Tang commentarial scene and that his act is not a rupture from the Sacred-Path gate; Jones (2019) cautions against Japan-centric Pure Land assumptions, holding that Shandao's name-holding remains embedded throughout in an *integrated contemplation-and-practice matrix*; Tanaka (1990) reconstructs the pre-Shandao commentarial *baseline* against which Shandao's *fánfū*-centering is rendered visible. The productive tension among the three structures the section.

### 4.1 Julian Pas (1995): The Kamakura-Retrojection Caution

Pas, in *Visions of Sukhāvatī*, devotes a chapter-length reconstruction to Shandao's *Guanjing Sitie Shu* within its Tang commentarial setting. His core position is that Shandao should be read as a *rigorous Tang commentator*, internally to the Chinese commentarial context (Jingying Huiyuan, Zhiyi, Jizang); that *fánfū rù bàotǔ*, while certainly Shandao's own move, remains a *commentarial-grammatical* adjustment internal to that scene, not a fundamental rupture from the Sacred-Path gate; that the sixteen contemplations retain a doctrinally central position within Shandao's system, with name-holding as the ordinary being's everyday operationalization rather than as a displacement of contemplative method. Pas's framing aims to correct an Anglophone Pure Land misreading that would treat Shandao as the *anticipation of Kamakura Pure Land* and his institutionalizing act as the *opening of a Chan/Pure Land opposition*; he holds that this retrojection misplaces Shandao's historical position and severs his hermeneutic continuity with the Tang commentarial scene.

Pas's position deserves *concession*: Shandao's continuity with prior Chinese commentators is real and methodologically significant; reading Shandao solely through a Kamakura lens distorts what he actually wrote. The opening retrojection-correction of §I of this paper concedes precisely this — Pas's caution is the framing point of departure for the present paper, not its target.

But Pas's position has a limit at the *push-back* layer: his reservations about *discontinuity* underestimate the institutional consequences. Even granting that *fánfū rù bàotǔ* is articulated within commentarial grammar — Shandao does indeed write in the verse-by-verse commentary genre — Shandao's liturgical output (T1979 / T1980 / T1981 / T1959, including the five-tone praise-liturgy) and Chang'an's practice infrastructure pivot the Pure Land axis into a new institutional order organized around name-holding; the institutional weight of this order is something neither Jingying Huiyuan, Zhiyi, nor Jizang accomplished. The boundary of Pas's commentarial frame is precisely here: commentarial continuity is real, *and* institutional discontinuity is real — the two co-exist rather than excluding one another. The *discontinuity* this paper's thesis names is *institutional discontinuity*, not *doctrinal rupture*; Shandao's doctrine remains expressed within the grammar of the Tang commentarial scene, even as his institutional act has shifted the center of gravity. This fine distinction is the present paper's principal divergence from Pas's position.

### 4.2 Charles B. Jones (2019): The Integrated-Matrix Reweighting

Jones, in *Chinese Pure Land Buddhism*, devotes the whole work to refuting the Japan-centric assumption pervasive in Pure Land studies — the assumption that Hōnen and Shinran are doctrinal terminus and Chinese Pure Land merely their preliminary or insufficient antecedent. Three core claims structure his work. *First*, that Shandao's name-holding is throughout embedded in an *integrated contemplation-and-practice matrix* (visualization, repentance, liturgy, vow-making, precept-keeping); his auxiliary works T1959 / T1979 / T1980 stand as direct evidence of this integration. *Second*, that the Chinese patriarchal succession (Huiyuan 334–416 → Shandao → Chéngyuǎn 712–802 → Fǎzhào ?–c.821 → … → Yìnguāng 1861–1940) constitutes a *non-single-axis* trajectory: the line is not a unidirectional development toward *absolute other-power* but a plural co-existence. *Third*, that the *self-power / other-power* lexicon under-describes Chinese Pure Land; the original Chinese vocabulary works in *strong-condition / weak-condition*, *meditative-good / distributed-good*, *right-practice / assisting-act / miscellaneous-practice* terms, with Suzuki's *self-power / other-power* binary representing a Japanese-language mapping rather than a Chinese-original semantic (a point that supports, in mutual reinforcement, P20's reconstruction of the *tariki* structure).

Jones's position deserves *concession*: the Chinese inheritance does indeed embed name-holding in an integrated practice matrix; Shandao's own T1959 / T1979 / T1980 confirm this; any account that reads Shandao as pure *exclusive practice* (*sēnju* in Hōnen's later sense) must ignore his liturgical works. The textual skeleton of §III in this paper (T1980's preface and T1979's seven-day liturgy) draws directly on the same integration evidence Jones identifies.

But Jones's position likewise has a limit at the *push-back* layer: even granting that the matrix is integrated, the institutional center has nonetheless shifted. The five-tone praise-liturgy and the Chang'an practice-altars *organize the matrix around* name-holding in a way that no prior Chinese commentator had done; the *primary-act / assisting-act / miscellaneous-practice* taxonomy is the documentary evidence of that re-centering. *Fánfū rù bàotǔ* is the doctrinal warrant of that re-centering — the warrant *is* the pivot, even though it is not a rupture. The boundary of Jones's matrix-frame is precisely here: the matrix's integration is real, *and* the center of gravity has shifted, also real. The *institutional discontinuity* this paper's thesis names is precisely that center-of-gravity shift, not the dissolution of the matrix.

Pas and Jones together compose the *productive tension* of §IV: Pas cautions against *over-rupturing*, Jones cautions against *over-integrating*; the tension between them serves as the bilateral correction for the present paper's thesis (*institutional discontinuity within doctrinal continuity*). The act of this paper is to maintain a *third position* between the two: doctrinal grammar is continuous (concede Pas), the practice matrix is integrated (concede Jones), but the institutional center has shifted (push back on both).

### 4.3 Kenneth Tanaka (1990): The Pre-Shandao Baseline and the Practice-Dimension Supplement

Tanaka's *The Dawn of Chinese Pure Land Buddhist Doctrine* serves as the present paper's third, secondary interlocutor. His contribution is twofold. *First*, he reconstructs the pre-Shandao *Guanjing* commentarial scene (Jingying Huiyuan, Zhiyi, Jizang), demonstrating that the three commentators read the nine grades as a *gradient of Sacred-Path cultivation*, with the lower grades as *exception rather than paradigm*; this reconstruction supplies the *baseline contrast* against which Shandao's *jiǔ-pǐn quán fán* move becomes visible. *Second*, his subsequent essays position Shandao's *Guanjing Sitie Shu* as the *deliberate inversion* of that consensus — the lowest grade becoming the scripture's paradigm and the entire ordinary-being category being doctrinally centered.

Tanaka's baseline work deserves *concession*: the pre-Shandao commentarial scene is the proper baseline; Shandao's *fánfū*-centering becomes salient only in contrast against that baseline, and Tanaka has done the foundational work for that contrast. The textual close-reading in §3.1 of this paper directly uses Tanaka's reconstructed baseline as its background.

But Tanaka's framework has, at the *push-back* layer, a complementary need: it under-develops the *practice* dimension. Doctrinal centering of the ordinary being is *half* the move; the institutional shift toward *name-holding as default* is the *other half* — and this second half is what makes Shandao a *pivot* (*turn*) rather than a *commentarial variant*. Tanaka has handled the doctrinal inversion in sufficient close-reading detail, but he has not given equal weight to the moves at the *practice* and *institution* layers. The act of this paper is, accordingly, to supplement Tanaka's baseline with the practice dimension — completing, through §3.3's institutional-pivot analysis, the analysis Tanaka's framework leaves under-developed.

The shared contribution of the three interlocutors is to liberate Shandao studies from a *mono-perspective* framework — neither "Kamakura precursor" nor "pure exclusive practice" nor "isolated doctrinal development" — and to render him as a multi-faceted historical-doctrinal-institutional act. The thesis of the present paper proposes, within this multi-faceted field, a structural framing: *discontinuity-not-development*, the completion of *exclusivization* and *the lifting of the samādhi ceiling* upon Tanluan and Daochuo's doctrinal warrant. The productive tension among the three interlocutors supplies the necessary *epistemic correction* for that framing.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections: Four Cautions

This section completes the paper's middle-way correction by means of four cautions. Each corresponds to a textual trap identified in the documentary record; their shared act is to establish, at the textual level, clear boundaries that prevent the thesis from sliding either toward over-rupturing or toward over-radicalizing.

❌ **Caution One: Do Not Attribute *Self-Nature Amitābha* to T1753**

In both Anglophone and Sinophone popular Pure Land literature, the locution *self-nature Amitābha* (*zì-xìng Mítuó* 自性彌陀) is regularly attributed directly to Shandao's *Guanjing Sitie Shu*. The textual reality: a full-text search of T1753 yields no occurrence of the four-character string *zì-xìng Mítuó*; further, the six-character formula *Námó Ēmítuó fó* (南無阿彌陀佛) is not present in the body of T1753 either, but appears in the auxiliary works T1980 *Wǎngshēng lǐzàn jié* and T1979 *Zhuǎnjīng xíngdào yuàn wǎngshēng jìngtǔ fǎshì zàn*. *Self-nature Amitābha* is the framing of Chan's inward-redirection reading — already documented in P21 §3.3 as belonging to the *Platform Sūtra* *Inquiry* chapter and Zongmi's *Chan Prolegomenon*. It is not Shandao's act. The present paper's discussion of Shandao's *name-holding doctrine* is permitted; citing *self-nature Amitābha* must be located within P21's Chan context, not within P22's Shandao context. Citing the *six-character name-formula* must source from T1980, not T1753.

⚠️ **Caution Two: The Linear-Gradient Narrative of Tanluan → Daochuo → Shandao**

In Tang-Song-and-after Sinophone Pure Land sectarian-genealogy narratives, Tanluan, Daochuo, and Shandao are routinely read as a linear gradient — Tanluan as seed, Daochuo as sprout, Shandao as maturation. The textual reality: Tanluan's ten-recollections is defined as *recollection-with-contemplation* (T1819 0834c13), with name-recitation appended only as an *also like this* secondary inclusion; Daochuo's *Ānlè jí* fascicle one preserves the seven-fold *niàn-fó* menu (with name-recollection as one of seven) and the samādhi ceiling. The three patriarchs' textual acts are *not* a linear gradient — Tanluan establishes warrant but does not center name-recitation; Daochuo establishes inversion but preserves menu and ceiling; Shandao completes exclusivization and ceiling-removal. To say that Tanluan and Daochuo *establish the doctrinal warrant* is correct; to say that they *complete the name-recitation pivot* is not. To say that Shandao's act is *institutional discontinuity built on doctrinal warrant* is correct; to subsume his act into a linear-gradient narrative obscures the differential.

❌ **Caution Three: *Fánfū rù bàotǔ* Is Not a Kamakura Invention**

In Anglophone introductory Pure Land literature, the proposition *fánfū rù bàotǔ* is regularly attributed to Japanese Kamakura Pure Land — to Hōnen's *Senchakushū* (1198) and Shinran's *Kyōgyōshinshō*'s absolute-other-power philosophy. The textual reality: T1753 fascicle one *Xuányì fēn* sixth section establishes the proposition explicitly (B1 passage; Shandao's anchoring 613–681); Hōnen 1133–1212 and Shinran 1173–1263 are inheritors and radicalizers seven and eight centuries later. To name Shandao as *the Chinese-scriptural anchor of fánfū rù bàotǔ* and to name the Japanese tradition as *the radicalizing inheritor of that anchoring* — the two are *not the same act*. Kamakura's radicalization (the *shè-bì-gé-pāo* declaration, *absolute other-power*, *faith-alone deliverance*) differs in doctrinal substance from Shandao's institutional re-centering; that differential is a topic the present paper does not develop in depth, but the distinction is necessary for the present paper's thesis (*discontinuity-not-development* as a Tang act, not a Kamakura act).

⚠️ **Caution Four: The Density of Shandao's Citations of Tanluan**

In some sectarian-genealogy narratives, a frequent presupposition holds that Shandao's *Guanjing Sitie Shu* cites Tanluan most extensively. The textual reality: Shandao's commentary cites verses and passages from the Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha (*Wuliangshou jing*, T0360) most heavily; direct quotations from Tanluan's *Wǎngshēng lùn zhù* are not densely distributed. The basis of his institutional move lies primarily in the *independent doctrinal classification of the three sūtras* (the *Wuliangshou*, the *Guanjing*, and the *Amituo*) and in his own citation of T0232's *eka-vyūha-samādhi* (B8). The significance of this correction: do not lean too heavily on a *three-patriarch-continuous-citation* narrative as support for the linear-gradient story; rather, weight the *independent doctrinal classification of the three sūtras* and the *self-citation of one-practice samādhi* — the latter being the most direct documentary basis for Shandao's institutional move.

The four cautions jointly maintain the paper's middle-way correction: Shandao is the agent of *discontinuity-not-development upon Tanluan-Daochuo's doctrinal warrant*, neither the terminus of a linear gradient nor a precursor of Kamakura radicalization, neither the source of Chan's inward-redirection reading nor a direct prolongation of Tanluan-citation. The distinctiveness of his act emerges with clear boundaries through the four corrective edges.

## VI. Conclusion

**Recapitulation**

The present paper, taking the *Xuányì fēn* of the *Guanjing Sitie Shu* (T1753) as its documentary skeleton, has argued that Shandao's (613–681) doctrinal move on *fánfū rù bàotǔ* and *yīxiàng zhuānchēng* is a *discontinuity-not-development* in four layers. *Doctrinal anchoring*: *Xuányì fēn* sixth section's *recompense-not-transformation* and *all-five-vehicles-equally-entering*, plus the fifth section's *all-nine-grades-are-ordinary-beings*. *Practice architecture*: the *Sànshàn yì*'s *twofold deep faith in capacity and dharma*, plus the *èrhé báidào* parable. *Institutional pivot*: T1980's preface as *observation-to-recitation* doctrinal hinge, T1753's *five-right-practices / primary-act / assisting-acts / miscellaneous-practices* taxonomic upgrade, the *wàng fó yuàn yì* framing, the *yīxiàng zhuānchēng* documentary terminus, and the seven-day liturgy of T1979. *Lineage discontinuity*: Tanluan establishes the other-power warrant but does not center name-recitation; Daochuo establishes the *mòfǎ* inversion but preserves the menu and the samādhi ceiling; Shandao completes the *exclusivization* and the *ceiling-removal*. The productive tension among the three Anglophone interlocutors (Pas 1995, Jones 2019, Tanaka 1990) supplies the necessary epistemic correction for this thesis: the doctrinal grammar is continuous, the practice matrix is integrated, but the institutional center has shifted — three claims that co-exist rather than excluding one another.

**Practice Implications**

This argument carries three direct implications for contemporary practitioners. *First*, *fánfū rù bàotǔ* is neither a Kamakura philosophical proprietary nor a Jōdo Shinshū exclusive proposition; its scriptural anchoring was completed in Tang Chinese soil. Contemporary Sinophone or Anglophone practitioners revisiting this proposition need not pass through the lens of Kamakura radicalization. Shandao's source text alone is sufficient as direct documentary basis for modern practice — the *twofold deep faith in capacity and dharma* offers an elegant interior pathway directly applicable in daily name-holding: deep faith in capacity (the deep recognition that one is, in eonic transmigration, without competence for liberation) and deep faith in dharma (the deep faith that Amitābha's fundamental vow fully receives sentient beings) are *two faces of a single deep faith*, not a binary choice between self-power and other-power. *Second*, the historical sense of Shandao's discontinuity offers a framing insight to contemporary practitioners: *institutionalization (exclusivization)* is not *doctrinal radicalization (rejection)*; the establishment of a name-holding axis did not abolish the legitimacy of other practice forms. Contemporary Sinophone Pure Land practitioners, in their daily name-holding, need not construct a sectarian opposition with practitioners of other forms (visualization, prostration, sūtra-recitation, precepts) — the matrix of the *five right practices* is intrinsically integrated; the establishment of *primary-act-of-right-concentration* is an institutional re-centering, not a doctrinal abolition. *Third*, the *èrhé báidào* parable receives, in contemporary defilements, a concrete operational application: the white path's narrowness and frailty *is* the actual condition of the ordinary being's wholesome mind in daily life; one need neither force-widen the white path nor lament its narrowness. Within the bilateral strong-condition field of Śākyamuni's urging from the eastern bank and Amitābha's calling from the western, even a narrow and frail white path can yet conduct one to the western shore — this is Shandao's most direct psychological instruction for daily name-holding practice.

**Open Question**

The present paper occupies the third position in Part VI's *Two Streams* arc, continuing the dual framing of P20 (Tanluan and Daochuo's *dependent-arising co-operation* foundation of other-power) and P21 (Chan's *inward redirection* of the Pure Land vocabulary), and completing the third motif of *discontinuity-not-development*. The forthcoming P23 will address Zhiyi's (538–597) Tiantai bridge of the *four samādhis* (name-recitation / image-contemplation / visualization / reality-contemplation) — the Tiantai move forming a tension-structure with Shandao's *exclusivization*: Tiantai neither cancels name-recitation nor elevates it to a singular axis but, through the parallel-arrangement of the four samādhis, holds name-holding *in its original position within an integrated matrix*. P22's thesis is *institutional discontinuity*; P23's thesis will be *doctrinal pluralism within practice*. P24 will address Yongming Yanshou (904–975), Yunqi Zhuhong (1535–1615), Zhongfeng Mingben (1263–1323), Hanshan Deqing (1546–1623), and Ouyi Zhixu (1599–1655) — the late-Ming confluents — who across six centuries of joined Chan-Pure Land cultivation re-read P22's discontinuity. The three together form the documentary groundwork for the latter three papers of Part VI.

The open question left for the reader: if Shandao's act is the *Tang-completed discontinuity* in Chinese soil, what is its relation to the later Kamakura radicalization in Japan — *transmission*? *divergence*? *re-radicalization*? Or *incommensurable* on some doctrinal point? This question is not answered within the present paper; it is left to the reader to unfold within their own ongoing practice and reading.
