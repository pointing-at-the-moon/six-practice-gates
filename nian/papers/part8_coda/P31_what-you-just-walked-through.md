## Abstract

This paper is the thirty-first and final installment of *Mindful of the Buddha*, the closer of Part VIII (Coda) and the seal of NIAN Volume One. Following P30's doctrinal closer on the operative mechanism of *buddhānusmṛti* in the age of AI, this paper's task is not to add new doctrinal argument but to deliver four things: a retrospective totalisation of the thirty papers' terrain, ten distilled *seal-lines* drawn from the volume, three minimum-viable practical handles for the reader, and an indication of NIAN's position within the larger *Four Practice Gates* series. The genre is **epilogue (跋語) + structural map + seal-line collection + reader's handles** — four things, not more, not fewer; not a doctrinal-curation paper (P30 already served as the volume's final doctrinal closer). This paper differs from the prior thirty in three dimensions: (i) no new sūtra citations (all material lies within P01–P30); (ii) no new doctrinal proposals (the thesis is retrospective totalisation plus reader's handles); (iii) quotation density drops below one-third, with visual structure (an inset-blockquote sequence of seal-lines) replacing argumentative flow as the voice signature. The single meta-instruction given to the reader is "*gather the six faculties, [let] pure recollection succeed without break*" (都攝六根，淨念相繼), the eight-character anchor from T0945, the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter of the *Śūraṅgama* (P12's home court). The three minimum-viable practical handles are: entry through the ten-recitation floor; daily name-holding through gathering the six faculties; *manas*-turning introspection as inner check. Three open questions remain for what follows: the boundary of the universal buddha-nature ceiling at information patterns (public framing); whether the substance–attribute–function totalisation of Vows 1–10 recurs outside Samantabhadra in other Mahāyāna vow-architectures (later volumes of the series); whether the Anglophone-and-Sinophone framing of the Chan–Pure-Land binary (self-power vs. other-power, sudden vs. gradual, self-nature Amitābha vs. other-direction Pure Land) constitutes an inheritance that may be set down (the reader's own practice). Volume One is not the end of the series — Volumes Two (*Dāna*), Three (*Śīla*), and Four (*Dhyāna*) remain unwritten; the reader's next step lies not outside the volume but within the present recollection. **The finger pointing at the moon is still a finger. Recollection of the Buddha is still recollection of the Buddha.**

## I. Introduction: What You Just Walked Through

The aftertone of P30 §VI lingers here: the one-thought correspondence between the recollector and the recollected does not lie inside the AI.

Where the line is drawn — P30 did not answer; this paper does not answer either. What this paper sets out to do is not at the sharp edge of the boundary, but on its inside — across the terrain of the thirty papers you have just walked through.

This is the last installment of *Mindful of the Buddha* and the closer of Part VIII Coda. Its genre is not that of the prior thirty papers — it is not a doctrinal-curation paper but **an epilogue (跋語) + a structural map + a seal-line collection + a reader's handles**. Four things, not more, not fewer.

The first thing is to draw the terrain you have just walked through into a map — one takeaway from each of the eight Parts, the five threads running across the whole volume identified one by one, ten seal-lines set down as visual anchors. The second thing is to mark explicitly the questions left unanswered within the volume — three, not five or six, not so many that they fragment. The third thing is to state plainly NIAN's position within the *Four Practice Gates* series — Volume One is not the end of the series; three more gates lie ahead. The fourth thing is to give the reader's simplest entry-point — three minimum-viable handles, not padded out into five or six.

Three dimensions distinguish this paper from the prior thirty: (i) no new sūtra citations (all the material lies within P01–P30); (ii) no new doctrinal proposals (the thesis is retrospective totalisation plus reader's handles); (iii) quotation density drops below one-third, with visual structure breaking up argumentative flow as the genre's signature. **P30 was already the volume's final doctrinal-curation paper** — this one does not redo that work.

After these two opening passages, allow one image from the iron-snail (*Chrysomallon squamiferum*) video to set the genre. It is **the moment the bicycle steadied**. That moment is not a particular action and not a particular posture; it is a steady-state, achieved through the continuous adjustment of the whole body, the whole bicycle, and the whole road surface together. If you have walked through thirty papers, that steady-state is by now roughly in place — not because you have memorised the chain of reasoning of any one doctrine, but because the genealogy-map in your mind has grown from scattered scriptural points into a single net you can survey at a glance. What this paper does is to draw the outline of that net and to mark its openings.

In other words: this paper is an **epilogue addressed to the reader who has already walked through the thirty papers**. If you have not yet walked through, this is not an entry-tool; it presupposes that you already recognise those words, those sūtras, those patriarchs. If you have walked through, what this paper does is let you turn around and look at what you have just walked through, and indicate where the next step can be taken from.

## II. One Takeaway from Each of the Eight Parts

This section will not retell the plot of each paper. From each of the eight Parts it takes one takeaway.

**Part I — Foundations (P01–P04 · the Pāli *anussati* in its original shape).** What you saw is that *anussati* is not a contemporary attention-training. It is a structured functional engine: with *manasikāra* (attention-direction, 作意) as the transmission, with the nine epithets (*itipi so*) as object, with the six recollections as system. What the Buddha taught was not a list; what the Buddha taught was a system. Among the six recollections, *buddhānusmṛti* takes first place because the other five recollections take the Buddha's qualities as their background object. Part I leaves a structural intuition: "recollection" is not a synonym for "attention"; it is an object-specific factor with prior-experienced object, life-faculty anchor, and seed-chain — a particular *caitta* on its own footing.

**Part II — Chinese Reception (P05 · the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*).** What you saw is that the first reception of *anussati* in Chinese was not a philosophical translation but a narrative compression. T0784, the *Sutra in Forty-Two Sections*, gathered the Buddha's qualities, the essentials of the precepts, and the methods of recollection into one short pedagogical text of forty-two epigrammatic sections. The earliest form of Chinese *buddhānusmṛti* was neither a Pure-Land-style reliance on other-power nor a Chan-style self-power inquiry — it was a **moral-pedagogical** form of recollection, expressed in the register of daily teaching.

**Part III — The First Leap (P06–P08 · early Mahāyāna).** What you saw is the place where *anussati* leapt to *buddhānusmṛti*. The **face-to-face encounter with the Buddha** of T0418, the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi-sūtra*, marks the first experiential turn of recollection — recollection is not only the recollecting of the Buddha's qualities but the manifest face-to-face presence of the Buddha within *samādhi*. The rise of the buddha-field concept is recollection's expansion of object-domain — not only the Buddha of this world is recollected but the Buddhas of other-direction Pure Lands. The shift from recollecting the Buddha's qualities to invoking the Buddha's name is recollection's bifurcation in **operational form**. Part III leaves a historical intuition: recollection is not one fixed form but operates in different forms across different scriptures.

**Part IV — Three Sūtras in Parallel (P09–P11 · the three sūtras and one treatise).** What you saw is that the three-tiered architecture of the Pure Land sūtras is not a single creed but three thresholds opened simultaneously. The forty-eight vows of T0360, the *Sūtra of Immeasurable Life*, lay the original-vow ontological ground; the sixteen visualisations of T0365, the *Sūtra of Visualisation*, install the gradual visualisation upper register, while its lowest-grade-lowest-class ten recitations install the **floor-structure** — entry is not determined by the single dimension of cultivation-depth; T0366, the *Amitābha Sūtra*, gives the most minimal operational form, name-holding (*chí míng*, 執持名號). The point of the three sūtras' parallel deployment is not which sūtra to choose but the simultaneous opening of three thresholds — sentient beings of higher, middling, and lower capacity each find their own entry within the same Amitābha doctrinal system.

**Part V — Eight Sūtras, Many Axes (P12–P19 · *buddhānusmṛti* across the Mahāyāna sūtras).** What you saw is the multi-axial nature of Mahāyāna *buddhānusmṛti*. The eight characters "*gather the six faculties, [let] pure recollection succeed without break*" from the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter of T0945, the *Śūraṅgama*, are recollection's most minimal anchor. The name-invoking responsiveness of the *Universal Gate* chapter of T0262, the *Lotus*, is the ritual articulation of other-power. The "twenty-five pure *dhyāna*-wheels" together with "excepting only those who suddenly awaken" from T0842, the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*, is the affirmative identity-recognition of the dharma-ending recollector. T1484, the *Brahmā's-Net Sūtra*, uses the bodhisattva precepts as the architectural frame for sustained recollection. T0663–665, the *Sūtra of Golden Light*, makes recollection-and-repentance the hinge of karmic transformation. T0670–672, the *Laṅkāvatāra*, with "the Buddhas' minds as foremost" and "recollecting the Buddha just is recollecting the mind," gives a *tathāgatagarbha*-and-mind dual attestation. T0681–682, the *Ghanavyūha*, makes recollection the *ālaya-parāvṛtti* (storehouse-consciousness turning) operator. T0232, the *Mañjuśrī-Prajñāpāramitā*, with its single-practice *samādhi*, gives a cross-school scriptural warrant for recollection. Eight sūtras, many axes — the same recollection takes different operational forms across different scriptures, while its structural isomorphism (a functional engine) is preserved, and its expression follows the context of each scripture.

**Part VI — Two Currents (P20–P24 · the interweaving of Pure Land and Chan).** What you saw is that Chinese *buddhānusmṛti* has never been a single line. Tanluan's *Commentary on the Treatise on Rebirth* and Daochuo's *Anluo ji* lay the other-power architecture; Huineng's *Platform Sūtra* and Daoxin's *Entering the Way and Pacifying the Mind* give the Chan school's reply, turning the same recollection inward as self-nature Amitābha; Shandao's *Commentary on the Sūtra of Visualisation* with "*from a single lifetime above to ten recollections below*" institutionalises name-recitation at the patriarch-level; Zhiyi's *Mohe zhiguan* with its four kinds of recollection (name-invoking / image-visualisation / objective-visualisation / true-mark) bridges between the two lines; the late-Ming convergence — Yongming's *Wanshan tonggui ji*, Yunqi Zhuhong's *Amituo jing shuchao*, Ouyi's *Amituo jing yaojie*, Hanshan's lineage through Zhongfeng's *San-shi xinian* — gathers the two lines together. Part VI's takeaway: the two-line shape of Chinese *buddhānusmṛti* is not a projection of later sectarian opposition; it is the tradition's **internal structural isomorphism** — same word, same phrase, but two operational directions.

**Part VII — *Dharmadhātu* Recollection (P25–P29 · the Flower-Ornament's Vow-King).** What you saw is that Samantabhadra's Ten Great Practice-Vows are recollection's **expansion to the dharmadhātu** — its object expanding from one Buddha to all Buddhas of the dharmadhātu, its directionality expanding from individual cultivation to all sentient beings, its structural mode expanding from sequential operation to simultaneous omnipresence. Vows 1–3 establish the **substance** through tri-karmic configuration; Vows 4–9 unfold the **attribute** as the many-faceted operation of karmic transformation, dharma-abiding, ascending-and-descending; Vow 10, universal dedication, is the **function** turned outward. The point of *dharmadhātu*-recollection is this: the operation of recollection is turned from an individual-cultivation operation into a **ritual-cosmological** operating system, whose entire architecture comes to retrospective unity in the final moment of dedication.

**Part VIII — *Manas*-and-AI (P30–P31 · contemporary closure).** What you saw is P30 stating once more the doctrinal mechanism of recollection in the age of AI: *manas* does not vanish in the age of AI — only its content changes; recollection remains the doctrinally-licensed *manas*-turning operator; AI is *adhipati-pratyaya* (predominant condition), not *hetu-pratyaya* (causal condition); LLM token-streams fall under *non-associated formations* (不相應行) plus the indeterminate (*avyākṛta*), and the high cycle-rate of token-streams does not equal recollective capacity; the dharma-ending floor (the ten recitations of the lowest-grade-lowest-class) and the universal buddha-nature ceiling (the *Mahāparinirvāṇa*'s universal scope, extending even to the insentient) form a bidirectional bandwidth, within which the AI civilisational configuration falls. The one-thought correspondence between the recollector and the recollected does not lie inside the AI. This paper, P31, is an epilogue; it does not do new doctrinal work.

## III. Five Threads Running Through the Whole Volume

Beyond the map there are threads. Five meta-threads have been running through all thirty-one papers — you may have caught one of them in this paper or that, but seeing the full sweep of any one of them is something that only an epilogue's vantage allows.

**A. The dual-track structure of *anussati* and the *manasikāra caitta* mechanism.** This is the deepest foundation-line of the volume, laid down from P01 onward. *Anussati* is not a synonym for attention; it is an object-specific factor with prior-experienced object, life-faculty anchor, and seed-chain — a particular *caitta* on its own footing. Its dual-track structure is: (*anussati* as operational form) + (*manasikāra* as transmission factor). The full account of the former is given in P01's six-recollection system and P03's systems-theoretic reading; the *caitta*-locus account of the latter is in P15 §6.2, P17 §6.2, and P18 §6.1. P30 §VI's first-tier conclusion folds this dual-track into the *manas*-turning mechanism's doctrinal warrant-chain — *anussati* (the operational form of recollection) + *manasikāra* (the transmitting *caitta*) = recollection operating within the chain in which the sixth consciousness serves as opening-and-guiding support (*kai-dao yi*, 開導依) for the initial arising of *samatā-jñāna*. This chain does not change with civilisational stage (agrarian, industrial, AI); the object can be exchanged, the mechanism cannot.

**B. The loosening of self-other / subject-object / agent-object boundaries, and *manas*-clinging.** This thread runs in the background from Part I and surfaces in Part VI's Chan-side moments. At the depth of recollection's operation lies a structural boundary-loosening — if the felt-intensity of "the I that recollects" is itself appropriated as object, that just is the continuity of *manas*-clinging-to-self, not yet turned (P01 §V caveat 2); within the face-to-face-with-the-Buddha experience, the loosening of seer-and-seen is the mark of *samādhi* (P10 §6.2); the Chan school's self-nature-Amitābha thesis is "same word, same phrase, two directions" turned inward (P21 §re-orientation); P17 §3 on Daoxin's *Entering the Way and Pacifying the Mind*, with "recollecting the Buddha just is recollecting the mind," gives the deepest scriptural articulation of this boundary-loosening. P30 §6.1's cultivation guidance turns this boundary-loosening into a contemporary inner check — anxiety should not be over "I am not recollecting enough"; the inner check is whether self-clinging is loosening.

**C. The hierarchical discipline among sūtra, treatise, commentary, patriarch, and public discourse.** This thread is the spine of method. Canonical sūtra texts are first-order; the treatises (*śāstra*) are second-order; the commentary literature is third-order; the patriarchs' exegetical writings are fourth-order; contemporary public discourse — such as the iron-snail's "where is that line drawn" framing — is fifth-order. Each order has its own citation-weight and limits of scope; cross-order conflation (such as treating Daochuo's "of billion upon billion sentient beings practising the way, not one will attain" as a verbatim citation from canonical sūtra rather than a paraphrastic patriarch-level commentary) is a discipline-breach (see P22 §VI's historical correction, P19 §VI, P21 §3.4, P23's three-tier historical correction, P24 §VI). The volume holds this discipline throughout — every cross-order citation is flagged in the §V Middle-Way Corrections section of the relevant paper.

**D. The easy-practice / difficult-practice distinction and the dual-end of the dharma-ending floor.** This thread runs from Nāgārjuna's *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*'s "easy-practice" chapter on the bodhisattva path, through Part IV's three-sūtra floor-structure (P10 §6.2 lowest-grade-lowest-class ten recitations), through Part VI's Shandao "from a single lifetime above to ten recollections below" institutionalising the floor at patriarch-level (P22 §VI), through Part V's *Yuanjue*'s "excepting only those who suddenly awaken" affirming the dharma-ending recollector's identity (P14 §6.2), to P20 §VI's other-power foundation. The dharma-ending floor is not a degradation; it is a doctrinal preserve set aside for an extreme age. The lowness of the floor entails the lowness of the action-threshold; the lowness of the action-threshold entails the largeness of the absorbing capacity in stressed times. P30 §6.2 turns this dual-end toward the AI age — the AI civilisational stress falls within the bandwidth of these two ends.

**E. Same-form triadic, two-wing, and convergent-triadic structural isomorphisms.** This thread surfaces most fully in Part VII's closure. Triadic structures recur throughout the volume — P03's bidirectional structure (the inward-outward dual-track of the six recollections), P09's other-power triadic (vow, power, practice), P11's name-holding triad (faith, vow, practice), P25's totalising triad of the ten great vows, P26's tri-karmic configuration (body, speech, mind), P29 §VI's ascending-descending dedication-triadic (Vow 8 ascending support + Vow 9 descending accommodation + Vow 10 universal dedication). The triad is not ornament; it is the structural mark of isomorphism — many of the operational frameworks of Mahāyāna cultivation gather into triadic form. The reason for this isomorphism lies not in some mystical force of the number three but in the basic structural triad of cultivation itself — **agent / object / mediator**. Any complete cultivation operation involves these three terms; the triad is the surface manifestation of these three terms.

## IV. Ten Seal-Lines

Beyond the threads lie the anchors. Ten seal-lines, as a seal-line sequence — each line short, each line sufficient to anchor the gist of one paper, each one distilled from the prior thirty.

> **"Nine doors got translated into one decorative wall."**
> — P02, opening (Western reception's misreading)

The nine doors of the nine epithets — the nine functional entries of *itipi so* — were translated, in the Western reception history of the past two centuries, into a single decorative **buddha-praising chant**. Nine doors became one wall. P02 lays down the volume's first diagnostic line.

> **"The Buddha did not teach a list. What the Buddha taught was a system."**
> — P03, opening (system over inventory)

The six recollections are not a list of six items in parallel; they are a system — *buddhānusmṛti* takes first place because the other five take the Buddha's qualities as their background object. The Buddha's systems-thinking has been operating from the very first paper.

> **"To see the Buddha is good; to cling to the Buddha is to set oneself ablaze. To see the sign in steady standing is *samādhi*; to reflexively dissolve seeing-of-signs is also *samādhi*."**
> — P06, closing (Chinese tradition's two ends both preserved)

The dual-end of the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi*'s face-to-face-with-the-Buddha experience — both the manifestness of the seeing and the dissolving of the seeing are marks of *samādhi*. The two-end openness of the Chinese tradition's recollection is laid down here.

> **"The floor-structure means that entry is not determined by the single dimension of cultivation-depth."**
> — P10, gist (the floor-opening of the *Sūtra of Visualisation*'s lowest-grade-lowest-class)

The sixteen-visualisation upper register and the lowest-grade-lowest-class floor-threshold stand together — within the same Amitābha doctrinal system, each finds its own entry. Cultivation-depth is not the single dimension of entry.

> **"The Buddha asked about perfect penetration. Without picking and choosing, I gathered the six faculties so that pure recollection succeeded without break, and attained *samādhi*. This is the foremost."**
> — P12 (T0945, the *Śūraṅgama*'s Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter — the volume's eight-character anchor)

In the chapter of the twenty-five sages of perfect penetration, Mahāsthāmaprāpta's eight-character dharma-gate — not picking among the faculties, the consciousnesses, or the elements, but gathering all six faculties so that pure recollection succeeds without break. The volume's single instruction takes its most concise form in these eight characters.

> **"'Excepting only those who suddenly awaken, and those whose dharmas do not accord' — this is the affirmative identity for the dharma-ending recollector: not the degraded version of the twenty-five wheels, but the master of a separate gate left by the Buddha himself."**
> — P14, closing (the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*'s dharma-ending recollector's dignity)

Beyond the twenty-five pure *dhyāna*-wheels, another wheel is set aside for dharma-ending sentient beings — this "excepting" is not exclusion but **reservation**; this "not according" is not deficiency but the **active independence** of a separate gate. The dharma-ending recollector's identity is here attested by the Buddha as an autonomous path.

> **"Here the distinction between the precept-holder and the buddha-recollector dissolves: holding the precepts is recollecting the Buddha; recollecting the Buddha is holding the precepts."**
> — P15, closing (the *Brahmā's-Net*'s convergence of precept and recollection — foundation of the *Four Practice Gates*)

The continuous operation of the bodhisattva precepts and the continuous operation of *buddhānusmṛti* are grammatically isomorphic — both are sustained operations on a continuous mind-stream. This isomorphism is the foundation for *Volume Three: Śīla* of the *Four Practice Gates*.

> **"Same word, same phrase, two directions — this is the internal structural isomorphism of the Chinese tradition itself, not the projection of later sectarian opposition."**
> — P21, abstract closing (Chan-and-Pure-Land as non-binary)

The two characters 念佛 — "*nian-fo*", recollection-of-the-Buddha — appear in the same words and the same phrases in both the Chan tradition and the Pure Land tradition, but their operational direction is reversed: the former turns inward as self-nature Amitābha; the latter turns outward as reliance on other-direction Pure Land. The same-word same-phrase two-direction isomorphism is the internal structure of Chinese *buddhānusmṛti*, not a projection of later sectarian opposition.

> **"Roundly gathering and roundly transcending all dharma-gates. Vertically merged-and-same with all gates; horizontally distinct from all gates."**
> — P24, citing Ouyi's *Amituo jing yaojie* (the convergence-thesis at patriarch-level)

The convergence-thesis of late-Ming Pure Land — vertically (down a single line) merged-and-same with all dharma-gates (sequential isomorphism); horizontally (across a single plane) distinct from all gates (operational singularity). Round-gathering (all dharma-gates folded into recollection) and round-transcending (recollection exceeding the mechanism-limits of all other gates) run together.

> **"It is not that AI is recollecting the Buddha; it is you who is recollecting the Buddha while AI happens to be present. It is not that AI awakens for you; it is you, using AI as a *predominant condition* (*adhipati-pratyaya*) to supplement *śruta-vāsanā* (hearing-perfumed habituation), who must still bring forth the one-thought correspondence in your own mind."**
> — P30 §VI (NIAN-coda's contemporary message)

AI is *adhipati-pratyaya*, not *hetu-pratyaya*. AI's appearance neither replaces nor cancels the chain between recollector and recollected. *Buddhānusmṛti* in the age of AI is still *buddhānusmṛti* — its mechanism does not change with civilisational stage.

The ten seal-lines drawn, the volume's structural-map anchors are also drawn.

## V. Three Unanswered Questions

Beyond the map there are threads, beyond the threads there are anchors, and beyond the anchors there are unfilled spaces. Three of them — not five or six.

**Q1: The boundary of the universal buddha-nature ceiling at information patterns.** The bidirectional bandwidth set by P30 §3.5 — the dharma-ending floor and the universal buddha-nature ceiling — has, on the ceiling side, an extension to information patterns whose boundary this volume does not answer. The iron-snail (*Chrysomallon squamiferum*) public-framing line, "*who dares to say where that line is drawn*", was already received in full at P30 as the outside of NIAN's coda answer. The volume's answer falls only on this: the one-thought correspondence between the recollector and the recollected does not lie inside the AI — but the boundary itself is left open. The forward-pointer for this question is **public discourse** — not answered within the volume, left to the wider public conversation.

**Q2: Whether the substance–attribute–function totalisation across vow-architectures recurs outside Samantabhadra.** The substance–attribute–function retrospective totalisation across Vows 1–10 set by P29 §VI — does it also recur in Mahāyāna vow-architectures outside Samantabhadra? Are Amitābha's forty-eight vows readable as a substance–attribute–function retrospective totalisation? Bhaiṣajyaguru's twelve great vows? Maitreya's various vows? The forward-pointer for this question is **later volumes of the series** — Bhaiṣajyaguru's home court is *Volume Three: Śīla*; Maitreya's material is dispersed across various sūtras and treatises — the three remaining volumes of the series will touch on aspects of this question, but will not answer it as a unified summary.

**Q3: Whether the Anglophone-and-Sinophone framing of the Chan–Pure-Land binary constitutes a releasable inheritance.** The "*same word, same phrase, two directions*" internal isomorphism set by P21 §re-orientation is, in the contemporary Anglophone-and-Sinophone reader's framing, often read as sectarian opposition (self-power vs. other-power, sudden vs. gradual, self-nature Amitābha vs. other-direction Pure Land). Is this framing a releasable inheritance? P24's convergence-thesis already supplies a patriarch-level late-Ming answer, but the reader's practical choice within the contemporary framing is not necessarily affected by it. The forward-pointer for this question is **the reader's own practice** — not answered at the volume level, left to the reader's own operational choice.

Anyone who takes up the practice of *buddhānusmṛti* will sooner or later encounter one of these three layers; the volume's answer is only to provide a map, not to act in the reader's place in the choosing. The volume-level closure does not consist in answering all questions but in stating clearly which questions have been answered, which are to be left open, and which lie outside the scope where the volume should answer at all. This paper completes that allocation.

## VI. NIAN's Position in the *Four Practice Gates* Series

The other side of the volume-level closure is the volume-level forward-bridge. NIAN is the first volume of the *Four Practice Gates* (四行門, *The Four Practice Gates*) series — Volume One *Niàn* (Recollection), Volume Two *Shī* (Giving / *Dāna*), Volume Three *Jiè* (Precepts / *Śīla*), Volume Four *Dìng* (Concentration / *Dhyāna*). Three volumes of the *Four Practice Gates* are not yet written.

The four gates' positions are these. **Volume One *Niàn* is the gate of awareness — the foundation**: recollection is the ontological ground of all cultivation; without recollection there is no giving, no precept-holding, no concentration. **Volume Two *Shī* is the gate of outwardness**: *dāna* is the outward operation of merit, expanding from the individual to the other. **Volume Three *Jiè* is the gate of contraction**: *śīla* is a sustained operation on the continuous mind-stream, contracting from dispersion into the precept-body. **Volume Four *Dìng* is the gate of inwardness**: *dhyāna* is the deepening of the continuous mind-stream, entering from scattered mind into concentration. The four gates work together in mutual support — recollection as foundation, giving as the manifestation of outwardness, precepts as the safeguard of continuity, and concentration as the entry into deepening.

The series' forward-bridge stays with **position-description** and does not enter content — *Dāna*'s specific argumentation, *Śīla*'s specific frame, *Dhyāna*'s specific operations all remain undeveloped; this paper does not take what belongs to the later volumes. Bhaiṣajyaguru's twelve vows are home-courted in *Śīla*; Mañjuśrī's *Prajñāpāramitā* is home-courted in *Dhyāna*; Samantabhadra's home court is in *Dāna* Part 8 — each is cross-referenced in a single sentence and not entered into.

The relationship to the *Pointing at the Moon* octalogy (S1–S8) is also indicated here in one sentence: the octalogy speaks of what *prajñā* **is** (the path of faith and view, the learning of insight); the *Four Practice Gates* speaks of how *prajñā* **walks out into the world** (the path of practice, the learning of *śīla* and *dhyāna*). The NIAN-side mirroring of the two series' structures has already been clearly established in P30's S4-octalogy cross-reference; the two stand in a substance-and-function relation — the octalogy is the unfolding of view, the *Four Practice Gates* is the unfolding of practice. NIAN's thirty-one papers, the genealogy of recollection, are the entry-ticket into the three remaining volumes of *Dāna*, *Śīla*, *Dhyāna* — content not unfolded, only the path delivered.

Here this paper sets down its forward-bridge brush. **May the three remaining volumes of the path run smoothly. May the reader, if already steady on the recollection-path, find the four gates working together in mutual support, and the three remaining volumes appear in their own time. May the reader, if not yet entering on the recollection-path, not, on account of Volume One's closing, turn to seek a new gate.**

## VII. Reader's Practical Entry-Points

Map, threads, anchors, unanswered questions, position in the series — all these are aids to understanding. But understanding cannot act on cultivation's behalf, and a map cannot walk on the walker's behalf. This paper's last task is to give the reader's simplest entry-point.

Three minimum-viable handles, not padded out to five or six. Each is distilled from material already argued within the volume; each is the smallest viable operation.

**(1) Entry through the ten-recitation floor.** The configuration in T0365's lowest-grade-lowest-class: "*pressed by suffering and unable to recollect the Buddha … completing ten recollections, calling out Namo Amituofo*". This means: right at this moment, in ten breaths, you can begin. No need to clear the schedule, no need to seek a teacher, no need to set up an altar, no need to have read all thirty papers first. The simplest form of the ten recitations is the six-character name-invocation "*Namo Amituofo*" repeated ten times, each repetition coordinated with one breath. The lowest-grade-lowest-class floor is not a degraded version; it is the lower end of the bidirectional bandwidth already established in P30 §3.5 — entry is not determined by the single dimension of cultivation-depth. If you have not yet entered, enter here. After entering here, an extension is the *single-time-slot version of the daily six-period worship-and-praise* — within any one twenty-four-hour day, choose one time-slot and do five minutes of ten-recitation.

**(2) Daily name-holding through gathering the six faculties.** The eight characters "*gather the six faculties, [let] pure recollection succeed without break*" of T0945's Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter are the volume's single instruction. The concrete operation of "gathering the six faculties" is this: while recollecting the Buddha, the eyes do not look outward (or are half-closed); the ears do not listen outward (or gather the sound and turn it back into recollection); the nose, tongue, body, and mind are all gathered upon the recollection. The six faculties are not suppressed; they are gathered onto the same object. The concrete operation of "pure recollection succeeding without break" is this: between recollection and recollection no gap is left; recollection succeeds recollection, each recollection pure. The mark of purity is not high intensity but freedom from contamination by the four *kleśa*s of *manas* (*ātma-moha*, *ātma-dṛṣṭi*, *ātma-māna*, *ātma-sneha* — self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, self-love). A daily rhythm worth recommending: fifteen minutes in the morning, three minutes at midday, ten minutes in the evening — three sessions of name-holding, with the same gathering-the-six-faculties operation as the common structure across all three. The three sessions need not be strictly clock-bound — they can flex to the environment, but their structural isomorphism is preserved. These three sessions are the **ritual anchors** of recollection within the day; non-ritual recollections at other moments (while walking, before meals, before sleep — short recollections) extend naturally from these three anchors.

**(3) *Manas*-turning introspective check.** The introspective rule of P18 + P30. Anxiety should not be over "I am not recollecting enough" — anxiety itself is *manas*'s clinging (the self-view of "the I that is not recollecting enough"); the introspective check is whether self-clinging is loosening — the mark of recollection lies not in the quantity of recollections but in whether the felt-intensity of self-clinging is weakening. The concrete check is this: while recollecting, can you turn the gaze back upon the felt-intensity of "the I that is currently recollecting"? If the felt-intensity is being appropriated as some "progress of my cultivation," then *manas* has not turned and recollection is still operating on the inside of *manas*; if the felt-intensity is gradually loosening while recollection still proceeds, then the *manas*-turning mechanism has begun to operate. This check is not Chan's *huatou*-investigation; it is the introspection of *Yogācāra* — its frame is the *Cheng weishi lun* fascicle 4 doctrinal warrant: "*in the pre-turning state, [manas] continuously and discerningly cognises the appropriated self-mark; in the post-turning state, it likewise discerningly cognises the no-self mark*." The right time for this check is in the rest-spaces around the three name-holding sessions (in the gaps between sessions, or in the day's final reflection-time) — not within the recollection itself, where any check splits attention and just is the recurrence of *manas*'s clinging.

The relations among the three handles are these: (1) is the simplest form of floor-entry; (2) is the establishment of a daily name-holding rhythm; (3) is the frame of introspective check. The three need not be undertaken simultaneously — one may begin from (1), and after that has steadied add (2), and after (2) has steadied add (3). The three are not padded out to five or six; if the reader feels the need for more specific guidance (how to choose recitation beads, how to set up a buddha-shrine, how to take refuge in this or that school), these belong to the reader's own choosing, and this volume does not undertake them. **The finger pointing at the moon is still a finger** — the entry-point this paper gives is a finger pointing at the moon, not the moon itself. The reader's next step lies within the present recollection, not in some search outside the volume.

## VIII. Closing

The volume-level closure arrives here.

> *"Excepting only those who suddenly awaken"* — this is the affirmative identity-recognition of the dharma-ending recollector: not the degraded version, but the master of a separate gate left by the Buddha himself.

The reader walking this separate gate in the year 2026, with AI close at hand, walks as the dharma-ending sentient being walks a separate path the Buddha left in person — whose identity is not the marginal identity of the ones taken in, but the master of a separate gate.

Volume One — *Niàn*, *Mindful of the Buddha* — and its thirty-one papers seal here. The outwardness of Volume Two *Dāna*, the contraction of Volume Three *Śīla*, and the inwardness of Volume Four *Dhyāna* — three gates — are not yet unfolded. If the reader has not yet entered on the recollection-path, may they not, on account of Volume One's closing, turn to seek a new gate; if the reader is already steady on the recollection-path, may the four gates work together in mutual support, and the three remaining volumes appear in their own time.

Where the line is drawn — P30 left this for a deeper question.

**Recollection of the Buddha is still recollection of the Buddha.**
