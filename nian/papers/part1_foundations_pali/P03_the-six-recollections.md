## Abstract
The six recollections (*cha anussatiyo*) recorded in the *Aṅguttara Nikāya*—recollection of the Buddha, the Dhamma, the Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, and the deities—are typically treated in contemporary Anglophone Buddhist literature as six independent devotional practices. This paper argues that the six are not a list but a system. AN 6.10 *Mahānāmasutta* repeats a single formulaic syntax six times, disclosing that all six share one dynamic engine; the six objects form a clear bidirectional anchoring structure—the first three (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha) borrow qualities outward from external referents, the last three (virtue, generosity, deities) verify qualities already present within the practitioner. The paper further identifies the special position of *devatānussati*: the five qualities enumerated in AN 6.10's internal formula (*saddhā*, *sīla*, *suta*, *cāga*, *paññā*—faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom) correspond exactly to the five qualities the Buddha gives Mahānāma in the SN 55.21 street-elephant episode as what carries the mind upward at death—*devatānussati* is the system's hidden terminus, designed for the **moment of death**. The cluster "a heart everywhere like space—abundant, expansive, limitless, free from enmity, free from ill will" in AN 6.26 *Mahākaccānasutta* shares its formula with the four *brahmavihāras*, embedding an *appamāṇa* (limitless) mechanism within the recollections—laying the early Āgama foundation for the later Mahāyāna theology of Infinite Light and Infinite Life. Working through the *Aṅguttara Nikāya* Books of the Sixes and Elevens and the *Saṃyutta Nikāya* Sotāpattisaṃyutta 55, with the Chinese *Saṃyukta Āgama* 930–933 and *Ekottara Āgama* "Wider Exposition" chapter, and consulting Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* chapter VII and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicles 21–22, the paper exhibits the internal architecture of the six recollections as a **complete recollective system, bidirectionally anchored, internally carrying the brahmavihāra mechanism, and designed jointly for daily life and for death**, while identifying Buddhaghosa as the systematizer (the *Ekottara Āgama* "Wider Exposition" ten-recollection set corresponds item for item to the *Visuddhimagga*'s ten, indicating he integrated rather than innovated) and Nāgārjuna as the Mahāyāna pivot (expansion of *cāga*, restructuring of *devatā*, the two seals of bodhisattva eight-recollection practice).

**Keywords:** six recollections, *cha anussatiyo*, bidirectional structure, external borrowing and internal verification, *devatānussati*, deathbed five qualities, faith-virtue-learning-generosity-wisdom, *appamāṇa*, four *brahmavihāras*, *Visuddhimagga*, *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, *buddhānussati*

## I. Introduction

There is a recurring blind spot in the way contemporary Western literature handles *buddhānussati* (recollection of the Buddha). The practice almost always gets isolated from its larger context and presented as a self-contained meditation method. Once the Buddha-recollection is introduced, the standard move is to introduce *dhammānussati* (recollection of the Dhamma) as another self-contained method. Then *saṅghānussati* (recollection of the Saṅgha) as yet another. Six iterations, six freestanding practices. The reader walks away with a list: six Buddhist devotional exercises.

But the Buddha did not teach a list.

The Buddha taught a system.

NIAN-P02 ended with three questions: why six, why these six, and how do the other five operate? This chapter answers them directly. The answer proceeds in three layers. First, the six recollections share a single dynamic engine—the same nine-step chain of mind transformation, the same syntactic structure, the same functional positioning. Second, the six objects themselves form a clear bidirectional anchoring structure: the first three (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha) borrow qualities from external referents, the last three (virtue, generosity, deities) verify qualities already present internally. Third, the recollection of the deities, which often looks like a residual category, is in fact the system's hidden terminus, designed for the moment of death.

Several things this chapter will not cover. The Chinese tradition's compression of recollection into the four-character *Námó Āmítuófó* belongs to NIAN Part VI. The chapter on Mahāsthāmaprāpta in the *Śūraṅgama* belongs to Part V (P12). The full treatment of the *jhāna* boundary belongs to P04 and Part IV. This chapter does one thing: it restores the six recollections to their original shape as a single recollective system, so that the internal architecture becomes visible.

## II. Sources

### 2.1 The Pāli Core Suttas

The six recollections appear most densely in the *Aṅguttara Nikāya*'s Books of the Sixes and the Elevens. Each occurrence repeats the same highly formulaic syntax six times. Five suttas are central:

| Sutta | PTS | Chinese parallel | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| AN 6.9 *Anussatiṭṭhānasutta* | AN iii 284 | — | Bare definitional list |
| AN 6.10 *Mahānāmasutta* | AN iii 285 | T0125 ch. 33 par. | Master template + nine-step chain per recollection |
| AN 6.25 *Anussatiṭṭhānasutta* (II) | AN iii 312 | — | Introduces *gedha* (sensual fixation) as the object of disengagement |
| AN 6.26 *Mahākaccānasutta* | AN iii 314 | — | "Opening amid confinement" + hidden brahmavihāra structure |
| AN 11.11 *Paṭhamamahānāmasutta* | AN v 328 | T0099 ch. 33 (931–933) | Five-faculties + six-recollections (5+6=11) architecture |

AN 6.9 is purely definitional, listing the six objects (*buddhānussati*, *dhammānussati*, *saṅghānussati*, *sīlānussati*, *cāgānussati*, *devatānussati*) without elaboration. AN 6.10 is the master text.[^1] Mahānāma asks the Buddha what kind of meditation a noble disciple "who has reached the fruit and understood the instructions" frequently practices. The Buddha answers: the six recollections. He then unpacks each in turn, and each unpacking walks the same nine-step transformation chain—mind not invaded by greed, hatred, delusion → mind unswerving → inspiration in the meaning → inspiration in the teaching → joy connected with the teaching (*pāmojja*) → rapture (*pīti*) → bodily tranquility (*passaddhi*) → bliss (*sukha*) → immersion (*samādhi*). NIAN-P02 §3.2.4 already analyzed this nine-step chain in full. This chapter does not retrace its internal mechanics. It simply notes that **the chain repeats six times in identical form across the six recollections**—and that the repetition is itself the structural cue.

AN 6.25 reframes the practice[^3]: the object of disengagement is *gedha*, the "sensual fixation" that the text identifies with the five strands of sensual stimulation. The mind, through each recollection, leaves *gedha* behind. AN 6.26, delivered by the venerable Mahākaccāna, adds a critical phrase: "the noble disciple dwells with a heart everywhere like space—abundant, expansive, limitless, free from enmity, free from ill will" (*sabbaso ākāsasamena cetasā viharati vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyāpajjena*). This phrase carries a hidden brahmavihāra structure that §3.4 will trace. AN 11.11 establishes the "five before, six after" architecture[^2]: first ground in five qualities (faith, energy, mindfulness, immersion, wisdom), then on top of these develop the six recollections.

### 2.2 Designed for Death: The Two Mahānāma Street Episodes

Adjacent in the *Saṃyutta Nikāya*'s Sotāpattisaṃyutta sit two suttas recording the same problem from Mahānāma:

| Sutta | PTS | Chinese parallel | Scene |
|---|---|---|---|
| SN 55.21 *Paṭhamamahānāmasutta* | SN v 369 | T0099 ch. 33 (930) part | Streets full of stray elephants, horses, carts, people; if I die now, where will I be reborn? Buddha answers: a pot of ghee or oil sunk and broken in deep water—shards sink, oil rises |
| SN 55.22 *Dutiyamahānāmasutta* | SN v 371 | T0099 ch. 33 (930) part | Same scene; Buddha answers: the four limbs of unbreakable confidence + a tree slanting eastward falling eastward |

These two suttas anchor §3.3. Mahānāma was the Sakyan elder of Kapilavatthu, a city dense with people and narrow with cul-de-sacs, where stray elephants, horses, carts, and people frequently brushed past pedestrians. He worried that if he died accidentally on such a street, his mind would have no time to recollect the Buddha, the Dhamma, or the Saṅgha—where then would he go? In SN 55.21 the Buddha replies with the image of the broken pot: a pot of ghee or oil sunk into a deep lake and shattered there—the shards and chips sink down, but the ghee or oil rises upward, headed for a higher place. In SN 55.22 the answer changes register: the four limbs of unbreakable confidence (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue) plus a tree-analogy—a tree slanting east, when cut at the root, falls east.

The Chinese parallel in *Saṃyukta Āgama* sūtra 930 preserves the tree analogy of SN 55.22, but the broken-pot image of SN 55.21 does not survive into Chinese. The consequence of that loss will become visible in §3.3.

### 2.3 Chinese Expansion Trajectories

Chinese Āgama materials show three distinct expansion trajectories around the six recollections, each revealing structural pressures absent in the Pāli:

| Sūtra | T no. | Structural feature |
|---|---|---|
| *Saṃyukta Āgama* 931 | T0099 ch. 33 | Mahānāma asks how a disciple still in training should practice → Buddha: practice the six recollections + nine-step chain. Parallel to AN 6.10 |
| *Saṃyukta Āgama* 932 | T0099 ch. 33 | Five qualities (faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom) + six recollections = eleven. Parallels AN 11.11 |
| *Saṃyukta Āgama* 933 | T0099 ch. 33 | **Five expanded into six (faith, virtue, generosity, learning, EMPTINESS, wisdom) + six recollections = twelve**—"emptiness" inserted into the foundation |
| *Ekottara Āgama* "Wider Exposition" 3 | T0125 ch. 2 | **Full set of ten recollections**: Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, deities, repose, breathing, body, death |

*Saṃyukta Āgama* 933 is the most striking. Same scene, same problem, same answering frame as in 932—but "emptiness" (*śūnyatā*) gets inserted into the foundational five, making it six, and the total eleven becomes twelve. Nothing corresponding stands in the Pāli parallel. The Chinese tradition has, at this stage, **already planted an early seed of Mahāyāna emptiness-thought** within the six-recollection framework. §3.6 returns to this point.

The ten-recollection set in *Ekottara Āgama* "Wider Exposition" chapter 3—Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, deities, repose, breathing, body, death—corresponds, item for item, to the ten recollections that Buddhaghosa later lays out in *Visuddhimagga* chapter VII. The methodological implication is large: two independent transmissions preserved the same skeletal set. Buddhaghosa was not the innovator. He was the systematizer. §3.6 develops this point.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 One Engine, Six Objects

Anyone reading AN 6.10 with attention will notice almost immediately that the syntactic frame of the six recollections is nearly identical. The Buddha first treats *buddhānussati*: the object is the nine epithets (the *itipi so* formula), and the noble disciple then walks the nine-step transformation chain, ending with "this is called a noble disciple who lives in balance among people who are unbalanced … entered the stream of the teaching." Then *dhammānussati*: the object switches to the six predicates of the Dhamma, and the disciple walks **the same nine-step chain**, ending with **the same closing formula**. Then *saṅghānussati*: the object becomes the four pairs and eight individuals, the supreme field of merit, and **again the same nine steps**, **again the same closing**. Then *sīla*-, *cāga*-, and *devatā-anussati*: each iteration reproduces the same architecture.

The Chinese parallel in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 931 preserves this feature.[^4] Each recollection ends with the same closing: "When the noble disciple recollects … this way, greed, hatred, and delusion do not arise … and through being suffused by the recollection of …, the disciple advances toward Nibbāna." Six iterations, almost word for word.

This structural feature is not stylistic laziness. It is deliberate design.

The deliberate point is this: the six recollections **are not six different practices**. They are one practice applied to six different focal objects. A single engine—the nine-step transformation a mind undergoes when it is sustained by an object of recollection—accepts six different inputs. The object varies; the engine does not. The engine *is* the act of *anussati* itself, the sustained, repeated, attentive bringing-to-mind of a chosen object. The object is one of six.

This observation is rarely made in Western literature. The reason is partly editorial: English translations conventionally use *peyyāla* (ellipsis) markers to abbreviate the second through sixth iterations into "in the same way…". This translation habit, however reasonable for length, **inadvertently effaces** the most important structural signal in the text. What the reader receives looks like six separate practices listed in sequence. What the Buddha actually delivered is one mechanism repeated six times, with the mechanism's identity asserted by the repetition itself.

NIAN-P02 §3.3 established a related observation about the Buddha-epithets: the Pāli nine are **predicates** (qualities ascribed to the Blessed One), while the Sanskrit-Chinese ten are **identities** (names of the Tathāgata). The same predicate-mode framing operates throughout the six recollections at the Pāli layer. The six predicates of the Dhamma in AN 6.10—well-explained, immediately effective, timeless, inviting inspection, leading inward, individually known by the wise—are predicates. The four predicates of the Saṅgha—practicing the way that is good, direct, systematic, and proper—are predicates. The Pāli system speaks throughout in the predicate mode: it says how the Dhamma functions, how the Saṅgha functions, how virtue functions—it does not posit Dhamma, Saṅgha, or virtue as identity-substances. This predicate mode is what allows *anussati* to borrow qualities without having to predicate a substance.

### 3.2 The Six Objects as a Bidirectional Structure

If the six recollections share one engine, the question that actually requires answering is: why these six objects, and not others? Why not seven, or five?

A close inspection of the internal ordering of the six objects reveals a remarkably clean bidirectional structure.

**The first three**—*buddhānussati*, *dhammānussati*, *saṅghānussati*—have objects located outside the practitioner. The Buddha is another historical figure. The Dhamma is the taught teaching. The Saṅgha is a body of others who have already attained. The function of these three is **external borrowing**: borrowing qualities from external referents and holding them as the object of recollection.

**The last three**—*sīlānussati*, *cāgānussati*, *devatānussati*—have objects located inside the practitioner. The virtue is one's own observed conduct. The generosity is one's own enacted giving. The five qualities recollected through "the deities" are qualities one is supposed to find within oneself. The function of these three is **internal verification**: confirming qualities the practitioner has already developed.

Each, in turn.

**The First Three: External Borrowing**

The object of *buddhānussati* is the nine epithets of the Tathāgata. The nine epithets are the qualities the Buddha has already attained—he has cut greed, hatred, and delusion; he is "accomplished in knowledge and conduct"; he has "well-gone" (*sugata*). When recollecting the Buddha, the practitioner holds in mind **the fact of someone else's accomplished cutting**. The borrowed item is "he has cut," and what it counters is the practitioner's panic at "I have not cut." NIAN-P02 §3.2.1 unpacks this in detail.

The object of *dhammānussati* is the six predicates of the Dhamma. The Dhamma is well-explained by the Blessed One (*svākkhāto*), apparent in the present life (*sandiṭṭhiko*), immediately effective (*akāliko*), inviting inspection (*ehipassiko*), leading toward the goal (*opanayiko*), individually known by the wise (*paccattaṃ veditabbo viññūhi*). The combined message is: **the Dhamma is verifiable**. It does not require faith; it invites checking. When recollecting the Dhamma, the practitioner holds in mind the borrowed fact that *verification is possible*.

The object of *saṅghānussati* is "the four pairs and eight individuals, the supreme field of merit." The four pairs are the path-attainers and fruit-attainers at the four levels of awakening—stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, arhat—each at the path-moment and the fruit-moment. The message is: **someone has already walked this path, not only the Buddha**. The noble disciple is not a solitary explorer; she is on a route already traversed by others. When recollecting the Saṅgha, the practitioner holds in mind the borrowed fact of *the path's reproducibility*.

Together the three external recollections establish a triangulated outer anchor: **cutting defilements is possible (Buddha) + the Dhamma is real and verifiable (Dhamma) + this path is not solitary (Saṅgha)**.

**The Last Three: Internal Verification**

The object of *sīlānussati* is one's own observed virtue—the eight standard predicates: unbroken (*akhaṇḍa*), untorn (*acchidda*), unspotted (*asabala*), unmottled (*akammāsa*), liberating (*bhujissa*), praised by the wise (*viññūpasattha*), unobsessed (*aparāmaṭṭha*), conducive to immersion (*samādhisaṃvattanika*). The Chinese parallel in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 931 reorders the list slightly but preserves the same eight features. The point is that the object is **what the practitioner has already done**. *Sīlānussati* is not a review of broken precepts; it is a confirmation of established stability. It is *confirmation*, not *confession*.

The object of *cāgānussati* is one's own enacted generosity. The Pāli formula has the disciple reflect: "Truly it is gain for me, truly it is well-gained for me, that among people whose hearts are full of the stain of stinginess, I dwell at home with a heart free from the stain of stinginess, freely generous, open-handed, delighted in relinquishment, available to those who ask, delighted in giving and sharing." The object is **the already-arisen fact of one's own freedom from miserliness**. It is not "I have not given enough"; it is "I am already able to give."

The object of *devatānussati* is the most easily misread. On the surface it appears to be "recollection of the deities"—the gods of the Four Great Kings, the Thirty-Three, the Yāma realm, Tusita, the Nimmānaratī, the Paranimmitavasavattī, and higher. But examine the internal formula: "Those deities have, by virtue of such-and-such **faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom**, passed from this world and arisen there. **And I, too, possess the same kind of faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom**" (*yathārūpāya saddhāya … mayhampi tathārūpā saddhā saṃvijjati*).

The object is nominally the deities; substantively it is **one's own five qualities**. The deities serve only as a benchmark.

Buddhaghosa makes this commentarial correction explicit in *Visuddhimagga* §VI: the deities are "placed as witness" (*sakkhiṭṭhāne ṭhapetvā*); what is actually recollected is the practitioner's own faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom. In other words, the *Visuddhimagga* directly classifies *devatānussati* as an internal-verification practice and clearly differentiates it from anything Western readers might intuitively call "thinking about gods."

Together, the three internal recollections establish a triangulated inner anchor: **virtue established + miserliness departed + the five qualities present**.

**The Bidirectional Structure**

External borrowing plus internal verification together form a complete anchoring loop. From the external awakened one (the Buddha), down through the external teaching (the Dhamma) and the external community of attainers (the Saṅgha), the structure pivots into the internal virtue, the internal generosity, and the internal qualities. **From "he has cut" to "I have established."**

Western literature on the recollections rarely identifies this structure. Two common misreadings exist. The first reads all six recollections as "veneration of the qualities of the Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha"—externalizing the last three by interpreting *sīlānussati* as "respect for moral exemplars," *cāgānussati* as "admiration for the virtue of generosity," *devatānussati* as "aspiration toward heavenly rebirth." The second reads all six as forms of self-affirmation—internalizing the first three by interpreting *buddhānussati* as "confirmation of one's Buddha-nature," *dhammānussati* as "confirmation of one's Dharma-nature," *saṅghānussati* as "confirmation of one's belonging to the community." Both readings collapse the bidirectional structure.

The Pāli syntax itself provides the unmistakable cue. The object-clauses of the first three recollections all use **third-person external reference**: "thus is that Blessed One" (*itipi so bhagavā*), "well-explained by the Blessed One is the Dhamma" (*svākkhāto bhagavatā dhammo*), "the Blessed One's community of disciples" (*bhagavato sāvakasaṅgho*). The object-clauses of the last three all use **first-person reflexive reference**: "one's own virtues" (*attano sīlāni*), "one's own generosity" (*attano cāgaṃ*), "I too possess such-and-such faith" (*mayhampi tathārūpā saddhā saṃvijjati*). The grammar makes the function explicit. The first three point outward, the last three turn back inward.

External borrowing and internal verification are two directions of the same system.

### 3.3 The Special Position of *Devatānussati*: An Internal Anchor for Death

If *devatānussati* gets read as merely one recollection among six, equally weighted with the others and equally focused on its surface object (the deities), the special function the Buddha designed for it goes unseen.

*Devatānussati* is the hidden terminus of the six-recollection system. It anchors the entire system to the moment of death.

To see this, three passages need to be placed side by side.

**First passage**: AN 6.10's internal formula for *devatānussati* enumerates five qualities—*saddhā* (faith), *sīla* (virtue), *suta* (learning), *cāga* (generosity), *paññā* (wisdom). These five are not a casual collection. They are a **standard set that recurs throughout the early Pāli canon**.

**Second passage**: SN 55.21, the street episode. Mahānāma was the Sakyan elder of Kapilavatthu, a city densely populated and tight in its alleys, where stray elephants, horses, carts, and people frequently brushed past him. He worried that if death struck on such a street, with his mind unable in that moment to recollect Buddha, Dhamma, or Saṅgha, where would he be reborn? The Buddha replies as follows:

> "Do not fear, Mahānāma, do not fear. Your death will not be a bad one; your passing will not be a bad one. Whoever's mind has long been imbued with **faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom**—their body, formed of the four great elements, even when devoured here by crows, vultures, hawks, hounds, jackals, and many kinds of small creatures—their mind nevertheless rises upward, headed for a higher place. Suppose someone were to sink a pot of ghee or oil in a deep lake and break it open: the shards and chips would sink down, while the ghee or oil would rise upward, headed for a higher place."[^5]

Notice the five qualities the Buddha enumerates—faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom—**are exactly the five qualities recollected in the *devatānussati* formula of AN 6.10**.

**Third passage**: SN 55.22, the same scene with a different answer. Mahānāma asks the same question; the Buddha gives a different reply:

> "A noble disciple endowed with four things slants, slopes, and inclines toward Nibbāna. What four? Here, Mahānāma, a noble disciple has experiential confidence in the Buddha—'thus is that Blessed One…'—in the Dhamma, in the Saṅgha, and in the virtues loved by the noble ones (*ariyakanta sīla*). Suppose a tree slants, slopes, and inclines toward the east: if cut at the root, where will it fall?" "Sir, it would fall in the direction it slants, slopes, and inclines." "In the same way, a noble disciple endowed with four things slants, slopes, and inclines toward Nibbāna."[^6]

Two adjacent suttas, the same person asking the same question, and the Buddha gives two answers. The first is the five qualities (faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom). The second is the four limbs of unbreakable confidence (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue). They look like two distinct paths; underneath they are the same structure. The four limbs of unbreakable confidence are the first three recollections (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha) plus the first internal recollection (virtue), extracted as *avecca-pasāda*. The five qualities are the internal formula of *devatānussati* (faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom), extracted as the qualities that "long imbue" the mind. **Both replies are different cross-sections of the six-recollection system.**

The Chinese parallel in *Saṃyukta Āgama* sūtra 930 collapses both Pāli suttas into a single passage but preserves only the tree analogy of SN 55.22: "Just as a great tree, slanting downward, sloping downward, inclining downward—when cut at the root, where will it fall?"[^7] The image of the broken pot in deep water, with shards sinking and oil rising, **does not survive into the Chinese tradition**. The structure (the five-quality-imbued mind rising upward) is preserved; the image (the oil floating up through water) is lost.

This loss has consequences. The Chinese recollection-of-the-Buddha tradition later developed a strong "deathbed reception" structure: Amitābha extending a golden lotus throne, the assembly of sages appearing, the practitioner's mind led across to that pure land. Part of the strong indigenous Chinese demand for this structure may stem from the fact that the Chinese Āgama layer **lacked the directly visualizable image of the mind self-rising**. Without the visible picture of oil floating up through water, *self-power ascent* needed to be replaced by a new visible picture—the *other-power reception* of Amitābha's coming. NIAN Part VI will trace this trajectory in full.

Returning to the present chapter's argument: reading the three passages together discloses what *devatānussati* is actually for. It is not "thinking about gods." It is not "aspiring to heavenly rebirth." It is **using the deities as an external benchmark to internalize the five qualities (faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom) into a portable death-insurance**. The end-design of the entire six-recollection system is to give the practitioner an internal anchor that **does not depend on present access** to the external triad (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha)—because the streets are too chaotic, the elephants too fast, and the deathbed may not allow time to recollect the Buddha. *Devatānussati* installs the safety net for moments when the first three are unavailable.

This observation is almost never explicitly made in Western literature. SN 55.21 is usually read as a personal pastoral exchange between the Buddha and Mahānāma; the five qualities of the *devatānussati* formula are usually read as "qualities deities possess and we should also possess"; the correspondence between the two is rarely connected. But the correspondence is textually overt—the same five qualities, the same grammatical position, the same functional use—merely distributed across two suttas.

Place them side by side, and the structure becomes visible.
### 3.4 Hidden Brahmavihāra Structure: The Critical Phrase in AN 6.26

There is a deeper thread inside the six-recollection system, one that points toward another famous Buddhist meditative system—the four *brahmavihāras* (loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity).

The thread is in AN 6.26, the Mahākaccāna sutta. The overall structure parallels AN 6.10—six recollections unpacked one by one, the same dynamic chain—but each recollection ends with a distinctive added line:

> *Sa kho so, āvuso, ariyasāvako sabbaso ākāsasamena cetasā viharati vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyāpajjena.*
> "That noble disciple dwells with a heart everywhere like space—abundant, expansive, limitless, free from enmity, free from ill will."[^8]

The six key terms—*ākāsasama* (everywhere like space), *vipula* (abundant), *mahaggata* (expansive), *appamāṇa* (limitless), *avera* (free from enmity), *abyāpajja* (free from ill will)—are immediately recognizable to anyone familiar with Pāli literature. They form **the standard formula for the four brahmavihāras**. Throughout DN 13, MN 99, and dozens of other suttas, whenever the Buddha describes the accomplishment of *mettā*, *karuṇā*, *muditā*, *upekkhā*, the same cluster of terms describes the resulting state of mind.[^9]

The cross-deployment of this cluster into the six-recollection context is not stylistic coincidence. The cross-reference says: **the six recollections internally carry the brahmavihāra's expansion mechanism**. They are not merely *saddhā*-style devotional repetition. At the moment of accomplishment, each recollection naturally opens into *appamāṇa*—the mind expands toward the limitless, free of enmity and harm.

This observation lays an important foundation for later chapters. NIAN Part V will treat the various Mahāyāna developments of recollection-of-the-Buddha, one of whose central threads is the theology of "infinite light, infinite life"—Amitābha (Infinite Light), Amitāyus (Infinite Life). The "infinite" (*amita* / *aparimita*) is often criticized as a Mahāyāna addition, an Indian-religious overlay disconnected from early Āgama meaning. But the *appamāṇa* evidence in AN 6.26 shows that "the limitless," as an internal accomplishment marker of the recollection of the Buddha, **already appears at the Āgama layer**. The Mahāyāna isolation of "limitless" as a constitutive attribute of Buddha-quality (Infinite Light, Infinite Life) is an extension of an early intra-recollection structure, not a parachute drop.

P12 (the chapter on Mahāsthāmaprāpta) will return to this thread. The present chapter notes only the existence of *appamāṇa* in AN 6.26 as evidence of the system's internal richness.

### 3.5 The *Visuddhimagga*'s Integration and Systematization

Buddhaghosa places the six recollections within the forty-meditation-subject system of *Visuddhimagga* chapter VII and gives each its own section. Following the paraphrase-only discipline established in P02, this section summarizes the structure as briefly as possible without quotation.

**§I Buddha-recollection**: covered in P02; not retraced here.

**§II Dhamma-recollection**: Buddhaghosa unpacks "well-explained" (*svākkhāto*) into multiple senses—suitable both for instruction and for the path; meaning-rich and elegantly phrased; beautiful at beginning, middle, and end; free from extremes (sense-pleasure and self-mortification, eternalism and annihilationism, being and non-being). The remaining five predicates (visible here-and-now, immediately effective, inviting inspection, leading inward, individually known by the wise) receive parallel treatment. **Structurally identical to the nine-epithet treatment**: dhamma-recollection presents a six-predicate map analogous to the Buddha-recollection's nine-epithet map.

**§III Saṅgha-recollection**: the four pairs and eight individuals are arranged by attainment level; "supreme field of merit" gets agricultural imagery. Buddhaghosa adds a striking concrete formulation: the practitioner of saṅgha-recollection comes to experience her own body as **"like the assembled Saṅgha in an Uposatha hall"**—the qualities of the Saṅgha internalize as the practitioner's own internal architecture.

**§IV Virtue-recollection**: the eight standard predicates of virtue (*akhaṇḍa*, etc.) are unpacked one by one, each illustrated through concrete imagery—a torn cloth, a spotted cow—so that subtle gaps in virtue become visualizable.

**§V Generosity-recollection**: Buddhaghosa lays down an operational discipline. The practitioner of cāgānussati must already be **cāgādhimutta**—someone naturally inclined to giving. If the beginner has not yet developed this disposition, she should first vow: "From this day on, if a worthy recipient comes, I will not eat without first giving even a single morsel." The practical habit must precede the recollection. The discipline is significant: the object of cāgānussati is "the generosity I have actually performed"; without actual generosity to recollect, the recollection cannot get off the ground.

**§VI Deity-recollection**: as already cited in §3.2 above—the deities are "placed as witness" (*sakkhiṭṭhāne ṭhapetvā*); what is actually recollected is the practitioner's own faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom. This is the explicit commentarial ruling that "deity-recollection ≠ thinking about gods."

**§VII Conclusion**: Buddhaghosa integrates five canonical contexts in which the Buddha taught the six recollections—Mahānāma, Gedha (AN 6.25), Sambādh-okāsa (AN 6.26), Uposatha, and AN 11.11—and issues two crucial verdicts.[^10]

First: because of the depth and multiplicity of the recollection-objects, the six recollections **only reach access concentration (*upacāra*); they do not reach absorption concentration (*appanā*)**. This is not a defect; it is a design choice. *Anussati* is built for ordinary life, not for retreat-hall absorption. P04 will pursue this line to its full statement—the boundary between *upacāra* and *appanā* is the principal axis of that chapter.

Second: the six recollections **truly succeed only for noble disciples (*ariyasāvaka*)**, because only for them are the qualities of Buddha, Dhamma, and Saṅgha fully evident, the virtue truly unbroken, the generosity actually arisen, and the five qualities verifiably present. Ordinary persons may also practice, but with limited reach. The verdict appears stringent but in fact protects the system's internal logic: if the object itself (Buddha-qualities, Dhamma-qualities, Saṅgha-qualities, one's own virtue, one's own generosity, the five qualities) has not yet been verified in the practitioner's experience, the dynamic chain cannot start.

### 3.6 From Six to Ten: The Expansion Dynamic

Buddhaghosa's full list in *Visuddhimagga* chapter VII is not six recollections but **ten**—the original six plus four additions: breathing (*ānāpāna*), body (*kāya*), death (*maraṇa*), and peace (*upasama*). The four additions have traditionally been read as Buddhaghosa's individual integrative work, a special product of the Theravāda commentarial tradition.

Open the Chinese *Ekottara Āgama*'s "Wider Exposition" chapter 3, however, and a striking correspondence appears. From the first sūtra to the tenth, in chapter 2, the text treats in sequence: "recollection of the Buddha, recollection of the Dharma, recollection of the Saṅgha, recollection of virtue, recollection of generosity, recollection of the deities, recollection of repose, recollection of breathing, recollection of the body, recollection of death."[^11] The set **corresponds item for item to Buddhaghosa's ten recollections**. The *Ekottara Āgama* was translated into Chinese in the Eastern Jin period by Gautama Saṅghadeva, deriving from a northern Sarvāstivāda or Mahāsāṃghika transmission, independent of Buddhaghosa's southern Theravāda lineage. Two independent transmissions preserved the same skeletal set.

The methodological significance is large. It means Buddhaghosa **was not the originator**—he did not personally invent "add four to the six." He was the **systematizer**—he organized into the *Visuddhimagga*'s commentarial structure a skeletal set already present in pre-sectarian Buddhism, inherited independently by both southern and northern traditions. The ten recollections were already formed before the sectarian period.

The two traditions also show distinctive expansion paths. Chinese *Saṃyukta Āgama* sūtra 933 represents one independent trajectory—inserting "**emptiness**" (*śūnyatā*) into the foundational five qualities, expanding five into six and the total of eleven into twelve.[^12] This early "emptiness" insertion, peculiar to the Chinese tradition, foreshadows later Mahāyāna emptiness-thought. Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* chapters 21–22 represent a third trajectory—six recollections plus recollection of breathing and recollection of death (eight total), without recollection of body or recollection of peace—Nāgārjuna's particular selection, with death-recollection repositioned as a tool against fear (§IV below).

Three trajectories together suggest: the six recollections form a stable core skeleton; expansion is a peripheral move. Each tradition adds its own items, but the choices are constrained by the tradition's overall doctrinal preferences—the Sarvāstivāda/Theravāda lineages select "body, death, breathing, peace"; the Chinese tradition foregrounds "emptiness"; Nāgārjuna foregrounds "death" and "breathing" with a new functional positioning. The skeleton is invariant; the periphery is elastic.

## IV. Cross-Tradition and Mahāyāna Preparation

### 4.1 Nāgārjuna's Expansion of *Cāga*: The Seed of *Dāna-Pāramitā*

In *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* chapter 22, Nāgārjuna gives a structural expansion of *cāgānussati*:

> "Generosity (*cāga*) has two kinds: first, giving; second, abandoning the defilements. Giving has two kinds: first, material giving; second, Dharma giving. The three kinds of generosity together are called *cāga*."[^13]

In the Pāli layer, *cāga* is principally **giving**—concrete, material, outward bestowal. Nāgārjuna expands the term into three directions: material giving (preserving the original sense), Dharma giving (a new addition), and abandoning the defilements (an internalization). With the three directions combined, the *cāga* category is **upgraded from a single recollection-object into a structural set** capable of carrying the unfolding of Mahāyāna *dāna-pāramitā* (the perfection of giving).

The expansion is not arbitrary. It extends the internal dynamic already operative in *cāgānussati*—"to release the grip of miserliness is itself to give"—from "giving things" out to "giving the Dharma" and "abandoning defilements (giving oneself liberation)." NIAN Part III will trace this trajectory into early Mahāyāna literature (the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi*, the rise of the buddha-field concept, the emergence of name-recitation). The present chapter marks the structural interface here.

### 4.2 Nāgārjuna's Reframing of *Devatā*: From Cosmological Heaven to Spiritual Heaven

In the same treatise, Nāgārjuna also restructures *devatānussati*:

> "There are four kinds of deities: nominal deities, deities by birth, pure deities, and pure deities by birth. The pure deities are the noble persons born among humans. The pure deities by birth are the noble persons born within the three realms of deities."[^14]

The category "deity" gets divided into four types. The first two preserve traditional usage—"nominal deities" refers to persons conventionally called "sons of heaven" (i.e., earthly rulers); "deities by birth" refers to the cosmological pantheon of devas. The latter two are new additions: **pure deities** are noble persons (*ārya*) attained among humans; **pure deities by birth** are noble persons attained while reborn in the three realms of devas. In other words, Nāgārjuna **expands "deity" from a cosmological position to a spiritual position**—a noble person is herself a "pure deity," whether born among humans or among devas.

This restructuring lays the ontological groundwork for the Mahāyāna conception of buddha-fields. The "pure" of "pure land" refers not to the deva-rebirth of the six destinations (the traditional object of *devatānussati*), but to **the gathering-place of pure deities**—the assembly of noble persons. Part IV will retrieve this thread when it treats the three Pure Land sūtras: a pure land is not "another heaven" but "a country where pure deities gather."

### 4.3 The Two Structural Seals of Bodhisattva Recollection

At the close of *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* chapter 22, Nāgārjuna cites the Buddha addressing Śāriputra:

> "A bodhisattva mahāsattva, **abiding non-abidingly in the perfection of wisdom**, should accomplish the perfection of giving … should accomplish the eight recollections, **because [the object] is unobtainable**."[^15]

Two structural seals are embedded—**"abiding non-abidingly"** and **"because [the object] is unobtainable"**—and together they transpose the śrāvaka eight recollections into the bodhisattva eight recollections.

The first seal, "abiding non-abidingly": when the bodhisattva practices the eight recollections, she does not abide in any of the eight, but abides in the perfection of wisdom. The content of practice is unchanged; the mode of abiding is changed. The śrāvaka abides in the recollection; the bodhisattva does not abide in the recollection but abides in *prajñāpāramitā*.

The second seal, "because the object is unobtainable": each of the eight recollections has its object (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, deities, breathing, death), and each may be practiced—but the object itself is unobtainable, dependently arisen, without intrinsic nature. This seal empties out each object, so that none functions as a basis for grasping.

Together the two seals demarcate the watershed between śrāvaka eight recollections and bodhisattva eight recollections. **What is practiced is the same; how it is abided in is different.** This principle runs throughout the unfolding of recollection-of-the-Buddha across NIAN Parts III–VIII—from the "seeing the Buddha" of the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi*, to the "name-holding" of the three Pure Land sūtras, to the "gathering the six faculties, pure-recollection in continuity" of the *Śūraṅgama* Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter, to the "vast offerings" of Samantabhadra in the *Avataṃsaka*—the objects of practice change endlessly, but the two seals continuously operate in the background.

### 4.4 The Western Reader's Framing Task

Two common misreadings circulate in contemporary Anglophone treatments of the six recollections.

The first reads the six as **six Buddhist affirmation practices**—a Buddhist variant of positive psychology or self-compassion exercise. The reading captures one element (positive confirmation of self-qualities) but loses three crucial features: the objects are verified facts rather than self-motivational language; the whole is a bidirectional system rather than a list of six; and the death-function of *devatānussati* is entirely obscured.

The second reads the six as **six forms of veneration of the qualities of the awakened ones**—a Buddhist version of Christian devotion or Hindu *bhakti*. This reading captures the outward-pointing character of the first three but completely misses the reflexive structure of the last three, flattening the entire system into one-directional reverence.

Both misreadings reduce the six recollections to a list. The Western-reader framing task of this book is to restore the six recollections to what they are: **a complete recollective system with internal bidirectional structure, a hidden brahmavihāra mechanism, designed jointly for daily life and for the moment of death**.
## V. Middle-Way Corrections

Before closing, three readings should be marked explicitly as **misreadings** (❌), and two as **suggestive cross-disciplinary analogies that should not be equated** (⚠️).

**❌ *Devatānussati* = theistic worship.**

The most common misreading. "Recollection of the deities" gets interpreted as "taking the deities as objects of worship." Correction: *Visuddhimagga* §VI explicitly states that the deities are "placed as witness" (*sakkhiṭṭhāne ṭhapetvā*); what is actually recollected is one's own faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom. The internal formula of AN 6.10 is also overtly reflexive: "I too possess such faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom." *Devatānussati* belongs to the internal-verification class, not the external-worship class. Reading it as theistic worship flattens the bidirectional structure into one-direction and entirely obscures the death-anchor function identified in §3.3.

**❌ *Sīlānussati* = guilt review of broken precepts.**

The second common misreading. "Recollection of virtue" gets interpreted as "reviewing whether I have broken any precepts." Correction: the object of *sīlānussati* is **the eight predicates of virtue one has already established** (unbroken, untorn, unspotted, unmottled, and so on), not the precepts one has broken. The dynamic chain produces *pāmojja*—gladness, the positive joy of the chain. If recollecting virtue produces remorse, self-blame, or guilt, the practice has gone wrong. The error is having flipped the object from "what I have established" to "what I have violated." *Sīlānussati* is not confession; it is confirmation.

**❌ *Saṅghānussati* = institutional veneration.**

The third common misreading. "Recollection of the Saṅgha" gets interpreted as veneration of a particular monastery, a living abbot, or a contemporary monastic administrative body. Correction: the object of *saṅghānussati* is "the four pairs and eight individuals, the supreme field of merit"—the **noble disciples who have attained the path**, regardless of monastery, sect, or era. The object is the noble attainment itself, not a contemporary institution. Reading it as institutional veneration demotes *saṅghānussati* to support for a local temple, completely losing the "reproducibility of the path" that §3.2 identifies as its core function.

**⚠️ The six recollections ≈ six Buddhist affirmation practices (CBT-style self-affirmation).**

Cross-disciplinary heuristic value: cognitive behavioral therapy's reframing techniques and self-compassion practices share a structure with the recollections—using positive statements to counter negative emotion. The analogy can help Western readers find an entry point.

Caution: affirmation operates at the ego level—"saying encouraging things to oneself"—and its efficacy rests on repetition itself, not on the truth of the content. The recollection-objects are **already-verified facts**: the Buddha has cut, the Dhamma has been verified, the Saṅgha has walked through, virtue has been established, generosity has arisen, the five qualities are present. If the object has not yet been verified in the practitioner's experience, the dynamic chain does not start (the *Visuddhimagga* §VII verdict). Affirmation lacks this "evidential grounding," and therefore differs from the recollections **structurally, not just in degree**.

**⚠️ The first three recollections ≈ Carl Jung's internalization of the *imago Dei*.**

Cross-disciplinary heuristic value: in Jungian psychology, the "internalization of the divine image" describes how an external sacred image gets internalized as a core element of psychic integration. The structural similarity to the borrowing function of the first three recollections is real.

Caution: the object in the Jungian model is an *archetype*—a universal structure of the collective unconscious. The object in the recollections is **specific and content-bearing**: the historical Buddha, a verified Dhamma, an attained Saṅgha, the practitioner's own confirmable virtue and qualities. The specificity and verifiability of the object place the recollections in a different epistemic category from archetypal internalization. The analogy can serve as a doorway tool but not as an equation.

## VI. Conclusion

This chapter has treated the six recollections. Six points summarize what it has done.

First, the six recollections share a single dynamic engine. The same nine-step transformation chain, the same syntax, the same functional positioning. The object varies; the engine does not. The Buddha did not teach six independent practices; he taught one system applied to six focal objects.

Second, the six objects form a bidirectional anchoring structure. The first three (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha) borrow qualities outward—borrowing "he has cut," borrowing "the Dhamma is verifiable," borrowing "the path has been walked." The last three (virtue, generosity, deities) verify qualities inward—confirming "virtue established," confirming "miserliness departed," confirming "the five qualities present." From outer evidence down to inner evidence, a complete anchoring loop.

Third, *devatānussati* is the hidden terminus of the system, designed for the moment of death. The five qualities of AN 6.10's *devatānussati* internal formula (faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom) are the same five qualities the Buddha gives Mahānāma in the SN 55.21 street-elephant episode as what brings the mind upward at death. The Chinese parallel in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 930 preserves the structure (the eastward-slanting tree falling east) but loses the image (the broken pot of oil rising in deep water)—and the loss of that image may be part of why the later Chinese tradition pivoted toward an other-power deathbed-reception structure.

Fourth, AN 6.26 carries a hidden brahmavihāra structure within it. The phrase "a heart everywhere like space, abundant, expansive, limitless, free from enmity, free from ill will" shares its formula with the four brahmavihāras. The recollections internally carry the *appamāṇa* (limitless) mechanism, foreshadowing the Mahāyāna theology of Infinite Light and Infinite Life—the Mahāyāna "infinite" is not a parachute drop but an extension of an early-recollection internal structure.

Fifth, Buddhaghosa was the systematizer, not the innovator. The Chinese *Ekottara Āgama*'s "Wider Exposition" chapter 3 contains the same ten-recollection set (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, deities, repose, breathing, body, death) that the *Visuddhimagga* presents in chapter VII. Two independent transmissions—southern Theravāda and northern Sarvāstivāda/Mahāsāṃghika—preserved the same skeletal set. Buddhaghosa organized into commentarial form what was already there.

Sixth, Nāgārjuna was the Mahāyāna pivot. He expanded *cāga* into material giving / Dharma giving / abandoning defilements (the seed of *dāna-pāramitā*). He restructured "deity" into nominal / by-birth / pure / pure-by-birth (the ontological foundation for Pure Land). And he sealed bodhisattva eight-recollection practice with two structural marks—"abiding non-abidingly in *prajñāpāramitā*" and "because the object is unobtainable." The watershed between śrāvaka and bodhisattva practice does not lie in *what* is practiced but in *how* it is abided in.

The three questions left open at the end of P02—why six, why these six, how do the other five operate—have now received their direct answer. What the Buddha left is not a list. It is a map.

The next chapter, P04, will treat one boundary the present chapter has not pursued: the interface within the six (and ten) recollections between *anussati* and *jhāna*—the distinction between access concentration (*upacāra*) and absorption concentration (*appanā*). The *Visuddhimagga* §VII marks this boundary but does not unfold it fully. The boundary is the key to understanding why *anussati* is *anussati* (rather than a kind of *jhāna*), and the key to understanding how the Chinese "buddha-recollection samādhi" and "name-holding recollection" both inherited and transformed the original *anussati* structure.

Nine words open nine small doors; the six recollections open six larger doors; the ten recollections open ten through-doors. One engine, six objects, bidirectional anchoring, internal infinity. This is the map the Buddha left.

*Namo tassa bhagavato arahato sammāsambuddhassa.*

On the question of *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* authorship, this paper follows the position established in P02: traditional attribution is retained without entering the debate.

[^14]: *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 22, T1509 (T25, p. 226b). Kumārajīva. Original: "There are four kinds of deities: nominal deities, deities by birth, pure deities, and pure deities by birth. The pure deities are the noble persons born among humans. The pure deities by birth are the noble persons born within the three realms of deities." This restructuring lays the ontological foundation for the Mahāyāna conception of buddha-fields.

[^15]: *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 22, T1509 (T25, p. 228b). Kumārajīva. Original: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, abiding non-abidingly in the perfection of wisdom, should accomplish the perfection of giving … should accomplish the eight recollections, because [the object] is unobtainable." The two seals "abiding non-abidingly" and "because [the object] is unobtainable" function as structural markers of bodhisattva eight-recollection practice and operate throughout the Mahāyāna recollection-of-the-Buddha unfolding across NIAN Parts III–VIII.
