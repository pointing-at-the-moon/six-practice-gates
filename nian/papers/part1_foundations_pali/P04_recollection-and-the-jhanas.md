**Access, Absorption, and the Limits of Multi-Faceted Objects**

## Abstract

This paper offers a precise delineation of the technical difference between *upacāra-samādhi* (access concentration) and *appanā-samādhi* (absorption), and explains, through a structural analysis of meditation objects, why only two of the ten *anussati* (*ānāpānasati* and *kāyagatāsati*) reach *appanā*, while the remaining practices stop at *upacāra* as their designed terminus. The paper proposes a structural re-classification of the forty *kammaṭṭhāna*: **single-faceted objects** can generate a stable *paṭibhāga-nimitta* that allows *ekaggatā* to lock in, reaching *appanā*; **multi-faceted objects** structurally resist such convergence. Drawing on Buddhaghosa's two reasons in *Visuddhimagga* VII.1 for why *buddhānussati* reaches only *upacāra* (the depth of the Buddha's qualities; the mind's commitment to recollecting various kinds of qualities), the paper advances its first methodological finding: "movement among multiple facets" is a *functional* requirement of *anussati*, not a deficiency of practice. A second original finding addresses the semantic latitude of the Chinese term *niànfó sānmèi* 念佛三昧 ("buddha-recollection samādhi"): in *Dà zhìdù lùn* and related texts this term carries at least three distinct meanings—the *upacāra*-platform sense, the *appanā*-compression sense, and the Mahāyāna visionary sense—and the later Chinese *niànfó* streams (nāma-recitation, visualization, reality-principle) in fact draw upon this tri-semantic latitude for their differentiation. This paper maps only the technical coordinates; the historical trajectory is reserved for Part VI. Cross-disciplinary engagement compares Csikszentmihalyi's flow state with *upacāra* across three phenomenological parallels, while naming three non-reducible structural differences (teleology, object ontology, upgrade path).

**Keywords:** anussati; jhāna; upacāra-samādhi; appanā-samādhi; ekaggatā; buddhānussati; niànfó sānmèi; object classification; Visuddhimagga; Dà zhìdù lùn; flow state; Phussadeva

## I. Introduction: Why Is Buddha-Recollection Not Jhāna?

A recurring question in Western mindfulness circles runs like this: does *niànfó*—Buddha-recollection—count as meditation? If a ten-minute secular mindfulness breathing app can induce a measurable meditative state, should not a dozen repetitions of *Itipi so*—the Pāli formula for the nine epithets of the Buddha—do the same, or more?

The direct answer comes from a 5th-century Sri Lankan monk named Buddhaghosa. In chapter VII of his *Visuddhimagga*, he states that **buddha-recollection, along with all six of the classical *anussati*, reaches only access concentration (*upacāra-samādhi*) and not absorption (*appanā-samādhi*)**. He gives two reasons at once: the depth of the Buddha's qualities, and the structural commitment of the mind to recollecting multiple kinds of qualities. His judgment is not "practitioners are not trying hard enough." It is that access concentration is the technical terminus of *anussati* as a category of *kammaṭṭhāna* (meditation object).

This paper has four tasks.

First, to delineate precisely what *upacāra* and *appanā* each are (§3.1), reading the five *jhāna*-factors directly out of the nine-step psychological dynamic chain in AN 6.10, without circumventing the sutta-primary evidence.

Second, to explain, through a structural account of object type, why only two (or three, depending on which analysis of *upasamānussati* one accepts) of the ten *anussati* reach *appanā* while the remaining seven or eight stop at *upacāra* (§3.2–§3.3). The paper introduces a core classification here: **single-faceted objects** versus **multi-faceted objects**.

Third, to argue that this boundary is not a deficit but a design choice. *Anussati* was engineered for the vicissitudes of daily life, not for the ideal conditions of retreat (§3.4).

Fourth, to show how this technical boundary opens a semantic latitude in the Chinese term *niànfó sānmèi* 念佛三昧, allowing it to carry three mutually distinct senses in subsequent Mahāyāna reception—a latitude within which later Chinese *niànfó* traditions in fact differentiated themselves (§3.5). The paper names the technical boundary only; the historical trajectory is reserved for Part VI.

The paper assumes familiarity with NIAN-P02 (the nine epithets and the grammatical transposition from predicate to identity) and NIAN-P03 (the six recollections and their bidirectional external-borrowing / internal-verification structure). The frame introduced in P03 §3.1—"one engine, six objects"—receives its first active application here: the same nine-step dynamic chain (engine), met by different object types (single-faceted versus multi-faceted), produces different terminations (*appanā* versus *upacāra*).

## II. Canonical Basis

The paper draws on four suttas as primary canonical ground, and on *Visuddhimagga* and *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (*Dà zhìdù lùn*, hereafter DZDL) passages as commentarial support, with parallel citation of Pāli and Chinese references.

### 2.1 Primary Sūtra Basis

| Sūtra | Reference | Paper's Use |
|---|---|---|
| AN 6.10 *Mahānāma-sutta* | PTS AN iii 285 ; parallel in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 931 | The full nine-step psychological dynamic chain—containing three of the five *jhāna*-factors (*pīti*, *sukha*, *samādhi*/*ekaggatā*)[^1] |
| SN 54 *Ānāpāna-saṃyutta* (parallels in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 803, 814) | PTS SN v 311ff ; T0099 scroll 29 | The sixteen-step *ānāpānasati* → first through fourth *jhāna* absorption—direct sutta evidence that breath-recollection reaches *appanā*[^2] |
| MN 10 *Satipaṭṭhāna-sutta* / DN 22 *Mahā-satipaṭṭhāna-sutta* | PTS MN i 55 / DN ii 290 | The four-satipaṭṭhāna framework; *kāyagatāsati* as body-based *appanā* object[^3] |
| AN 11.11 *Paṭhama-Mahānāma-sutta* | PTS AN v 328 | Cultivating the six recollections in all postures—walking, standing, sitting, lying down—the sutta support for daily-life executability[^4] |

### 2.2 Sutta Anchor for the Five Jhāna-Factors

The Pāli stock formula for first *jhāna* appears in many places in the Nikāyas (among others MN 27, MN 36, DN 2):

> *Vivicc';eva kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi savitakkaṃ savicāraṃ vivekajaṃ pītisukhaṃ paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati*—having secluded himself from sensual desires and unwholesome states, with applied and sustained thought, with the rapture and bliss born of seclusion, he enters and dwells in the first jhāna.

The five factors are *vitakka* (applied thought), *vicāra* (sustained thought), *pīti* (rapture), *sukha* (bliss), and *ekaggatā* (one-pointedness, stable in *samādhi*)[^5].

The second *jhāna* drops *vitakka* and *vicāra*, keeping *pīti*, *sukha*, and *ekaggatā*; the third drops *pīti*; the fourth drops *sukha*, leaving *upekkhā* (equanimity) and *ekaggatā*. The Chinese *Dà zhìdù lùn*, scroll 21, gives a parallel account: "In the first *dhyāna*, investigation and scrutiny move the mind; in the second, great rapture moves it; in the third, great bliss moves it; in the fourth, nothing moves"—the Pāli and Chinese analyses of the four-*jhāna* architecture converge on the same essentials[^6].

### 2.3 Commentarial Basis

**Visuddhimagga VII.1 *Buddhānussati-niddesa***: Buddhaghosa unfolds each of the nine epithets in turn and, at the end of the *bhagavā* discussion, explicitly states that *buddhānussati* reaches only *upacāra*, not *appanā*. He gives two reasons—the depth of the Buddha's qualities (*gambhīratā*) and the mind's commitment to recollecting various kinds of qualities (*nānappakāra-guṇānussaraṇa-adhimutti*)—which form the direct doctrinal basis for §3.2 below[^7].

**Visuddhimagga VII.7 *Cha-anussati-niddesa-samāpana***: The conclusion of the six-recollection chapter reaffirms the *upacāra* limit and illustrates it with the example of the elder Phussadeva, who developed *vipassanā* from the *upacāra* platform of *buddhānussati* and attained arahatship. This is the principal textual support for §3.4[^8].

**DZDL scroll 21** (*Exposition of the Eight Releases, bā bèi shě yì* 八背捨義): The Chinese commentarial account of the four-*dhyāna* stratification; *bā bèi shě* releases, the later four *abhibhū-āyatanāni*, and *kasiṇa* totality-practices are all placed within the fourth *dhyāna*. This scaffolding converges with Pāli *appanā*-depth analysis[^9].

**DZDL scroll 22** (end of *Exposition of the Eight Recollections, bā niàn yì* 八念義): *Nāgārjuna* positions *buddhānussati* as "in the seventh stage," "either tainted or untainted," "associated with three feeling-faculties—pleasure, joy, and equanimity." The depth-judgment is deliberately indeterminate. This indeterminacy is the subject of §3.5[^10].

**DZDL scroll 7** (passage on *niànfó sānmèi* 念佛三昧): "*Niànfó sānmèi* is the *samādhi* in which one sees with the mind's eye the Buddhas of the ten directions and three times, as though face to face"—the passage infuses a visionary sense into the Chinese term without using the classical *upacāra*/*appanā* framework. This is the principal anchor for §3.5 sense C[^11].

The Chinese *Ekottara Āgama* T0125, scroll 2 (*Guǎngyǎn pǐn* 廣演品, "Chapter on Expansive Discourses"), lists the ten *anussati* in full: recollection of the Buddha, the Dharma, the Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, the deities, peace (*upasama*), the in-breath and out-breath, the body, and death. This matches the *Visuddhimagga*'s ten *anussati* exactly. The two traditions converge on the list; they diverge in that the Chinese material does not provide an explicit *upacāra*/*appanā* discrimination[^12].

## III. Central Argument

### 3.1 Access and Absorption, Precisely Defined

At the operational level, *upacāra-samādhi* and *appanā-samādhi* differ along three criteria.

**Criterion 1: The state of the five hindrances (*nīvaraṇa*).**
- *Upacāra*: the five hindrances (sensual desire, ill-will, sloth-and-torpor, restlessness-and-remorse, doubt) have been temporarily suppressed (*vikkhambhita*); the mind has entered a stably concentrated condition.
- *Appanā*: the hindrances are suppressed, *and* the mind has entered the first jhāna state of seclusion from sensuality and from the unwholesome.

**Criterion 2: The completeness of the five *jhāna*-factors.**
- *Upacāra*: *vitakka* and *vicāra* continue to operate; *pīti* and *sukha* have arisen; but *ekaggatā* has not yet locked onto a single point.
- *Appanā*: all five factors (*vitakka, vicāra, pīti, sukha, ekaggatā*) are stably present, and the mind has locked onto the *paṭibhāga-nimitta* (counterpart sign, the subjectively purified image of the object) as a single point.

**Criterion 3: The relation between mind and object.**
- *Upacāra*: the mind maintains an integral absorption in the object, but can move across the object's multiple facets without losing its overall grip.
- *Appanā*: the mind collapses onto a single point on the object; the multi-facetedness of the object is bracketed for the duration of absorption.

Of these three, criterion 2 is the technical center. The stable lock-in of *ekaggatā* is the threshold of *appanā*; short of this threshold but with the other four factors present, the state is *upacāra*.

**Reading the five factors out of the AN 6.10 nine-step chain.**

NIAN-P03 already laid out the full nine-step chain of AN 6.10: *ujugata citta* (unswerving mind) → *attha-veda* (inspiration in the meaning) → *dhamma-veda* (inspiration in the teaching) → *pāmojja* (gladness) → *pīti* (rapture) → *passaddhi kāya* (tranquilized body) → *sukha* (bliss) → *samādhi* (concentration). Three of the five jhāna-factors (*pīti, sukha, samādhi*) are explicitly named in this sutta text. The remaining two (*vitakka, vicāra*) are supplied by the operation of *anussarati* itself: the act of recollecting is both *manasikāra* (the bringing of the object to awareness: the function of *vitakka*) and *anuvicāra* (the sustaining of awareness on the object: the function of *vicāra*)[^13].

Buddhaghosa's VII.1 commentary writes this chain as *Buddhaguṇapoṇā vitakkavicārā pavattanti*—"the applied and sustained thoughts inclining toward the Buddha's qualities arise"—explicitly naming four of the five factors. He is systematizing the sutta structure, not inventing it. This resonates with P02 §3.6's characterization: "Buddhaghosa is a systematizer, not an innovator."

### 3.2 Object Classification: Single-Faceted versus Multi-Faceted

The forty *kammaṭṭhāna* (meditation objects) are systematically enumerated in *Visuddhimagga* III.104–III.111, and each is assigned to a jhāna-depth in the subsequent chapters. This paper proposes a structural re-classification to explain the technical differences in jhāna-depth attainment—**single-faceted objects versus multi-faceted objects**.

**Single-faceted objects** have structurally simple internal content and a unified direction. In the course of repeated recollection they can generate a stable *paṭibhāga-nimitta* (counterpart sign). The mind can continue to converge upon them until lock-in occurs.

Representative instances: the ten *kasiṇa* (totality-devices: earth, water, fire, wind, blue, yellow, red, white, space, consciousness-light); the ten *asubha* (corpse-decay stages); *ānāpānasati* (mindfulness of breath); *kāyagatāsati* (mindfulness of the body, via the thirty-two parts to the white-skeleton image); *upasamānussati* (recollection of peace) in its compressed form.

**Multi-faceted objects** have structurally complex internal content and multiple directions. The object itself resists convergence to a single point. Each facet is an independent semantic unit; when the mind reaches the next facet, it must move.

Representative instances: the nine epithets of the Buddha (*buddhānussati*); the six *svākkhāta*-qualities of the Dharma (*dhammānussati*); the four practice-characters and eight classes of the Saṅgha (*saṅghānussati*); the five characteristics of unbroken ethical conduct (*sīlānussati*); the five qualities of generosity (*cāgānussati*); the celestial stratifications and shared qualities of the deities (*devatānussati*); the eight manifestations of death (*maraṇasati*).

A plain-language analogy helps here. A single-faceted object is like staring at a candle flame: the gaze narrows, and the flame gradually becomes a single interior point of light. A multi-faceted object is like taking in a nine-paneled gallery wall at once: each panel must be read on its own, and once one has been read, the gaze must move to the next. The first mode can collapse into deep, locked attention; the second remains, from start to finish, a mobile movement across the integrating whole.

Buddhaghosa, at the end of his VII.1 commentary on *buddhānussati*, gives two reasons for the *upacāra*-only outcome, which can be paraphrased as follows: first, the depth of the Buddha's qualities (*gambhīratā*)—each epithet comprehends immeasurable merits, so the mind cannot exhaust and therefore cannot rest on any one of them; second, the mind's commitment to recollecting various kinds of qualities (*nānappakāra-guṇānussaraṇa-adhimutti*)—the training of *anussati* itself consists in moving the mind among multiple qualities, and this *adhimutti* (committed orientation) is structurally incompatible with the lock-in of *ekaggatā*[^14].

The second reason is doctrinally important. It is not the case that practitioners are insufficiently focused; rather, **movement across multiple facets is a *functional* requirement of *anussati***. If the mind were to lock fixedly on a single epithet, the *anussati* practice would itself cease; what remained would be *kasiṇa* or *ānāpāna* technique, not *anussati*. "Movement among multiple facets" is an operational element of the practice, not a deficiency. This is the first methodological finding of this paper.

### 3.3 Why Three Out of Ten?

On the *Visuddhimagga*'s overall classification, among the ten *anussati* only *ānāpānasati*, *kāyagatāsati*, and (in the traditional formulation) *upasamānussati* reach *appanā*; the other seven stop at *upacāra*. The table below shows the distribution, with object-type assignment:

| Recollection | upacāra | appanā | Object Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buddha-recollection (*buddhānussati*) | ✓ | — | multi-faceted (nine epithets / ten names) |
| Dharma-recollection (*dhammānussati*) | ✓ | — | multi-faceted (six *svākkhāta* qualities) |
| Saṅgha-recollection (*saṅghānussati*) | ✓ | — | multi-faceted (four practice-characters, eight classes) |
| Virtue-recollection (*sīlānussati*) | ✓ | — | multi-faceted (five characteristics: *akhaṇḍa, acchidda, asabala, akammāsa, aparāmaṭṭha*) |
| Generosity-recollection (*cāgānussati*) | ✓ | — | multi-faceted (five qualities: *muttacāga, payatapāṇi, vossaggarata, yācayoga, dānasaṃvibhāgarata*) |
| Deity-recollection (*devatānussati*) | ✓ | — | multi-faceted (celestial strata + shared five virtues: faith, ethics, learning, generosity, wisdom) |
| Death-recollection (*maraṇasati*) | ✓ | — | multi-faceted (eight manifestations: *vadhaka-paccupaṭṭhāna* etc.) |
| Breath-recollection (*ānāpānasati*) | ✓ | **✓** | single-faceted (unified breath stream → *paṭibhāga-nimitta*) |
| Body-recollection (*kāyagatāsati*) | ✓ | **✓** | single-faceted (thirty-two parts → unified skeleton image) |
| Peace-recollection (*upasamānussati*) | ✓ | — | multi-faceted (multi-aspected praise of *nibbāna*) |

**A caveat about peace-recollection.** Although Theravāda commentarial traditions sometimes summarize "three recollections reach absorption," the *Visuddhimagga* VIII.8 *Upasamānussati-niddesa* in fact treats peace-recollection as an *upacāra* object—because the descriptions of *nibbāna* in MN 64 and AN 9.36 are multi-faceted praise (peaceful, sublime, free of desire, ceased, absolute, and so on), not a unified indication. Only when a practitioner transitions from *upasamānussati* to the direct experience of *nibbāna* as *anāgāmi* or *arahant* does she enter another kind of *samādhi* (*phala-samāpatti*, the fruition attainment), and this does not fall within the forty *kammaṭṭhāna* of the *samatha* curriculum.

So on a strict reading there are only **two** *anussati* that reach *appanā*—breath and body. For continuity with the more widely circulated "three" formulation this paper preserves the traditional count in its summary, with the commentarial nuance now flagged.

**The three criteria restated.**

1. **Can a *paṭibhāga-nimitta* be generated?** Single-faceted objects can; multi-faceted objects cannot—there are too many qualities to synthesize into a unified purified image.
2. **When the mind collapses onto a single point, does the object remain intact?** For single-faceted objects, yes; for multi-faceted objects, if the mind collapses onto one facet, the other facets are lost, and the *anussati* practice itself terminates.
3. **Is there explicit sutta support for the jhāna-depth claim?** For *ānāpānasati*, yes: the chain linking breath-practice to all four *jhānas* and beyond is explicit in *Saṃyukta Āgama* T0099 scroll 29, sutta 814. For *kāyagatāsati*, yes: MN 10 and DN 22 treat it as the principal *kāya* object of *satipaṭṭhāna*. For the other recollections, there is no explicit sutta linkage to four-*jhāna* attainment.

**Why is death-recollection not single-faceted?** An intuitive objection is that "death is a single event" and should therefore be a single-faceted object. But the *Visuddhimagga* and *Ekottara Āgama* scroll 2's death-recollection passage both classify it as *upacāra*. The reason is that "death" as an object of *anussati* is not the bare event but its eight aspects—death as executioner, death as loss of fortune, death as common to all, death through disease or accident, and so on—each a distinct object of *vicāra*. **It is not the singular or plural character of death as such, but the way *anussati* approaches it**, that determines the object type.

### 3.4 This Is Not a Defect

A core reframing of this paper: *upacāra* is not "absorption not yet attained." *Upacāra* is the designed terminus of *anussati*.

**Evidence one: anussati is designed for daily life.**

AN 11.11 and AN 11.12, the two *Mahānāma-suttas*, explicitly state that the six recollections should be cultivated while walking, standing, sitting, lying down, while working, and while at home with one's children[^15]. *Appanā* can only be entered in deep seclusion; *upacāra*, by contrast, can be maintained across all postures. The design objective of *anussati* is precisely **portability**—the six recollections were made for the lay monk-householder living in the noise of Kapilavatthu and Sāvatthī, not for the meditator in retreat.

This also explains why *anussati* in the early Sangha was primarily used by lay practitioners and junior monastics. For them, *upacāra* was a practical meditative target; deep *appanā* was not the primary need. The *upacāra*-format of *anussati* is the Buddha's portable-stability design for in-the-world conditions.

**Evidence two: the upacāra platform is sufficient for vipassanā.**

The *Visuddhimagga* VII.7 records the example of the elder Phussadeva[^16]. The essence of the story, paraphrased: the elder one day saw a phantom Buddha-image created by Māra. His mind reasoned: "If even this counterfeit image is so radiant, how much more radiant must the real Buddha be, who is free from greed, hatred, and delusion." From this reflection *pīti* arose; from *pīti* he developed *vipassanā*; and he attained arahatship. The entire trajectory moves from *buddhānussati* on its *upacāra* platform to liberation, without passing through *appanā*.

The example has direct implications for the Chinese *niànfó* tradition. Chinese practitioners often take "niànfó sānmèi" 念佛三昧 to imply that one must penetrate deeply into absorption before insight can arise. But the structural lesson of Phussadeva is that the *upacāra* platform—stable mind, hindrances suppressed, *pīti* arisen—is already sufficient as a launching point for insight. One need not ascend first to *appanā*.

**Contemporary implications.**

Reading *upacāra* as an immature form of *appanā* is equivalent to reading *anussati* as a variant of *kasiṇa* technique. This is a common structural misreading in certain Western meditation teachings that foreground *jhāna*-attainment as the primary goal. In Chinese Buddhist *niànfó* practice it yields a specific, and specifically misleading, judgment: if *niànfó* does not bring about obvious *jhānic* phenomenology (locked attention, absorption signs, stability of the five factors), it is deemed "weak." The root of the misjudgment is not recognizing that *anussati*'s design terminus is *upacāra*.

A design terminus is not a failure terminus. When the six recollections reach *upacāra*, the work of *anussati* has been completed; the next work is to launch *vipassanā* from that platform, not to push further toward *appanā*.

### 3.5 The Three-Fold Semantic Latitude of *Niànfó Sānmèi* 念佛三昧

This section advances the paper's most important original finding. The Chinese term *niànfó sānmèi* 念佛三昧, in the translation-workshops and commentaries of the 2nd through 5th centuries CE, carries at least three mutually distinct meanings. These three senses are not confusions on the part of the translators; they are a semantic latitude produced by the Mahāyāna-transitional expansion of *anussati-samādhi* functionality, an expansion still not stabilized at the moment of its Chinese reception. The same latitude would later be inhabited by distinct streams of the Chinese *niànfó* tradition, each taking its preferred sense.

**Sense A: the *upacāra*-platform sense (inherited from the Nikāya-Visuddhimagga tradition).**

This sense directly inherits the sutta and *Visuddhimagga* structure of *buddhānussati*: the recollection of the Buddha reaches the stable platform of *upacāra-samādhi*, with hindrances suppressed, *pīti* and *sukha* arisen, but without entry into *appanā*. The function is to stabilize a dispersed mind into a pre-*vipassanā* platform.

The *Dà zhìdù lùn* scroll 22, at the end of the *Bā niàn yì* exposition, places *buddhānussati* close to this sense: "*Niànfó* is in the seventh stage; it is either tainted or untainted; it is associated with three feeling-faculties: pleasure, joy, equanimity." The referent of "seventh stage" is ambiguous (possibly a combination of first through fourth *dhyāna* with the formless planes), but the "three-faculty association" (*pīti, sukha, upekkhā*) relates to *jhāna* five-factor structure without asserting explicit *appanā* attainment. This is *Nāgārjuna*'s (or the *Dà zhìdù lùn* compiler's) more cautious position on *śrāvaka buddhānussati*.

**Sense B: the *appanā*-compression sense (multi-faceted object compressed to single-faceted).**

When the object of Buddha-recollection is compressed from "the Buddha's nine epithets / ten names" to a single feature—a single visualized form-body (*buddha-rūpa*), a single luminous mark (the *ūrṇā* between the brows, or the crown-radiance), or a single name (as in the later nāma-recitation tradition)—the object's structure shifts from multi-faceted to single-faceted. At that point *ekaggatā* can lock in, and *appanā* can be attained.

This mechanism is given concrete operational instructions in the early Chinese meditation texts—*Zuòchán sānmèi jīng* 坐禪三昧經 T0613 (translated by Kumārajīva) and *Guānfó sānmèi hǎi jīng* 觀佛三昧海經 T0643 (translated by Buddhabhadra)—but this paper does not develop them; NIAN Part III P06 will take the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi-sūtra* T0418 as its main venue for full treatment.

The *Dà zhìdù lùn* scroll 22 also hints at this sense in passing: "Those who attain it by fruition, like the beings of the Pure Land, are naturally able to recollect Buddha from birth"—this "natural" recollection is no longer the *upacāra*-style continuous *vicāra* across nine epithets but a steady-state of *appanā*-level stability.

**Sense C: the Mahāyāna visionary sense (a kind of *samādhi* outside the Nikāya-*jhāna* framework).**

This sense is the most difficult to absorb into the classical *samatha-vipassanā* framework. The *Dà zhìdù lùn* scroll 7 describes: "*Niànfó sānmèi* is the *samādhi* in which the Buddhas of the ten directions and three times are ever seen with the mind's eye, as though face to face. … This *niànfó sānmèi* can dissolve many kinds of afflictions and prior-life offenses. Other *samādhis* can dissolve some kinds but not others—some eliminate lust but not hatred, some eliminate hatred but not lust … *niànfó sānmèi* alone can dissolve many kinds of afflictions and many kinds of offenses."

The *samādhi* in which "the Buddhas of ten directions and three times are ever seen with the mind's eye" is neither an *upacāra* platform nor an *appanā* attained through object-compression. It is a Mahāyāna extension of *buddhānussati* into an **ontological visionary *samādhi***, whose function exceeds the *śrāvaka samatha-vipassanā* framework: seeing the Buddhas, entering the Buddha path, dissolving prior-life offenses, keeping the Buddhas perpetually present to one's awareness. The *samādhi* ontology presupposed here requires *dharmakāya* or equivalent ontological categories; the forty *kammaṭṭhāma* of *śrāvaka* meditation cannot contain it.

The *Pratyutpanna-samādhi-sūtra* T0418's "face-to-face Buddha-seeing" and the *Avataṃsaka*'s *Gaṇḍavyūha* chapter's visions of *samādhi* by Sudhana in his encounters with bodhisattvas are later developments along this trajectory.

**The structural significance of the three senses co-existing.**

*Nāgārjuna* (or the *Dà zhìdù lùn* compiler), in scrolls 7 and 22, uses *niànfó sānmèi* without explicitly distinguishing these three senses. This is not an oversight. The Mahāyāna-transitional expansion of *anussati-samādhi* function was multi-directional and still unstable; the text deliberately preserves semantic latitude, so that inheritance from the *śrāvaka* tradition and extension into Mahāyāna possibility can both be accommodated.

Later Chinese *niànfó* traditions in fact differentiate themselves within this latitude: nāma-recitation streams (*chímíng* 持名) gravitate toward sense B (reaching *appanā* through object compression); visualization streams (*guānxiǎng* 觀想) gravitate toward sense B with reach toward C (image-recollection deepening into the visionary); reality-principle streams (*shíxiàng* 實相), including Tiantai's Zhiyi and late-Ming figures such as Ouyi and Hanshan synthesizing Chan and Pure Land, gravitate toward sense C (*niànfó is niànxīn*, recollection of Buddha is recollection of mind, recollection of Buddha is reality itself). NIAN Part VI will develop this threefold differentiation in full.

**The technical position of this chapter.**

The paper makes no historical evaluation. It only names the tri-semantic structure of this Chinese term. When readers encounter *niànfó sānmèi* in Chinese Buddhist literature, they can use this coordinate system to locate the usage: Is it a sense-A *upacāra* platform? A sense-B *appanā* through compression? Or a sense-C Mahāyāna visionary *samādhi*? The three require different technical comprehension and different practice expectations. NIAN Parts III (early Mahāyāna), IV (the three Pure Land sūtras), V (the great Mahāyāna sūtras), and VI (the traditional streams) will all invoke this coordinate system.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Engagement: Recollection and Flow State

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi proposed the "flow state" concept in 1975 and systematized it in his 1990 book *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience*. The phenomenology he describes includes narrowed attention, backgrounded self-referential thought, attenuated time perception, deepening task absorption, and a dynamic balance between skill and challenge.

There are three observable parallels between flow and *upacāra-samādhi*:

**First**, narrowed attention ≈ *ujugata citta*. The "straightened mind" of *upacāra* and the narrow focus of flow share a phenomenological surface: extraneous stimuli recede; the mind-object coupling stabilizes.

**Second**, backgrounded self-referential thought ≈ *rāgadosamoha-pariyuṭṭhita citta*-absence. The "loss of self-consciousness" often noted in flow literature and the Nikāya's "mind not seized by greed, hatred, or delusion" have structural isomorphism at the descriptive level.

**Third**, time-sense attenuation ≈ *pāmojja* → *pīti* experiential continuity. The subjective time-compression of flow and the increasing experiential fluidity across the nine-step *anussati* chain look similar at the descriptive surface.

**⚠️ Three structural differences.** The three parallels do not make these the same phenomenon. Three non-reducible differences must be named.

**Teleological difference.** The *telos* of flow is performance optimization and intrinsic motivation enhancement—improving skill execution, enhancing work experience, facilitating creativity. The *telos* of *anussati* is the establishment of a pre-*vipassanā* platform, support for the *samatha* wing of the liberation path, and ultimately *nibbāna*. These two *teloi* are not interchangeable.

**Object difference.** The object of flow is a skill-task challenge—a dynamic balance between technical demand and present capability (piano, climbing, painting, coding). The object of *anussati* is the Buddha's qualities (an ethical-ontological predicate with a three-fold indication: ethical, ontological, soteriological). These two ontologies are different.

**Upgrade-path difference.** Flow has no *appanā* or *jhāna* five-factor structure, no *vipassanā* linkage; its terminus is "this particular experience ends." *Anussati* belongs to the *samatha* wing of the twin *samatha-vipassanā* path; its terminus is not the experience itself, but the liberative observation that the experience platforms.

**Guarding against a McMindfulness-style misreading.** To read *anussati* as a form of flow-style attention management is to demote liberative practice to the status of a performance tool. This misreading has in recent years become widespread in "mindfulness as productivity hack" discourse—secular mindfulness has been absorbed by corporate HR as an employee stress-management device. The warning here echoes P02 §IV's cross-disciplinary methodology: when Buddhist concepts enter secular contexts, they must carry their soteriological anchor with them; they cannot be permitted to settle into lifestyle register.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

- **❌-1**: "The six recollections reach only access concentration, so they are an incomplete *samatha* training." *Mistaken.* The designed terminus of the six recollections is *upacāra*; this is a function of *anussati*'s portability design (for in-the-world conditions). Reading *upacāra* as "not-yet-attained *appanā*" is to use the retreat-standard to judge a daily-life practice by its wrong criterion.

- **❌-2**: "*Niànfó sānmèi* means *niànfó sānmèi*; no semantic distinction is needed." *Mistaken.* The Chinese term carries at least three mutually distinct senses (A *upacāra* / B *appanā* / C Mahāyāna visionary). Reading Chinese *niànfó* literature without this distinction leads to misalignment in doctrinal positioning and in practice expectations. The technical root of the later differentiation among Chinese *niànfó* streams lies precisely within this tri-semantic latitude.

- **⚠️**: The phenomenological parallels between flow state and *upacāra-samādhi* do not constitute soteriological equivalence. The three non-reducible differences (teleology, object ontology, upgrade path) mark a structural gap. To invoke flow-state framing to make *anussati* accessible is helpful, but warning labels are non-optional.

---

## VI. Conclusion

A technical boundary has been clearly mapped. Within the forty *kammaṭṭhāna*, the *anussati* family as a whole reaches *upacāra-samādhi* and not *appanā-samādhi*. The structural ground for this judgment lies in object type: single-faceted objects can generate the *paṭibhāga-nimitta* that permits *ekaggatā* to lock in; multi-faceted objects structurally resist this convergence, and the mind's movement among their facets is a *functional* requirement of the practice, not a deficit. Of the ten *anussati*, only breath-recollection and body-recollection reach *appanā* on a strict reading; the remaining eight—including buddha-, dharma-, saṅgha-, virtue-, generosity-, deity-, peace-, and death-recollection—stop at *upacāra* as their designed terminus, from which *vipassanā* is to be launched.

This boundary opens a tri-semantic latitude in the Chinese term *niànfó sānmèi*—*upacāra*-platform sense, *appanā*-compression sense, Mahāyāna visionary sense—within which the later Chinese *niànfó* streams (nāma-recitation, visualization, reality-principle) in fact differentiated themselves. The paper makes no historical judgment here; it only provides the coordinate system that NIAN Parts III through VIII will use.

The frame P03 §3.1 introduced—"one engine, six objects"—is here given its first active application: the same nine-step psychological dynamic chain (engine), meeting different object types (single-faceted versus multi-faceted), yields different termini (*appanā* versus *upacāra*). The frame has proved applicable to structural comparison; NIAN Parts V (the great Mahāyāna sūtras) and VI (the traditional streams) will invoke it repeatedly.

The next paper, P05, takes up the first wave of Chinese *niànfó* reception: the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* T0784. This 2nd-century text does not introduce *Visuddhimagga*'s *upacāra*/*appanā* terminology, but its practical instructions disclose presuppositions about meditative depth. P05 will read those presuppositions against this chapter's technical coordinate system, to see at which layer the earliest Chinese *niànfó* practitioners were actually receiving *buddhānussati*.

*Namo tassa bhagavato arahato sammāsambuddhassa.*
