# The Nine Epithets

**The *Itipi so* Formula and What the Buddha Left for Mindfulness of Himself**

## Abstract
Chinese Buddhist tradition has for two millennia recited a list of ten titles for the Buddha—*Rúlái, Yìnggòng, Zhèngbiànzhī, Míngxíngzú, Shànshì, Shìjiānjiě, Wúshàngshì, Diàoyùzhàngfū, Tiānrénshī, Fó-Shìzūn* (Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Rightly and Fully Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Tamer of Persons, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha-World-Honored-One)—usually called the "Ten Names." Its Pāli prototype, however, consists of nine terms in a single standardized formula: *itipi so bhagavā arahaṃ sammāsambuddho vijjācaraṇasampanno sugato lokavidū anuttaro purisadammasārathi satthā devamanussānaṃ buddho bhagavā*. This paper argues that *itipi so* is not a hymn of praise, not an honorific list, but the only standardized attentional template the Buddha himself gave for *buddhānussati* (recollection of the Buddha)—nine terms constituting a topological map covering the complete quality of the awakened one, each term functioning as an independent doorway, the nine doorways together composing the full meditation object. The paper opens the nine one by one. It also addresses a cross-linguistic structural seam: Pāli has nine; Sanskrit and Chinese expand to ten by adding *tathāgato* / 如來 at the head. This expansion is not a scribal error but a grammatical transposition—from "predicates of the Blessed One" to "identities of the Tathāgata"—and this shift in grammatical structure provides the linguistic foundation on which later *dharmakāya* thought becomes grammatically expressible. Working through AN 11.11–12 and SN 11.3 in Pāli, the Chinese *Saṃyukta Āgama* 930–933 and *Ekottara Āgama* "Three Jewels" chapter, with commentary from Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* VII and Nāgārjuna's (or the tradition's) *Dà zhìdù lùn* scrolls 21–24, the paper exhibits *buddhānussati* as a portable technique for recollection of the Buddha—operable on the battlefield, in a terrifying forest, in a home crowded with children—and excavates its complete internal structure.

**Keywords:** *itipi so*, nine epithets, ten names, Tathāgata, *buddhānussati*, mindfulness of the Buddha, *Visuddhimagga*, *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra*, predicate, *dharmakāya*

## I. Introduction

In contemporary English Buddhist literature, *buddhānussati* is typically translated as "recollection of the Buddha," sometimes elaborated as "mindful remembrance of the Buddha's qualities." Bhikkhu Bodhi renders the nine-term Pāli formula as a continuous stream of honorifics: "The Blessed One is an arahant, perfectly enlightened, accomplished in true knowledge and conduct, fortunate, knower of the world…" Sujato and Ṭhānissaro's translations vary in wording but share the same strategy—reading the Pāli's nine distinct terms as a single stream of praise directed at a single Blessed One.

This is a reasonable translation strategy. English prose favors cumulative adjectival flow; a series of noun-phrases following "The Blessed One is" reads as a dignified liturgy.

The cost is that **nine doorways are rendered as a single decorative wall.**

The Pāli does not operate this way. The Buddha's answer to Mahānāma—a layman, leader of the Śākyan clan, spokesman for five hundred lay followers—is not "watch your breath," not "practice the four establishments of mindfulness," but nine very specific terms:

> *Itipi so bhagavā arahaṃ sammāsambuddho vijjācaraṇasampanno sugato lokavidū anuttaro purisadammasārathi satthā devamanussānaṃ buddho bhagavā.*

Each term points to an independent dimension of the Buddha's awakened quality. The nine together do not form a linear enumeration but a **topological map covering the complete character of the awakened one.** The practitioner does not recite the nine in a single breath and move on; rather, the mind rests on each term in turn, unfolds it, saturates in it, then moves to the next. Buddhaghosa in the *Visuddhimagga* chapter VII, section 1, describes this operation as *anuvitakka* and *anuvicāra*—repeatedly investigating and repeatedly examining each epithet so that it becomes, in his formulation, an independent entrance.

This fulfills the promise left at the end of NIAN-P01. The previous paper argued that *anussati* is attentional work with content, not content-less attention training. This paper answers the natural follow-up: what does the object the Buddha himself prescribed—*itipi so*—actually look like? How is it internally constructed? What space does each of its nine terms open?

Beyond this, the paper addresses a harder question: **nine or ten?** The Pāli standardized formula has nine. The Chinese *Saṃyukta Āgama* scroll 33, sutra 931—the parallel to Mahānāma's question about the six recollections—uses ten names: 如來、應、等正覺、明行足、善逝、世間解、無上士、調御丈夫、天人師、佛世尊 (Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Rightly and Fully Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Tamer of Persons, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha-World-Honored-One). The Sanskrit prototype is similarly ten, adding *tathāgato* at the head. This expansion is not the Chinese translator's error—Guṇabhadra (5th century CE), working in a rigorous translation bureau, rendered faithfully from the Sanskrit manuscript he had. The question therefore cuts deeper: **why did the northern Sanskrit tradition insert *tathāgato* before the nine?**

This paper argues that the expansion is not mere accumulation but a structural grammatical transposition. The Pāli formula *iti pi so bhagavā arahaṃ…* takes *so bhagavā* ("that Blessed One") as grammatical subject, and the nine terms that follow are **predicates**—descriptions of how that Blessed One is the Blessed One. The Sanskrit-Chinese formula elevates *tathāgato* to the head, dissolves the subject, and renders the ten names as **independent parallel identities**—he is the Tathāgata, he is the Worthy One, he is the Rightly and Fully Enlightened, and so on. "Predicates of the Blessed One" constitute a meditation object; "identities of the Tathāgata" constitute an ontological category. The seam between nine and ten is the transit zone from **practice formula** to **doctrinal category**. The grammatical space within which later Mahāyāna concepts like *tathāgatagarbha* and the *dharmakāya* of the Tathāgata become formulable opens precisely in this seam.

This is not an easy observation to see. Precisely because it is not easy to see, Chinese Buddhist tradition has for two millennia accepted the "Ten Names" as the standard formula for *buddhānussati* without registering its structural distance from the Pāli nine. Even scholarly histories of Buddhism rarely treat this seam as a topic in its own right. This paper attempts to open it once, so that readers who today chant *Námó běnshī Shìjiāmóuní Rúlái, Yìnggòng, Zhèngbiànzhī*… can see that they are simultaneously inheriting two tracks—the Pāli predicate-mode *buddhānussati* and the Sanskrit-Chinese identity-mode *buddhānussati*—and that these two tracks are not quite the same in structural mechanism.

The paper does not claim the Chinese ten are wrong, nor that the Pāli nine are more original and therefore correct. It claims only that they are two tracks, each with its own structural achievement and each with its own structural cost. Conflating them is the first level of misreading the standardized formula the Buddha himself left for recollection of himself.

## II. Textual Basis

### 2.1 *Sūtra* basis (dual-track citation)

The sūtra basis for this paper falls across three groups, each listed in both Pāli and Chinese parallels, so that the reader can see the same practice leaving different traces in the two textual traditions.

**Group 1: The standardized formula in canonical locus**

- *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 11.11, *Paṭhama-Mahānāma Sutta* (the first Mahānāma sutta)[^1]
- *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 11.12, *Dutiya-Mahānāma Sutta* (the second Mahānāma sutta)[^2]
- *Saṃyutta Nikāya* 11.3, *Dhajagga Sutta* (the banner-crest sutta)[^3]
- Chinese parallels: *Saṃyukta Āgama* scroll 33, sutra 931 (Mahānāma on the six recollections)[^4]; *Ekottara Āgama* scroll 2, Three-Jewels chapter, the six-recollections passage[^5]

This group establishes the standardized formula. AN 11.11 is the most complete: it opens with five grounding qualities (faith, energy, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom), moves to the six recollections, and unfolds the psychological dynamics of each one in turn; the nine-epithet formula appears as the full object of the first recollection, *buddhānussati*. AN 11.12, spoken after Mahānāma's recovery from illness, repeats the same six-recollection structure and adds a critical clause: "Mahānāma, you should develop this recollection of the Buddha while walking, while standing, while sitting, while lying down, while engaged in work, and while at home in a house crowded with children."[^6] This is the clearest canonical testimony that *buddhānussati* is a **portable lay technique**—not reserved for monastic halls, not dependent on retreat conditions, not requiring special circumstances to be operable.

SN 11.3, the *Dhajagga Sutta*, supplies a different use-case: terror management. The Buddha tells the monks: "If, while dwelling in a forest, at the root of a tree, or in an empty hut, fear, trembling, or horripilation arises in you, then recollect me: *itipi so bhagavā arahaṃ sammāsambuddho*…—and your fear, trembling, and horripilation will subside."[^7] He then explains why Indra's banner cannot reliably serve the same function: Indra himself is not free of greed, hate, and delusion; he too fears, flees, and panics. The Buddha can be recollected with effect because he **is** free of greed, hate, and delusion—fearless, steady, ungiven to flight. This passage supplies the structural ground for why *buddhānussati* works: the quality of the object is what gets borrowed over and stabilizes the practitioner's mind.

**Group 2: The expanded Chinese formula**

*Saṃyukta Āgama* 931 records the Buddha's answer to Mahānāma concerning how a practitioner on the path can develop the six recollections to swiftly reach nibbāna. The Buddha's answer includes:

> "The noble disciple recollects the Thus-Gone One as follows: 'The Thus-Gone One, the Worthy One, the Rightly and Fully Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Tamer of Persons, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, the Buddha-World-Honored-One.'"[^8]

Ten names. Their correspondence with the Pāli nine is as follows:

| Pāli nine epithets | Chinese ten names |
|---|---|
| — | 如來 *rúlái* / Tathāgata (**new**) |
| *arahaṃ* | 應 *yìng* / the Worthy One (i.e., Arhat) |
| *sammāsambuddho* | 等正覺 *děngzhèngjué* / the Rightly and Fully Enlightened |
| *vijjācaraṇasampanno* | 明行足 *míngxíngzú* / Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct |
| *sugato* | 善逝 *shànshì* / the Well-Gone |
| *lokavidū* | 世間解 *shìjiānjiě* / the Knower of the World |
| *anuttaro purisadammasārathi* | 無上士、調御丈夫 / the Unsurpassed One **+** the Tamer of Persons (**split**) |
| *satthā devamanussānaṃ* | 天人師 *tiānrénshī* / the Teacher of Gods and Humans |
| *buddho bhagavā* | 佛世尊 *fóshìzūn* / Buddha-World-Honored-One (**combined**) |

Two editorial operations, each with structural implications. *Anuttaro* and *purisadammasārathi* form a single compound in Pāli ("the unsurpassed tamer of persons"); the Chinese splits this into two independent titles ("the Unsurpassed One" + "the Tamer of Persons"), expanding the object's attentional surface. *Buddho* and *bhagavā* are two independent terms in Pāli; the Chinese combines them into a single compound title ("Buddha-World-Honored-One"), contracting that surface. Expansion-by-one plus contraction-by-one yields a net of ten. The editorial logic is developed in §3.3.

The parallel sutta includes two further contextualizations: *Saṃyukta Āgama* 930 gives Mahānāma's daily concern that he might die suddenly amid "mad elephants, mad people, mad carriages"[^9] without being able to recollect Buddha, Dhamma, or Saṅgha; sutras 932 and 933 add five foundational qualities to the six recollections, stacking to eleven or twelve recollections respectively[^10]. The *Ekottara Āgama* Three-Jewels chapter gives the full six-recollection set in a somewhat different format, but the *buddhānussati* component uses the same ten-name structure[^11].

**Group 3: Technical description of the psychological dynamics**

Each recollection section in AN 11.11 includes the same psychological-dynamic formula, repeated verbatim:

> "When, Mahānāma, a noble disciple thus recollects the Thus-Gone One, on that occasion his mind is not overwhelmed by lust, not overwhelmed by hatred, not overwhelmed by delusion; his mind is straight at that time, taking the Thus-Gone One as its object. A noble disciple whose mind is straight obtains a taste of meaning, obtains a taste of the *Dhamma*, obtains gladness connected with the *Dhamma*; from gladness joy is born; with a joyful mind the body becomes tranquil; the tranquil-bodied feel ease; the mind of the one at ease becomes concentrated."[^12]

This is the clearest technical description of the psychological dynamics of *buddhānussati*—a nine-step chain running from recitation through three-poison displacement through straightening of mind through meaning-taste and Dhamma-taste through gladness, joy, tranquility, ease, and concentration. NIAN-P01 §3.4 named this chain the "eight-step sequence" (skipping the initial recitation step); this paper reintroduces the recitation step, yielding a complete nine-step chain. §3.4 below examines how the nine epithets as meditation object drive this particular chain.

### 2.2 Commentarial basis

The paper draws on three commentarial traditions in supporting roles:

**The *Visuddhimagga* chapter VII, section 1, *Buddhānussati-niddesa*** ("Exposition of the Recollection of the Buddha")[^13]. Buddhaghosa (5th century CE, Sri Lanka) treats the nine epithets as one of forty *kammaṭṭhāna* (meditation objects) and unfolds each epithet in turn. This paper does not reproduce his exuberant philological play—for *arahaṃ* he offers five distinct etymologies (*āraka* "far-removed," *arīnaṃ hata* "having destroyed enemies," *arānaṃ hata* "having destroyed the spokes [of saṃsāra]," *paccayādi arahattā* "being worthy of requisites," *pāpa-raho-abhāvā* "absence of secret-evil-doing"), and the other epithets receive similar multi-layered word-play. Two technical conclusions from his treatment anchor the argument here: first, each epithet is an independent doorway, and the meditator should dwell on each through repeated *anuvitakka* and *anuvicāra* until it yields its quality, before moving to the next; second, *buddhānussati* attains only access concentration (*upacāra-samādhi*), never absorption (*appanā-samādhi*), because the multi-layered nature of the object resists the unification (*ekaggatā*) required for full absorption[^14]. This conclusion seeds a theme developed in NIAN-P04.

**The *Dà zhìdù lùn* (*Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra*) scrolls 21–22**. This treatise (attributed to Nāgārjuna, though scholarly debate has since Lamotte questioned this attribution) expands the Pāli six recollections into eight—adding *ānāpāna-sati* (mindfulness of breathing) and *maraṇa-sati* (mindfulness of death)—reflecting the editorial pressure of early Mahāyāna on the Āgama-era framework[^15]. The *buddhānussati* section retains the ten-name formula as its core object, consistent with 5th-century Chinese translation conventions.

**The *Dà zhìdù lùn* scroll 24**. This scroll treats the Bodhisattva's "Buddha-recollection samādhi" (*niànfó sānmèi*), advancing beyond recollection of the Buddha's physical body toward recollection of his "*dharma*-body of merits" (*gōngdé fǎshēn*). It unfolds an expanded list of over twenty meritorious designations—"All-Knowing One," "All-Seeing One," "Unobstructed All-Knowing-and-Seeing One," "Great Compassionate One," "World-Saver," "Thus-Gone One," "Worthy One"… and so on—suggesting a strong expansionary impulse in early Chinese Mahāyāna: nine epithets → ten names → over twenty designations[^16]. This trajectory is revisited in full in NIAN Part VI's treatment of the Pure Land masters; this paper only marks the origin point.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 *Itipi so* is not a list but an entry-map

The nine epithets are not a flat enumeration of nine tags, still less a stack of nine honorific titles. The latter reading mistakes a practice formula for a hymn of praise. If we rearrange the nine according to their attentional function—following Buddhaghosa's organizational logic in *Visuddhimagga* VII.1—a three-layered topological structure emerges.

**First layer: the achieved fruit.** *Arahaṃ*, *sammāsambuddho*, *vijjācaraṇasampanno* — these three answer the question "what has the Buddha achieved?" From the complete severance of defilements (Worthy One) to the full awakening to all *dhammas* (Rightly and Fully Enlightened) to the perfection of knowledge and conduct together (Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct), these three describe the Buddha as a practitioner who has arrived at the end of the path.

**Second layer: the directionality of awakening.** *Sugato*, *lokavidū*, *anuttaro purisadammasārathi* — these three answer the question "how does the Buddha move, see, and lead?" From going-well-without-regression (Well-Gone) to complete penetration of the world's threefold constitution (Knower of the World) to the unsurpassed skill in taming beings (Unsurpassed Tamer of Persons), these three describe the Buddha as a dynamic presence.

**Third layer: the sociality of awakening.** *Satthā devamanussānaṃ*, *buddho*, *bhagavā* — these three answer the question "what is the Buddha in the world?" From the teacher of gods and humans (Teacher of Gods and Humans) to the essential name of an awakened being (Buddha) to the dignified designation of one revered by all (World-Honored), these three describe the Buddha as a relational presence.

This three-layered structure appears in the sūtra text only implicitly, but it surfaces clearly in Buddhaghosa's ordered exposition and in the scroll-24 list of the *Dà zhìdù lùn*. A meditator moving through the nine epithets in sequence is in effect unfolding these three layers—contemplating first the fruit, then the movement, then the relational function; moving from the static exhaustion of defilements through the dynamic directions of awakening to the relational connections with the world.

This structure also explains why Buddhaghosa concludes that *buddhānussati* yields only access concentration, not absorption. Absorption (*appanā-samādhi*) requires single-pointedness (*ekaggatā*)—the mind unmoving at a single point. The meditation object of nine epithets is inherently multi-layered. The mind moves from one epithet to the next in a continuous motion of *anuvitakka* and *anuvicāra*; so long as this motion continues, the mind cannot fully unify to a single point. The structural achievement of the nine epithets—a comprehensive map of awakened quality—is simultaneously their structural boundary.

### 3.2 The Nine Epithets Opened One by One

What follows opens the nine one at a time. The format for each is uniform: etymology, semantics, canonical attestation, and exegetical core. The paper does not reproduce Buddhaghosa's extensive word-play (for *arahaṃ* he offers five separate etymologies), instead taking in each case only the one or two senses that carry the epithet's function as **an independent doorway into recollection of the Buddha.**

**1. *arahaṃ* / Worthy One / 應供**

Etymology: Pāli *arahaṃ* (masculine nominative singular *arahā*) derives from the verb *arhati* ("to be worthy of," "to deserve"), with the agent noun *arahant* ("one who is worthy"). The Sanskrit cognate is *arhat*, transliterated into Chinese as *āluóhàn* 阿羅漢. The principal Chinese translation is *yìnggòng* 應供 — "one who should be offered to," that is, worthy of offerings.

Semantics: In the Pāli canon, this term designates two classes of persons: fully liberated disciples (the great arhats among the Buddha's followers), and the Buddha himself. Used as an epithet of the Buddha, three senses layer: (a) he is remote from all defilement-enemies (*kilesa-ari*), (b) he is worthy of supreme offerings from both humans and gods, and (c) there is no inner chamber of secrecy in him—under no circumstances does he commit evil that would need concealing, so the "secret-evil-doing" sense of *raha* is absent in him.

Canonical attestation: SN 11.3 (*Dhajagga Sutta*), immediately after the nine-epithet formula, explicates why the Buddha's recollection dispels fear: "the Thus-Gone One is the Worthy One, Rightly and Fully Enlightened; he is without lust, without hatred, without delusion; he is fearless, unshaken, unterrified, not given to flight."[^7] "Worthy One" here is not a polite honorific; it is a definitional statement of the Buddha's complete severance from the three poisons.

Exegetical core: When the meditator dwells on *arahaṃ*, what is contemplated is not a sentimental judgment of the form "the Buddha is admirable and deserves my veneration" but a structural comparison: "he has severed; I have not. He is free; I am not." This comparison is itself the doorway—opening it, the practitioner simultaneously sees his or her own state of defilement and sees an already-completed reference point. The comparison is neither oppressive nor judgmental; it is simply accurate positioning.

**2. *sammāsambuddho* / Rightly and Fully Enlightened / 正遍知・正等覺**

Etymology: *sammā* ("rightly," "correctly") + *sam-* ("together," "fully," "wholly") + *buddho* ("awakened"). Sanskrit *samyak-saṃbuddha*. Early Chinese translators (Kumārajīva, Guṇabhadra) rendered it *zhèngbiànzhī* 正遍知; Xuanzang standardized it as *zhèngděngjué* 正等覺.

Semantics: Awakened without a teacher (*sam-*) and awakened to all *dhammas* with completeness and correctness (*sammā-*). The distinction from *pacceka-buddha* (the solitary or "silent" buddha) is that a *sammāsambuddha* not only awakens alone but establishes a teaching, a *dhamma*, and a community that can guide others to the same awakening.

Canonical attestation: AN 11.11, third in the formula, after *arahaṃ*. Buddhaghosa in *Vism* VII.1 unfolds "awakened to all *dhammas*" through the structure of the Four Noble Truths: all *dhammas* are known either as to be fully understood (suffering), to be abandoned (origin), to be realized (cessation), or to be developed (path). "Awakened to all *dhammas*" therefore does not mean omniscience in a theistic sense; it means complete penetration of the soteriological structure of the Four Truths.

Exegetical core: When the meditator dwells on *sammāsambuddho*, the object is not "the Buddha knows everything" (a theological fantasy of infinity) but "the Buddha has completely penetrated the structure of suffering, origin, cessation, and path." This distinction matters functionally. The first reading leads the meditator into abstract wonder at unbounded divine knowledge; the second anchors each recitation to the recoverable structure of the teaching—each time *sammāsambuddho* is recalled, the meditator reaffirms that the *dhamma*'s structure is still accessible to return to.

**3. *vijjācaraṇasampanno* / Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct / 明行足**

Etymology: *vijjā* ("knowledge," "clear vision") + *caraṇa* ("conduct," "practice") + *sampanno* ("endowed with," "complete in"). The Chinese *míngxíngzú* 明行足 renders all three with pristine economy: brightness (*míng* = *vijjā*), conduct (*xíng* = *caraṇa*), completeness (*zú* = *sampanno*).

Semantics: *Vijjā* refers either to the three knowledges (recollection of past lives, the divine eye, and the knowledge of the destruction of the taints) or, in the broader treatment of *Vism* VII.1, to eight knowledges (adding psychic powers and direct insight). *Caraṇa* refers to the fifteen conduct-qualities: restraint through virtue, guarding the sense faculties, moderation in food, devotion to wakefulness, seven good qualities, and the four form-realm *jhānas*. *Sampanno* means both are complete in the Buddha. This epithet is a comprehensive statement about the Buddha: he combines the deepest wisdom with the most stable conduct, neither ever outpacing the other.

Canonical attestation: AN 11.11 in the standard formula. Buddhaghosa emphasizes a key correlate: the *vijjā* dimension grounds the Buddha's *sabbaññutā* (all-knowing); the *caraṇa* dimension grounds his *mahākaruṇā* (great compassion). "Knowledge-and-conduct" is therefore one of the earliest commentarial loci for the doctrine of wisdom-and-compassion jointly operating (*prajñā-karuṇā-yuganaddha*).

Exegetical core: This epithet opens the doorway most directly pointed at the meditator herself. When dwelling on *vijjācaraṇasampanno*, the object is not the abstract assertion "the Buddha has wisdom and virtue" but the structural principle "knowledge and conduct must both be complete." Each recitation reaffirms this principle in the meditator's own attention — wisdom without conduct is flighty, conduct without wisdom is blind.

**4. *sugato* / Well-Gone / 善逝**

Etymology: *su* ("good," "well") + *gato* ("gone," past participle of *√gam* "to go"). The Chinese *shànshì* 善逝 translates by sense.

Semantics: Buddhaghosa offers four senses: (a) gone-well—the Buddha has gone by the noble path without regression; (b) gone-to-a-good-destination—the Buddha has gone to nibbāna, without further rebirth; (c) gone-rightly (that is, in a right manner)—avoiding both the extreme of eternalism and that of annihilationism, traveling the middle way; (d) *sammā gadati* ("rightly speaks")—speaking only what is true, timely, and beneficial. The fourth sense is a hermeneutic creation of the *Vism*, extending *sugato* from a description of trajectory to one of speech quality.

Canonical attestation: AN 11.11 and all sūtras carrying the formula. In SN 11.3, this epithet is implicit in the contrast between Sakka (who flees) and the Buddha (who does not)—the Buddha is *sugato*, gone-well and gone-without-return, while Sakka is not.

Exegetical core: When dwelling on *sugato*, the meditator does not contemplate "a Buddha who has departed" but a presence who has completed the entire path—if he can walk it, I can walk it; if he has arrived, there is an arrival. This epithet performs a particular function in the psychology of *buddhānussati*: it is the **proof-of-possibility** element. Because he has gone well, the path exists as a real possibility; because he has arrived, the path has an end.

**5. *lokavidū* / Knower of the World / 世間解**

Etymology: *loka* ("world") + *vidū* ("knower," a derivative of *√vid* "to know"). The Chinese *shìjiānjiě* 世間解 corresponds directly.

Semantics: The Buddha has penetrated the three worlds: the world of sentient beings (*satta-loka*, the differentiations among living beings), the world of physical formations (*bhājana-loka*, the structure of the material cosmos), and the world of conditionings (*saṅkhāra-loka*, the operation of all dependently-arisen things). Buddhaghosa in *Vism* VII.1 uses this epithet as the occasion for a full exposition of 5th-century Buddhist cosmology—Mount Meru, the four continents, the levels of the heavens. What he means by "knower of the world," however, is not an accumulation of cosmographic data but a thorough understanding of the world **as a structure of dependent origination.**

Canonical attestation: AN 11.11. The *Dà zhìdù lùn* scroll 24 glosses it as "knower of the general characteristics and particular characteristics of the world"—general characteristics being what all phenomena share (impermanence, suffering, non-self), particular characteristics being what each distinct phenomenon has as its own mark.

Exegetical core: When dwelling on *lokavidū*, the meditation object is "the Buddha has seen through this world's architecture." This architecture is not some transcendental domain apart from the world we inhabit, but the actual structure of this very world. Through this epithet, the meditator redirects attention from "recollection-of-the-Buddha-as-escape-from-the-world" to "recollection-of-the-Buddha-while-in-the-world"—because the Buddha is *lokavidū* precisely in that he did not leave the world to establish a separate Pure Land; he saw through the world while remaining in it. This point was seeded in NIAN-P01 §3.5, citing AN 6.10: *anussati* is designed for lay life, not for escape from it.

**6. *anuttaro purisadammasārathi* / Unsurpassed Tamer of Persons / 無上士調御丈夫**

Etymology: *anuttaro* ("unsurpassed") + *purisa-damma* ("person to be tamed") + *sārathi* ("charioteer," "driver"). *Anuttaro* modifies the whole compound that follows.

Semantics: The key structural observation is that in Pāli this is **a single compound term**: "the unsurpassed charioteer-of-persons-to-be-tamed." Chinese *Saṃyukta Āgama* 931 splits it into two independent titles: *wúshàngshì* 無上士 ("the Unsurpassed One") and *diàoyùzhàngfū* 調御丈夫 ("the Tamer of Persons"), opening one Pāli door into two. This split is not a mistranslation—it precisely captures the internal tension of the Pāli compound. *Anuttaro* contains both an absolute aspect ("there is none to compare with him") and a functional aspect ("he tames those no one else can tame"). Reading as one compound holds the tension together; reading as two isolates each aspect for independent attention. The Pāli reads it as one; the Chinese reads it as two; the attentional resolution increases with the split, but the total attentional surface remains the same.

Canonical attestation: AN 11.11. *Vism* VII.1 develops this epithet uniquely through concrete historical cases: the Buddha's taming of the nāga-king Apalāla, the serial killer Aṅgulimāla, the Jain debater Saccaka, the flesh-eating yakṣa Āḷavaka—this is the one epithet among the nine that Buddhaghosa unfolds primarily through narrative rather than abstract attribute.

Exegetical core: The doorway this epithet opens is **tameability.** The Buddha does not reject the difficult, does not avoid the violent, does not abandon the recalcitrant; he has developed the precise skill that can tame them. When the meditator dwells on this epithet, what is being contemplated is not an admiration of the Buddha's ability but an opening in the meditator's own mind to the possibility of being tamed. "He sees the difficult places in me, and he knows how to tame them"—this recognition itself is the epithet's attentional function.

**7. *satthā devamanussānaṃ* / Teacher of Gods and Humans / 天人師**

Etymology: *satthā* ("teacher," from *√sās* "to teach") + *deva-manussānaṃ* (genitive plural: "of gods and humans"). The Chinese *tiānrénshī* 天人師 corresponds directly.

Semantics: The Buddha is the teacher common to gods and to humans. In the Āgamas this epithet carries two implications: first, the Buddha's instruction is not restricted to humans—gods, yakṣas, nāgas, gandharvas, all can receive his teaching; second, even animals, as Buddhaghosa points out through the *Vism*'s narrative of a frog who died while absorbed in the Buddha's voice at the edge of the Gaggarā pond and was reborn as a deva, can through the power of *saddhā* cross the species boundary to benefit from the Buddha's instruction. This example is directly from the *Vism* VII.1 narrative stock.

Canonical attestation: AN 11.11 and all sūtras containing the formula. The *Dà zhìdù lùn* scroll 24 glosses this epithet as "one who liberates beings through the three kinds of teaching"—a threefold structure of instruction by body, by speech, and by mind.

Exegetical core: When dwelling on this epithet, the object is not the quantitative claim "the Buddha's reach as teacher is broad" but the relational position "teacher." The meditator recognizes, through this epithet: I am not alone in recollecting the Buddha, I am not acting blindly; I am in a teacher-student relation, and the Buddha is the teacher, I am the student. This recognition itself stabilizes consciousness—"I have a teacher" is a factual statement that dissolves directionlessness, not an emotional attachment.

**8. *buddho* / Buddha / 佛**

Etymology: Past participle of *√budh* ("to awaken," "to wake up"). *Buddho* = one who has awakened. Sanskrit preserves the identical form.

Semantics: The simplest and most central of the nine. In a sense, all seven preceding epithets are elaborations of this one—why is he called Buddha? Because he is the Worthy One, the Rightly and Fully Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed Tamer of Persons, the Teacher of Gods and Humans. The seven together define the specific content of *buddho*. But *buddho* still has its own place as an independent epithet: it is the **center** of the topological map, with the other eight arranged around it.

Canonical attestation: AN 11.11. *Vism* VII.1 observes that this epithet is the hardest to unfold precisely because it is the most fundamental; Buddhaghosa's exposition does not dissect *buddho* but rather ties it directly to omniscience regarding the Four Truths: because he himself awoke to the truths and further causes others to awaken to them, he is Buddha.

Exegetical core: When dwelling on *buddho*, the meditator contacts the anchor of the entire structure. The seven preceding epithets are entry-doorways; *buddho* is the room they all open into. When a meditator finds nine epithets too many, the tradition permits reduction to this one term alone—"*buddho*, *buddho*, *buddho*…"—as in the modern Thai and Burmese southern traditions, where it develops into a mantra-like practice called *buddho bhāvanā*. The canonical prototype, however, is not a mantra but a concentration of all seven preceding epithets into a single recollective point.

**9. *bhagavā* / Blessed One / World-Honored / 世尊**

Etymology: *Bhagavant* (masculine nominative singular *bhagavā*) derives from *bhaga* ("fortune," "portion," "merit," "destiny"). Sanskrit *bhagavat*. Two Chinese renderings coexist: *shìzūn* 世尊 ("World-Honored-One") by sense, and *bójiāfàn* 薄伽梵 by transliteration.

Semantics: Buddhaghosa offers six distinct etymologies: *bhāgyavā* ("possessor of fortune/merit"), *bhaggavā* ("destroyer," destroyer of the three poisons), *yogī ca vibhattavā* ("analyzer," one who has discriminated the dharmas), *bhattavā* ("practitioner," one who has cultivated), *bhagehi yutto* ("endowed with the six bhagas," i.e. sovereignty, *dhamma*, fame, form, desire, effort), and *bhavesu vantagamano* ("one who has cast off existence," expeller of the three realms of becoming). The six layer: he is comprehensively one who possesses all that is venerable.

Canonical attestation: AN 11.11 and all sūtras containing the formula. Throughout the entire Pāli canon, *bhagavā* is the most frequently used honorific for the Buddha, far exceeding *buddho*.

Exegetical core: This epithet performs a **closure** function. The preceding eight epithets each open a doorway; *bhagavā* closes all eight, returning the mind to a single contact point: "a complete, quality-endowed being." This is why the Pāli formula positions *bhagavā* at both the beginning and the end: *iti pi so **bhagavā** arahaṃ … buddho **bhagavā** 'ti*—the first and last *bhagavā* form a closing ring within which the seven epithets are held. This ring structure is the Pāli formula's simultaneous operation of rhythm and meaning: the musical bracketing reinforces the semantic function of closure.

### 3.3 Nine or Ten: A Grammatical Transposition from Predicate to Identity

With the nine complete, we can now return to the question raised in the introduction: why did the Sanskrit and Chinese traditions prefix *tathāgato* / 如來 to the nine?

This is not simple expansion and not a copyist's error. It is a **grammatical transposition** with far-reaching structural consequences.

Compare the grammatical subject in the two formulas.

**The Pāli formula** (nine epithets):

> *Iti pi so bhagavā arahaṃ sammāsambuddho…*
> "Thus indeed [is] that Blessed One: Worthy One, Rightly and Fully Enlightened…"

The subject is *so bhagavā*—"that Blessed One." The nine terms that follow are **predicates** describing how that Blessed One is the Blessed One. The grammatical structure is: subject + nine-fold predication.

**The Sanskrit-Chinese formula** (ten names):

> 如來、應、正等覺、明行足、善逝、世間解、無上士、調御丈夫、天人師、佛世尊。
> Tathāgata, Worthy One, Rightly and Fully Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha-World-Honored-One.

The subject is gone. The ten names stand in **parallel**, as **independent identities**—he is the Tathāgata; he is the Worthy One; he is the Rightly and Fully Enlightened; and so on. Each name is its own category.

The difference appears small—aren't both expressions describing the same Buddha?—but at the deeper grammatical stratum they open two entirely different spaces.

**The predicate structure** keeps "Blessed One" as grammatical subject and treats the nine epithets as attributes of this subject. This means the nine are **dependent**—they attach to a concrete, historically-existing "that Blessed One" (*so bhagavā*). The nine epithets have meaning only as descriptions of how *he* is *bhagavā*; detached from that concrete *bhagavā*, they have nothing to attach to.

**The identity structure** parallelizes ten names without a shared subject to hold them together. This means each name is **independent**—*Tathāgata* is no longer "how he is the Tathāgata" but *Tathāgata* as an independent category, an independent type, an independent identity-layer.

The consequence of this grammatical transposition: *Tathāgata* shifts **from predicate to subject.**

Once *Tathāgata* is a subject, it can carry new predicates. One can say "the Tathāgata **has** three bodies," "the Tathāgata **has** a *dharma*-body," "the Tathāgata **pervades** all places." None of these formulations arises naturally in Pāli grammar, because in Pāli *tathāgato* is an attribute of *so bhagavā*, not an independent entity. In Sanskrit-Chinese grammar, however, *Tathāgata* as independent subject can carry any number of new predicates. The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s *tathāgatagarbha*, the *Awakening of Faith*'s *Tathāgata-dharmakāya*, the Huayan school's "Vairocana Tathāgata's *dharmadhātu* body"—this entire lineage of core Mahāyāna concepts becomes, at the grammatical level, possible.

Extreme care is required here. This paper is not claiming that *tathāgatagarbha* and the *dharmakāya* are products of grammatical error. Nor is it claiming that the Chinese tradition at some historical moment "misread" the Pāli. The paper only points out a structural fact: **grammatical transposition precedes doctrinal elaboration.** The Sanskrit-Chinese tradition chose to elevate *tathāgato* to the head of the formula; this choice opened a grammatical space at the linguistic level, within which subsequent Mahāyāna developed rich theological accounts of the *dharma*-body.

Why did the Sanskrit-Chinese tradition make this choice? The most plausible reconstruction: the Mahāyāna phase of Buddhist thought began to need a category that **exceeded the historical Buddha.** The historical Buddha is "that Blessed One" (*so bhagavā*); but Mahāyāna needed a vocabulary to speak of the common nature of all Buddhas across the three times and ten directions—a common nature that could not be housed within the Pāli's "that Blessed One" subject-frame. So *Tathāgata* was elevated to subject position, becoming a grammatical seat capable of bearing "the common Buddha-nature."

**A reading-as-finger-pointing-at-the-moon.** The Pāli nine epithets are a **practice formula**—they configure the meditator's attentional map when doing *anussati* toward "that Blessed One" (a concrete, already-parinibbāned historical Buddha, two and a half millennia distant from now). The Sanskrit-Chinese ten names are a **doctrinal formula**—they configure the subject-position for Mahāyāna's ontological elaboration of *Tathāgata* as a *dharmakāya* category that exceeds any specific historical Buddha and spans the three times and ten directions.

Both are valid. Both carry costs.

The Pāli formula's achievement: *buddhānussati* as a concrete, operable practice technique, with a specific, historical, retraceable object. Its cost: difficulty in bearing Mahāyāna-core claims such as "all Buddhas share a single *dharmakāya*."

The Sanskrit-Chinese formula's achievement: a grammatical space for *dharmakāya* thought, for *tathāgatagarbha*, for Pure Land Buddhas, that allows Mahāyāna cosmology to unfold in language. Its cost: *buddhānussati* as attention to "that concrete historical Buddha" becomes diluted—when the object expands from "that Blessed One" to "all Tathāgatas," the precision of attention must decline.

**The two-thousand-year practical history of Chinese *niànfó* (recollection of the Buddha), in fact, operates in the pendulum swing between these two structural achievements and costs.** From early image-contemplation (contemplating specific Buddha-forms) to the *pratyutpanna-samādhi* (meeting the Buddha face-to-face) to Tiantai's four *samādhis* to Chan's "who is reciting the Buddha's name?" to Pure Land's nominalist recitation (reducing to four characters), each shift repositions the question: *who exactly* is the object of recollection? That Blessed One of history? All Tathāgatas of the three times and ten directions? The *dharma*-body at this mind, at this moment? These trajectories belong to NIAN Part VI; this paper does not develop them.

What this paper establishes at this point is the starting-point: nine-or-ten is not a trivial translational variance but the most fundamental grammatical-structural choice in the entire Chinese *niànfó* system. The choice was completed at the translation bureaus of the 5th century CE. When we today chant *Námó běnshī Shìjiāmóuní Rúlái, Yìnggòng, Zhèngbiànzhī*…, at the grammatical level we are chanting the ten-name formula, and our *buddhānussati* object has therefore been preset, prior to any conscious choice, as **the identity-mode Tathāgata** rather than **the predicate-mode Blessed One.** This difference has shaped our entire tradition's unconscious presuppositions about the object "Buddha."

Seeing this difference is not to return to the Pāli, to discard the Chinese—that would be neither necessary nor possible. Seeing this difference is only to let the meditator know, while reciting, what exactly she is doing: am I reciting him as a specific Blessed One, or as a transcendent Tathāgata? I can recite either; but I should know which I am reciting.

### 3.4 The Psychological Dynamics of *Itipi so*

Return now to AN 11.11's formulation. The Buddha's full description to Mahānāma, parsed stepwise, yields the following chain:

1. The noble disciple recollects the Thus-Gone One, reciting the nine epithets—**object established.**

2. "His mind is not overwhelmed by lust, not overwhelmed by hatred, not overwhelmed by delusion"—the object's quality (freedom from the three poisons) is borrowed over, covering the meditator's mind, and the three poisons recede temporarily.

3. "His mind is straight at that time" (*ujugatam cittaṃ*)—the mind no longer inclines or wavers but stands upright, oriented along the object's direction. The Pāli *ujugata* literally means "gone straight"; the image is of a bent mind being pulled taut by the stabilizing effect of the object.

4. "He obtains a taste of meaning" (*attha-veda*), "he obtains a taste of the *dhamma*" (*dhamma-veda*)—the former is the taste of doctrinal meaning being contacted; the latter is the taste of the *dhamma* qua *dhamma*. These two tastes differ from ordinary pleasure—they are the natural response of the stabilized mind to the recognition of what is correct.

5. "He obtains *dhamma*-connected gladness" (*dhammūpasaṃhitaṃ pāmojjaṃ*)—*pāmojja* is a lightly pleasant state, steadier than joy.

6. "The glad one, joy arises" (*pīti*)—*pīti* is a more energetic mental state than *pāmojja*. The texts describe it in forms ranging from "hair standing on end" to "tears streaming down the cheeks." This *pīti* is not worldly excitement but inner energetic arising after attention stabilizes.

7. "The joyful-minded one, the body becomes tranquil" (*passaddhi*)—after joy passes, the body enters a state of deep tranquility. This is not joy's decay; it is joy transitioning to the level of body.

8. "The tranquil-bodied one experiences ease" (*sukha*)—*sukha* is deeper than *pāmojja* and steadier than *pīti*, the settled comfort that follows tranquility.

9. "The eased one, the mind becomes concentrated" (*samādhi*)—the mind naturally unifies within ease, entering concentration.

The nine-link chain—recite → be-freed-from-enfoldment → straighten → obtain-meaning-and-dhamma-taste → gladden → rejoice → tranquilize → ease → concentrate—is the standard psychological-dynamic description the sūtras give for *anussati*-class practices. NIAN-P01 §3.4 named it the "eight-step sequence" (skipping the first, recitation); this paper restores the recitation step, yielding a complete nine-step chain.

Now look again at the nine epithets as object: why does this object drive precisely this chain?

**First, the quality of the object determines the quality of what is borrowed.** When the meditator dwells on *arahaṃ*, what is borrowed is "freedom from defilements." When on *sammāsambuddho*, "complete awakening." When on *sugato*, "going well without regression." The nine epithets supply nine distinct qualities available for borrowing; across a full cycle through the nine, the meditator's mind is covered by nine successive borrowings.

**Second, the object's multi-layered character prevents the mind from stiffening.** If the object were a single term (a single *buddho*, say), the mind would easily fall into mechanical repetition at a single point, and the dynamic chain would stall somewhere in the middle. The multi-layered nine keeps fresh space open for the mind to move through—each epithet is a new object, and each new object drives fresh *anuvitakka* (investigative thought) and *anuvicāra* (sustained examination).

**Third, the object's structural topology maintains directionality.** The nine are not randomly arranged; §3.1 showed the three-layered topology (fruit, directionality, sociality). The mind moves along this topology, not drifting but flowing through structure.

These three structural conditions are also the object's structural limits. The dynamic chain terminates at *samādhi*, but as Buddhaghosa makes clear, this *samādhi* only reaches access concentration (*upacāra-samādhi*), never absorption (*appanā-samādhi*). The reason has already been indicated: absorption requires single-pointedness (*ekaggatā*), the mind unmoving at a single point; the multi-layered character of the nine epithets structurally resists unification, because the mind must continually move among the nine and cannot stop at any one. This boundary is not a flaw; it is the structural cost of the nine epithets as **portable technique**—what the nine purchase in operable flexibility across all scenarios, they surrender in not reaching the deepest concentrative absorption. NIAN-P04 will treat the access/absorption distinction as its primary topic, and will articulate why Chinese *niànfó* tradition eventually moved to compressed four-character recitation ("*Námó Āmítuófó*")—precisely to cross the *upacāra/appanā* boundary that this paper here marks.

### 3.5 Three Use-Scenarios

The sūtras position *buddhānussati* in three distinct use-scenarios, each corresponding to a different set of practice conditions.

**Scenario one: terror in the forest (SN 11.3, *Dhajagga Sutta*).**

When practicing in a forest, at the root of a tree, or in an empty hut, if fear, trembling, or horripilation arises, recollect the Buddha. This is the wandering monk's scenario—the solitary practitioner in forest solitude facing wild animals, bandits, nocturnal visions, and inner fear. Why does the Buddha recommend *buddhānussati* here? Because terror paralyzes every other practice—you cannot observe the breath in terror, cannot establish the four foundations of mindfulness in terror, cannot observe dependent origination in terror. But you can recite the nine epithets. The recitation itself is a simple operation—nine words, nothing more—and it remains executable even in the mind's most unstable moments. After reciting, the borrowed quality (the Buddha's "freedom from greed, hatred, and delusion; fearlessness, steadiness, no-flight") enters the mind and stabilizes it.

The Buddha's explanation for this scenario is remarkably precise: why is Indra's banner inferior to the Buddha as an object of recollection? Because Indra himself still has greed, hatred, and delusion; he himself will also panic and flee. The quality that gets borrowed is determined by the object's own quality—an object that is itself fearful will transmit fear rather than calm. This **logic of quality-transfer** is the central mechanism of *buddhānussati*'s psychological dynamics.

**Scenario two: the domestic daily round (AN 11.12).**

When Mahānāma asks the Buddha a second time, recently recovered from illness, the Buddha adds a critical clause: "develop this recollection while walking, while standing, while sitting, while lying down, while engaged in work, and while at home in a house crowded with children" (*puttasambādhasayanaṃ ajjhāvasanto*—literally, "dwelling in the sleeping-place crowded-with-children"). The vividness of the Pāli phrase is partially flattened in Chinese translation, but the intent of the sūtra is unmistakable: it refers precisely to the domestic state of having children filling the house, parents sharing the sleeping-quarters with their offspring.

This scenario is the canonical proof that *buddhānussati* is **a lay technique.** It forecloses two common misreadings—that *buddhānussati* requires a meditation hall, that *buddhānussati* requires silence—by positioning the practice directly at the most interruption-rich moment of lay life. Why does the Buddha deliberately emphasize this scenario? Because Mahānāma is a layman, spokesman for five hundred lay followers; if the Buddha taught only practices suitable for monastics, his lay disciples would have nowhere to begin. The nine-epithet formula's design is precisely calibrated to remain operable in such high-interruption environments—it requires no meditation cushion, no silence, no extended time. Nine words, recitable at any moment.

**Scenario three: worldly danger (*Saṃyukta Āgama* 930).**

*Saṃyukta Āgama* scroll 33, sutra 930 records a third scenario. Mahānāma reports to the Buddha: Kapilavatthu each day is crowded; when he goes out, "mad elephants, mad people, mad carriages" (*kuángxiàng, kuángrén, kuángchéng*) are always around him—mad elephants trample, mad people strike, runaway carriages crush. He worries that if he dies suddenly in such an accident, he may not have time to recollect Buddha, *Dhamma*, and Saṅgha.

The Buddha's answer is a passage of great influence in Chinese Buddhist tradition: "Do not fear, do not be frightened. After your life ends, you will not fall into evil destinies. You have for long nights cultivated recollection of Buddha, *Dhamma*, and Saṅgha; if your life ends, this body—whether burned by fire, cast into a charnel ground, blown by winds, baked by the sun, eventually reduced to dust—still, the mental consciousness long saturated by right faith, by virtue, by giving, by learning, by wisdom, will rise and incline toward a peaceful place, a future birth in the heavens."

This passage is the direct prototype for the later Pure Land tradition's doctrine of "ten recitations at the hour of death"—the principle that long-sustained cultivation of *buddhānussati* establishes a mental orientation that does not dissolve at unexpected death. This paper does not develop this trajectory further; that belongs to NIAN Part VI (on the Pure Land masters) and to the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s chapter on Mahāsthāmaprāpta's recollection-of-Buddha practice (Part V). The present paper marks only the structural point: AN 11.12's "in a house crowded with children" and *Saṃyukta Āgama* 930's "amid mad elephants, mad people, mad carriages" are the same structural principle emerging in different textual variants—**recollection of the Buddha is designed for a chaotic daily life, not for the ideal conditions of retreat.**

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Re-Framing

Contemporary psychology has a family of techniques named "cognitive reframing"—consciously restructuring the linguistic interpretation of an event to modify emotional response. Its extensions include "self-compassion statements" in mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT), positive-psychology "affirmations," and various clinical self-talk protocols; all share a structure of stabilizing emotional state through linguistic repetition.

*Buddhānussati* shares with these techniques observable similarities at the outer layer: both use linguistic operations to work on consciousness; both exploit the binary structure of "is / is not subdued by negative emotion"; both traverse a chain from cognitive shift through bodily relaxation to felt improvement.

Structural parallel ends here. At the deeper layer, the two diverge.

**Self-talk's object is the self's own positive description**—"I am worthy," "I can face this challenge," "I deserve love." The object equals self. The mind is fixed on a positive image of self, and negative emotion is dissolved through this image's reinforcement.

**Buddhānussati's object is the complete quality-map of an other**—he has severed greed, hatred, delusion; he has rightly awakened; he has gone well… The object equals an already-accomplished, non-self consciousness. The mind is not fixed on self but inclines toward this other, borrowing the other's quality into itself.

The difference looks subtle but is in fact two different operational directions: the first is **self-consolidation**; the second is **self-suspension**. Self-talk strengthens the "I"; *buddhānussati* temporarily sets down the "I" and leans toward the Buddha. Later Mahāyāna developed, on this foundation, the further structural turn of "self and other non-dual" *buddhānussati*—to recollect the Buddha is to recollect the mind, and to recollect the mind is to recollect the Buddha—but that is a deeper transformation, outside this paper's scope.

**⚠️ The analogy has value but must not be pushed too far.** Outer-mechanism similarity does not equal content equivalence. Self-talk belongs to the toolkit of self-psychology; *anussati* belongs to the expedient means of the liberation path. The former aims at adaptation to the world; the latter aims at liberation-without-abandoning-the-world. Conflating them risks downgrading *buddhānussati* into "the Buddhist version of a positive affirmation practice," erasing its depth as a *dhammic* method.

In Western contemporary writing on *buddhānussati*, this downgrading has partially occurred. Once Kabat-Zinn's mindfulness was de-religionized into a generic attention-training technique, *buddhānussati* was naturally pulled into the same frame, appearing as "the Buddhist version of applied positive psychology." This paper here performs a structural separation: parallel at the outer layer, two distinct tracks at the inner.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This paper's argument traverses cross-linguistic and cross-traditional structural comparisons, so the risk of misreading is higher than for expositions within a single tradition. The following marks three misreadings this paper aims to foreclose, together with two cross-disciplinary analogies that must be kept bounded.

**❌ Misreading one: *itipi so* read as a hymn of praise.**

The Christian liturgical tradition's conception of a litany, and the Indian *bhakti* tradition's conception of *bhajana*, both make it easy to read a series of divine names as praise directed at a deity. *Itipi so* superficially resembles such a form. But §3.4's dynamic analysis shows that its operative mechanism is not praise at all—it is a quality-borrowing attentional technique, the nine terms being nine attentional anchors, not nine sentences of praise. To read it as a hymn downgrades a practice formula into emotional expression.

**❌ Misreading two: the nine epithets read as abstract honorifics.**

A common exegetical approach treats the nine as "nine appellations for the Buddha" and then invests extensive energy in tracing each appellation's historical evolution within Buddhist tradition. This historical-philological approach has its uses, but if adopted as the meditator's mode of understanding, it misplaces the functional level. What the meditator needs is not the historical philology of the nine but the functional operation of the nine **as doorways.** When dwelling on *arahaṃ*, what must be opened is the structural comparison "he has severed; I have not," not a recollection of the evolution of *arhat* concepts across the Sthaviravāda, Sarvāstivāda, and Mahāsāṃghika schools.

**❌ Misreading three: nine-to-ten read as Chinese mistranslation.**

Some modern Theravāda-inclined scholars treat the Chinese ten names as "inflation" or "deviation from the original," implicitly assuming "Pāli is more original and therefore more correct." §3.3 has shown that this is not error but a structurally meaningful grammatical transposition. Classifying it as error both misunderstands the structural dynamics of translation and overlooks the depth of doctrinal achievement the Sanskrit-Chinese tradition developed on this structural foundation. The reverse misreading—treating the Pāli nine as an "incomplete version"—is equally mistaken. They are two tracks, not a right-and-wrong pairing along a single track.

**⚠️ Analogy caution one: cross-religious comparison with *bhakti*.**

The *bhakti* movements of medieval India—deep devotional surrender to Kṛṣṇa, Rāma, Śiva—superficially closely resemble *niànfó*. But structural comparison reveals the key difference: *bhakti*'s core operation is **self-surrender** (merger into the divine); *anussati*'s core operation is **quality-transfer** (drawing the object's quality into one's own mind to stabilize one's own mind). The former is merger; the latter is leverage. The meditator doing *buddhānussati* is not dissolving into the Buddha but borrowing the Buddha's stability to maintain the continuity of her own mindfulness. The endpoints also differ—*bhakti* aims at permanent devotional union; *anussati* aims at the meditator's own *samādhi* and the insight (*vipassanā*) that follows.

**⚠️ Analogy caution two: cross-psychology comparison with CBT cognitive reframing.**

§IV has developed this analogy. Reiterated here: cognitive behavioral therapy's cognitive reframing and *buddhānussati* are similar at the outer level of "stabilizing emotional state through linguistic repetition," but the object-structure (self vs other), the goals (adaptation vs liberation), and the final development (mental health vs nibbāna) are all fundamentally different. Reading them as "both are linguistic techniques for emotional stabilization" is right at the outer layer and misplaced at the inner.

---

## VI. Conclusion

This paper opened with a reflection against Kabat-Zinn's *mindfulness* definition—"paying attention on purpose, in the present moment, non-judgmentally"—and showed that the attentional format the Buddha gave for recollection of himself is not this kind of single-layer instruction but a multi-layered attentional map constituted by nine terms (or Sanskrit-Chinese ten names).

The three-layered topology of this map: fruit (*arahaṃ* / *sammāsambuddho* / *vijjācaraṇasampanno*), directionality (*sugato* / *lokavidū* / *anuttaro purisadammasārathi*), sociality (*satthā devamanussānaṃ* / *buddho* / *bhagavā*)—covers the complete quality of the awakened one.

The nine-step psychological-dynamic chain of this map: recite → be-freed-from-enfoldment → straighten → obtain-meaning-and-dhamma-taste → gladden → rejoice → tranquilize → ease → concentrate—is driven by the multi-layered object of the nine and stops at access concentration rather than absorption. This boundary is the structural cost of the nine as portable technique, and it seeds the theme developed in NIAN-P04.

The three use-scenarios of this map: forest terror, domestic daily round, worldly danger—show that from its design *buddhānussati* is intended for chaotic ordinary life, not for the ideal conditions of retreat.

The paper also advances an original structural observation: between the Pāli nine epithets and the Sanskrit-Chinese ten names lies a **grammatical transposition**—from "predicates of the Blessed One" to "identities of the Tathāgata"—and this transposition precedes the doctrinal elaboration, the grammatical possibility of subsequent *dharmakāya* thought opening in this very transition. Identifying this seam is not to judge one track superior to the other, but to let the meditator know what layer of object she is recollecting: the specific historical Blessed One, or the Tathāgata who transcends the three times and ten directions.

The next paper, NIAN-P03, takes up what the Buddha did *not* teach in giving *buddhānussati*: he did not teach recollection of the Buddha alone. He taught a complete system of **six recollections** (*cha-anussati*)—recollection of the Buddha, the *Dhamma*, the Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, and the deities. Why six? Why these six? How does each of the remaining five operate? What is their relationship to recollection of the Buddha? Why does Mahānāma keep asking in the same sutta, and why does the Buddha keep answering? The next paper treats the internal logic of this system, and folds the "nine-epithet map" opened in this paper into a larger six-recollection ecology.

A distant foreshadowing left here: the access/absorption boundary repeatedly noted in this paper will be fully revisited in NIAN Part VI, on the Pure Land masters. There the reader will see how the Chinese *niànfó* tradition compressed the nine epithets (ten names) into four characters—*Námó Āmítuófó*—that is, compressed a multi-layered object into a single-pointed object—and by this compression crossed the *upacāra/appanā* boundary that *Vism* marks. This compression is one of the most important structural innovations of the Chinese *niànfó* tradition, but it also means that the structure of the object of *buddhānussati* has fundamentally changed. The "predicate-versus-identity" grammatical seam this paper has identified plays a critical role in that compression—precisely because the northern tradition had already completed the transposition from "predicated Blessed One" to "identity-mode Tathāgata," the further compression of the ten names into a single identity-name "Amitābha Buddha" became, grammatically, possible. This thread awaits Part VI.

*Namo tassa bhagavato arahato sammāsambuddhassa.*
