# What *Anussati* Actually Means

**The Semantic, Attentional, and Structural Separation from *Sati***


*中文主題：隨念的字義、作意結構、與 *sati* 的分軌*

## Abstract
Contemporary Western Buddhist discourse routinely treats *anussati* (recollection) as a specialized application of *sati* (mindfulness) — "mindfulness of the Buddha," "mindfulness of the Dhamma," and so on. This paper argues that this framing is a structural misreading. *Anussati* is not *sati* plus a religious object; it is a separate track of practice with its own attentional structure, its own cluster of mental factors, and its own soteriological endpoint. Drawing on MN 10, DN 22, AN 6.10, AN 6.25, and AN 11.11 alongside their Chinese-translation parallels in the *Saṃyukta-*, *Madhyama-*, and *Ekottara-āgamas*, and supplemented by Buddhaghosa's treatment of the six recollections in *Visuddhimagga* VII, this paper demonstrates *anussati* as the paradigmatic case of "directed *yoniso manasikāra*" — an attentional practice with a fourfold rhythm: establishing the object, adhering along it, arising of confidence and joy, and softening into workability. Recognizing this track as distinct from *sati* is the interpretive key for reading the thirty chapters that follow, from the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* through the Pure Land corpus to Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows in the *Avataṃsaka*.

**Keywords:** anussati, sati, recollection, mindfulness, yoniso manasikāra, six recollections, Visuddhimagga, Buddhānussati

## I. Introduction

Jon Kabat-Zinn's canonical definition of mindfulness has been cited in hundreds of thousands of contexts: "paying attention on purpose, in the present moment, non-judgmentally." It appears in the opening pages of meditation apps, in MBSR course handouts, in corporate wellness slides.

But the sentence has no source in the Pāli canon.

This is not to say it is wrong. It is a *Westernized silhouette* — a distillation of certain dimensions of *sati* that could be received by clinical, educational, and managerial contexts. That silhouette has done real work: for four decades it has given millions of Westerners their first contact with an attentional training rooted in the dharma.

But the silhouette has a cost. Two dimensions that are central to *sati* in the Pāli discourses are trimmed off: *ārakkha* — the sense of *guarding*, of the mind's self-protection against loss of its own footing — and the specifically doctrinal framing of its objects: body, feeling, mind, and mental objects (*kāya*, *vedanā*, *citta*, *dhamma*) as the four *satipaṭṭhānas*. "Non-judgmental present-moment awareness" can be cultivated over a raisin, a breath, or a footstep — useful, but not the *sati* of the Nikāyas.

That is the story of *sati* being trimmed once.

A close relative of *sati* — *anussati*, usually rendered *recollection* — has been trimmed far more severely.

In most English-language Buddhist writing, *anussati* is positioned as a subordinate of *sati*: "mindful recollection of the Buddha," "mindfulness of the Sangha." This placement already builds in a conclusion: that *anussati* equals *sati* plus a religious object.

The argument of this chapter is that this placement is wrong.

*Sati* and *anussati* are two tracks. They coexist in Buddhaghosa's forty meditation subjects with distinct positions and complementary functions, but their *attentional structures* differ, their *mental factors* differ, and their *concentration trajectories* differ. Reading *anussati* as a religious variant of *sati* flattens the entire subsequent history of recollective practice — from the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* to the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi-sūtra* to the *Sixteen Contemplations*, right through to the recitation of Amitābha's name and Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows — into what looks like a sequence of increasingly ornate visualization exercises.

They are not. They are the historical unfolding of a single attentional technology. To see that unfolding, one must first see *anussati* itself clearly.

That is what this chapter does.

## II. Scriptural Basis

### 2.1 Sūtra references (dual-tradition parallel citation)

Throughout this book, Pāli passages are cited alongside their Chinese-translation parallels to let the reader see how the same practice left not-quite-identical traces in two textual traditions.

**Group one: the *sati* baseline**

- MN 10 *Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta* [^1]
- DN 22 *Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta* [^2]
- Chinese parallels: *Madhyama-āgama* 98 *Nianchu-jing*, *Saṃyukta-āgama* 607 (among others)

These passages establish the basic structure of *sati*: four observational domains (body, feeling, mind, mental objects) and the mind's sustained "knowing as it is" within those domains. *Sati*'s function here is *methodological* — it specifies *how to attend*, not *what to attend to* (each of the four *satipaṭṭhānas* is a broad domain containing many possible specific objects).

**Group two: the *anussati* baseline**

- AN 6.10 *Mahānāma Sutta* (the Buddha's teaching of the six recollections to the Sakyan layman Mahānāma) [^3]
- AN 11.11 *Mahānāma Sutta* (the eleven-recollection expanded version) [^4]
- AN 6.25 *Anussati-ṭṭhāna Sutta* (the six bases of recollection with their functional description) [^5]
- Chinese parallels: *Saṃyukta-āgama* 931 (Mahānāma's question), *Ekottara-āgama* chapter on the Three Jewels (the six recollections as a set)

These passages establish the basic structure of *anussati*: six named objects (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, devas) and the mind's action of repeatedly adhering to and tracing along these objects. *Anussati*'s function here is *contentful* — it specifies *what to attend to* and *what kind of mental state that attention will give rise to*.

**Group three: the technical description of attentional structure**

- AN 6.25 *Anussati-ṭṭhāna Sutta* in full
- Chinese parallels in *Saṃyukta-āgama*

This passage gives the clearest technical description of *anussati*'s operational sequence: the Buddha explicitly traces the sequence of mental states — establishment of object, adherence, arising of confidence (*pasāda*) and joy (*pāmojja*, *pīti*), temporary abandonment of the five hindrances, pliancy and workability of body and mind. This sequence is the primary analytical material for §3.4 below.

### 2.2 Treatise references

- **Buddhaghosa, *Visuddhimagga* VII — *Cha-Anussati-Niddesa* (Exposition of the Six Recollections)**. Two technical points from this treatise are central to the present argument: (1) *anussati* as a meditation subject reaches *access concentration* (*upacāra-samādhi*) but does not enter *absorption* (*appanā-samādhi*); this is not a limitation but a structural feature that sets up the discussion in chapter P04. (2) Each of the six recollections follows a shared formula ("*Itipi so bhagavā arahaṃ...*" for the Buddha, with parallel structures for the others), demonstrating that *anussati* is not effusive devotion but a *formatted* practice with internal rigor. [^6]
- ***Yogācārabhūmi*, *Śrāvakabhūmi* chapters on *smṛti* and *anusmṛti*** — the Yogācāra systematic analysis distinguishes "*smṛti* as non-loss of object" from "*anusmṛti* as repeated illumination of object," aligning with the Pāli distinction. [^7]

This paper does *not* draw on later Chinese Pure Land commentaries (Shandao, Yongming, Zhuhong, Hanshan, Ouyi). Those materials belong to Part VI. The task of this chapter is to set the foundation cleanly, not to unfold sixteen centuries of reception.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The semantic and attentional structure of *sati*

The verbal root *√smṛ* in both Sanskrit and Pāli carries two mutually supportive senses: *to remember* and *to hold*.[^9] The first points backward — not letting what one already knows fall off the mind. The second points at the present — keeping the mind in place, not letting it slip. In the actual operation described in MN 10, these two senses converge: the practitioner walking, sitting, lying down, eating, drinking, *clearly knows what she is doing* (the present-tense activation of "remembering") *and this clear knowing does not lapse with changing circumstances* (the continuous operation of "holding").

The Western rendering of *sati* as *mindfulness* captures the "present-moment awareness" dimension. This was its real contribution. But it trims away two things.

First, it trims the *guarding* register. In the Pāli materials, *sati* is frequently paired with *ārakkha* (guarding, protection), describing the mind's self-defense against loss of its own position. The "non-judgmental awareness" formula of Western mindfulness edits out this protective register, because within the Western clinical idiom *judgment* is understood as a contaminant of pure awareness — whereas in the dharma, *sati* is precisely the precondition for *correct* judgment ("this is wholesome, this is unwholesome, this is to be cultivated, this is to be abandoned"). *Sati* is not non-judgmental; it is the ground on which right judgment becomes possible.

Second, it trims the doctrinal content of the object. The four domains of *satipaṭṭhāna* — body, feeling, mind, mental objects — are not arbitrary. They constitute a complete Buddhist mapping of *the observable layers of human existence*. Extracting *sati* from this fourfold frame and applying it to breath, footstep, dishwashing, or raisin yields an *operationally intact but doctrinally de-contented* attentional training. This has its uses in contemporary mental health, but it is not the *sati* of the Pāli texts.

These two trimmings, however, are not the main concern of this chapter. The concern is that even in its trimmed form, *mindfulness* is still *closer to the original* than the corresponding trimming of *anussati*. The reason is structural: *mindfulness* remains at least a *methodological* term — it describes *how to attend*. The common English rendering of *anussati* as *recollection* does not even operate on the same methodological plane. *Anussati* is not a "mode of attention" at all. It is an *independent practice category* with a specified object, specified mental factors, and a specified soteriological fruit.

### 3.2 The semantic and attentional structure of *anussati*

*Anussati* is formed from the prefix *anu-* joined to the root *√smṛ*. The prefix *anu-* in Sanskrit and Pāli carries the sense of *along, following, repeatedly*: *anu-gacchati* ("to follow after"), *anu-modanā* ("to rejoice with")[^8], *anu-śāsana* ("to instruct in sequence"). When *anu-* combines with *smṛ*, the result is not "to remember once" but "to repeatedly adhere, along a specific object, through sustained recollection."

The prefix itself tells the reader that *anussati*'s core action is not "to hold the present moment" but "to return, again and again, along a specific content."

The first movement of *anussati* is *establishing the object*. The six recollections — of the Buddha, of the Dhamma, of the Saṅgha, of virtue, of generosity, of the devas — each specify a determinate object. These are not arbitrary sacred items. They are *clusters of qualities* to which the mind can repeatedly return, each return yielding nourishment.

Here the first structural difference between *sati* and *anussati* comes into view:

- *Sati* has a method, specifies no object (any actual object within the fourfold framework will do).
- *Anussati* has an object, specifies no separate method (the method is built into the repeated adherence to that object).

Put differently: *sati* is the *vehicle*, *anussati* is the *destination together with its route*. One carries the mind along; the other tells the mind where it is going and what each step is stepping on.

This difference will echo throughout the thirty chapters that follow. The full evolution of Chinese Buddhist recollective practice — from the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*' "remembrance during the interval of a single meal," to the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi-sūtra*'s "seeing the Buddha face to face," to the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus*'s sixteen contemplations, to the recitation of Amitābha's name — displays apparent diversity of surface form, but at base it is the unfolding of a single track: *repeated adherence to an object possessing liberative qualities, allowing the mind to be turned by this adherence*. Without this reading key, the tradition will look like an increasingly elaborate cluster of ritual forms. With it, the continuity becomes visible.

### 3.3 Why the distinction matters

This is not an academic nicety. If *anussati* were merely *sati* with a religious object added, then the six recollections would simply be "maintaining mindfulness of these six things" — an add-on module that any Western mindfulness teacher could incorporate. In fact, much Western Buddhist writing of the past two decades has proceeded on exactly this assumption: *Mindfulness of the Buddha*, *Mindfulness of the Dhamma*, *Mindfulness of the Sangha*. The book titles themselves already presuppose that *anussati* is a subset of *sati*.

This presupposition has three technical consequences, each distorting subsequent practice in a specific way.

**First, the attentional structure is flattened.** *Sati*'s attentional action is "guarding the present object against loss." *Anussati*'s attentional action is "adhering along an object to generate confidence, joy, and pliancy." The former takes *observation* as core; the latter takes *recollection-with-confidence* as core. Reading *anussati* as a religious application of *sati* erases the dimension of *pasāda* — confidence, brightening of mind — entirely. The six recollections are then read as "maintaining sober observation of the Buddha," rather than "recollecting the Buddha's qualities so that the mind generates confidence and temporarily sets down the five hindrances."

**Second, the mental factors are simplified.** In the Pāli descriptions of *anussati*, the recurring cluster of mental factors is *saddhā* (faith), *pasāda* (brightening confidence), *pāmojja* (delight), *pīti* (joy), *passaddhi* (tranquility), *sukha* (happiness). This is a *sequence in which emotional and mental states are carried along and transformed*. *Sati*'s mental factor cluster centers on *sati* itself together with *sampajañña* (clear comprehension) — observational in character. Conflating the two erases *anussati*'s signature rhythm of "mind being carried by the object into confidence, joy, and pliancy."

**Third, the soteriological endpoint is misplaced.** The *Visuddhimagga* states plainly that the six recollections reach access concentration but do not enter absorption. This is not a limitation but the *structural signature* of *anussati*: its endpoint is not depth of samādhi but *pliancy and workability of mind* — the mind rendered fit to receive further insight practice. Reading *anussati* as a subset of *sati* creates an expectation that it should drive through to the depths of insight as the *satipaṭṭhānas* do, so that Buddhaghosa's "access only" verdict looks like a deficiency. It is not a deficiency; it is the design specification.

These three consequences combine to flatten *anussati* from a *distinct, confidence-driven track that turns the mind* into a religious flavor variant of *sati*. Everything the remaining thirty chapters will say presupposes that this flattening has been corrected.

The only way to correct it is to return to the texts and look, in detail, at how *anussati*'s attentional structure actually operates.

### 3.4 The place of *manasikāra* within *anussati*

*Manasikāra* — attention, or more precisely *the mind's turning toward an object* — is one of the seven universal mental factors (*sabba-citta-sādhāraṇa cetasika*); every moment of consciousness contains it. But it is not a passive background hum. It is the very *act* by which the mind turns toward this object rather than that one, rests on it, leaves it, returns to it.

*Manasikāra* has two fundamental modes: *yoniso manasikāra* (wise attention, attention that "goes to the root," attention that adheres to the real structure of the object) and *ayoniso manasikāra* (unwise attention, attention that misaligns with the object's real structure). These two terms carry extraordinary weight in the Pāli canon — the Buddha identifies *yoniso manasikāra* in multiple places as the entrance to the liberative path.

*Anussati* is the paradigmatic case of *directed yoniso manasikāra*.

Its direction: specific objects possessing liberative qualities — the Buddha's virtues, the Dhamma's qualities, the Saṅgha's qualities, the purity of virtue, the breadth of generosity, the wholesome qualities supporting the devas' abodes.

Its action: repeated adherence.

Its effect: the mind, carried by this adherence, turns away from the five hindrances and generates confidence and pliancy.

The fourfold rhythm of *anussati*, abstracted from AN 6.25, can be summarized:

1. **Establishing the object**: the mind brings to mind one of the qualities or epithets ("the Blessed One is an arahant, perfectly enlightened...").
2. **Adherence**: wise attention unfolds along the actual content of that epithet.
3. **Arising of confidence and joy**: because the mind is adhering to an object possessing liberative qualities, it is no longer entangled with greed, aversion, and restlessness; *pasāda*, *pāmojja*, and *pīti* arise.
4. **Pliancy and workability**: with the five hindrances temporarily absent, body and mind become light, soft, fit for further contemplative work.

A key insight here is easy to miss: *this fourfold rhythm is effective not because the object possesses mystical power, but because the attentional structure itself is transformative*.

In the *Mahānāma Sutta*, the Buddha's instruction to Mahānāma is remarkably plain. He does not say "recollect me and you will be saved." He says: *when you recollect the Tathāgata, your mind is not entangled with greed, aversion, or delusion.* The fruit is *structural*, not *graced*. The object (the Buddha's qualities) supplies the direction; the attentional structure (repeated adherence along those qualities) effects the transformation.

This insight is the doctrinal spine of the entire book.

From the "remembrance during a single meal" of the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* in Part II, to the "seeing the Buddha face to face" of the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi-sūtra* in Part III, to the three Pure Land sūtras of Part IV, to the eight-sūtra polyhedron of Part V, to the two-stream interweaving of Part VI, to the *Avataṃsaka*'s unfolding in Part VII of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows — the surface forms grow more elaborate, the objects more magnificent, the liturgies more refined. But the underlying operational logic is one and the same: *precise operation of an attentional structure, driving turn of mind*.

This is not mysticism. It is a *craft of mind*, designed twenty-five centuries ago.

### 3.5 One sūtra read through: AN 6.10 *Mahānāma Sutta*

Abstract definitions can sketch the outline. To see *anussati*'s actual rhythm, one must walk through a sūtra.

AN 6.10 is a short discourse. The setting: the Buddha is near Kapilavatthu; the Sakyan householder Mahānāma comes to see him and asks a practical household question: *what should a lay disciple take up in daily practice?*

The Buddha's answer is the full sequence of six recollections. The first segment concerns *Buddhānussati*:

> Here, Mahānāma, a noble disciple recollects the Tathāgata thus: "The Blessed One is an arahant, perfectly enlightened, accomplished in true knowledge and conduct, fortunate, knower of the world, unsurpassed trainer of persons to be tamed, teacher of devas and humans, the Buddha, the Blessed One."

The structure deserves pause. The Buddha is not asking Mahānāma to *visualize* his form, nor to *maintain mindfulness of the Buddha* as though he were a neutral object like the breath. He is asking Mahānāma to *recollect the nine epithets*. Each of these nine points to a *quality dimension* of the Buddha's awakening — worthy of offerings, fully awakened, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, well-gone, knower of the world, unsurpassed, trainer of the tameable, teacher of devas and humans, awakened one, blessed one.

To recollect these nine is not to read a list. It is to *let the mind adhere along these nine qualities, repeatedly*.

The second segment is the technical heart:

> When a noble disciple recollects the Tathāgata, on that occasion the mind is not obsessed by lust, not obsessed by hatred, not obsessed by delusion; the mind is straight, based on the Tathāgata. A noble disciple whose mind is straight gains *inspiration from the meaning, inspiration from the Dhamma, gladness connected with the Dhamma* (*pāmojja*). When there is gladness, joy (*pīti*) arises. When the mind is joyful, the body becomes tranquil (*passaddhi*). When the body is tranquil, one feels pleasure (*sukha*). When one feels pleasure, the mind becomes concentrated (*samādhi*).

This passage is a *technical description*, not a literary doxology. The Buddha here lays out with precision the chain of mental changes that operate during *anussati*:

**no obsession by greed/hatred/delusion → mind made straight → inspiration → gladness → joy → tranquility → pleasure → concentration**

Eight links, interlocking. This is not an emotional arc; it is a *structural dissolution and re-composition* of mind. Each link depends on the link before it, and the entire chain is triggered by a single action: the noble disciple's recollection of the Tathāgata.

The third segment gives the practical directive:

> Such a noble disciple, Mahānāma, dwells evenly among an uneven populace; dwells in accord with the Dhamma among a non-Dhamma populace; attains concentration among a distracted populace.

This tells the reader where *anussati* operates. *Not in cloistered retreat.* It requires no secluded site, no cross-legged posture. It runs in markets, households, everyday commerce — so long as the mind can return to those nine epithets, the structural rhythm will engage.

This is precisely why *anussati* is so friendly to lay life. It is also why the Buddha prescribes the six recollections, and not the four *jhānas*, to Mahānāma — a layman of a great clan, surrounded by household affairs.

Read this sūtra from beginning to end, and the complete anatomy of *anussati* lies open. It is not a religious application of *sati*. It is not an affective devotional exercise. It is *a craftsmanship of attention, driven by confidence, fruited in pliancy of mind — designed for lay life from the start*.

### 3.6 The cooperative relation of *sati* and *anussati*

A final clarification is needed to forestall a different misreading: this chapter is not arguing that *sati* is inferior to *anussati*, or that *satipaṭṭhāna* is inferior to the six recollections.

Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* arranges the forty meditation subjects in categories; the six recollections form one class, while the body-contemplation and feeling-contemplation of the *satipaṭṭhāna* framework appear as independent meditation subjects in other classes. Both sit within the same system.

Their cooperative relation runs as follows:

- *Anussati* generates confidence, quiets the hindrances, renders the mind pliant — a *craft of preparing mind*.
- *Sati* guards mindfulness, sustains observation, preserves clarity — a *craft of employing mind*.

One prepares, the other employs. *Anussati* takes the mind from coarseness, distraction, and hindrance-coveredness into softness, brightness, workability; *sati* takes that workable mind and turns it onto body, feeling, mind, and mental objects, driving toward insight.

For lay practitioners, *anussati* often serves as the point of entry — it requires no secluded site, no meditation posture, and integrates into daily life. *Satipaṭṭhāna* demands more supportive conditions. For full-time monastic practitioners, the two mutually support each other: a *Visuddhimagga* practitioner may adopt *Buddhānussati* as one meditation subject in combination with others, forming a complete configuration of the path.

The Yogācāra *Śrāvakabhūmi*, in its Chinese translation, employs the single term *nian* 念 to cover the field, but internally distinguishes "*nian* as non-forgetting of the object" from "*anusmṛti* as repeated clear illumination of the object" — corresponding to the Pāli structural distinction.

Recognizing the cooperative relation prevents *anussati* from being seen as an affective practice standing apart from right view and right wisdom. It is *one tool in the full toolbox of the liberative path*, with a defined range of application, a defined fruit, and defined boundaries.

## IV. Cross-disciplinary Touchpoints (restrained)

This chapter is the book's semantic foundation. Cross-disciplinary analogies must be few and precise, so as not to dilute the textual argument. The following two brief notes are offered only to give Western readers a minimal point of entry, and are immediately qualified by the middle-way corrections that follow.

**First, two categories in attention research.** Contemporary attention research (Lutz, Slagter, Dunne, and Davidson 2008) distinguishes *focused attention meditation* from *open monitoring meditation*, and identifies measurably different patterns of neural activity and cognitive effect for the two. On the surface, *anussati* resembles the former (sustained adherence to a specific object) and *sati* resembles the latter (open observation across the domains of body, feeling, mind, mental objects). This analogy helps Western readers ground the two-track distinction in familiar vocabulary. ⚠️ But it is a heuristic, not an equivalence. The objects of attention research are typically neutral (breath, tones, visual targets), whereas *anussati*'s object carries specific soteriological content; the confidence-and-joy dimension of *anussati* is, by methodological design, excised from the experimental paradigm. The analogy can serve as an entry point, but it cannot serve as a terminus.

**Second, *recollection* in Western religious contexts.** The English word *recollection* is already an established term in Western religious thought — notably in Augustine, in medieval contemplative traditions, and in Ignatian spirituality. Augustine's *memoria Dei* (recollection of God) bears structural similarity to *anussati*: both involve repeated return to a sacred object, with that return carrying the turning of the mind. ⚠️ But the purpose and the terminus differ. Christian recollection aims at *unio mystica* — mystical union with God; *anussati* aims at pliancy of mind and temporary abandonment of the hindrances, not at any substantial union. Borrowing *recollection* as a translation has its convenience, but readers should be aware of the Western religious teleology that the word carries, and not let that teleology backflow into the Buddhist reading.

These two analogies exist to offer a starting reference frame. The following thirty chapters will not repeat them — readers are asked to remember both ⚠️ markers.

## V. Middle-way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading one: "*Anussati* is mindfulness of something"

This is the core misreading this chapter works to correct. As argued above, *mindfulness* is already a Westernized silhouette of *sati*; adding a religious object to that silhouette to define *anussati* layers one distortion atop another. *Anussati* is not *sati* plus an object. It is an independent track of practice, with its own attentional structure, its own mental factors, and its own soteriological endpoint. Reading it as a religious application of *sati* is a *structural* misreading, not a matter of degree.

### ❌ Misreading two: "Buddhānussati means imagining the Buddha's physical appearance"

Common in modern devotional writing. *Anussati*'s core action is *recollection of the Buddha's qualities* — the quality dimensions pointed to by the nine epithets — not *visualization*. Visualization is a later technique (for example, the sixteen contemplations of the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus*) with its own doctrinal location, but it is not foundational to *anussati*. The Pāli recollection of the Buddha never requires the practitioner to "see" the Buddha's form; it requires *recollecting the nine quality dimensions and letting the mind repeatedly adhere along them*. Reading *Buddhānussati* as visualization transplants a late technique onto the early foundation.

### ❌ Misreading three: "*Anussati* is the Buddhist version of *bhakti*"

Common in comparative religion contexts. The Hindu *bhakti* tradition centers on devotional affect, love for and union with the deity. On the surface, *anussati* also involves recollection of the Buddha and a turning of the mind, and so looks like Buddhist *bhakti*. But *saddhā* and *pasāda* within the *anussati* structure are *not* devotional affect; they are *a brightening of mind, a non-hesitation, a quality of the mind oriented toward the liberative direction*. They do not require the practitioner to surrender self to the Buddha, and they do not aim at union. Reading *anussati* as Buddhist *bhakti* misplaces the six recollections as devotional practice, obscuring their structural signature as an attentional craft — the signature that is, precisely, the true thread connecting early Buddhism through the Mahāyāna to the full Chinese recollective tradition.

### ⚠️ Caution one: The focused-attention / open-monitoring neuroscientific analogy

As noted in §IV, the direction is roughly correct but imprecise. *Anussati*'s object carries content; the focused-attention experimental paradigm uses neutral targets. The confidence dimension is excised by experimental design — yet it is precisely the most distinctive feature of *anussati*. Useful as an entrance, not as a terminus.

### ⚠️ Caution two: The Augustinian *memoria Dei* analogy

Structurally similar, teleologically different. Christian recollection aims at mystical union; *anussati* aims at pliancy of mind and temporary release from the hindrances. Borrowing *recollection* as translation is convenient, but readers must not allow the Western religious teleology carried by the word to recolonize the Buddhist reading.

## VI. Conclusion

Treating *anussati* as *sati* plus a religious object is the most widespread — and most costly — misreading in recent English-language Buddhist discourse. It flattens the six recollections into "mindfulness of six items," deletes the confidence dimension, deletes the pliancy-of-mind as fruit, and reduces an entire practice category to a religious-flavored branch of mindfulness.

This chapter's work has been to reverse that flattening. *Anussati* is an independent track. Its attentional structure — establishing the object, adhering, confidence and joy arising, pliancy — has its own integrity. Its mental factor cluster — *saddhā*, *pasāda*, *pāmojja*, *pīti*, *passaddhi*, *sukha* — is distinct from the observational cluster of *sati*. Its soteriological fruit — access concentration, not absorption, and with that the rendering of mind *workable* for further practice — is its design, not its limit. The structure itself is transformative; it does not rely on the object's mystical charge.

This understanding is the interpretive key for the thirty chapters that follow.

From the "remembrance during a single meal" in the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* in Part II, to the "seeing the Buddha" in the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi-sūtra* in Part III, to the three Pure Land sūtras of Part IV, to the eight-sūtra polyhedron of Part V, to the historical interweaving of Chan and Pure Land in Part VI, to Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows unfolding across the dharma-realm in Part VII — the surface forms grow ever more elaborate, ever more magnificent, ever more ornate. But the underlying operation, from first chapter to last, is one and the same: *an attentional craftsmanship with a specified object, a specified attention, a specified rhythm of mental factors, and a specified soteriological endpoint — the craftsmanship of anussati*.

See this one track clearly, and the full sweep of the tradition becomes coherent. Miss it, and the Chinese recollective tradition will appear as a sequence of increasingly mystical ritual forms.

The six recollections — of the Buddha, of the Dhamma, of the Saṅgha, of virtue, of generosity, of the devas — are six doors. This chapter has sketched the shape of the door itself. In the next chapter, we open the first.

In the Pāli canon, *Buddhānussati* — the first of the six, and the most developed across the tradition — does not begin with the word "Buddha." It begins with *nine epithets*. The next chapter starts with those nine words — *itipi so bhagavā arahaṃ sammā-sambuddho* — and watches the Buddha himself teach people how to recollect him.
