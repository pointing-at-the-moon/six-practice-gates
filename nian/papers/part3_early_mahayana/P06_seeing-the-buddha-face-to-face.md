# Seeing the Buddhas Face to Face

***The Pratyutpanna-Samādhi Sūtra* and the Arrival of the Visionary Register in China**

## Abstract
This paper addresses the first register-jump in the Chinese reception history of recollection-practice: Lokakṣema's translation of the *Pratyutpanna-Buddha-Sammukhāvasthita-Samādhi-Sūtra* (T0418), completed at Luoyang in 179 CE. NIAN P05 established the dual-track origins of early Chinese *niànfó*—the ethical register of T0784 and the technical register of T0602. This paper argues that T0418 introduced a third register, the **visionary register**, whose canonical markers include the debut of the compound term *niànfó* as a central doctrinal lexeme, the first appearance of Amitābha Buddha and Sukhāvatī in Chinese Buddhist literature, and the configuration of *pratyutpanna-samādhi* around a three-fold semantics of "presence" (temporal immediacy, spatial facing, cognitive immediacy). The paper develops three arguments. First, the Pāli *buddhānussati* system has no procedure whatever for face-to-face encounter with a living buddha, and this structural absence reflects an ontological gap between two buddhologies—a single historical Buddha versus innumerable presently-living buddhas in the ten directions—rather than any Pāli deficiency or Mahāyāna completion. Second, the five-character Chinese phrase *xiàn-zài fó xī zài qián lì* ("all the presently-existing buddhas stand manifestly before one") precisely maps the tripartite semantics of *pratyutpanna-buddha-sammukhāvasthita* and can serve as a technical coordinate system for reading this *samādhi*. Third, T0418 internally couples visionary seeing-of-buddhas with *prajñā*-style self-deconstruction from its first textual moment—the injunction to see but not cling, the red-hot-iron simile, the mirror-oil-crystal parable of "neither from within nor from without," and the "mind is Buddha" formulation as hermeneutic closure of the visionary experience. This structural coupling, inherited by the whole Chinese *niànfó* tradition, refutes the common Western reading of visionary practice as an Eastern variant of mysticism. The paper thereby historicizes the three-sense framework of *niànfó sānmèi* established in NIAN P04 §3.5: the textual birth of sense C (Mahāyāna visionary) is located in T0418, in Luoyang, 179 CE.

**Keywords:** *Pratyutpanna-samādhi*, T0418, Lokakṣema, *sammukhāvasthita*, vision of buddhas, Amitābha, Sukhāvatī, visionary register, ten-direction present buddhas, absence of Pāli parallel, *prajñā* coupling, early Mahāyāna

## I. Introduction: When Recollection Starts to See

On the bookshelf of a typical Western English-language reader of Buddhism there is usually a tacit cognitive partition. On one side are the "rational" materials—Pāli *vipassanā* instruction, secular mindfulness, a certain kind of breath-attention technique that can be brought into a corporate conference room. On the other side are the "devotional" materials—Pure Land, tantra, bodhisattva cults, the sort of practices that require a reader to accept an entire cosmology before they can be begun. The first category is treated as Buddhism's exportable core; the second is treated as cultural packaging. The partition is nowhere an official doctrine, but its weight is perceptible—in the Western paperback market, in the marketing copy of mindfulness retreats, in the set of dissertation topics that seem natural in a graduate Buddhist studies program.

What is most suspected of all within this partition is **visionary practice**—the class of practices in which one not only recollects a buddha but **sees** the buddha, not only calls to mind the buddha's qualities but **encounters the buddha face to face**. Modern readers tend to carry two default predispositions toward this class. The first: that visionary experience is a religious packaging for what is really dissociation or hallucination. The second: that visionary practice is a devotional side-branch of Buddhism, unrelated to the "real" thing (which is usually imagined as the cool psychology of the early *Nikāyas*). The two predispositions together place the visionary tradition, as a whole, in the "suspect" column.

The text taken up in this chapter meets these two predispositions head-on.

In the second year of the Guānghé era of the Later Han emperor Líng, corresponding to 179 CE, the Yuezhi translator Lokakṣema (Zhī Lóujiāchèn 支婁迦讖) produced at Luoyang the first Chinese rendering of the *Pratyutpanna-Buddha-Sammukhāvasthita-Samādhi-Sūtra*—the three-fascicle text catalogued as T0418. This is a sūtra that openly promises a practitioner can **see presently-living buddhas, and converse with them face to face**. It gives operational specifics: seven days and seven nights, or a single day and night, or three months of uninterrupted walking-meditation. The *samādhi* it sets forth bears, in Chinese, a second title—*Xiànzài fó xī zài qián lì sānmèi* 現在佛悉在前立三昧, "the *samādhi* in which the presently-existing buddhas stand manifestly before one." This second title is printed alongside the first (*bānzhōu sānmèi* 般舟三昧, transliterating *pratyutpanna-samādhi*) in the sūtra's own opening lines.

This chapter argues three things.

**First**, T0418 constitutes the **first register-jump** in the Chinese reception history of recollection-practice. NIAN P05 identified the dual-track origin of first- and second-century Luoyang: the ethical register of T0784 and the technical register of T0602. T0418 introduces a third register—the **visionary register**. The Chinese compound *niànfó* 念佛, which occurs zero times in T0784 and zero times in T0602, makes its **first appearance** as a central doctrinal lexeme precisely in T0418. The vocabulary-birth of Chinese *niànfó* is not in the three Pure Land sūtras (T0360 / T0365 / T0366 would not be fully translated into Chinese until the 4th or 5th centuries); it is in this text, nearly a century earlier.

**Second**, the visionary *samādhi* is not an internal deepening of the Pāli *anussati* system but a **structural rupture** with it. Part I established the technical finding that *buddhānussati* reaches only *upacāra-samādhi* (access concentration; P04 §3.2), and the structural reason: the object is multi-faceted (the nine epithets, ten names), and the mind must move among the facets rather than lock onto one. T0418 breaks this boundary by two operations performed together: **object compression** (from abstract qualities to the concrete living form-body of a buddha) and **object activation** (from the already-parinirvāṇa-ed historical Buddha Śākyamuni to the presently-living Amitābha in another world-system). These two operations are not refinements within the Pāli architecture; they are a different species of *samādhi*-technique. The Pāli tradition has no parallel for this practice; that absence is itself part of the argument.

**Third**—and this is the most important point in responding to Western readers' mysticism worry—T0418 has already **built in, from within the text itself**, the very critique that such readers would want to bring to it. The sūtra says on one page, "let the mind recollect the buddha Amitābha who is now present in the West," and on another, "the bodhisattva who sees the buddhas must not cling to the buddhas," illustrating the latter with the red-hot-iron simile: seeing the buddha is good; seizing the form of the buddha one has seen will burn the hand. The visionary *samādhi* of T0418 is not mysticism with an Eastern accent. It is a practice whose **hermeneutic self-deconstruction is embedded from the moment of its textual birth**. T0418 produces the technique of seeing-the-buddha and, in the same breath, produces the internal critique of that technique.

The chapter inherits the equipment established in Part I (P01–P04): the *anussati* engine, the dual-track citation discipline, the *upacāra*/*appanā* coordinate system, and the three-sense latitude of *niànfó sānmèi* named in P04 §3.5 (sense A *upacāra* platform / sense B *appanā* compression / sense C Mahāyāna visionary). The work here is mainly with sense C, and the specific question is its **historical moment of arrival**. P07 will take up the buddha-field (*buddha-kṣetra*) ontology that this visionary arrival entails; P08 will take up the verbal shift from buddha-recollection to buddha-invocation (from *niàn* to *chēng*); Part IV (P09–P11) will take up the systematic development of Amitābha and the Pure Land canon. T0418 is the source of that long trajectory.

## II. Canonical Basis

### 2.1 Basic Information on the *Pratyutpanna-Samādhi Sūtra*

The primary source of this chapter is the *Bānzhōu sānmèi jīng* 般舟三昧經 in its three-fascicle recension, *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 13, no. 418, translated by Lokakṣema at Luoyang in the second year of the Guānghé era of Emperor Líng of the Later Han (179 CE). The text runs across sixteen chapters (*pǐn* 品) and comprises roughly 25,000 CJK characters. A shorter single-fascicle translation by the same hand is catalogued as T0417, and a further variant—the *Bá-pō púsà jīng* 拔陂菩薩經 attributed to Lokakṣema—as T0419. All citations in this chapter are from the three-fascicle T0418 text unless otherwise noted.[^1]

Lokakṣema belonged to the Yuezhi (Kuṣāṇa) people, and his translation activity spans roughly 167–186 CE. He is one of the three foundational translators of early Chinese Buddhism (the other two being the somewhat earlier An Shigao and the somewhat later Zhi Qian), and his translation style is distinctively austere and archaic, often preserving oral-transmission features of the Central Asian source texts. Many specialized terms make their first Chinese appearance in his renderings. T0418 is among his most doctrinally consequential works and is part of the bedrock corpus that carried early Mahāyāna materials into the Chinese sphere.[^2]

One bibliographical detail of the text deserves attention. The Chinese title is *Bānzhōu sānmèi jīng* (in which *bānzhōu* transliterates *pratyutpanna*); but the opening of the first fascicle carries an alternate title, self-marked as "also named *Shífāng xiàn-zài fó xī zài qián lì dìng jīng*" 十方現在佛悉在前立定經—"the *samādhi*-sūtra in which the presently-existing buddhas of the ten directions all stand manifestly before one." The two titles are printed together. The second is an element-by-element Chinese rendering of the Sanskrit *Pratyutpanna-buddha-sammukhāvasthita-samādhi-sūtra*:

| Sanskrit element | Meaning | Chinese rendering |
|---|---|---|
| *pratyutpanna* | present, now-arising | 現在 *xiànzài* |
| *buddha-sammukha* | buddha face-to-face | 佛悉在前 *fó xī zài qián* |
| *avasthita* | standing, abiding | 立 *lì* |
| *samādhi* | *samādhi*, concentration | 定 *dìng* |

The dual-title structure will serve in §III.2 as direct textual warrant for the three-fold semantics of "presence."

### 2.2 List of Key Passages

The chapter draws on nine core passages, distributed across three chapters of the sūtra:

**Chapter 2 (*Xíng pǐn* 行品, "Practice"), fascicle 1** — the body of the method

- "From the causes and conditions of *niànfó*, turning toward *niànfó*, the mind is not confused."[^3] The canonical debut of the compound term *niànfó* as a central doctrinal lexeme.
- "Let the mind recollect the buddha Amitābha who is now in the West. … That land is named Sumati; amid the assembly of bodhisattvas, he is teaching the Dharma."[^4] The first appearance of Amitābha and Sukhāvatī in the Chinese Buddhist corpus.
- "Holding the precepts unbroken, dwelling alone in one place, let the mind recollect the buddha Amitābha of the West. … For a day and a night, or for seven days and seven nights. After seven days—he will see the buddha Amitābha; not in waking but in the dream."[^5] The seven-day-and-night temporal protocol; the first vision arises in the dream.
- "The mind makes the buddha; the mind itself sees. The mind is buddha; the mind is Tathāgata (*dáshà'ājié* 怛薩阿竭). … The mind does not know itself; the mind does not see itself. When the mind has thoughts, that is delusion; when the mind is without thoughts, that is nirvāṇa."[^6] The hermeneutic closure of the visionary experience.
- "Like a newly polished mirror, like a flawless crystal, wishing to see its own reflection. … The reflection neither comes out from inside nor enters from outside."[^7] The mirror-oil-crystal parable.

**Chapter 3 (*Sì-shì pǐn* 四事品, "Four Matters"), fascicle 1** — the conditions of intensive practice

- "Let no worldly thought arise, for the span of three months as brief as a finger-snap; let there be no lying down, for the span of three months as brief as a finger-snap; let the walking meditation not pause, let there be no sitting, for three months, except for meals and the toilet."[^8] The three-month walking-meditation protocol that grounds Tiantai Zhiyi's later *chángxíng sānmèi* (constantly-walking *samādhi*) institution (P23's main venue).

**Chapter 5 (*Wú-zhuó pǐn* 無著品, "Non-Clinging"), fascicle 2** — the *prajñā* self-deconstruction

- "Thus, Bhadrapāla: let the bodhisattva who holds this *samādhi* see the buddha in this way—yet not cling to the buddha."[^9] The central paradox: seeing plus not-clinging.
- "Like a great lump of iron put in a fire and heated to redness, the wise do not take it in hand."[^10] The red-hot-iron simile.
- "The mind attains purity and thus sees the buddhas. Having seen them, one then sees no more: this is understanding this supreme *samādhi*."[^11] The self-dissolution of the *samādhi*.

### 2.3 The Pāli Baseline (Statement, Not Citation)

This chapter does not re-cite Pāli primary material. Part I (P01 on the dynamics of *anu-smṛ*, P02 on the nine epithets, P03 on the six-recollection system, P04 on the *upacāra*/*appanā* boundary) has already built up the full technical portrait of *buddhānussati* at the Nikāya layer: the object is the nine-epithet (ten-name) Itipi-so formula; the operation is *anu-√smṛ*, a rotation of awareness across multiple facets of buddha-quality; the meditative depth is *upacāra-samādhi*, not *appanā*; the state-characteristics are the *pāmojja–pīti–passaddhi–sukha* dynamic chain with the five hindrances temporarily suppressed; the use-case is as a pre-*vipassanā* platform (witness the Phussadeva example).[^12]

**This baseline contains no procedure for face-to-face encounter with a living buddha.** AN 6.10, AN 11.11–12, the four *satipaṭṭhāna* loci, *Saṃyutta Nikāya* SN 55 on the four-fold stream-entry limb, and *Visuddhimagga* VII have no parallel to such a practice. This chapter treats that absence as a structural datum of its §III.1 argument, rather than passing over it in silence.

### 2.4 A Special Form of the Dual-Track Discipline

The NIAN series' dual-track citation discipline (NIAN-A provision) calls, wherever *anussati* at the Nikāya layer is at issue, for the Chinese and Pāli sides to be cited together. T0418 sits outside the normal reach of NIAN-A—it is not a Nikāya text but an early Mahāyāna work. The chapter therefore extends the dual-track principle to a **dual track of absences**: the Pāli side's structural absence is named and marked, rather than quietly skipped.

NIAN P05 closed with a forward-looking flag: the move from T0784 to T0418 is the **first register-jump** of Chinese reception. This chapter receives that jump directly, without softening its discontinuity.

## III. Central Argument

### 3.1 The Structural Significance of the Pāli Absence

Part I demonstrated technically that *buddhānussati* has a complete and self-consistent place in the Pāli system: its object is the multi-faceted nine epithets, its depth stops at *upacāra*, its purpose is to platform *vipassanā*. Buddhaghosa devotes a passage of *Visuddhimagga* VII to arguing this exact point: the mind's "dispersion of adherence across various kinds of qualities" (*nānappakāra-guṇānussaraṇa-adhimutti*) and the single-pointed lock-in of *ekaggatā* are structurally incompatible, and therefore *buddhānussati* does not cross into *appanā*. The Phussadeva example extends the demonstration: from the *upacāra* platform, *vipassanā* can launch directly to arahatship without the intermediate ascent to absorption.

Within this system, **there is no operational need whatever to face-meet a living buddha**. The Pāli *Nikāya*s do, of course, record scenes in which disciples see the Buddha; but those are real physical encounters during the Buddha's lifetime, not visionary events arising from seated cultivation. The operation of *anussati* is recollection of buddha-qualities, not invocation of buddha-form.

T0418 proposes something structurally new: through seated cultivation, to bring a buddha who has already left this world—or who resides in another world—into **present** (*pratyutpanna*), **face-to-face** (*sammukha*), **standing** (*avasthita*) encounter with the practitioner. This operation has no Pāli parallel, and the Pāli architecture has reserved no empty slot for it.

This is not a difference in detail. It is a difference of **architecture at the ontological level**:

- **The buddhology presupposed by Pāli *buddhānussati*.** The Buddha (Śākyamuni) has entered *parinirvāṇa*; what the disciple can do is recollect the qualities (*guṇa*) he manifested while alive; the *pīti*–*sukha* generated by that recollection is a dynamic consequence inside the disciple's mind, not the present self-showing of a buddha.
- **The buddhology presupposed by T0418's *pratyutpanna-samādhi*.** In the ten-direction worlds, innumerable buddhas (plural) are **now living** and **now teaching**; through *samādhi*-technique the practitioner can see them at this very moment; the seeing is not a subjective projection inside the disciple's mind, but the manifestation of a living buddha in the disciple's cognitive field through three cooperating powers—the "buddha's awesome spiritual power, the power of (one's) original merit, the power of the *samādhi* itself," as the first fascicle states explicitly.

This ontological gap is a **structural leap at the level of buddhology**. The Buddha of the Pāli system is **one** historical person, now in *parinirvāṇa*. The buddhas of T0418 are **many**, presently-living, dispersed across the ten directions. The Luoyang translation workshop of the late second century had already produced, before T0418, the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* (T0784) and An Shigao's breath-treatise (T0602)—and the buddhology of both remained Pāli-shaped: a single historical buddha, now in *parinirvāṇa*. T0418 is the **first time** Chinese readers meet the framework of **innumerable presently-living buddhas of the ten directions**.

The Pāli absence is therefore not a blank of "not yet developed" or "not yet recorded." It is a structural fracture between two buddhologies. Reading T0418 with a Pāli *buddhānussati* framework, a reader runs aground in two places. First, how could recollecting Śākyamuni's historical qualities summon the presently-existing Amitābha? Second, if Amitābha is not a historical figure, what is his ontological status? Both questions will be unfolded in Part IV; this chapter marks only the moment of their first emergence.

**The methodological implication.** Recognizing a "structural absence" is an operational analytic tool for reading Buddhist literature. What Pāli does not contain is neither a Pāli defect nor a Mahāyāna completion; two systems can each have their own internal completeness, differing in that **they address different problems**. Pāli *buddhānussati* addresses the problem of an ethical platform for lay life and a pre-*vipassanā* base. T0418's *pratyutpanna-samādhi* addresses—as §III.2 will detail—the problem of how a practitioner, in an age increasingly distant from the historical buddha, can still have a **living** buddha available for dialogue and for hearing the Dharma. This is a problem the Pāli system does not pose, because it does not place itself in that temporal predicament.

### 3.2 Unpacking the Three-Fold Semantics of "Presence"

Return to the dual-title structure. *Bānzhōu sānmèi* transliterates *pratyutpanna-samādhi*; *shífāng xiàn-zài fó xī zài qián lì dìng* renders *pratyutpanna-buddha-sammukhāvasthita-samādhi* element by element. Both titles designate the same *samādhi*, but the second lays its semantic architecture open. This section unfolds three semantic dimensions from the five-character Chinese phrase *xiàn-zài fó xī zài qián lì* ("the presently-existing buddhas all stand before").

**First dimension: temporal immediacy** (*pratyutpanna* → 現在 *xiànzài*).

*Pratyutpanna* derives from *prati-ud-√pad*—literally "coming forth, arising, manifesting"—and in adjectival use means "now-arising" or "presently-occurring." Its Pāli cognate *paccuppanna* is the familiar third term of the three-time triad (alongside *atīta*, past, and *anāgata*, future).

The object-buddha of Pāli *buddhānussati* is the historical Śākyamuni—who, along the disciple's own timeline, is *atīta*, already in *parinirvāṇa*. Recollection of Śākyamuni's qualities is an orientation toward the past: a retrospection, a remembering, a retroactive engagement.

T0418 demands a different temporal posture. "The presently-existing buddhas stand manifestly before"—the buddhas are neither in the past nor in the future, but **now, in the act of self-manifesting**. The phrase *jīn xiànzài* (*今現在* "now present") recurs in fascicle 1, each occurrence stressing the *now*. For the practitioner, this is not the retrieval of a historical event but **entry into an event presently in progress**.

The shift in temporal posture reconfigures the practitioner's relation to the buddha, from retrospective to contemporary. The psychological dynamics are no longer a question of how I pay homage to a buddha who has gone away, but a question of how I attune to a buddha who is right now present here, right now teaching the Dharma, right now waiting to be seen. The functional meaning of *niàn* in this context is not a memorial gesture but an act of **attunement**: the adjustment of one's own awareness-frequency so as to receive a signal that is presently broadcasting.

**Second dimension: spatial facing** (*sammukha* → 悉在前 *xī zài qián*).

*Sammukha* is literally "face" (*mukha*) "toward / together with" (*sam-*)—face to face. In the compound *buddha-sammukha*, it locates the buddha within the same spatial field as the practitioner, the two oriented toward one another. The Chinese *xī zài qián* renders this precisely: *xī* (all), *zài* (are there), *qián* (in front).

The spatial structure of Pāli *buddhānussati* is inward. The disciple's mind rotates the buddha's qualities inside its own thinking-space. The epithets are cognitive objects, not spatial ones.

T0418 places the buddha in the practitioner's **frontal space**. The first fascicle reads, "the bodhisattva thus sees, pure, the innumerable buddhas of the ten directions"; and again, "he sees the buddha Amitābha right here in this very place where he sits." The practitioner sits *here* ("in this place"), and the buddha stands *in front* ("before"). The spatial structure is not one in which the practitioner enters some imagined space; it is one in which the buddha **enters** the practitioner's here-and-now space, out of his own native world.

One key phrase in the first fascicle sharpens the point: "not by supernormal locomotion reaching that buddha-realm; not ending here to be born there before seeing." The practitioner does not fly by *ṛddhi* to the buddha's world; nor does he die here and take birth there before meeting the buddha. He sees the buddha **right here, right now**. This draws a clear line between the visionary *samādhi* and a later sense of "rebirth into a pure land": rebirth is spatial relocation, present-standing is a spatial crossing at this place. The distinction matters for Part IV's framing of Pure Land doctrine: T0418's visionary seeing precedes, and is not to be equated with, any teaching of birth into Sukhāvatī.

**Third dimension: cognitive presence** (*avasthita* → 立 *lì*).

*Avasthita* is from *ava-√sthā*, literally "to stand, to abide," extended to "to be in a state, to be stably manifest." In compounds the word often carries "installed," "presently standing." The Chinese *lì* is spare but carries a real technical function here: the buddha does not merely flash into sight (that would be the punctual event of *dṛś*, "to see"); he **stands** stably there.

This stability is corroborated internally, in the second fascicle's line "the mind attains purity and thus sees the buddhas; having seen them, one then sees no more: this is understanding this supreme *samādhi*." "Having seen them, one then sees no more" is not the failure of the *samādhi* but its advanced form: first stable standing-manifestation (*avasthita*), then the deconstruction of the seen form (the "no more seeing" of this advanced state). Without first a stable standing-manifestation there is nothing to deconstruct. The two layers will be taken up in §III.3.

Cognitive presence is therefore two-layered. The first layer is the stable being-present of the buddha inside the *samādhi* (*avasthita*); the second is a hermeneutic reflexivity toward that presence. The two layers are not successive developmental stages; they are two dimensions coexisting within one *samādhi*.

**The three dimensions together.**

Recombining: T0418's *pratyutpanna-samādhi* is a *samādhi* of **now (temporal) + in front (spatial) + standing (cognitive)**, each dimension supporting the others. Strip the temporal dimension and one falls back to Pāli-style memory-recollection. Strip the spatial dimension and one becomes pure introversion. Strip the cognitive stability and one gets fragmentary flashes or self-suggestion. The *samādhi* T0418 names is the simultaneous co-presence of the three.

Against Pāli *buddhānussati*:

| Dimension | Pāli *buddhānussati* | T0418 *pratyutpanna-samādhi* |
|---|---|---|
| Temporal | retrospective (toward a *parinirvāṇa*-ed buddha) | contemporary (toward a now-living buddha) |
| Spatial | internal (cognitive space) | external-facing (face-to-face presence) |
| Cognitive | epistemic engagement (running through qualities) | perceptual encounter with a hermeneutic layer |

The columns do not represent a gradient of depth. They represent a **difference of mode**. P04 §3.5 has already supplied the correspondence framework: sense A (*upacāra* platform) corresponds to Pāli *buddhānussati*; sense C (Mahāyāna visionary) corresponds to T0418's *pratyutpanna-samādhi*. Sense B (*appanā* through object-compression) sits between them—compression of the multi-faceted object into a single image can reach *appanā*, but does not yet carry the three-fold semantic structure of T0418. The full arrival of sense C requires T0418.

This chapter thus gives P04's three-sense framework a **historical precision**: **the textual birth of sense C is T0418, in Luoyang, in 179 CE**.

### 3.3 The Built-In Coupling of Visionary and *Śūnyatā*

The easiest misreading of T0418, by both Western readers and modern Chinese readers, is to treat it as a purely visionary text—a sūtra that promises one will see buddhas but offers no critical reflection on what it is to "see" a buddha. This misreading is refuted inside the text itself. T0418 is not visionary first and then glossed later by *śūnyatā* commentary; from the outset it welds the two together.

**Evidence one: seeing plus not-clinging, set side by side.**

The second fascicle's chapter of Non-Clinging turns on a single explicit thesis: "the bodhisattva who sees the buddhas must not cling to the buddhas." The "seeing the buddhas" here picks up directly from the seven-day-and-night seeing-of-Amitābha of fascicle one; and the Non-Clinging chapter immediately requires that whatever seen-form has arisen not be seized. The sūtra specifies the psychological operation concretely:

> In this *samādhi*, the bodhisattva must not cling. The one who does not cling attains this *samādhi* swiftly.

Note the logic: the not-clinging one "attains … swiftly." Not-clinging is not a critical postscript added after the *samādhi*; it is **itself the accelerator of the *samādhi***. Visionary experience and *prajñā* self-awareness are two faces of a single gesture in T0418.

The red-hot-iron simile in the same fascicle gives the structural coupling concrete form:

> Like a great lump of iron put in a fire and heated to redness: the wise do not take it in hand. Why? Because it burns the hand. So too, Bhadrapāla: the bodhisattva who sees the buddhas must not cling—not to form, not to feeling, not to perception, not to formation, not to consciousness. Why? Because the clinger burns his own body.

The simile is not a warning against visionary experience itself. It is a warning that **clinging to the seen-form reverses the direction of the *samādhi* and consumes the practitioner**. This is an internal critique, not an external disclaimer. The iron's redness is itself evidence of the *samādhi*'s power; what must be avoided is only the specific act of "taking it in hand."

**Evidence two: the mirror-oil-crystal parable of "neither from within nor from without."**

Fascicle one carries a deeper reflexive move, unpacking the onto-epistemic status of the seen-buddha through three material similes (oil, crystal, newly polished mirror):

> Like a newly polished mirror, like a flawless crystal: one wishing to see one's own reflection, so looking at it, sees one's own reflection.

The sūtra then poses a question:

> The oil, the crystal, the mirror's water-surface—when a person looks into them, does the reflection enter from without?

Bhadrapāla answers: it does not.

The structure is Madhyamaka-style in advance of Madhyamaka. It dismantles directly the "ontological location of the seen-buddha." The seen buddha does not enter the mirror from outside (the outside-world would then have one buddha fewer); does not exit the mirror from inside (the inside-world would then have one buddha more). The reflection is the dependent-arising manifestation of the mirror's clarity conditioning this image.

Map the simile to the *samādhi*. The seen buddha does not enter the practitioner from outside (the buddha-realm would then have one buddha fewer); does not exit from inside (the practitioner would then somehow produce an extra buddha). The seen form is the **dependently-arisen manifestation of three cooperating conditions**: the power of the *samādhi*, the awesome spiritual power of the buddha, and the force of one's original merit.

This is **not** an ex-post analytic added by later readers. It is T0418's native treatment of the question. And it is worth remarking that T0418 was translated in 179 CE—earlier than, or contemporary with, Nāgārjuna's (c. 150–250 CE) systematic Madhyamaka. The mirror-simile in T0418 is a sūtra-level precursor of Nāgārjuna's logic, not a downstream application of it.

**Evidence three: "mind is Buddha" as hermeneutic closure of the visionary.**

The first fascicle contains a dense short verse-like passage that has been cited in later Chinese Buddhism as a source-point for the doctrine "mind is Buddha":

> The mind makes the buddha; the mind itself sees. The mind is buddha; the mind is Tathāgata (*dáshà'ājié*); the mind is my own body. The mind sees the buddha. The mind does not know itself; the mind does not see itself. When the mind has thoughts, that is delusion; when the mind is without thoughts, that is nirvāṇa.

This passage was drawn on across subsequent Chinese Buddhism: Tiantai's *guānxīn* (contemplation of the mind), the Chan "seeing one's nature," Yogācāra consciousness-only, and the Pure Land concept of "the Amitābha of one's own nature" (*zìxìng Mítuó*). This chapter does **not** unfold those later careers (they are material for Parts V, VI, and VII). It notes only one textual fact of position: **the "mind is Buddha" passage in T0418 appears immediately after** the seven-day-and-night seeing of Amitābha, the dream-vision, and the mirror-simile. It is, in its original context, **the hermeneutic closure of the visionary experience**—not an independent metaphysical proposition.

The narrative order of T0418 is:

1. The practitioner for seven days single-mindedly recollects the buddha Amitābha.
2. The practitioner sees the buddha Amitābha in the dream.
3. On waking, the practitioner reflects: whence comes this seeing? (The mirror-parable Q-and-A.)
4. Conclusion: the mind makes the buddha, the mind itself sees, the mind is buddha.

In this sequence, "mind is buddha" functions in T0418's original horizon as the **reflexive dissolution** of the visionary: the ontological condition of seeing-the-buddha is that the mind itself possesses buddha-nature, and therefore the buddha one sees is both **real** (issuing from the mind's buddha-nature) and **empty** (no separate buddha arriving from outside the mind). Later traditions took this structural insight in different systematic directions—Tiantai, Chan, Yogācāra each built distinctive edifices on it—but the embryonic form is already complete in T0418, in 179 CE.

**Summary of §III.**

The three-layered argument converges on a single claim: T0418, as the headwater of the Chinese *niànfó* visionary register, carries **from its first textual appearance** two co-present structures—one outward (seeing the buddha), one inward (not clinging to what is seen). The two are not in tension; they are a coupling. A Western reader who brings the modern partition of "mysticism versus rational meditation" will misread this coupling as two incompatible tendencies awkwardly cohabiting; but the sūtra's own logic is: **precisely because visionary practice could slip into mysticism, it must be coupled with *śūnyatā*; precisely because bare *śūnyatā* has no concrete fulcrum of practice, it must be coupled with the specific technique of seeing-the-buddha.** Neither side is dispensable.

This coupling is the **structural inheritance** of the entire Chinese *niànfó* tradition. The later three streams—name-recitation (*chímíng* 持名), image-contemplation (*guānxiàng* 觀像), reality-recollection (*shíxiàng* 實相)—in effect calibrate the relative weight of the two poles: name-recitation foregrounds the concrete technique of seeing-the-buddha (the Name now taking the place of the form); image-contemplation stays at the form-visualization pole; reality-recollection tilts toward the *śūnyatā* reflective pole. But no stream abandons the other pole. Part VI will address the long-term balance question directly. The present chapter establishes only the source-coupling.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Bridge: Dreaming and the Phenomenology of Non-Veridical Perception

The contemporary field most productively in dialogue with T0418 is the cognitive science and philosophy of non-veridical perception—the study of dream, imagination, and memory-imagery. The most serviceable interlocutor is Evan Thompson's *Waking, Dreaming, Being: Self and Consciousness in Neuroscience, Meditation, and Philosophy* (2014), which analyzes the three states of consciousness (waking, dreaming, deep sleep / pure awareness) from the combined perspectives of phenomenology, neuroscience, Yogācāra, and Advaita.[^13]

One of Thompson's core claims: the continuity among dreaming, imagining, and perceiving is not a binary of "true versus false" but an array of construction-modes of consciousness. Dreaming is not "false perception" but a mode in which consciousness actively constructs an experiential field in the absence of exteroceptive input. Imagining is not "internal fake-perception" but the operation of perception under a different temporal orientation. This position inherits the enactivist tradition of Francisco Varela and Humberto Maturana and has a structural resonance with Yogācāra's account of the transforming-manifestations of consciousness (*vijñāna-pariṇāma*).

Three observable levels of dialogue emerge with T0418:

**Level one: dream-vision of the buddha as a canonical case of the visionary mode.**

T0418's canonical description of the first vision of a buddha is: "not in waking but in the dream." The first seeing of the buddha is specifically placed in the dream state. Thompson's phenomenological stance gives this detail a direct significance: the dream is not inferior to waking as a knowledge-claim, but another mode of lived experience with its own phenomenological integrity. T0418 placing the first post-seven-days vision in the dream forms a structural dialogue with Thompson's stance—visionary experience is not a distortion of waking experience but **a mode of experience with its own phenomenological completeness**.

**Level two: "neither from within nor from without" and enactivist constructionism.**

The mirror-simile conclusion in T0418's first fascicle—"the reflection neither comes out from within nor enters from without"—shares structural isomorphism with the enactivist constructionism Thompson draws on. The enactivist position holds that perception is neither a mapping of the external world into consciousness nor a projection from consciousness outward onto the world, but an **event enacted** in the interaction of organism and environment. The mirror-simile says something formally parallel: seeing the buddha is neither an external buddha entering the mind (mapping) nor the mind projecting a buddha (idealist projection), but an event co-enacted by three conditions under *samādhi*.

**Level three: hermeneutic self-dissolution and epistemic humility.**

Thompson's book repeatedly emphasizes at its close that experiential reports of states of consciousness must be paired with systematic critical reflection—phenomenological description alone is insufficient; it must be joined by reflection on the epistemic status of the description itself. T0418's formulation "having seen the buddhas, one then sees no more: this is understanding this supreme *samādhi*" walks a structurally parallel path: after the experience of seeing-form, the hermeneutic dissolution of the "seeing-form" fact is still required. Both share a methodological attitude: take first-person experience seriously, yet do not let it solidify into unreflective knowledge-claim.

### ⚠️ Three Non-Reducible Differences

Three levels of structural convergence do not make these two the same discourse. Three non-reducible differences must be named.

**Teleological difference.** Thompson's phenomenological inquiry is secular philosophy joined with cognitive science. Its aim is a better understanding of consciousness, an improvement in mental health, a sharpening of philosophical rigor. T0418's soteriological aim is to see buddhas, to hear Dharma from them, to attain the *anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti* (forbearance toward the non-arising of *dharmas*), to enter the rank of *avaivartika* bodhisattva, and ultimately to attain buddhahood. The two *teloi* are not structurally interchangeable. Reducing T0418 to "an early-form inquiry into consciousness" is a teleological-level reductive misreading.

**Ontological difference.** Thompson's framework presupposes an enactivist non-dualism (subject and object co-arising). T0418 presupposes a Buddhist ontology of dependent arising plus the reality of buddhas of the ten directions as ontological existents (not merely as constructs in the practitioner's mind). The "neither from within nor from without" of the mirror-simile, in T0418, is not an enactivist co-arising thesis but a visionary manifestation when three conditions are complete. The buddha is **really existent**—in his own native buddha-field; it is only that he does **not enter the practitioner by mapping**. To enactivize this nuance is to lose T0418's buddhological ontological commitments—commitments that are the very premise on which Part IV's Pure Land doctrine stands.

**Upgrade-path difference.** Thompson's framework carries no soteriological upgrade structure. Deeper phenomenological inquiry does not bring one into any final liberative state. The visionary *samādhi* of T0418, by contrast, is a specific stage of the bodhisattva path, connected to *avaivartika* status, to the ten stages (*bhūmi*) and ultimately to buddhahood. T0418's upgrade structure is part of the Buddhist soteriology; it cannot be sliced off.

### A Specific Warning for Western Readers

Recent Western Buddhist writing—particularly the genre at the confluence of mindfulness and cognitive science—has sometimes tended to reduce the Buddhist visionary tradition to "early experiments in consciousness studies." Such framing seems respectful; in fact it demotes the most soteriologically weighty practices of the Mahāyāna tradition (seeing buddhas, rebirth in buddha-fields, bodhisattva vows) to case studies for contemporary consciousness research. T0418 resists this framing through its **built-in soteriological commitment**: seeing the buddha is not a consciousness experiment; it is a node on the bodhisattva path. To abstract this node and treat it as an object of phenomenology while ignoring its soteriological location is a McMindfulness-adjacent methodological demotion.

Thompson himself is explicit about the danger. *Waking, Dreaming, Being* repeatedly states that what he is doing is philosophical inquiry—not a manual of meditation, and not a modern replacement for Buddhist doctrine. This chapter preserves his carefulness: the framework serves as heuristic dialogue, not as theoretical substitution.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

- **❌ Correction one.** "*Pratyutpanna-samādhi* = Eastern religious packaging for hallucination or dissociation." *Mistaken.* T0418 has critical reflection on the seen-form built into it from within: the injunction against clinging, the red-hot-iron simile, the mirror-simile's "neither from within nor from without," the "mind is Buddha" hermeneutic closure. Reading this *samādhi* as unreflective visionary mysticism is the consequence of not reading through the second fascicle's Non-Clinging chapter, and of not completing the mirror-simile's Q-and-A. The first-order self-critique was already finished in the sūtra itself, translated in 179 CE. It was not added as a later commentarial disclaimer.

- **❌ Correction two.** "*Pratyutpanna-samādhi* = conjuring a buddha out of nothing, a form of wishful thinking or self-suggestion." *Mistaken.* T0418 is explicit that seeing the buddha requires three conditions to cooperate—the buddha's awesome spiritual power, the force of original merit, the power of the *samādhi* (fascicle 1). The buddha's own agency is part of the cause-and-effect structure; the seeing is not a one-sided construction by the practitioner. The sūtra has ruled out purely subjectivist readings. Its ontology presupposes that the buddha really exists in his buddha-field, and that under sufficient conditions he can manifest; this presupposition cannot be replaced by an enactivist or constructivist frame without losing T0418's buddhological substance.

- **❌ Correction three.** "T0418 is the watershed at which Chinese Buddhism falls from the Pāli rational tradition into a devotional tradition." *Mistaken.* This reading inherits the old "Pāli Protestant Reformation" narrative of Western Buddhist discourse (Pāli cast as proto-Protestant rationalism, Mahāyāna as Catholic excess). §III.1 has shown that Pāli and T0418 are two systems addressing **different problems**, not two ends of a single rational-to-devotional spectrum. To force both onto such a spectrum is an anachronism at the level of methodology, and a coarsening of each system's internal logic.

- **⚠️ Caveat one.** The dialogue with Evan Thompson's phenomenology is heuristic, not a theoretical equivalence. The three non-reducible differences (teleology / ontology / upgrade path) constitute a structural boundary that cannot be crossed.

- **⚠️ Caveat two.** The built-in visionary–*śūnyatā* coupling in T0418 does not entail that all later Chinese *niànfó* lineages preserved the coupling in balance. Part VI will take up the imbalances, and their correction-histories, among the name-recitation, image-contemplation, and reality-recollection streams. The present chapter only marks the source-coupling of T0418; it does not underwrite subsequent historical developments.

---

## VI. Conclusion

A register boundary has been clearly crossed. In Luoyang in 179 CE, Lokakṣema's workshop produced the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi Sūtra*, and the Chinese reception history of recollection-practice encountered for the first time a cluster of structures: the canonical debut of the compound *niànfó* as a central doctrinal term; the first appearance of Amitābha Buddha and Sukhāvatī; the full configuration of the three-fold semantics of "presence" (temporal, spatial, cognitive); the specific temporal protocol of seven days and seven nights; the specification of three months of walking meditation; and—most crucially—the built-in coupling of visionary seeing of buddhas with *prajñā* non-clinging. This cluster has no parallel in Pāli *buddhānussati*, and it is not an internal extension of the Pāli system. It is a **new mode-level arrival**.

The three-sense framework of *niànfó sānmèi* in P04 §3.5 receives here its historical precision: sense A (*upacāra* platform) corresponds to Pāli *buddhānussati*, fully covered in Part I; sense C (Mahāyāna visionary) has its textual birth in T0418. Sense B (*appanā* compression) is the transitional position between the two; its mature form will unfold in later meditation-manuals such as Kumārajīva's *Zuòchán sānmèi jīng* T0614 and Buddhabhadra's *Guānfó sānmèi hǎi jīng* T0643 (Part V material). T0418 is the starting point of sense C.

**For the practitioner, directly.** Visionary practice is neither an Eastern variant of mysticism nor the marker of Buddhism's descent into devotionalism. T0418 tells its own readers, at the moment of its first appearance: to see the buddha is good; to cling to the buddha is self-burning. Stable standing-manifestation of the seen form is *samādhi*; the reflexive dissolution of the seen form is also *samādhi*. The mind makes the buddha; the mind itself sees; yet the mind does not know the mind. This coupling does not release the practitioner from either side. If one's *niànfó* collapses into a one-way visionary pursuit, T0418's red-hot-iron simile is there to check it. If one's *niànfó* collapses into pure *śūnyatā* reflection without a concrete fulcrum, T0418's seven days and seven nights is there to anchor it. Neither pole can be dispensed with. This structural wisdom is an inherited competence of the Chinese *niànfó* tradition from the second century on, not a paradox invented by later Chan masters.

**The open question.** T0418 has introduced the buddhology of "innumerable presently-living buddhas of the ten directions"—multiple, elsewhere, now. This ontology implies a question it does not yet answer: if the buddhas are plural and dispersed across the ten directions, what kind of existence is the **world** (*buddha-kṣetra*, buddha-field) in which each of them dwells? In T0418, the name Sukhāvatī appears only as a focusing-target for the practitioner; it has not yet been elaborated into a destination with its own complete cosmology. That elaboration will be carried out by later sūtras—the forty-eight vows of the *Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha* T0360, the sixteen contemplations of the *Contemplation Sūtra* T0365, the description of the Land of Bliss in the *Shorter Sukhāvatīvyūha* T0366. The next paper, P07, will take up the emergence of the buddha-field concept: from T0418's bare mention that "each buddha teaches the Dharma within his own buddha-land" to the three Pure Land sūtras as full cosmological destinations, what happened in between? The transition from buddha-field as visionary backdrop to buddha-field as soteriological destination is the doctrinal enabler of the Chinese *niànfó* tradition's move from T0418-style visionary *samādhi* to Pure Land-style name-invocation for rebirth.

*Namo tassa bhagavato arahato sammāsambuddhassa.*
