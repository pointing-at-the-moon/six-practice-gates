# From Recollection to Calling

**The Four-Step Coupling of *Niàn* and *Chēng* in Chinese Buddha-Recollection**

## Abstract

Continuing from P07's treatment of the buddha-field ontological shift from T0418 to T0361, this paper takes up the open question left at P07's close: how does *niàn* 念 (recollection) yield to *chēng* 稱 (calling-by-name) in the Chinese Pure Land tradition? Three theses. First, the two verbs do not stand in a relation of replacement but of *dynamic coupling*. Four textual nodes—T0361's seventeenth vow ("*hearing* my name" as access-verb), T0360's eighteenth vow ("even *ten recollections*" as semantic joint), T0365's lowest-grade rebirth cases (where *chēng* first emerges as an independent access-verb, and where *chēng* and *niàn* alternate within a single passage), and Shandao's *Commentary on the Contemplation Sūtra* T1753 (where *chēng* is systematized as "the primary settled act," 正定業)—open the coupling space step by step. None of the four steps displaces *niàn*. Second, at the systematization terminus in Shandao, the load-bearing verb in the canonical definition of 正定業 remains *niàn*: "single-mindedly *hold-in-mind* Amitābha's name, *hold-holding* unceasingly." The popular reading of Shandao as having "replaced *niàn* with *chēng*" is a later doctrinal reframing (most visible in Japanese Pure Land traditions from Hōnen and Shinran), not the text of T1753 itself. Third, the coupling structure is not specific to Pure Land: the *Mañjuśrī Prajñā Sūtra* (T0232), a Prajñā-stream text, defines One-Practice Samādhi through the same three-verb chain—"tether the mind to one buddha, devotedly call the name, and *hold-hold* continually on one buddha"—showing that *niàn–chēng* coupling is a general feature of Chinese buddha-recollection verb-grammar. The paper engages Austin's and Searle's speech-act theory as heuristic interlocutor, arguing that *chēng* functions as an *illocutionary* act whose sincerity condition is precisely *niàn*, and delimits the dialogue with three non-reducible differences (teleology / ontology / upgrade path) in the pattern established in P06 and P07.

**Keywords:** *niàn*, *chēng*, hearing-the-name, ten recollections, calling-the-name, lowest-grade lower rebirth, Shandao, 正定業 (primary settled act), one-mindedly-holding-the-name, One-Practice Samādhi, speech-act theory, illocutionary act, verbal coupling, Chinese Pure Land, T0361, T0360, T0365, T1753, T0232

## I. Introduction: When the Two Verbs Begin to Weave Together

Any Western English-speaking Buddhist reader—and any contemporary Chinese-speaking reader formed by English-language academic vocabulary—comes, sooner or later, upon a translation problem: how should the Chinese phrase *niànfó* 念佛 be rendered into English? *Buddha-recollection*? *Buddha-invocation*? *Mindfulness of the Buddha*? *Name-recitation*? *Nenbutsu*? The instability is not a translator's oversight. It reflects a deeper fact about the Chinese tradition itself: **Chinese Pure Land texts deploy two different verbs**, not one.

*Niàn* 念 is a cognitive-affective verb. It means to recollect, to hold in mind, to keep one's attention on, to remember with care. It can take as its object the Buddha's qualities, the Buddha's body, the Buddha's name; it can be audible or silent; its home territory is the mind.

*Chēng* 稱 is a speech-act verb. It means to call, to name, to invoke aloud, to praise by uttering. It almost always has a name or title as its object; it is, at least in its primary sense, vocal; its home territory is the mouth.

Contemporary Pure Land studies in English tends to render *niànfó* flatly as "name-recitation"—a choice that simplifies the social fact of East Asian *nenbutsu* as a mass practice but that obscures the early Chinese tradition's distinction between the two verbs. The secular-mindfulness movement, by contrast, often renders *niànfó* as "mindfulness of the Buddha"—a choice that rescues the recollective depth of *niàn* but that erases the doctrinal standing of *chēng* as a performative act.

Both renderings have historical warrant. Both also distort.

This paper picks up the open question left at the close of P07. P07 traced the ontological shift from T0418 to T0361: from Amitābha's given buddha-field in the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi Sūtra* to Dharmākara's selection-and-combination construction of Sukhāvatī through twenty-four vows. In its closing section, P07 identified a parallel shift—this time at the level of *verb*—that it had left unaddressed. From T0418's "always recollect me" (*cháng niàn wǒ* 常念我) to T0361's seventeenth vow, "all who *hear my name* leap in joy to be born in my land"; from T0360's "even *ten recollections*" to T0365's "*call* the name of Amitābha"—the verb of access appears to migrate from *niàn* to *chēng*.

What is the character of that migration?

This paper argues three theses.

**First**, *niàn* and *chēng* in the Chinese Pure Land canonical corpus stand not in a relation of replacement but of *dynamic coupling*. Four textual nodes—T0361's seventeenth vow, T0360's eighteenth vow, T0365's lowest-grade rebirth cases, and Shandao's T1753—open this coupling space step by step; but none of the four steps removes *niàn*. Across all four, both verbs remain present, though with shifting relative weight and shifting functional role.

**Second**, even at the systematization terminus—Shandao's *Commentary on the Contemplation Sūtra*, in which "calling the name" is promoted to the status of 正定業, "the primary settled act"—the canonical definition's *principal verb* remains *niàn*: "single-mindedly *holding-in-mind* Amitābha's name, *hold-holding* unceasingly." This refutes the popular narrative that Shandao "turned *niànfó* into name-calling" and that "after Shandao, *chēng* replaced *niàn*." That narrative is a later doctrinal reframing, most visible in the Japanese Pure Land tradition from Hōnen's *Senchakushū* and Shinran's *Kyōgyōshinshō* onward. It does not describe T1753's own wording.

**Third**, *niàn–chēng* coupling is not a doctrinal peculiarity of the Pure Land school. The *Mañjuśrī Prajñā Sūtra* (T0232), a Prajñā-stream text outside the Pure Land corpus, gives its canonical definition of One-Practice Samādhi through a three-verb chain: "*tether the mind* to one buddha, *devotedly call the name*, [thereby] *hold-hold* continually on that one buddha." The coupling is a general feature of Chinese buddha-recollection verb-grammar, not a Pure Land sectarian structure.

Section IV engages J. L. Austin's and John Searle's speech-act theory as the heuristic interlocutor—the contemporary framework most able to illuminate *chēng*'s status as an *illocutionary* rather than *locutionary* act. I argue that the *sincerity condition* of *chēng*, in Searle's technical sense, is precisely *niàn*. From the speech-act side the coupling thesis receives additional theoretical support. As in P06 (Thompson) and P07 (Kripke / Lewis), three non-reducible differences are named to prevent theoretical substitution.

## II. Canonical Basis

### 2.1 Grammatical Preliminaries: *Niàn* and *Chēng* as Different Verbs

Before entering the four textual nodes, the grammatical ground must be set.

*Niàn* 念, in early Chinese Buddhist translation, carries three main senses. First, *cognitive holding*—tethering an object to the mind. The Pāli correlates most often translated into Chinese as *niàn* are *sati* (mindfulness) and *anussati* (recollection), both indicating sustained attention (the Pāli ground has been developed in P01–P04). Second, *affective remembrance*—missing, longing for, cherishing. This sense continues a pre-Buddhist Chinese usage already present in the *Shijing*'s poetry of longing. Third, *iterative operation*—"*niàn-niàn* continually," "*niàn-niàn* not abandoning"—as a temporal-count structure. The first two senses are the semantic core; the third is an iterative specialization that later provides grammatical space for discrete counting (such as "ten *niàn*").

*Chēng* 稱 is, by contrast, a speech-act verb. In Chinese it carries two tightly related senses: *calling / naming* and *praising*. When used of buddha-practice, both are active at once: to *chēng* the Buddha's name is both to address the Buddha by that name and to praise the Buddha by uttering it. *Chēng* is almost always vocal: to hold a name silently in mind is not normally called *chēng* in Chinese. Silent name-holding is *niàn*.

The **overlap zone** between the two verbs lies precisely here: when the object of *niàn* is *a name*, *niàn* and *chēng* can refer to the *inner* and *outer* faces of a single practice. To *niàn* Amitābha can mean to hold his name continuously in mind; to *chēng* Amitābha is to let that name sound in the mouth. If one is holding-in-mind *and* uttering at once, the same act is simultaneously *niàn* and *chēng*; neither face can be eliminated.

This semantic overlap is the ground on which the coupling operates. The four textual nodes that follow perform different structural operations on this shared ground.

### 2.2 Node 1 — T0361's Seventeenth Vow: "Hearing the Name" as New Access-Verb

Lokakṣema's *Fó shuō wúliàng qīngjìng píngděng jué jīng* (T0361), vow seventeen of the twenty-four:

> "When I become a buddha, let my name be heard in the buddha-lands of the eight directions, above and below, innumerable in number; let the buddhas each praise, in the midst of their disciples, the virtues of my field and its goodness; let all gods, humans, and crawling creatures who *hear my name* leap in joy to be born in my land; if this is not so, I shall not become a buddha."[^1]

P07 §3.3 argued that T0361's twenty-four vows are structured by an *outward-optimization*: the subject of the vows is, in most cases, the inhabitants of the land, or those who come to be born. Vow seventeen is a special case within that optimization. Its access-mechanism does not require the practitioner to *do* anything; it requires only that the Buddha's *name* should reach the ears of the practitioner. The practitioner's action is simply *to hear*.

Relative to T0418, this is a decisive shift. T0418's access-verb is *niàn*: "those who wish to be born in my land, let them always recollect me." In T0361, the subject of access is still the practitioner, but the action has turned *receptive*—**to hear** the name.

**The key observation.** "Hearing the name" is a *passive* verb, but its precondition is an *active* distribution: "let my name be heard in the buddha-lands of the eight directions." If the practitioner is to hear the name, the name must be circulated, uttered, passed from mouth to mouth. T0361 does not say who, precisely, circulates the name; but the conditional space in which *chēng* will later arrive has been opened. The seventeenth vow does not yet bring *chēng* into the access-verb position, but it builds the cosmological machine in which *chēng* will become necessary.

The secondary evidence in vow nineteen extends the structure:

> "When I become a buddha, those from other buddha-lands whose past deeds were evil—if they hear my name, and turn toward the right way and wish to be born in my land—at the end of their lives, all shall escape the three evil destinies and be born in my land in accordance with their vow."[^2]

The access-chain here is fuller: *hear the name* + *turn toward the right way* + *wish to be born* → *rebirth at death*. Hearing is receptive, but it must be paired with a volitional vow.

A later passage (after the twenty-four-vow list) confirms the ontological weight of the name:

> "The light of Amitābha Buddha—its name is heard in the buddha-lands of the eight directions, above and below, innumerable and without limit; gods and humans all come to hear of it; those who hear of it all cross over."[^3]

The name, as circulable object, carries salvific force in itself. This prepares the mechanics that will later appear in T0365's lowest-grade passages, where "because of calling the Buddha's name" the practitioner is liberated from kalpas of karmic burden.

**Node 1 summary.** T0361 introduces the first non-*niàn* access-verb: *hearing the name*. The access-subject's action shifts from cognitive holding to receptive hearing, and the Name becomes a circulable salvific object. *Chēng* has not yet appeared as access-verb, but the conditional space for it is now firmly in place.

### 2.3 Node 2 — T0360's Eighteenth Vow: "Even Ten Recollections" as Semantic Joint

Saṅghavarman's *Wúliàng shòu jīng* (T0360; the translator attribution is contested in modern scholarship, and the actual date of production is more likely to fall somewhere between the late fourth and the early fifth centuries). Vows seventeen and eighteen stand in adjacent pairs:

> "Suppose, when I become a Buddha, that there are not countless buddhas throughout all world-systems of the ten directions who praise and *call my name*—then I shall not attain perfect awakening.
> Suppose, when I become a Buddha, that all sentient beings in the ten directions, sincerely trustful and desiring to be born in my land, should *even [in as few as] ten recollections*, not be born there—then I shall not attain perfect awakening, excepting only those who have committed the five heinous offenses or who have slandered the true Dharma."[^4]

Vow seventeen: subject is *the innumerable buddhas*; action is *to praise and call my name* (*chēng wǒ míng*). Here *chēng* appears as a vow-verb for the first time in this textual line—but its subject is *the buddhas*, not the practitioner, and the act is of *upward praise* rather than *downward invocation*. T0360's seventeenth vow functions to let the Name be publicly proclaimed in the inter-buddha cosmic register, thereby preparing an audience chamber for sentient beings' hearing-and-recollection. This is distinct from T0361's seventeenth vow, whose subject is sentient beings hearing. The paper marks this distinction in passing; the full philological comparison is not pursued.

The pivotal line is vow eighteen.

Vow eighteen introduces four new structural elements:

- **Sincere trust** (*zhìxīn xìnlè* 至心信樂)—an affective-cognitive condition
- **Desiring to be born in my land** (*yù shēng wǒ guó* 欲生我國)—a volitional intention
- **Even ten recollections** (*nǎi zhì shí niàn* 乃至十念)—an access-unit, count-bounded
- **If they are not born, I shall not attain perfect awakening**—a conditional guarantee (continuing T0361's conditional-vow skeleton)

"Ten recollections" is the pivot of this entire paper.

The phrase is *not defined* inside vow eighteen. What is a "recollection"? Is it ten cognitive acts of *niàn* (recollection in the pure sense)? Ten invocational acts of *chēng* (calling-the-name)? Ten breaths of sustained attention? The vow is silent.

The semantic indeterminacy of "*shí niàn* 十念" is not a textual weakness but a textual *feature*. It establishes *niàn* as a discrete access-unit that can be read in multiple directions.

Fascicle 2's lower-grade rebirth section gives a partial anchoring:

> "The Buddha said to Ānanda: 'For those of the lower grade—the gods and men of the ten directions, if any be sincerely desirous of being born in that land, who, not being able to perform the various meritorious practices, should generate the mind of unsurpassed awakening, *single-mindedly and whole-heartedly, even ten times recollect* Amitāyus Buddha, with the wish to be born in his land—even if they [should fail to recollect in a sustained manner and should] even once recollect that Buddha, with sincere heart wishing to be born in his land—this person, at the hour of death, shall see that Buddha in dream, and shall attain rebirth.'"[^5]

Here "ten recollections" (*shí niàn*) is explicitly followed by "recollect Amitāyus Buddha" (*niàn Wúliàngshòu fó*), anchoring the count-unit to an object-specific practice. Then, remarkably, the number is further contracted: "even *one* recollection" (*nǎi zhì yī niàn*) of that Buddha is said to suffice. The verb throughout remains *niàn*, not *chēng*.

**Key structural observation.** T0360 builds "ten recollections" as a *semantic joint*—count-bounded (ten), object-anchored (recollecting Amitāyus), intention-conditioned (sincere trust, wish to be born)—but *verb-open*. This verb-openness is the textual precondition that makes possible the later commentarial filling-in by Tanluan, Daochuo, and Shandao, who each, in different registers, read "ten recollections" as "ten utterances of the name" (*shí shēng chēngniàn*).

The introduction of a cardinal number is itself decisive. T0418's "always recollect me" has no count; T0361's "always recollect my pure-hearted [self]" has no count; T0360 introduces for the first time an explicit cardinal: *ten*. A cardinal invites *discretized* action, and discrete iteration's most natural realization is discrete utterances—that is, *chēng*. But T0360 does not yet take that step. It rests at the joint. The verb remains *niàn*, but the semantic shape has been re-organized so that *niàn* and *chēng* can now occupy the same textual position.

**Node 2 summary.** T0360's "even ten recollections" is the first point in the canonical vow-corpus where *niàn-chēng* coupling forms a semantic joint. Count-bounded, object-anchored, verb-open: these three features together constitute the structural template for the coupling that the next two nodes will more fully expose.

### 2.4 Node 3 — T0365's Lowest-Grade Rebirth: The Emergence of *Chēng* as Independent Access-Verb

Kālayaśas's *Guān wúliàng shòu jīng* (T0365). The sixteenth contemplation, "the contemplation of the lower-grade rebirths," divides into three sub-levels. Reading all three together is essential.

**Lowest-grade upper-birth** (*xiàpǐn shàngshēng* 下品上生):

> "The Buddha said to Ānanda and Vaidehī: 'As for one who will receive lowest-grade upper-birth: there are some beings who commit various evil deeds but do not slander the vaipulya sūtras—foolish men, doing much evil, without shame or remorse. At the time of death, they meet a good friend who expounds for them the titles and names of the twelve-division Mahāyāna sūtras. Because of hearing these sūtra names, a thousand kalpas of very grave evil karma are destroyed. The wise one then instructs them to press palms together and *call [the name] "Namo Amitābha Buddha."* *Because of calling the Buddha's name*, fifty kotis of kalpas of birth-and-death karma are destroyed. Then that Buddha sends forth a transformed Buddha, a transformed Avalokiteśvara, a transformed Mahāsthāmaprāpta, who come before the practitioner and say: 'Well done, good son! *Because you have called the Buddha's name*, your sins are extinguished; I have come to greet you.'"[^6]

Here *chēng* first appears as an **independent access-verb** in the Chinese Pure Land canonical tradition. The action is not *niàn*; it is "press palms together, *call* 'Namo Amitābha Buddha'"—a posturally concrete, vocally concrete performative action. The transformed Buddha's own speech also uses *chēng*: "*because you have called the Buddha's name*, your sins are extinguished."

This is the first-appearance locus of *chēng* as an access-verb in its own right.

**Lowest-grade middle-birth** (*xiàpǐn zhōngshēng*), for comparison:

> "As for one who will receive lowest-grade middle-birth: there are those who have broken the five, eight, or complete monastic precepts—foolish men, stealing from the Saṅgha, stealing from the present monastic community, preaching the Dharma without purity, without shame—having adorned themselves with such evil deeds. Because of these deeds, such a sinner should fall into the hells. At the time of death, when the fires of hell arrive all at once, one meets a good friend who, with great compassion, *expounds the ten powers and awesome virtues of Amitābha Buddha*, extensively praising that Buddha's light and supernormal power, also praising precepts, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and the vision of liberation. This person, having heard, destroys eighty kotis of kalpas of birth-and-death karma."[^7]

Notice: in the middle sub-level, *neither* *chēng* nor *niàn* appears. The access-mechanism is simply *hearing the praise-discourse* (*wén zànshuō* 聞讚說). This matters as a comparison: the *chēng/niàn* vocabulary is not applied uniformly across the three sub-levels. We cannot, from the lower grades as a whole, derive the thesis "lower rebirth always goes through *chēng*." The second sub-level goes through hearing only.

**Lowest-grade lower-birth** (*xiàpǐn xiàshēng*):

> "The Buddha said to Ānanda and Vaidehī: 'As for one who will receive lowest-grade lower-birth: there are beings who commit unwholesome deeds—the five heinous offenses, the ten evils, all kinds of evil. Such a foolish man, because of evil karma, is due to fall into evil destinies and suffer without end for countless kalpas. At the time of death, such a foolish man meets a good friend who, with many consolations, expounds the subtle Dharma and instructs him to *recollect the Buddha* (*jiào lìng niànfó* 教令念佛). But the man, pressed by suffering, *cannot complete the recollection* (*bù huáng niànfó* 不遑念佛). The good friend says: 'If you cannot recollect that Buddha, *call [the name] with self-surrender*: Namo Amitāyus Buddha.' Thus, with sincere mind, [letting] the voice not be broken, completing ten recollections, *call 'Namo Amitābha Buddha.'* *Because of calling the Buddha's name*, in recollection-upon-recollection, eighty kotis of kalpas of birth-and-death karma are destroyed.'"[^8]

Lowest-grade lower-birth is the canonical locus where *chēng* and *niàn* are juxtaposed and alternated **within a single passage**—and it is the single most structurally explicit textual evidence for the coupling thesis in this paper.

Parsing the five-step verb-flow:

- (a) "the good friend *instructs to recollect* the Buddha"—the starting point is *niàn*
- (b) "the man, pressed by suffering, *cannot complete the recollection*"—*niàn* fails under crisis conditions
- (c) "the good friend says: 'If you cannot recollect that Buddha, *call* [the name] with self-surrender'"—the switch to *chēng* is motivated by *niàn*'s functional failure; *chēng* is positioned as *niàn*'s accessible backup, not as a higher form
- (d) "with sincere mind, letting the voice not be broken, *completing ten recollections*, *call* 'Namo Amitābha Buddha'"—the mechanics of *chēng* are explicitly given: voice unbroken, *ten recollections* (again the joint of Node 2, but now realized through *chēng*), call the Buddha's name
- (e) "*because of calling the Buddha's name*, **in recollection-upon-recollection** (*yú niàn-niàn zhōng* 於念念中), eighty kotis of kalpas of birth-and-death karma are destroyed"—the *effect* is *measured in the frame of niàn*: *chēng* is cause, *niàn* is the unit of measurement

**Two structural insights.**

*Insight A.* *Chēng* is activated precisely at the point where *niàn*'s cognitive bandwidth is exhausted by external conditions (the "suffering pressing upon" the dying person). *Chēng* is not a higher alternative to *niàn*; it is *niàn*'s accessible backup, and more than that, its vocal outer shell.

*Insight B.* The effect of *chēng* is measured back in the frame of *niàn*. The text does not say "in calling-upon-calling" eighty kotis of kalpas are destroyed; it says "in recollection-upon-recollection." The evaluative unit is *niàn*. *Chēng* is the cause-term; *niàn* is the measurement-term.

Taken together, the two insights yield the structural form of the coupling thesis: *niàn* and *chēng* are not two independent actions, but the *inner and outer faces of a single practice*. Inner without outer is still practice (as in T0418's *pratyutpanna*-type visionary practice); outer without inner is empty shell (which Shandao, we will see, describes as "not-sincerely-from-the-mind"). The full practice has both faces at once.

### 2.5 Node 4 — Shandao's T1753: Systematization Terminus and the Persistence of Coupling

Shandao's *Commentary on the Contemplation Sūtra* (T1753, also known as *Guānjīng sìtiè shū* 觀經四帖疏) is the doctrinal hinge of the Chinese Pure Land tradition. In the *Xuányì fēn* 玄義分 (fascicle 1) Shandao re-structures the *Contemplation Sūtra* as a bifurcation into *concentrated good* and *dispersed good*:

> "As for the essential gate, it is precisely this *Contemplation Sūtra*'s gate of *concentrated good* and its gate of *dispersed good*. *Concentrated good* means quieting thought to steady the mind; *dispersed good* means giving up evil to cultivate good; turning both of these practices over to the vow to be born there. … Question: What is concentrated good, what is dispersed good? Answer: From the contemplation of the sun down through the thirteenth contemplation—these are concentrated good. The threefold merit and the nine grades are dispersed good."[^9]

Concentrated good comprises the first thirteen contemplations—of the sun, of water, of the ground, of the true body of the Buddha, of Avalokiteśvara, and so on—all concentrative visualization practices. Dispersed good comprises the threefold merit (worldly, moral, conduct) plus the nine grades (three upper, three middle, three lower)—vow-framed but non-absorption ethical and devotional practices.

But 正定業—the concept by which Shandao establishes the primary access-mode of the Amitābha Pure Land tradition—is found neither in the narrow frame of concentrated good nor in the narrow frame of dispersed good. It appears in the *Sànshàn yì* 散善義 (fascicle 4), within the explication of the *deep mind*, in the passage "on establishing faith through practice":

> "Next, establishing faith through practice: there are two kinds of practice—the right and the various. The right is: practice that relies exclusively on the sūtras about rebirth. What is it? Single-mindedly recite the *Contemplation Sūtra*, the *Amitābha Sūtra*, the *Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha*, and so on. Single-mindedly contemplate and *recollect the twofold adornments of that land*. If prostrating, single-mindedly prostrate to that Buddha. If vocally calling, single-mindedly call that Buddha. If praising and making offerings, single-mindedly praise and make offerings. These are named the right. And within this right there are two further kinds: one—**single-mindedly holding-in-mind Amitābha's name** (*yī xīn zhuān niàn Mítuó mínghào* 一心專念彌陀名號), in walking, standing, sitting, lying, not asking about time—long or short—*holding-holding unceasingly* (*niàn-niàn bù shě* 念念不捨)—this is named the primary settled act (*zhèngdìng zhī yè* 正定之業), for it accords with the Buddha's vow. The others—prostrating, reciting, and so on—are named the assisting acts."[^10]

This passage is the structural summit of the paper's argument.

**The five right practices.** Shandao lists them: recitation of sūtras (*dúsòng*), contemplative recollection (*guānchá*), prostration (*lǐ*), **vocal calling** (*kǒuchēng* 口稱), and praise-and-offering (*zàntàn-gòngyǎng*). *Chēng* here is one among five. It is *not* raised above the others at this juncture; it sits within the cluster.

**Then within the five, two sub-classes.** The primary settled act (*zhèngdìng zhī yè*) is identified as:

- **"single-mindedly *holding-in-mind* Amitābha's name"**—*niàn*, not *chēng*
- **"*holding-holding* unceasingly"**—*niàn-niàn*, not *chēng-chēng*

The principal verb of the canonical definition is *niàn*. Not *chēng*.

This is the second critical finding flagged in the source preparation for this paper, and it is the most direct textual evidence against the popular narrative of Shandao as the "canonizer of name-recitation-as-replacement-for-recollection."

**Grammatical reading.**

- Principal verb: *niàn* (cognitive holding)
- Object: *the name of Amitābha* (the name, not the body, not the virtues)
- Modifier: *single-mindedly, exclusively*
- Temporal distribution: "walking, standing, sitting, lying, not asking about time—long or short"
- Iteration marker: *niàn-niàn bù shě* (*niàn* is repeated as the iteration unit)

Shandao, having already identified *kǒuchēng* as one of the five right practices, could have defined the primary settled act as "*single-mindedly calling Amitābha's name, [letting] voice-after-voice be unbroken*." He did not. He chose *niàn* as the load-bearing verb, and *niàn-niàn* as the iteration metric.

**Why?**

Because the evaluative unit of this iterative practice—the unit by which one measures whether a given practice *counts as* 正定業—is *the continuity of mental holding*, not *the repetition of sound*. A practitioner may produce voice-after-voice of the name and nevertheless be *niàn-scattered* (the mind elsewhere, not holding); this would not count as 正定業. A practitioner may go through a short stretch without producing sound, while the mind does not drop the name; this would still fall within the canonical definition. Shandao fixes the verb at *niàn* precisely because he intends the reader to return, however *chēng*-heavy the outer practice may be, to *niàn* as the inner evaluative frame.

**The argument against two popular readings.** First: "Shandao turned *niànfó* into name-calling." No—T1753's canonical definition uses *niàn* as the principal verb; vocal calling is one of five parallel right practices, not the core of the primary settled act. Second: "After Shandao, *chēng* replaced *niàn* in the Chinese Pure Land tradition." No—*chēng* is elevated (it becomes one of the five right practices, and it is the primary outer form of 正定業 in practice), but *niàn* is preserved in the verb-layer of the canonical definition.

**Coupling-at-terminus.** Even at the systematization terminus, the coupling persists at the textual level. *Niàn* and *chēng* are not in a relation of replacement; they are in a *define-by-inner-form / realize-by-outer-form* coupling.

**Scope-guard.** This paper extracts from T1753 only the two passages above—the concentrated/dispersed bifurcation (fascicle 1) and the 正定業 definition (fascicle 4)—and only for their bearing on the *chēng* mechanics question. Shandao's doctrines of the three minds (*sān xīn*), the two-fold deep faith (*jīfǎ èrzhǒng shēnxìn*), and the ordinary-person's entry into the recompense-land (*fánfū rù bàotǔ*) are the primary material of P22 and are not treated here.

### 2.6 Cross-Sectarian Evidence: T0232's One-Practice Samādhi

The coupling is not a Pure Land sectarian structure. Mandrasena's *Mañjuśrī Prajñā Sūtra* (T0232), fascicle 2, in the chapter on One-Practice Samādhi:

> "Good son, good daughter: one who wishes to enter One-Practice Samādhi should dwell in a quiet place, let go of all scattered thought, take no appearance, **tether the mind to one buddha**, and **devotedly call the name**. Facing the direction of that buddha, body upright, one becomes able to [maintain], on that one buddha, **recollection-upon-recollection unbroken**; and in that recollection one can see the buddhas of past, future, and present."[^11]

Three verbs in sequence:

- **Tether the mind** (*xì xīn* 繫心)—attentional focus on the object
- **Devotedly call the name** (*zhuān chēng míngzì* 專稱名字)—vocal invocation of the name
- **Recollection-upon-recollection unbroken** (*niàn-niàn xiāngxù* 念念相續)—iterative recollection as temporal pulse

The three verbs are all *simultaneously* required in the canonical definition. None can be eliminated.

T0232 belongs to the Prajñā stream (the Mañjuśrī-*prajñā* branch), not the Pure Land. Its doctrinal lineage runs parallel to T0361 / T0360 / T0365 / T1753—but the coupling structure is the *same*.

This demonstrates that *niàn-chēng* coupling is a general feature of Chinese buddha-recollection verb-grammar, not a Pure Land specificity. The four nodes of §2.2–§2.5, taken together with the cross-sectarian witness of §2.6, jointly ground the central thesis of this paper.

A full treatment of One-Practice Samādhi—especially the question of how it simultaneously carries Prajñā doctrinal content and recollection-practice operational content—is the primary material of P19, not here.

## III. Central Argument

§II has laid out five textual nodes (four primary + one cross-sectarian). §III organizes them into a coherent argument: the dynamics of a four-step evolution, and the structure of coupling-rather-than-replacement.

### 3.1 Step One (T0361) — The Paradox of "Hearing the Name"

T0418's access-verb is *niàn*; the practitioner actively holds the buddha in mind. T0361's seventeenth vow introduces *hearing the name*, shifting the access-subject's action from active holding to receptive hearing. A paradox is thereby formed: hearing is a passive verb, but its precondition—"let my name be heard in the buddha-lands"—requires an active circulation of the name. T0361 does not say who circulates the name; but the conditional space in which *chēng* must eventually arrive has now been opened. *Chēng*, as the canonical function of moving a name from hidden to circulating, is the verb T0361 does not yet contain but must eventually invite.

### 3.2 Step Two (T0360) — The Semantic Joint of "Even Ten Recollections"

T0360's eighteenth vow is the first canonical vow-text in which *niàn-chēng* coupling forms a genuine semantic joint. Three features of the joint:

- *count-bounded* (ten)—a cardinal invites discretized action
- *object-anchored* (recollecting Amitāyus, per the lower-grade passage in fascicle 2)
- *verb-open* (the vow itself does not specify whether "recollection" is cognitive or vocal)

The introduction of a cardinal is particularly decisive. T0418's and T0361's "always recollect" have no count; T0360 introduces *ten*. A cardinal number cannot be smoothly realized by the *continuous* attention of *niàn* alone—it naturally invites discrete units. The most natural discrete unit is *discrete utterance*: *chēng*. But T0360 stops at the joint; its verb remains *niàn*. The commentarial tradition from Tanluan through Shandao fills in what the vow itself leaves open, reading "ten recollections" as "ten vocal recollection-callings"—not a reinterpretation against the text, but a filling-in of a space the text itself left open.

### 3.3 Step Three (T0365) — The Emergence of *Chēng* and the Explicit Coupling

T0365's lowest-grade rebirth passages bring two decisive moves. The first: in lowest-grade upper-birth, *chēng* appears for the first time in the Chinese Pure Land canonical tradition as a **stand-alone access-verb**. Prior texts had used *chēng* only with a buddha as subject (e.g., T0360 vow seventeen, "the buddhas praise and call my name"); here, with the dying practitioner as subject, *chēng* becomes the access-verb in its own right.

The second, and structurally more important: in lowest-grade lower-birth, *chēng* and *niàn* alternate **within a single passage**, and the logic of the alternation is made explicit. The good friend instructs *niàn*; suffering prevents *niàn*; *chēng* is offered as the accessible fallback when *niàn* is blocked; the full unit completed is "ten *niàn*, calling 'Namo Amitābha Buddha'"; and the effect is registered "in *niàn-niàn* unit-of-measurement."

Two insights follow.

*First*, *chēng* is activated when *niàn* is blocked, and is positioned not as higher alternative but as accessible backup—the outer vocal shell that can maintain the practice when the inner cognitive bandwidth is crowded out by external duress. *Second*, the effect of *chēng* is registered back in the evaluative frame of *niàn*. *Chēng* is cause-term; *niàn* is measurement-term.

Together these insights crystallize the coupling: the two verbs are not independent actions but the inner and outer faces of a single practice.

### 3.4 Step Four (T1753) — Systematization Terminus with Coupling Preserved

Shandao's *Commentary* is the doctrinal hinge of the Chinese Pure Land tradition. Its architectural bifurcation of the *Contemplation Sūtra* into concentrated good and dispersed good, and its elevation of *kǒuchēng* to one of five right practices and of "one-mindedly-holding-the-name" to 正定業, provides the textual ground for the later East Asian dominance of name-recitation as mass practice. Shandao's text is the systematization terminus at which, one might expect, *chēng* finally displaces *niàn*.

But the canonical definition of 正定業 deliberately leaves *niàn* as its principal verb. "Single-mindedly *holding-in-mind* Amitābha's name, in walking, standing, sitting, lying, not asking about time—long or short—*holding-holding* unceasingly." The principal verb is *niàn*, not *chēng*. The iteration marker is *niàn-niàn*, not *chēng-chēng*. Shandao had the vocabulary and the framework to define 正定業 as "one-mindedly calling the name, voice unbroken"; he chose not to.

The choice is deliberate. The evaluative unit of this iterative practice is *the continuity of mental holding*, not *the repetition of sound*. A practitioner who produces unbroken voice with scattered mind is not performing 正定業; a practitioner who does not produce sound for a short stretch while mind continues to hold the name is still within the definition. Shandao fixes the verb at *niàn* to force the reader back to the inner frame, regardless of how *chēng*-heavy the outer practice becomes.

**Coupling-at-terminus** is the name of what has happened here. At the systematization terminus the coupling has not been closed by reducing one verb to the other. It has been *locked in* as a stable inner-outer pair: *chēng* as primary outer form, *niàn* as evaluative inner frame. This pair is the canonical template for a thousand years of Chinese Pure Land practice—and, through transmission, for the Japanese tradition that received it—even where later doctrinal reframings in Japan (Hōnen's *Senchakushū*, Shinran's *Kyōgyōshinshō*) re-describe the practice in terms that move away from Shandao's verb-choice.

### 3.5 Cross-Sectarian Closure: One-Practice Samādhi

T0232's three-verb chain—*tether the mind*, *devotedly call the name*, *recollection-upon-recollection unbroken*—presents the coupling outside the Pure Land corpus. The sūtra belongs to the Prajñā stream, a parallel Mahāyāna doctrinal current. The three verbs in T0232's definition extend the *niàn–chēng* pair with an attentional precondition (*tether the mind*), but the coupling of *chēng* with *niàn-niàn* is intact and canonical.

The cross-sectarian witness establishes that *niàn–chēng* coupling is not a sectarian specificity of Pure Land but a general structural feature of Chinese buddha-recollection verb-grammar. Part V of this book will show additional instances in the *Śūraṅgama*'s Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter, in the *Lotus*'s *Avalokiteśvara* chapter, in the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "all-buddhas' mind is foremost" passage, and elsewhere. Here the single witness of T0232 suffices as closure for the four-step argument.

### 3.6 Summary: Four Pieces of Evidence for Coupling-Not-Replacement

- T0361 vow 17: hearing-the-name opens the conditional space for *chēng*; *chēng* itself not yet present as access-verb
- T0360 vow 18: "even ten recollections" forms a count-bounded / object-anchored / verb-open semantic joint
- T0365 lowest-grade rebirths: *chēng* emerges as stand-alone access-verb (upper-birth); *chēng/niàn* alternate within one passage (lower-birth); coupling dynamic explicit in narrative flow
- Shandao T1753: *chēng* systematized as primary outer form; *niàn* preserved as principal verb and evaluative inner frame of the canonical definition—coupling-at-terminus
- T0232 One-Practice Samādhi: coupling present outside the Pure Land corpus; general structural feature

**Coupling, not replacement.** This is the central thesis of this paper, supported across five textual loci in three distinct doctrinal streams.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Bridge: Speech-Act Theory and the Illocutionary Status of Calling-the-Name

The contemporary discipline most productively in dialogue with the mechanics of *chēng* is *speech-act theory* (*Sprechakttheorie*, *philosophie du langage ordinaire*), founded by J. L. Austin in *How to Do Things with Words* (1962) and systematized by John R. Searle in *Speech Acts* (1969).[^12]

Austin's core distinction is three-tiered:

- **Locutionary act**: the mere utterance of a sentence with sense and reference.
- **Illocutionary act**: the act one performs *in* making the utterance (promising, warning, naming, invoking, taking refuge).
- **Perlocutionary act**: the act one performs *by* making the utterance (persuading, convincing, frightening).

The three-tier distinction allows us to ask a question that is decisive for the analysis of *chēng*: **what kind of act is the calling of "Namo Amitābha Buddha"**?

### 4.1 *Chēng* as Illocutionary Act

If *chēng* is purely a locutionary act—the mere production of the string of phonemes—then the criticism "*chēng* is mechanical recitation" has some ground. A parrot can produce the phonemes; no one would take the parrot to be performing *niànfó*.

If *chēng* is, instead, an illocutionary act, then its doctrinal significance is not in the sound-production itself but in **the action performed in the production**—taking refuge, invoking, declaring allegiance, affirming a vow.

Austin's explicit-performative formulas are instructive: "I name this ship the *Queen Elizabeth*"; "I promise to return"; "I take refuge in the Buddha." These utterances do not report a fact; they *perform* an action. To say them, under the right conditions, *is* to do them.

The full form of "Namo Amitābha Buddha" makes this explicit. *Namas* is a Sanskrit grammatical case—the dative of reverential submission. The full utterance is "I take refuge in (*namas*) Amitābha Buddha." Its illocutionary force is *taking refuge*. At the moment the utterance completes, the act of refuge has been performed—not because some social convention recognizes it, but because the utterance *is* the act.

T0365's lowest-grade lower-birth supports this reading. The good friend does not say "you should call the name"; he says "you should *call with self-surrender* (*chēng guīmìng* 稱歸命) Amitāyus Buddha." *Guīmìng*—"returning life," self-surrender, taking refuge—is the illocutionary content. The vocal production of the phonemes "Namo Amitābha Buddha" is the act of *guīmìng*.

### 4.2 Searle's Sincerity Condition

Searle extended Austin's framework by identifying the **four conditions** an illocutionary act must satisfy to be *non-defectively performed*: propositional content, preparatory, *sincerity*, and essential. The *sincerity* condition is decisive here: an illocutionary act requires that the speaker have the matching psychological state. A promise made with no intention of fulfillment is defective—not merely ethically, but *act-theoretically*—the promise has not been fully constituted as a promise.

**What is the sincerity condition of *chēng*?**

Shandao answers clearly: *zhìxīn* 至心, *sincere mind* (here also *zhìchéng xīn* 至誠心, "the most-sincere mind," in the three-mind system of T1753 whose full doctrinal articulation is reserved for P22): "where the sincere mind is absent, the practice done is not true; where it is not true, in the end there is no real content."[^13]

T0365's lowest-grade lower-birth gives the same condition in the passage itself: "with *sincere mind*, letting the voice not be broken, completing ten recollections, call 'Namo Amitābha Buddha.'" *Sincere mind* qualifies the calling. Calling without sincere mind is defective.

**What *is* sincere mind?**

*Sincere mind* is the mind's *arrival*—which is to say, the cognitive-affective layer of *niàn*. If, while calling the name, the mind is scattered and not truly present, the calling as illocutionary act fails. Therefore:

- *Chēng* (calling-the-name) = illocutionary act
- *Niàn* (sincere mind, cognitive-affective presence) = the sincerity condition of *chēng*

The coupling thesis receives, from this angle, an independent theoretical confirmation: *niàn* is not an external ornament attached to *chēng*. It is the condition under which *chēng* can be fully constituted as an illocutionary act in Searle's sense. *Chēng* without *niàn* is a *defective chēng*. It is not an alternative to *niàn*.

### 4.3 Three Non-Reducible Differences

Continuing the pattern established in P06 (Thompson) and P07 (Kripke / Lewis), the dialogue with speech-act theory is heuristic, not equivalence. Three structural boundaries.

**Teleological difference.** Austin's and Searle's speech-act theory serves the goals of analytic philosophy of language: clarifying how meaning extends beyond propositional content, accounting for the pragmatic layer of communication, providing foundations for the semantics of natural language. The *telos* of *chēng* in the Chinese Pure Land tradition is soteriological: rebirth in Sukhāvatī, destruction of karmic burden, eventual vision of the Buddha, and ultimate liberation. The two *teloi* are not interchangeable, and the soteriological telos cannot be generated internally from within a speech-act framework.

**Ontological difference.** Austin's analysis, and more explicitly Searle's later development in *The Construction of Social Reality* (1995), grounds the performative force of a speech act in *social convention*—promising works because there are socially recognized conventions for accepting and enforcing promises; naming a ship works because there are institutional procedures for making a ceremony count. *Chēng*'s performative force is grounded not in social convention but in **vow-ontology**: the Buddha's original vow (especially T0360 vow 18) provides a structural guarantee that makes calling-the-name a salvific act. This is not a feature of social organization but a feature of *pratītyasamutpāda* itself, at the level of *adhipati-pratyaya* (predominant condition). If one maps *chēng*'s force onto social-convention performativity, the ontological status of the Buddha's vow disappears.

**Upgrade-path difference.** Speech-act theory has no upgrade path: deepening engagement with the theory will not bring a speaker to some terminal state; speech-act theory is a descriptive instrument. *Chēng* has an explicit upgrade path—vocal practice → *niàn-chēng* coupling → one-mindedness-not-scattered (T0366's *yī xīn bù luàn* 一心不亂) → *niànfó sānmèi* (recollection-*samādhi*, of the *Pratyutpanna* type) → rebirth and vision of the Buddha → non-regression → eventual buddhahood. This trajectory is a complete soteriological arc, not derivable from speech-act framework and not replaceable by it.

### 4.4 A Warning for Western Readers

A genre of recent comparative philosophy has tried to read Buddhist recollection-practice as a "Buddhist analogue of speech-act theory"—in part to raise its philosophical standing for Western audiences who may otherwise read it as emotional religious vocalization. The gesture is well-intentioned and, in part, valid: *chēng* does have illocutionary structure. But if taken too far, it demotes the soteriological commitment of *chēng* to a case study of speech-act theory.

This chapter uses the Austin / Searle framework only as *heuristic*—to let Western readers see that *chēng* is not "the mechanical repetition characteristic of ancient religion" or "the magical thinking of religious mantra," but a performative action with explicit illocutionary structure. The chapter does not reduce *chēng* to a token instance of speech-act theory. The three non-reducible differences constitute the boundary.

This warning continues the Kripke-Lewis dialogue structure of P07 §IV and the Thompson dialogue structure of P06 §IV: the same methodological pattern, instantiated in a different contemporary field.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

**❌ Correction one.** "*Chēng* replaced *niàn*." *Mistaken.* The four textual nodes of §II and §III do not support the replacement thesis anywhere. T0361's seventeenth vow introduces "hearing the name" but preserves "always recollect"; T0360's eighteenth vow's "ten recollections" accommodates both verbs; T0365's lowest-grade lower-birth alternates the two within a single passage; Shandao's T1753 canonical definition of 正定業 keeps *niàn* as load-bearing verb. The replacement thesis is a retrospective simplification, generally projected backward from later doctrinal reframings (most clearly from the Japanese Pure Land tradition of Hōnen and Shinran). Across T0361 → T0360 → T0365 → T1753, no step in the canonical layer replaces *niàn* with *chēng*.

**❌ Correction two.** "*Chēng* = mechanical recitation." *Mistaken.* §IV has argued that *chēng* is an illocutionary, not locutionary, act, and that its sincerity condition—*zhìxīn*—is precisely the cognitive-affective presence that *niàn* names. To read *chēng* as mechanical recitation is to collapse illocutionary performance into phonation, severing the structural coupling of the two verbs. T0365's textual phrase "with *sincere mind*, letting the voice not be broken" textually rules out this reading.

**❌ Correction three.** "*Niàn* is self-power (*zìlì*), *chēng* is other-power (*tālì*)." *Mistaken.* The self-power / other-power distinction is a doctrinal reframing established after Shandao, most clearly in Hōnen's *Senchakushū* and Shinran's *Kyōgyōshinshō*. It is not the framing of T0361 / T0360 / T0365 / T1753 themselves. In Shandao's canonical definition, *niàn* and *chēng* sit together within the cluster of right practices; no self-power / other-power assignment is made. This paper strictly stays at the verb-grammar layer. The self-power / other-power analysis belongs to P22, where it can be handled in its own context. Imposing it on the present texts produces anachronism—using a later division to retrofit earlier material.

**⚠️ Caveat one.** The dialogue with Austin / Searle speech-act theory is heuristic, not theoretical equivalence. The three non-reducible differences (teleology / ontology / upgrade path) constitute a structural boundary. Speech-act theory is particularly well-suited to illuminate the performative face of *chēng*, but it cannot reduce *chēng*'s soteriological commitment to a case study of speech-act theory. In particular, Searle's later framework in *The Construction of Social Reality*, grounding institutional facts in social convention, would—if directly mapped onto *chēng*—lose the ontological standing of the Buddha's vow (as *adhipati-pratyaya*). *Chēng*'s performative force is not constituted by social convention but is supported by the ontological structure of vow-and-dependent-origination.

**⚠️ Caveat two.** The claim that *niàn* and *chēng* are coupled in the canonical corpus does not imply that the two verbs have remained symmetric in practice-history. In fact, from Shandao onward, *chēng* as *mass* practice has decisively outrun *niàn* in East Asian Pure Land culture—name-calling has become the signature practice of the East Asian (especially Japanese) Pure Land schools, while purely cognitive *niàn* has receded in mass practice, surviving as the specialty of Chan, of Tiantai, and of advanced concentrative states. This uptake asymmetry is the result of institutional-social-cultural factors, not a structural feature of the doctrinal texts addressed in this chapter. Part VI (P20–P24) will treat the historical dynamics of this uptake asymmetry; the present paper underwrites none of the narratives that address it.

**⚠️ Caveat three.** This paper's treatment of T0361 in §3.1 needs to be reconciled with P07 §3.3. P07 argued for the *outward-optimization* of T0361's twenty-four vows—that the subject of the vows shifts from the bodhisattva's self-discipline (as in T0313's Akṣobhya line) to the inhabitants of the land and those who come to be born. The present paper extends that argument at §3.1: the outward-optimization of the vow-subject naturally brings with it an outward-shift of the access-verb (from *niàn* held by the practitioner to *hearing* the name by receptive sentient beings). These are two faces of a single structural move, and this chapter does not re-argue P07's framing.

## VI. Conclusion

**Recapitulation.** Four textual nodes—T0361's seventeenth vow ("hearing my name"), T0360's eighteenth vow ("even ten recollections"), T0365's lowest-grade rebirths (especially the emergence of *chēng* as stand-alone access-verb in lowest-grade upper-birth and the intra-passage alternation of *chēng* and *niàn* in lowest-grade lower-birth), and Shandao's T1753 ("single-mindedly holding-in-mind Amitābha's name, holding-holding unceasingly"—the canonical definition of the primary settled act)—constitute a four-step evolution of Chinese buddha-recollection verb-grammar. This evolution is neither a narrative of *chēng* replacing *niàn* nor one of *niàn* being completed by *chēng*. It is a *dynamic coupling* of two verbs in a shared practice-space: *niàn* provides the cognitive-affective frame, *chēng* provides the performative external form; *chēng*'s sincerity condition is *niàn*, and *niàn*'s iterative enactment naturally supports *chēng*. T0232's One-Practice Samādhi, outside the Pure Land corpus, confirms the coupling as a general structural feature. Even at the systematization terminus in Shandao, the coupling persists at the level of the canonical text.

**For the practitioner, concretely.** Western readers—and contemporary Chinese readers reading in English-shaped categories—come to "buddha-recollection" expecting to choose between *mindfulness-type recollection* and *nenbutsu-type name-recitation*. The canonical grammar of the Chinese tradition does not force this binary. It offers a *coupling space*, within which mindful attention and vocal invocation can be adjusted in relative weight: *niàn* primary with *chēng* as support; *chēng* primary with *niàn* as frame; both simultaneously; both alternating. All these are within the same traditional rhema. Contemporary secular mindfulness practice has developed the non-verbal attentional axis extensively; East Asian *nenbutsu* has developed the vocal-invocational axis extensively; this book's position is that the canonical grammar of Chinese buddha-recollection is *coupling*, and that the dominance of any one axis in a given period is a later institutional development, not the intrinsic structure of the foundational texts. Practically, when *niàn* becomes difficult (as in T0365's lowest-grade lower-birth's "suffering pressing upon"), switching to *chēng* is a canonically sanctioned fallback; once *chēng* becomes habitual, deepening into *niàn* is the other direction within the same coupling space.

**Open question.** This paper has mapped the four-step verb-evolution from T0361 through Shandao. The starting point of the argument, however, presupposes T0360's forty-eight-vow system as already-systematized, without treating the system as a whole. What is the architecture of T0360's forty-eight vows? How are the vows organized logically with respect to one another? How has Dharmākara's selection-and-combination operation (treated in P07 §3.3 as a twenty-four-vow structure in T0361) been expanded in T0360—which categories are newly added, which are refined, which are structurally altered? Where does the eighteenth vow sit within the whole? These questions are the material of the next paper, P09—the opening paper of Part IV, *The Three Pure Land Sūtras*. After P09 treats the vow-architecture, P10 will treat the sixteen contemplations of T0365 as architecture (with its system of concentrated-and-dispersed good reserved in this paper for P22), and P11 will treat T0366's depiction of Sukhāvatī and the "upholding the name" (*zhíchí mínghào* 執持名號) passage as the third access-verb family. Reading the three together will give the complete canonical ground of the Pure Land textual corpus.

*Namo tassa bhagavato arahato sammāsambuddhassa.*

Traditionally ascribed to Saṅghavarman of the Cao-Wei; modern scholarship contests the attribution, and the actual date is more likely in the range of the late fourth through early fifth centuries. The paper does not enter the philological debate and relies on the date-range consensus. Vow 17's subject is "the innumerable buddhas throughout world-systems of the ten directions" (praising-and-calling my name); vow 18's subject is "sentient beings of the ten directions, sincerely trustful, wishing to be born, in even as few as ten recollections." Together they form the name-circulation (inter-buddha) + name-reception (sentient being) closed loop.

[^5]: T0360, fascicle 2, lower-grade rebirth passage. "Single-mindedly and whole-heartedly, even ten times recollect Amitāyus Buddha" anchors the "ten recollections" of vow 18 to an object-specific practice of recollecting Amitāyus; the subsequent "even once recollect that Buddha" contracts the count-unit to n = 1. The verb throughout remains *niàn*, not *chēng*—a partial intra-sūtra anchoring of vow 18 that nonetheless keeps verb-openness intact.

[^6]: T0365, fascicle 1, sixteenth contemplation (lower-grade rebirth-imagination), sub-level *lowest-grade upper-birth*. Translated by Kālayaśas, Liu-Song dynasty. This passage is the first-appearance locus of *chēng* as stand-alone access-verb in the Chinese Pure Land canonical tradition. "Call 'Namo Amitābha Buddha'" is followed by "because of calling the Buddha's name, fifty kotis of kalpas of birth-and-death karma are destroyed"; the transformed Buddha's own speech, "because you have called the Buddha's name, your sins are extinguished," reinforces *chēng*'s canonical status.

[^7]: T0365, fascicle 1, sub-level *lowest-grade middle-birth*. Functions as a comparison point: neither *chēng* nor *niàn* appears; the access-mechanism is "hearing the praise-discourse" of Amitābha's ten powers and awesome virtues, and "eighty kotis of kalpas of birth-and-death karma are destroyed." This passage blocks the simplification "lower-grade rebirth always uses *chēng*."

[^8]: T0365, fascicle 1, sub-level *lowest-grade lower-birth*. The structural core of §3.3. The five-step verb-flow within a single passage: instructed to recollect → unable to recollect → advised to call with self-surrender → completing ten recollections while calling the Buddha's name → because of calling the Buddha's name, in recollection-upon-recollection, karma is destroyed. This passage is the most structurally explicit textual evidence for *chēng-niàn* coupling dynamics in the Chinese Pure Land canonical tradition.

[^9]: T1753, fascicle 1, *Xuányì fēn*, in the "preface-topic gate" (*xù tí mén*) and the "classification of the gates of concentrated and dispersed [good]" (*liàojiǎn dìng-sàn liǎng mén*) sections. Authored by Shandao of the Tang (613–681). "Concentrated good is quieting thought to steady the mind; dispersed good is giving up evil to cultivate good" is Shandao's architectural bifurcation of the *Contemplation Sūtra*: contemplations 1–13 as concentrated good, three-fold merit and nine grades as dispersed good. This paper establishes Shandao's doctrinal ground through the passage but does not enter the full articulation of concentrated-and-dispersed good (that is within P22's scope).

[^10]: T1753, fascicle 4, *Sànshàn yì*, "establishing faith through practice" (*jiù xíng lì xìn*), within the explication of the *deep mind* (*shēnxīn*). Shandao's five right practices—recitation, contemplation, prostration, vocal calling, praise-and-offering—and the canonical definition of 正定業 are in this passage. The core passage of §2.5 and §3.4. The definition uses *niàn* as its principal verb: "single-mindedly *holding-in-mind* Amitābha's name… *holding-holding* unceasingly." This is the textual evidence against the popular reading that Shandao "replaced *niàn* with *chēng*." Scope-guard: only the *chēng*-mechanics face of this passage is extracted here; the three-mind system, the two-fold deep faith, and the ordinary-person's entry into the recompense-land are reserved for P22.

[^11]: T0232, fascicle 2, One-Practice Samādhi chapter. Translated by Mandrasena, Liang dynasty. The three-verb chain "tether the mind to one buddha, devotedly call the name, recollection-upon-recollection unbroken" is the cross-sectarian witness—the coupling structure is a general feature of Chinese buddha-recollection verb-grammar, not a Pure Land specificity. The full treatment of One-Practice Samādhi (especially how it simultaneously carries Prajñā doctrinal content and recollection-practice operational content) is the primary material of P19.

[^12]: J. L. Austin, *How to Do Things with Words* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1962); John R. Searle, *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1969). Austin's locutionary / illocutionary / perlocutionary triad is the foundational framework of speech-act theory; Searle's four-condition systematization (propositional content / preparatory / sincerity / essential) further articulates illocutionary acts. Searle's later work, *The Construction of Social Reality* (New York: Free Press, 1995), develops the social-convention grounding of institutional facts; §IV.3 above argues that directly mapping this framework onto *chēng* would lose the vow-ontological grounding of the Chinese Pure Land tradition.

[^13]: T1753, fascicle 4, *Sànshàn yì*, within the explication of *zhìchéng xīn* 至誠心 ("the most-sincere mind")—the first of Shandao's three minds (sincerity, depth, transferring-vow-making). The canonical articulation, "where the sincere mind is absent, the practice done is not true; where it is not true, in the end there is no real content," grounds the sincerity condition of *chēng*. Scope-guard: the full doctrinal articulation of the three minds is the material of P22; here only the sincerity-condition extract is used as textual basis for §4.2's speech-act-theory dialogue.
