## Abstract
This paper takes as its point of departure a philological fact: Guṇabhadra's four-fascicle recension of the *Laṅkāvatāra-sūtra* (T0670) contains not a single instance of *niàn-fó* 念佛, *niàn-rúlái* 念如來, or *guān-fó* 觀佛. The absence is not a coverage gap but a structural invitation. The paper argues in three movements. **First**, the ontological reconstruction of "the hearts of all buddhas first" (*zhū-fó xīn dì-yī* 諸佛心第一, T0670, p.481c01-03): the original interlinear gloss "like the solid heartwood of a tree, not a deliberating mind" (*shùmù jiānshí xīn, fēi niànlǜ xīn* 樹木堅實心，非念慮心) locks the term *xīn* here as *hṛdaya* — the essential heart-substance — rather than as *citta* — deliberating consciousness. The object of buddha-recollection thereby shifts from external name to internal heart-essence. **Second**, the absence of *niàn-fó* language is itself the structural invitation. T0672's *Kṣaṇika-parivarta* welds *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna* into the compound *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*, collecting the object of recollection into "one mind that is the common ground of the defiled and the pure." The *Laṅkāvatāra*'s silence on *niàn-fó* invites its radical interiorization rather than symptomizing a textual omission. **Third**, Daoxin's *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* (*Rù-dào ānxīn yào fāngbiàn fǎmén* 入道安心要方便法門, preserved at T2837, T85, 1286c–1287a) encodes this interiorization in a seventh-century Chan formula: "Relying on the *Laṅkā*'s 'hearts of all buddhas first,' and relying further on the *Mañjuśrī Prajñā Sūtra*'s 'single-practice samādhi' — thus the mind that recollects the Buddha *is* the Buddha" (*jí niàn-fó xīn shì fó* 即念佛心是佛). This shows that the turn from *buddha-recollection* to *mind-recollection* was complete within early Chan before the rise of the Southern School under Huineng. §V's Middle-Way Corrections grounds the warning against collapsing *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna* not in editorial voice but in the Dharmapāla-Xuanzang *Cheng Weishi Lun* (T1585) itself, which explicitly reframes "intrinsically pure mind" as "the suchness disclosed by the emptiness-principle of mind" (*xīn kōng lǐ suǒ xiǎn zhēnrú* 心空理所顯真如); the false-friend warning concerning T0671's "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" (whose content is actually the *manomaya-kāya* doctrine, not *hṛdaya*) is likewise articulated from within the translation-divergence register. The seventh verb-layer — *seeing-the-mind* — completed, P18 (the *Ghanavyūha*) carries it into the eighth layer: buddha-recollection as *ālaya-parāvṛtti*, the transmutation of the storehouse consciousness.

## I. Introduction — Why Did Bodhidharma Take a Sūtra That Never Speaks of Buddha-Recollection as His Mind-Seal?

Anglophone Western readers entering the history of Chinese Chan almost inevitably encounter a legend restated over and over: Bodhidharma came from the West, conferred the four-fascicle *Laṅkāvatāra* on Huike, and said, "**In all the teachings of this land of Chendan, I see only the four fascicles of the *Laṅkā* by which the mind can be sealed**."[^1] This sentence is recorded in the preface Su Shi composed for the *Laṅkāvatāra-sūtra* under the Northern Song; it is subsequently repeated across early Chan compilations — the *Jingde Record of the Transmission of the Lamp*, the *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkā*, and others — and in the standard Western sinological narrative it is taken as the foundational utterance of the "sealing-the-mind by the *Laṅkā*" tradition. Yet if a reader approaches Guṇabhadra's four-fascicle T0670 with the expectation of a *buddha-recollection* teaching, a philological shock awaits:

**The entire T0670 contains not a single occurrence of *niàn-fó* 念佛, *niàn-rúlái* 念如來, *guān-fó* 觀佛, or *xiǎng-fó* 想佛.**[^2]

This is not a sampling bias but the result of a full-text scan. Guṇabhadra's four fascicles, some sixty thousand characters in Chinese, contain only a handful of occurrences of *yìniàn* 憶念 ("calling to mind"), and all are in the sense of "the mind-made body that recollects what was formerly seen," "the objective field recollected in accordance with the original vow," "an earlier field of experience arising along with its recollection," "recollecting within a dream" — that is, the faculty of memory or vow-carried retention in the Yogācāra sense. **None of these occurrences is in the semantic field of the *buddhānusmṛti* that would later ground the Pure Land tradition.** How did a sūtra that never speaks of buddha-recollection come to occupy the foundational position within Chinese Chan? For Western readers who carry the framework of "buddha-recollection as the central praxis of Chinese Buddhism," the question poses a direct hermeneutic difficulty.

The thesis of this paper is that **this "absence of buddha-recollection" is neither accidental nor a philological gap, but a structural feature of the *Laṅkāvatāra* itself, and that it constitutes a structural invitation to the radical interiorization of the buddha-recollection verb.** Bodhidharma's line did not choose this sūtra as their mind-seal because it ignored the recollection tradition; it chose it because, through the *Laṅkā*'s ontological architecture of "hearts of all buddhas first," *tathāgatagarbha*, *ālaya-vijñāna*, and "everything is mind-only," the object of buddha-recollection is radically withdrawn from external name into internal heart-essence — recollection is no longer "the act of invocation that takes the name as its object," but "the act of seeing-the-mind that takes the hearts of all buddhas first, already one's own, as its object." This structure is brought to formulaic completion by Daoxin's mid-seventh-century *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* in the phrase "**the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha**"; the subsequent developments of the Southern School are the further social diffusion of this structure, not its origin.

This thesis bears directly on the contemporary Western phenomenon of McMindfulness as well. The secularizing turn of the modern mindfulness movement — detaching Buddhist *niàn* / *sati* from the objective fields of "recollecting the Buddha," "recollecting the Dharma," "recollecting the Saṅgha," and preserving only the technical face of concentration — has provoked, within the Buddhist traditions, reflection on the fundamental damage done by the de-religionization of recollection. The path displayed by the *Laṅkā* stands in mirror-image to this: Laṅkā's absence of *niàn-fó* language is not the result of de-religionization but **the result of its opposite — the radical locking-in of buddha-recollection at the level of the ontology of its object**. The object is withdrawn from external name into one's own *hṛdaya*-of-all-buddhas. This turn is nowhere written explicitly in the surface of T0670, and yet it is structurally built into the *Laṅkā*'s entire ontological architecture of *tathāgatagarbha*, *ālaya-vijñāna*, own-mind, and mind-only. If Western readers are to understand why the Chinese Chan tradition has strong reservations about contemporary readings of "mindfulness as simple concentration technique," one of the grounds lies in the *Laṅkā*'s foundational insistence that **buddha-recollection must take the hearts of all buddhas as its object**.

The paper advances on three levels. **Level one** (§II): the canonical grounding. Taking T0670 as the primary axis and T0671 and T0672 as the expansion-and-verification anchors, it establishes the textual basis in three-translation comparison — T0670's ontological lock-in of "hearts of all buddhas first" (*hṛdaya*), T0672's *Kṣaṇika-parivarta* lock-in of *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna* in nonduality, and T0671's triune identification of "Tathāgata-mind / *ālaya*-consciousness / *tathāgatagarbha*"; with a false-friend warning regarding T0671's "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" (whose actual content is the *manomaya-kāya* doctrine, not the *hṛdaya* of the buddhas). **Level two** (§III): the core argument. Following the six-layer vertical of the NIAN verb-axis from P11 through P16 — *holding* → *gathering the six faculties* → *Universal Gate name-invocation* → *binding the three periods* → *grounding-in-precepts* → *confession* — this paper establishes the seventh layer: **seeing-the-mind**. The content of this layer is that the object of buddha-recollection is radically withdrawn into the *hṛdaya* of all buddhas already present as one's own; the shape of the act passes from invocation to seeing; and the operational mechanism is given in Daoxin's chain-formula "the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha." **Level three** (§IV): interdisciplinary convergence. Daoxin's *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* (T2837) serves as the §IV historical interlocutor, showing that the turn from buddha-recollection to mind-recollection was complete within early Chan by the mid-seventh century, well before the later inventions of the Southern School.[^3]

§V treats three principal misreadings. **First**, reading "hearts of all buddhas" as *citta* (the deliberating consciousness) rather than *hṛdaya* (solid heart-essence). **Second**, collapsing *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna* into naive identity — the corrective anchor here is the Dharmapāla-Xuanzang *Cheng Weishi Lun* (T1585) and its distancing-move phrase "suchness disclosed by the emptiness-principle of mind," treated as a position internal to the Yogācāra canon rather than an editorial gloss superimposed by the present author. **Third**, the "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" false friend in T0671. §VI closes on three registers — the essentials, guidance for practice, and the open questions pointing toward P18 on the *Ghanavyūha-sūtra* and the theme of **buddha-recollection as *ālaya-parāvṛtti***.

⚠️ **Philological note.** The distinction among the four-fascicle T0670, the ten-fascicle T0671, and the seven-fascicle T0672 is not a contest of translator quality but the Chinese refraction of three distinct layers of the Sanskrit textual tradition. The Sanskrit witness behind T0670 is an early layer without the *Sagāthakam* (the verse-appendix); the witnesses behind T0671 and T0672 both include the *Sagāthakam*, and T0672's chapter-division is the closest to the extant Nanjō 1923 edition.[^4] This paper does not undertake word-by-word collation with the Sanskrit; the analytic layer is the three-Chinese-translation comparison. Readers interested in the Sanskrit textual strata should consult Suzuki's 1932 preface, Takasaki 1982, and Nanjō 1923.

## II. Canonical Grounding — A Five-Anchor Structure Across Three Chinese Recensions

This paper organizes its canonical grounding through a "five-anchor division of labor" across T0670, T0671, and T0672. Three primary anchors carry distinct thetic functions: T0670 at p.481c01-03 is the **ontological lock-in** of *hṛdaya*; T0672's *Kṣaṇika-parivarta* at p.619c01-620a09 is the **nonduality lock-in** of *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*; T0671 at p.556c28-557a06 is the **triune lock-in** of Tathāgata-mind / *ālaya*-consciousness / *tathāgatagarbha*. After these three, two supplementary anchors — T0671's *Sagāthakam* verse "constantly recollecting suchness" as the sole non-mundane use of the recollection-verb within the *Laṅkā*, and T0672's *Chapter on the Constancy and Impermanence of the Tathāgata* verse "thus constantly contemplating the Buddha" as the only "contemplating-the-Buddha" phrase attested in any of the three recensions. Operating together, the five anchors supply a complete canonical foundation.

### 2.1 The Four-Fascicle *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "Hearts of All Buddhas First" — the *Hṛdaya* Ontological Lock-In

Early in fascicle one of T0670, within the *Chapter on the Heart of the Speech of All Buddhas, Part One* (*Yīqiè fó-yǔ xīn pǐn zhī yī* 一切佛語心品之一), Bodhisattva Mahāmati opens by presenting one hundred and eight questions, and the World-Honored One responds with a verse that gathers up the whole: "**Of all the gates of great-vehicle perfections, the hearts of all buddhas stand first**."[^5] This verse appears in all three recensions (T0671, p.520b12-15; T0672, p.592a05-07), but **T0670 preserves, directly beneath the verse, an interlinear gloss that is decisive**:

> "The unsurpassed knower of the world, having heard the verses they spoke: Of all the gates of great-vehicle perfections, the hearts of all buddhas stand first. (*This "heart" is like the solid heartwood of a tree — not the deliberating mind.*)"[^6]

In the CBETA XML, this gloss is marked with the tag `<note place="inline">`, indicating that it belongs to the body of the original sūtra as a small-type interlinear note, not to any later editorial apparatus.[^7] Its structural effect on the thesis of P17 is decisive: **the gloss locks the term *xīn* directly onto *hṛdaya* (the solid heart of the tree, the core heart-essence, the vital core) and explicitly excludes *citta* (deliberating mind, activity of consciousness).** Without the gloss, a Western reader will read "hearts of all buddhas first" as "the deliberative activity of the buddhas is the most excellent" — a thoroughly psychologizing misreading. With the gloss, the "heart" of "hearts of all buddhas first" is immediately displaced from the register of "deliberative activity" into the register of "essential substance": the shared **heart-essence** of all buddhas, their core **substance**.

The philological case for this lock-in can be confirmed directly at the Sanskrit etymological level. The Sanskrit *hṛdaya* means first of all "heart" (as the bodily organ) and then "core," "essence," "pith"; it is regularly used to mark the essential doctrinal pith of a scripture — as, for example, in *Prajñāpāramitā-hṛdaya-sūtra*, "the sūtra of the pith of the perfection of wisdom." *Citta*, by contrast, denotes mental activity, cognition, consciousness, and in Yogācāra serves as a technical term for the stream of mind. The Sanskrit behind T0670's "Chapter on the Heart of the Speech of All Buddhas" is *Sarva-buddha-pravacana-hṛdaya*, and its "heart" unambiguously is *hṛdaya*, not *citta* — as the chapter title of fascicle one, "**Chapter on the Heart of the Speech of All Buddhas**" (*Yīqiè fó-yǔ xīn pǐn*), structurally confirms.[^8] The function of T0670's interlinear gloss here is thus a **philosophical-philological cue to Chinese readers**: a reminder that in this text's context the Chinese word "heart" is *not* to be read as *citta*.

Once the lock-in is established, the structure of the object of buddha-recollection shifts fundamentally. If "the heart of all buddhas first" is read as *citta*, then buddha-recollection becomes "recollecting the deliberative activity of the buddhas" — the object is the stream of mind of another, and the shape of the act must be external invocation. If it is read as *hṛdaya*, then buddha-recollection becomes "recollecting the shared essence of all buddhas" — the object is the core substance of the buddhas, and in the reading of the Bodhidharma-line Chan tradition and its later Yogācāra adopters (see §2.3), this substance is identical, at the level of *hṛdaya*, with the practitioner's own *tathāgatagarbha* — so the shape of the act must be internal seeing-the-mind. **The transition of buddha-recollection from an outward to an inward act is structurally completed in this one-character lock-in on *hṛdaya*.**

### 2.2 The Seven-Fascicle *Laṅkāvatāra*'s *Kṣaṇika-parivarta* — the Nonduality Anchor of *Tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*

Śikṣānanda's seven-fascicle T0672 is the latest of the three recensions and the most editorially rigorous. In the opening prose of the *Kṣaṇika-parivarta* (the *Chapter on the Momentary*, *Chapter Six*), the Blessed One unfolds the relation between the *tathāgatagarbha* and the *ālaya-vijñāna* in the translation choice that most closely fits the Sanskrit compound *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*:

> "The *tathāgatagarbha* is the cause of the wholesome and the unwholesome; it has the capacity to give rise to all destinies of birth.… Enfumed by beginningless false and habitual force, it is named the *ālaya-vijñāna*; it gives birth to the seven consciousnesses' ignorance-ground, like a great ocean that has its waves, its substance continuous and ever-flowing without break; its own nature is pure, free of the faults of impermanence and free from the discourse of self.… In false thought they imagine they have attained liberation, while in reality they have neither discarded nor transmuted **the name of the *ālaya* within the *tathāgatagarbha***. Without the *ālaya*, the seven consciousnesses would be extinguished.… **This *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna* is pure in its own nature and is rendered impure by adventitious defilements.**… For the sake of Queen Śrīmālā and other subtle and profoundly pure bodhisattvas of wisdom, I teach that **the *tathāgatagarbha* is called the *ālaya-vijñāna*** and arises together with the seven consciousnesses.… This *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna* is the objective field of the buddhas.… Therefore you and the great bodhisattvas must diligently contemplate **the *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna***." (translation mine)[^9]

This passage articulates the relation of *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna* on three clearly layered registers: compound naming (**tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna**), explicit identification (**"the name of the *ālaya* within the *tathāgatagarbha*,"** **"the *tathāgatagarbha* is called the *ālaya-vijñāna*"**), and functional dependence (**"without the *ālaya*, the seven consciousnesses would be extinguished"**). The parallel passage in T0670's fascicle four collapses these into a single compressed formula, "the store of the Tathāgata is called the consciousness-store" (p.510b27, with Ming and Palace witnesses reading *ālaya-consciousness*); the parallel in T0671's fascicle seven reverses the word order as "the *ālaya-vijñāna* is named the *tathāgatagarbha*" (p.556b29). **Of the three Chinese recensions, only T0672's compound rendering preserves the full four-member compound structure of the Sanskrit *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*.**[^10]

The structural effect of this compound naming is direct: **once the object of buddha-recollection is withdrawn into "the hearts of all buddhas" (*hṛdaya*), this "heart" is then further identified with the *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*** — the one mind that is the common ground of the defiled and the pure. This one mind holds a double-axis structure of "pure in its own nature, rendered impure by adventitious defilements"; its pure face is the *tathāgatagarbha*, its flow-face is the *ālaya*; the two are not two substances but two faces of a single substance. Within this structure, the object of buddha-recollection is neither a purely pure other nor a purely flowing self, but **the practitioner is already situated within this defiled-and-pure one mind** — to recollect the Buddha is to see this one mind; to see this one mind is to recollect the shared essence of all buddhas.

One philological detail must be added here. The prose in T0672's *Kṣaṇika-parivarta* is immediately followed by a block of verses (p.620a11-16), beginning with the quatrain "The profound *tathāgatagarbha* / Arises together with the seven consciousnesses / When two dharmas are grasped, arising occurs / When known as they truly are, non-arising is seen." These four lines are citable here. However, the verse-couplet at p.620a15-16 ("like a fool who sees the finger pointing at the moon") and the "artisan troupe" verse at p.620a17-18 belong to **material reserved for the sister-series S5-P02 of the Eight-Part Cycle**, which this paper strictly avoids.[^11] The *Laṅkā*'s finger-pointing-at-the-moon trope appears in all three recensions (T0670, fascicle four, p.510c17; T0671, fascicle seven, p.557a20-21; T0672, fascicle five, p.620a15-16) and serves as one of the sources of this book-series' "pointing-at-the-moon" title. This paper cites only the T0671 passage (p.557a20-21) — "like a fool who sees the finger pointing at the moon, / who contemplates the finger and not the moon; / who clings to names and words / does not see my truth" (translation mine) — as a book-series self-reference; the parallel passages in T0670 and T0672 are likewise avoided.

### 2.3 The Ten-Fascicle *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "Tathāgata-Mind / Ālaya-Consciousness / Tathāgatagarbha" — the Triune Thetic Anchor

Bodhiruci's ten-fascicle T0671, in the *Chapter on Buddha-Nature, Chapter Eleven* (*Fó-xìng pǐn dì shíyī* 佛性品第十一), provides a three-term juxtaposition absent from T0672 and not given in this word-order by T0670:

> "Mahāmati, if the *tathāgatagarbha* and the *ālaya-vijñāna* were said to be non-existent, then apart from the *ālaya-vijñāna* there would be neither arising nor ceasing.… Relying on the *ālaya-vijñāna*, practitioners enter the objective field of the sages realized within their own inner body, a present dharma of joy-conducting action that never rests. Mahāmati, **this realm of the Tathāgata-mind, the *ālaya-vijñāna*, and the *tathāgatagarbha*** is not something the śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and other non-Buddhists can discriminate. Why? Because the *tathāgatagarbha* is the mark of purity, while adventitious defilements are the mark of impurity." (translation mine)[^12]

The thetic weight of this passage is considerable: **"Tathāgata-mind / *ālaya-vijñāna* / *tathāgatagarbha*"** are laid side-by-side as a single referent. T0670's parallel passage at fascicle four (p.510b16-c11) identifies only the last two ("the store of the Tathāgata is called the consciousness-store") and does not add "Tathāgata-mind" to the list; T0672's *Kṣaṇika-parivarta* parallel (p.619c01-620a09) handles the relation through the compound "tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna" without displaying the term "Tathāgata-mind." **Only T0671, at this point, adds "Tathāgata-mind" (*tathāgata-citta* or *tathāgata-hṛdaya*) into the juxtaposition** — so that the *xīn* of "the hearts of all buddhas first" (locked in §2.1 as *hṛdaya*) is paralleled with the *ālaya-vijñāna* and the *tathāgatagarbha* as a single reference.

The function of this triune identification in the thesis of P17 is as follows. **The object of buddha-recollection (hearts of all buddhas / *hṛdaya*) = the *ālaya* = the *tathāgatagarbha*** — not three substances but three names of one substance. Within this structure, to recollect the Buddha is to recollect the *ālaya*; to recollect the *ālaya* is to recollect the *tathāgatagarbha*; to recollect the *tathāgatagarbha* is to recollect the shared essence of all buddhas. And because the *ālaya* and the *tathāgatagarbha* are already the practitioner's own root-mind in their mental continuum (and this root-mind is, at the level of *hṛdaya*, of one substance with the root-mind of all buddhas), **to recollect the root-mind of all buddhas is to recollect one's own root-mind** — which is precisely the canonical scriptural ground of Daoxin's formula, discussed in §IV: "**to recollect the Buddha is to recollect the mind; to seek the mind is to seek the Buddha**."

This triune identification is confirmed in the *Sagāthakam* (the verse-appendix absent from T0670 and unique to T0671 and T0672). The verse from T0671's fascicle nine — "**The dharmas seen in one's own mind / Have no external dharma outside / Seeing delusion in this way / One constantly recollects suchness**"[^13] — is the *only* place in the entire *Laṅkā* that directs *yìniàn* ("calling to mind") explicitly toward **suchness** — not to a buddha-body, not to a name, not to the marks of form, but to the **suchness seen after the resolution of delusion**. This verse is the most direct textual support for the §III reconstruction of "buddha-recollection = mind-recollection → suchness-recollection," and it fully responds to §2.2's compound *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna* structure — suchness is, ontologically, the pure face of the *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*, the node-emergence of the *hṛdaya* of all buddhas within the practitioner.

And at the close of the *Sagāthakam* (fascicle ten), the verse "**The mind's own nature is pure; the pure Dharma-body of the Tathāgata**"[^14] answers, at both ends of the text, the opening chapter's threefold equation — "**What is quiescent is called one mind; one mind is called the *tathāgatagarbha***"[^15] — so that T0671 singly, in three plain equations (quiescence = one-mind / one-mind = *tathāgatagarbha* / mind's-own-nature-pure = Tathāgata's-pure-Dharma-body), locks the whole sūtra's ontological thesis as "**own-mind = *tathāgatagarbha* = Dharma-body**." Within this structure, P17's turn from "buddha-recollection to mind-recollection" is not a hermeneutic overlay imposed by Bodhidharma or Daoxin; it is the reading invited by the *Laṅkā*'s own text.

### 2.4 The Seven-Fascicle *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "Thus Constantly Contemplating the Buddha" — the Only "Contemplating-the-Buddha" Locution in the Three Recensions

Across the three translations in their entirety, *niàn-fó*, *niàn-rúlái*, and *niàn-zhū-fó* are zero-hit; *yìniàn* appears in one non-mundane occurrence at T0671's *Sagāthakam* ("constantly recollecting suchness"), already flagged at §2.3. There is, however, one point in T0672's *Chapter on the Constancy and Impermanence of the Tathāgata, Chapter Five* (*Rúlái cháng wú-cháng pǐn dì wǔ* 如來常無常品第五), at p.619b19-24, where a verse contains the only use of **"contemplating-the-Buddha"** (*guān-fó* 觀佛) as a direct locution anywhere in the three recensions:

> "Apart from constancy and impermanence / yet manifesting constancy and impermanence: / **thus constantly contemplating the Buddha**, / no evil view will arise. / If there were constancy and impermanence, / the accumulation of both would be without benefit; / to remove the cognition of discrimination, / neither constancy nor impermanence is taught.… / **If one sees that it is only one's own mind**, / then no opposition or dispute arises." (translation mine)[^16]

The structural weight of the verse is this: the *Laṅkā* contains, in this one place, a vocabulary that explicitly takes "Buddha" as the target of practice as its object — and yet, within the very same verse, it completes the inward turn of that object with "**if one sees that it is only one's own mind**" (*svacitta-mātra-dṛṣṭi*). Contemplating the Buddha is not seeing an external buddha-body; it is not holding an external name; it is **seeing only one's own mind**. T0670's parallel passage in fascicle three reads "thus contemplating the Tathāgata, no evil view arises" (p.498a, inclining toward the abstract Dharma-body sense); T0671's parallel passage in fascicle six reads "thus always seeing the Buddha, no inverted view arises" (p.548a, inclining toward the sensory perception of mark-and-form). **Only T0672, with "thus constantly contemplating the Buddha," poises exactly between the two** — neither the purely abstract "contemplating the Tathāgata" of T0670 nor the purely sensory "seeing the Buddha" of T0671, but the precise language in which *guān* (a compound of śamatha-vipaśyanā, not mere visual seeing) and "the Buddha" (a concrete buddha-figure) operate in tandem.

The function of this verse in §III's "buddha-recollection ⇌ mind-contemplation nonduality" reading is that **the *Laṅkā* itself provides a direct demonstrative transition from "contemplating the Buddha" to "seeing only one's own mind"** — the act of contemplating the Buddha and the act of seeing only one's own mind are set in parallel, as two faces of one practice, within a single verse. Buddha-recollection (as an extended form of contemplating the Buddha) and mind-recollection (as an extended form of seeing only one's own mind) are, in the *Laṅkā*'s unique "contemplating-the-Buddha" locution, built into a single act.

### 2.5 The Ten-Fascicle *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" — A False-Friend Warning

Fascicle two of T0671 contains a chapter titled **"Buddha-Mind, Chapter Four"** (*Fó-xīn pǐn dì sì* 佛心品第四). The title poses an immediate surface temptation to the reader familiar with the "hearts of all buddhas first" theme: without careful inspection, the reader will automatically read the chapter as "a dedicated exposition of the doctrine of the *hṛdaya*-of-all-buddhas in its positive form." Yet close reading of the chapter's actual content shows that **the "buddha-mind" of "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" refers to the *manomaya-kāya* (the mind-made body) doctrine, not to the "hearts of all buddhas" *hṛdaya*.**[^17]

*Manomaya-kāya* is a technical term within the Laṅkā's teaching system: it refers to the special body that bodhisattvas manifest through the mastery of samādhi — its formative mechanism is "produced by mind" (the self-mastering manifestation of the stream of consciousness), hence the name "mind-made." This body is neither the biological body born of parents nor the pure substantial Dharma-body; it is the operable body generated, within advanced cultivation, through the force of intention. T0671's "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" unfolds the three-mode teaching of this body — the *manomaya-kāya* of samādhi-joy-right-reception, the *manomaya-kāya* of the awakening-to-the-self-nature of dharmas, and the *manomaya-kāya* of the unwrought accompaniment-by-kind — and has no direct bearing on the "hearts of all buddhas" (*hṛdaya*) theme. The "mind" in "mind-made body" is *manas* ("thought") within the *citta*-family of concepts; it is categorically distinct from *hṛdaya*.

The structural risk of this **similar-in-form-divergent-in-meaning** feature, if not flagged in §II, is that a reader drawing on T0671 to support the "hearts of all buddhas first" thesis of P17 would be drawn into category error — mistaking "the mind-mastered manifestation of a body" for "the shared heart-essence of all buddhas." The T0671 passage that actually corresponds to T0670's p.481c01-03 ("hearts of all buddhas first") is located at **fascicle two, p.520b12-15** (within the *Chapter on Questions and Answers, Chapter Two*, in the transitional verse after Mahāmati's one hundred and eight questions — already cited in footnote to §2.3), **not in "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four"** (fascicle five). This philological placement must be explicit; otherwise P17's citation chain risks being misread.

The five anchors, taken together, establish the canonical grounding of P17 — T0670's *hṛdaya* lock-in, T0672's nonduality compound, T0671's triune identification, the *Sagāthakam*'s "constantly recollecting suchness" verse, and T0672's "thus constantly contemplating the Buddha" verse — together with the "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" false-friend warning that protects against misreading. The paper now proceeds to the core argument.

## III. Core Argument — The Seventh Layer of the Verb-Axis: Seeing-the-Mind

This paper establishes, as the seventh layer, **"seeing-the-mind"** (*jiàn-xīn* 見心), building on the six-layer progression of the NIAN verb-axis from P11 to P16. The six prior layers are, in order: **P11 holding-the-name** (resolute severance paired with steady preservation) → **P12 gathering the six faculties** (inward spatial integration) → **P13 Universal Gate name-invocation** (outward three-karmic unfolding) → **P14 binding the three periods** (time-segment structuring) → **P15 grounding-in-precepts** (precept-boundary action-system) → **P16 confession** (event-chain reception of the distributed subject).[^18] The first six layers treat "the unfolding of the buddha-recollection act across distinct structural dimensions" — from the verb itself (P11) through space (P12), the three karmas (P13), time (P14), act-boundary (P15), and subject-distribution (P16), they progressively unfold the outward reach of the recollection verb across six registers. P17's "seeing-the-mind" enters the **ontological dimension of the object** of that verb — the object is withdrawn from external name and external form into one's own *hṛdaya* of all buddhas; the shape of the act turns from invocation, holding, and confession into **seeing** (direct illumination in the conjoint operation of śamatha and vipaśyanā).

The argument advances in three stages. **§3.1** unfolds the ontological reconstruction of "the hearts of all buddhas" (*hṛdaya* ≠ *citta*). **§3.2** argues that the *Laṅkā*'s "absence of buddha-recollection" constitutes a structural invitation to the radical interiorization of that verb. **§3.3** takes Daoxin's seventh-century chain-formula in T2837 as the canonical culmination of that turn.

### 3.1 The Ontological Reconstruction of the Hearts of All Buddhas — From Deliberating Mind to Core Heart-Substance

§2.1 established that T0670's interlinear gloss at p.481c01-03 — "like the solid heartwood of a tree, not a deliberating mind" — locks "the hearts of all buddhas" onto *hṛdaya*. This section unfolds the ontological consequences of that lock-in: how the object-dimension of the buddha-recollection verb moves from the *citta* category into the *hṛdaya* category, and how that move in turn reorganizes the shape of the recollection act as a whole.

*Citta* and *hṛdaya*, though both renderable in Chinese as *xīn*, occupy different categories within Buddhist ontology. *Citta* refers to the immediate presentness of mental activity — moment-to-moment continuity, momentary arising and ceasing, the conditioned arising in response to an objective field, and, together with *manas* and *vijñāna*, the core components of the Yogācāra structure of the "eight modes of mind, thought, and consciousness."[^19] Within Yogācāra, *citta* is further operationalized by its functional definition as "aggregation-and-arising" — the seed-aggregation, the arising of defiled dharmas, the dynamic field in which present activity perfumes seeds. The marks of *citta* are **dynamism, immediacy, objectivity** — a given *citta* must be a *citta* of some object (the conditioning-object as *ālambana-pratyaya*), must be the *citta* of some present moment.

*Hṛdaya*, by contrast, refers to the core heart-essence of a substance — non-momentary, non-active, non-objective. Its Sanskrit root sense is "heart" as the bodily organ, extended to "core," "essence," "root," "pith." In Buddhist literature, *hṛdaya* regularly marks "the core passage of a teaching" (as in *Prajñāpāramitā-hṛdaya*, "the pith of the perfection of wisdom"); in ontological contexts, *hṛdaya* names the core substance of a given ontological level (as "the hearts of all buddhas," *sarva-buddha-hṛdaya*, means "the shared core substance of all buddhas"). The marks of *hṛdaya* are **stasis, substantiality, the sub-substrate of objective activity** — not an event at some moment, but a cross-momentary ontological structure; not an activity directed at some object, but the substrate of activity directed at an object.

This differentiation has far-reaching structural consequences for the buddha-recollection verb. If "the hearts of all buddhas first" is read with *xīn* as *citta*, then:

- the **object** is the deliberative activity of the buddhas — the stream of mind of another;
- the **act** is the pursuit of the buddhas' deliberations by the practitioner's own — a mental act objectified into subject-and-object;
- the **temporal structure** is moment-to-moment — moment succeeded by moment, recollection lost at each succession;
- the **ontological state** is the parallel co-existence of the practitioner and the buddhas' stream — the pursuit between two independent streams.

This reading necessarily leads to two pathologies. **First**, subject-object-objectifying recollection — the very condition that Guifeng Zongmi warned of in P14 under the verdict "thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind"[^20] — where buddha-recollection becomes an eternally-failing act of one ordinary stream pursuing a buddha's stream. **Second**, the structural separation of buddha-recollection from the practitioner's own mind — buddha-recollection recollects "another" (the buddhas), while one's own mind is the subject of that recollection, and the two never cohere.

If "the hearts of all buddhas first" is read with *xīn* as *hṛdaya*, then:

- the **object** is the shared core substance of the buddhas — not some buddha's mental activity at some moment, but the heart-essence common to all buddhas;
- the **act** is the connection of the practitioner's own core substance to the buddhas' core substance — a **same-substance linkage** of substance to substance, not an objectifying pursuit of one mental activity by another;
- the **temporal structure** is non-momentary — the *hṛdaya*-of-all-buddhas, as ontological substrate, endures across the coming and going of moments; the practitioner's every act of linkage, at this very node of the present, participates in that enduring substrate;
- the **ontological state** is that the practitioner's own *hṛdaya* and the buddhas' *hṛdaya* are of one substance — under §2.3's triune identification (Tathāgata-mind / *ālaya-vijñāna* / *tathāgatagarbha* as co-referential), the practitioner's *tathāgatagarbha* is the buddhas' *tathāgatagarbha*, the practitioner's *hṛdaya* is the buddhas' *hṛdaya*.

The structure that follows is this: buddha-recollection **is** seeing-the-mind — to see one's own *hṛdaya* is to see the buddhas' *hṛdaya*; the act of buddha-recollection is completed at this very moment of seeing, with no need for an outward invocational interface.

A thought-experiment makes the radicalism of this shift explicit. Under the *citta* reading, if the practitioner ceases invocation, the buddha-recollection act terminates — the act's shape is invocation, and without invocation there is no act. Under the *hṛdaya* reading, if the practitioner ceases invocation but seeing-the-mind persists, **the buddha-recollection act does not terminate** — the act's shape is seeing-the-mind, and as long as seeing-the-mind persists, the act persists. This is why the Bodhidharma-line Chan slogans "do not set up words," "directly pointing at the human mind," and "seeing the nature and attaining buddhahood" do not stand in opposition to the buddha-recollection tradition — they are, on the contrary, **direct corollaries of the lock-in of the object of buddha-recollection onto *hṛdaya***. Bodhidharma did not oppose buddha-recollection; he **radically locked buddha-recollection onto the *hṛdaya* object**. Once that lock-in is complete, buddha-recollection and seeing-the-mind become inseparable and non-opposable.

This structure finds its clearest expression in Daoxin's symmetric formula cited in §IV: "**to recollect the Buddha is to recollect the mind; to seek the mind is to seek the Buddha**." The buddha-recollection act and the mind-recollection act are two faces of one act at the level of *hṛdaya*-object ontology.

### 3.2 Absence of Buddha-Recollection as Structural Invitation — A Rhetoric of *Laṅkā*'s Silence

§I established, as philological fact, that T0670 contains no direct occurrence of *niàn-fó* / *niàn-rúlái* / *guān-fó* / *xiǎng-fó*. This section develops the structural significance of this **absence** for the thesis of P17: the absence is not a coverage failure but a built-in mechanism of the *Laṅkā*'s ontological reorganization of the buddha-recollection verb.

Consider a counterfactual: if T0670, immediately after the "hearts of all buddhas first" passage, had deployed concrete directions for "therefore recollect the buddhas, hold the buddhas' names, never interrupt moment by moment, and attain rebirth in the buddha-lands," the structural space for the Bodhidharma-line reading of "mind-sealing" would have been foreclosed. The *Laṅkā* would have been read as a Pure Land sūtra, and Bodhidharma's "mind-seal" tradition as a particular form of Pure Land buddha-recollection. What T0670 actually unfolds after "hearts of all buddhas first" is **entirely unconnected to invocation or the contemplation of form**: what unfolds is the *tathāgatagarbha*, the *ālaya*, the "objective fields manifest from one's own mind," "mind-only," "empty and without self," "neither arising nor ceasing," "dharma beyond letters" — **pure ontological and epistemological construction**. The absence of the recollection verb from this unfolding is not authorial oversight; it is the author's deliberate structural choice — **a demand that the reader settle the object-dimension of the recollection verb on the *hṛdaya* at which this ontological construction points**, rather than fall back on the inherited framework of outward invocation.

The Bodhidharma tradition's reception history gives structural confirmation of this choice. Bodhidharma's *Treatise on Two Entries and Four Practices* (*Èr rù sì xíng lùn* 二入四行論) opens with "**the principle-entry, namely, attaching to the teaching and awakening to the source**" (*jiè-jiào wù-zōng* 藉教悟宗) — where "**teaching**" is the doctrine of the hearts of all buddhas carried in the four fascicles of the *Laṅkā*, and "**source**" is the true nature realized within the practitioner's own mind.[^21] Bodhidharma does not unfold explicit directions for invocational buddha-recollection within this treatise — not because invocation is disregarded, but because the *Laṅkā* on which he relies already takes as given that "the object of buddha-recollection has been locked onto *hṛdaya*"; under that premise, the shape of the act automatically becomes "**to see one's own *hṛdaya* = to see the buddhas' *hṛdaya***" — no further outward-invocation layer is required. What Bodhidharma calls "attaching to the teaching and awakening to the source" is precisely the relation of "teaching" (the *Laṅkā*'s ontological unfolding) to "source" (the practitioner's substantive linkage of mind): one direct correspondence.

In this structure, the *Laṅkā*'s absence of buddha-recollection carries three rhetorical functions.

**First, it forecloses the distraction of outward invocation.** Had T0670 simultaneously unfolded its ontological construction and its invocational directions, the reader would have confronted a choice among practice paths — is invocation primary with ontology as ground, or is ontology primary with invocation as auxiliary? This choice would have fragmented the unity of the practitioner's act. The *Laṅkā*'s excision of the outward-invocation layer forecloses the choice — **the practice path is one**: direct ontological linkage to *hṛdaya*.

**Second, it prevents the externalization of the object.** Outward invocation as an act-shape carries a built-in structural risk: it places the object (the buddhas, the buddha's name) outside the practitioner's mind; even when "own-mind is the buddhas' mind" has been ontologically established, the outwardness of invocation may re-externalize the object by the outwardness of the act itself. The *Laṅkā*'s excision of the outward-invocation layer structurally prevents that re-externalization — **the act-shape is already inward** (seeing, contemplating, awareness, realization), and the object has no opportunity to be externalized.

**Third, it invites the reader to complete the inward turn of the object on her own.** The *Laṅkā* does not explicitly say "the hearts of all buddhas is one's own mind"; it does not explicitly say "buddha-recollection is mind-recollection"; it only locks "the hearts of all buddhas" onto *hṛdaya*, locks "own-mind" onto "the pure face of the *ālaya*, which is the *tathāgatagarbha*," and locks "the *tathāgatagarbha*" onto "the *tathāgatagarbha* present within the bodies of all sentient beings." If the reader follows the line through these three lock-ins, she arrives of her own accord at the triune identification "*hṛdaya*-of-all-buddhas = own-*tathāgatagarbha*-of-all-sentient-beings = the pure face of the *ālaya*," and from there at the symmetric identity "to recollect the buddhas' heart is to recollect one's own mind; to recollect one's own mind is to recollect the buddhas' heart." The rhetorical choice of **leaving the structural conclusion to be completed by the reader** ensures that the act of reading the *Laṅkā* is itself an exercise in "the inward turn from buddha-recollection to mind-recollection" — **reading the *Laṅkā* is performing the *Laṅkā*'s interiorization of buddha-recollection**.

This rhetorical structure is confirmed further by the "dharma beyond letters" reading of the Bodhidharma-line Chan. T0670's fascicle four passage — "**The Tathāgata does not utter a dharma that falls into letters**… **bodhisattva mahāsattvas rely on the meaning, not on the letter**"[^22] — makes explicit the *Laṅkā*'s own vigilance against taking letters as the ultimate object; and the *Laṅkā*'s absence of buddha-recollection is the precise instantiation of that "dharma beyond letters" spirit at the level of the recollection verb — **the substance of buddha-recollection is not the letter "buddha-recollection" but the seeing of the practitioner's own *hṛdaya***. The letter ("buddha-recollection") as signpost is hidden, and the reader is forced to confront the ontological referent (the *hṛdaya*-of-all-buddhas = own-*hṛdaya*). This is precisely the "pointing-at-the-moon" structure of the *Laṅkā* at the level of the buddha-recollection verb: **what the sūtra does not say is "buddha-recollection," in order that the reader may see the substance to which "buddha-recollection" points**.

### 3.3 Daoxin's Chain-Formula — "The Very Mind That Recollects the Buddha Is the Buddha"

§3.1 established the structural consequences of the *hṛdaya* lock-in; §3.2 unfolded the rhetorical structure of the *Laṅkā*'s invitation through its absence of buddha-recollection. If the analysis rested here, it would remain a hermeneutic reconstruction by the present author. To establish the canonical culmination of P17's thesis, the paper must locate a **self-articulation** within the early Chan tradition of the formula completing "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection." In the literature of Bodhidharma, Huike, Sengcan, Daoxin, Hongren, and Shenxiu, is there a passage that formalizes that turn?

The answer is found within fascicle one of Jìngjué's (c. 683–750) *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkā* (T2837), in the section under "Fifth Generation: Daoxin," which preserves a fragment of Daoxin's *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind*:

> "Meditation Master Xin [Daoxin] opened the gates of meditation anew, and his teaching spread throughout the realm. He had one scroll on bodhisattva precept-dharma, and composed the *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* for those of ripe capacity and karmic connection. Of my teaching he said: **'It relies on the *Laṅkāvatāra-sūtra*, "the hearts of all buddhas first"; and relies further on the *Mañjuśrī's Teaching of Prajñā Sūtra*, "the single-practice samādhi" — thereby the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha**. Deluded thought is the ordinary being.'… The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* says, 'No-thought is called recollecting the Buddha.' What is 'no-thought'? **This very mind that recollects the Buddha is no-thought. Apart from the mind there is no other Buddha; apart from the Buddha there is no other mind. To recollect the Buddha is to recollect the mind; to seek the mind is to seek the Buddha.** Why? Consciousness has no shape; the Buddha has no shape, the Buddha has no bodily appearance. If one knows this principle, this is the pacified mind. **Constantly calling the Buddha to mind, while clinging does not arise, will dissolve into formlessness.**" (translation mine)[^23]

This passage is the most important canonical citation of the entire paper. Its weight must be analyzed across three registers.

**First, the historical register.** Daoxin (580–651), founder of the East Mountain Teaching, lived and died within the first half of the seventh century; Jìngjué's *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkā* was compiled at the start of the Kaiyuan era (713–714 CE), and the Daoxin fragment there is the earliest extant layer of this text in transmission.[^24] The historical significance is this: **the formalization of the "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection" turn was already complete within the early Chan tradition by the mid-seventh century**, roughly **one hundred years before the articulation of the Southern School's position in Huineng's *Platform Sūtra* (mid-eighth century)**. This dating contradicts the standard Western sinological narrative ("Chan–Pure Land opposition — the Chan tradition rejecting buddha-recollection — only after the Southern School's rise is buddha-recollection reintegrated") directly: **the "buddha-recollection is mind-recollection" formula was, already by the middle of the seventh century, one of the core theses of Chan dharma within the tradition itself**. The later developments of the Southern School are the further social diffusion of this formula, not its origin.

**Second, the textual-structural register.** In extremely compressed form, Daoxin's passage completes a five-link chain:

1. **Relying on the *Laṅkā*, "hearts of all buddhas first"** — the ontological lock-in of the object: hearts of all buddhas as *hṛdaya*.
2. **Relying on the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra*, "single-practice samādhi"** — the establishment of the method: *yī-xíng sānmèi* as the single-object concentration-method.[^25]
3. **The very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha** — the same-substance identity of subject and object: the subject of buddha-recollection — the mind that recollects the Buddha — *is* the Buddha who is recollected.
4. **To recollect the Buddha is to recollect the mind; to seek the mind is to seek the Buddha** — the symmetric identity of the acts: buddha-recollection and mind-recollection mirror each other.
5. **Constantly calling the Buddha to mind, while clinging does not arise, will dissolve into formlessness** — the shape of practice: the twin operation of sustained calling-to-mind and the non-arising of clinging.

The structural effect of this five-link chain is that **buddha-recollection, as a complete shape of practice, is formally integrated across five sentences in this one passage**. Object (link 1), method (link 2), subject-object identity (link 3), act-symmetry (link 4), and practice-shape (link 5) — each layer directly answers a thetic requirement of §3.1 or §3.2: the object is *hṛdaya* (not *citta*), the method is seeing-the-mind (not outward invocation), the subject is the object (not subject-object objectification), the acts are symmetric (buddha-recollection = mind-recollection), and the shape is "sustained recollection + non-arising of clinging" (sustained action together with formless substrate).

**Third, the canonical-composition register.** Daoxin's passage is not a creative new thesis; he declares his sources explicitly as **two sūtras** — "it relies on the *Laṅkā*" and "relies further on the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra*." The division of labor between the two is:

- **The *Laṅkāvatāra* supplies the ontology of the object** — hearts of all buddhas first (*hṛdaya*), and its triune identity with the practitioner's own *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya* (§2.3).
- **The *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra* supplies the method of practice** — the single-practice samādhi (*yī-xíng sānmèi*), a single-object concentration method whose concrete operation is "invoking a single buddha by name."[^26]

In combination, the concrete object of the method's operation (the buddha named) is settled on the ontological substance (the *hṛdaya*-of-all-buddhas = own-*tathāgatagarbha*) — **the act of invocation is not an outward act directed at an external other; it is an inward act directed at the ontological substance of one's own *tathāgatagarbha***. The structural significance of this combination is that **outward invocation and inward seeing-the-mind are not two distinct paths but two faces of one path** — once the object is locked onto *hṛdaya*, the outward invocation automatically becomes an inward seeing-the-mind; conversely, once the method is settled on the single-practice samādhi, the inward seeing-the-mind automatically takes outward invocation as one of its shapes.

Daoxin's passage thus functions as the **canonical culmination** of P17's thesis: his formula "the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha" is the complete canonical discharge of the arc "absence of buddha-recollection as structural invitation → invitation completed in the interiorization of buddha-recollection." §3.1's ontological reconstruction (*hṛdaya* ≠ *citta*), §3.2's rhetorical invitation (absence of buddha-recollection), and §3.3's Daoxin chain-formula combine to establish the complete canonical ground for the seventh layer of the verb-axis: seeing-the-mind.

§IV now takes Daoxin's *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* as the interdisciplinary interlocutor, unfolding its doctrinal weight as the historical node of completion for the Chan tradition's inward turn — why the interiorization was completed in the mid-seventh century, how it relates to the later developments of the Southern School, and how its shape continues in contemporary Chinese-Buddhist practice.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence — Daoxin's *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* as the Seventh-Century Historical Node of the Chan Tradition's Inward Turn

P13 (the *Lotus*) took the three-karmic structure as its §IV interlocutor; P14 (the *Perfect Enlightenment*) took Guifeng Zongmi; P15 (the *Brahma Net*) took Fazang of the Huayan tradition; P16 (the *Golden Light*) took Zhiyi-Zhili of the Tiantai repentance liturgy. The commentator-and-liturgist axis of Part V enters a new register at P17: **§IV no longer takes an individual commentator as its interlocutor; it takes an early Chan doctrinal text** — Daoxin's *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind*. The significance of this choice is that the completion of the *Laṅkā*'s interiorization of buddha-recollection within Chinese Buddhist history does not occur at the commentarial layer (such as Fazang's *Profound Meaning of the Laṅkā*, T1790, or any other later commentary); it occurs within the **early Chan tradition's own interior-to-practice literature**. Daoxin is not an exegete of the *Laṅkā*; he is the *Laṅkā*'s practitioner — his *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* is not a commentary on the *Laṅkā* but the **direct operational landing** of the *Laṅkā*'s ontological structure within the early Chan tradition's practice.

### 4.1 Daoxin and the Historical Location of the East Mountain Teaching

Daoxin was born in 580 CE (the twelfth year of the Taijian era of the Chen Dynasty) and died in 651 CE (the second year of the Yonghui era of Tang Gaozong); he was from Shuangfeng Mountain in Huangmei, Qizhou (modern Hubei). His position within the Chan lineage is the fourth successor to Bodhidharma (Bodhidharma → Huike → Sengcan → Daoxin), and together with his disciple Hongren (601–674) he established the doctrinal system of the "**East Mountain Teaching**."[^27] The East Mountain Teaching was the first mature Chan school in Chinese history: from the Daoxin-Hongren era (first to second halves of the seventh century), centered at Shuangfeng and Huangmei, it drew large numbers of students; the mark of its dharma was the **dual-axis structure of "sealing the mind by the *Laṅkā* + practicing through the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā* single-practice samādhi,"** which is the concrete landing of Daoxin's explicit formula cited in §3.3.

The structural significance of the East Mountain Teaching in Chan history is that **it is the key node at which Bodhidharma's Chan passes from a form of itinerant ascetic practice into a form of settled monastic community**. Bodhidharma, Huike, and Sengcan — the three generations of itinerant practitioners — centered their dharma on "wall-gazing," "attaching to the teaching and awakening to the source," and "two-entries-and-four-practices," with the practical layer largely a matter of individual deep-meditation practice; Daoxin, by contrast, settled at Shuangfeng, gathered disciples, and taught, converting Bodhidharma's Chan from an individual itinerant practice into a group-settled system. The structural consequence of this settlement is that the Chan dharma had to be **systematized, formulated, and made teachable** — no longer merely an individual's experiential state, but something articulable in words and operable collectively. Daoxin's *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* is the direct product of that systematization need: the document is written with the stated aim "**speaking of my teaching**" "**for those of ripe capacity and karmic connection**," making explicit the core structure of the Chan dharma to students.

The *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* does not survive as an independent work; what survives is its fragment within fascicle one of Jìngjué's *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkā* (T2837), under the Daoxin section. Jìngjué was a disciple in the line of Shenxiu (Daoxin–Hongren–Shenxiu); his *Record* was compiled at the beginning of the Kaiyuan era (713–714 CE) as the East Mountain Teaching's official self-historiography, preserving the core writings and transmission narratives of the three generations of Daoxin, Hongren, and Shenxiu.[^28] Jìngjué is therefore a direct heir of the Northern School / East Mountain Teaching; his records of Daoxin are the earliest extant layer of those texts. The text is a **doctrinal source** (direct quotation of Daoxin's dharma-teaching), not a **narrative filter** (later retelling or reconstruction of Daoxin).

### 4.2 The Five-Link Chain — from the *Laṅkā* to the *Mañjuśrī-Prajñā* to Mind-Recollection

§3.3 cited Daoxin's core formula and analyzed its function at the historical-dating, textual-structural, and canonical-composition registers. This section unfolds further the **internal structural logic** of the five-link chain.

**Link one: "relying on the *Laṅkāvatāra*, 'hearts of all buddhas first.'"** Structural function: establishing the ontological category of the object. Daoxin grounds the foundation on no other passage from the *Laṅkā* — not "everything is mind-only," not "the *tathāgatagarbha*'s intrinsic purity," not "the four kinds of dhyāna" — but on "hearts of all buddhas first" exclusively. This choice is already a precise doctrinal move. Out of the entire *Laṅkā*, Daoxin selects the one thesis that most tightly locks the ontological object as the basis of his dharma. This corresponds directly to §2.1: the *hṛdaya* lock-in of "hearts of all buddhas first" is the pivot of the *Laṅkā*'s entire ontological construction.

**Link two: "relying further on the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra*, 'single-practice samādhi.'"** Structural function: establishing the operational category of the method. Daoxin introduces a concrete practice method from a second sūtra — the single-practice samādhi of the *Mañjuśrī's Teaching of Prajñā-pāramitā-sūtra* (T0232).[^29] The core specification of this method is "**one practice**": within the multiplicity of dharmas, take a single practice as the object of concentration, letting no other practice enter. In the original passage of the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra*, the concrete operation is "**tethering the mind to a single buddha, exclusively invoking the name**" (T0232, p.731b): choose a single buddha's name as the invocational object, do not shift, do not mix, a single direct invocation. This is the standard shape of the buddha-recollection verb at the methodological level.

The structural meaning of the sūtra-pair's combination emerges here: the *Laṅkā* locks the ontology of the object (*hṛdaya* = the shared essence of all buddhas), and the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra* locks the operation of the method (single-practice samādhi = exclusive invocation of a single buddha's name). The *Laṅkā* alone gives no method — the reader knows what the object is but not how to operate; the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra* alone gives no ontology — the reader knows how to operate but not what the object ontologically is. **In combination, the object of operation of the method and the object of the ontological lock-in settle onto each other** — the name invoked is the *hṛdaya* of all buddhas, and the *hṛdaya* of all buddhas is the name invoked; the two are unified in this combination.

**Link three: "the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha."** Structural function: establishing the same-substance identity of subject and object. This is the philosophical heart of the whole formula. "**The mind that recollects the Buddha**" is the mind of the practitioner in the present moment of recollecting; "**is the Buddha**" means that this very mind is itself the Buddha. The structural logic of the sentence is this: under §2.3's triune identification (Tathāgata-mind / *ālaya* / *tathāgatagarbha* as a single referent), at the moment of the practitioner's recollection, the subject-mind that recollects (the *hṛdaya*-face of own-mind, that is, own-*tathāgatagarbha*) and the object-buddha that is recollected (the *hṛdaya* of all buddhas, that is, the buddhas' *tathāgatagarbha*) are of one substance ontologically; therefore **the practitioner's mind of recollecting, in the very present moment, is itself the Buddha**. Subject-object objectification is dissolved here: subject and object of recollection are unified at the ontological level of *hṛdaya*, no longer mutually pursuing one another as two independent substances.

**Link four: "to recollect the Buddha is to recollect the mind; to seek the mind is to seek the Buddha."** Structural function: establishing the symmetric identity of the acts. The sentence is grammatically a pair of mutually mirroring identity-statements: A = B, and B = A. **The buddha-recollection act and the mind-recollection act are two formulations of one and the same act** — spoken from the object-direction, it is "recollecting the Buddha"; spoken from the subject-direction, it is "recollecting the mind"; but this "recollecting the Buddha" and this "recollecting the mind" are, in the substance of the act, one and the same act. The structural import of the symmetry is that the Chan tradition (whose rallying cry is mind-recollection) and the Pure Land tradition (whose rallying cry is buddha-recollection) are not opposing schools within Daoxin's framework but **two formulations of one act** — identical at the level of the act's substance, complementary in the direction of their articulation.

**Link five: "constantly calling the Buddha to mind, while clinging does not arise, will dissolve into formlessness."** Structural function: establishing the concrete shape of practice. This line handles the daily operation of the practitioner: "**constantly calling the Buddha to mind**" is the sustained act (corresponding to P15's grounding-in-precepts vertical and P16's confession liturgy, both of which point to "a sustainedly activated buddha-recollection structure"); "**clinging does not arise**" is the corrective warning (countering the Zongmi-flagged pathology of "thinking-of-the-Buddha is also a samsaric mind" in P14, guarding buddha-recollection from falling into subject-object objectification); "**will dissolve into formlessness**" is the ontological target (corresponding to §I's "seeing-the-mind = buddha-recollection," whose object is *hṛdaya*). The three operate together: sustained action + vigilant against clinging + oriented toward formlessness, a three-layered structure in mutual restraint and mutual support.

Taken together, the five links show that Daoxin's formula is no isolated slogan but a complete dharma-structure: ontology of the object (link one), operation of the method (link two), same-substance subject-object identity (link three), symmetry of the acts (link four), shape of practice (link five). Each link is doctrinally grounded, each has operational consequences; the five links interlock into a complete system capable of directly guiding practice. That this formula was already complete within the early Chan tradition by the mid-seventh century means that, within the history of Chinese Buddhism, the structural location of the "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection" turn is **not within the reactive phase of a Chan resurgence after Pure Land, but within the foundational phase of Chan's settlement**.

### 4.3 Historical Continuation — Hongren, Shenxiu, and the East Mountain Teaching's Continuity

Daoxin's formula continues unbroken in the hands of his disciple Hongren (601–674) and Hongren's disciple Shenxiu (606–706). Jìngjué's *Record* preserves Hongren's instruction to Xuanze: "**This [*Laṅkā*] is a sūtra that can only be realized through the mind; it is not something letter-commentaries can explain**.… **Shenxiu and I have discussed the *Laṅkā* together; the profound principle came through keenly for us.**"[^30] The significance of this passage for P17's thesis is that Hongren explicitly identifies the *Laṅkā* as a "mind-realization sūtra" — a sūtra whose exhaustive grasp cannot be reached by letters and commentaries, but only through the linkage of realization in one's own mind. This position continues Daoxin's formula directly: mind-realization is seeing-the-mind, seeing-the-mind is recollecting the buddhas' heart, recollecting the buddhas' heart is recollecting the *hṛdaya* to which the *Laṅkā* points.

Shenxiu, in his audience with Empress Wu of the Zhou-Tang transition, further makes explicit the dual-source grounding of the East Mountain Teaching:

> "The Great Sage Empress Then [Wu Zetian] asked Meditation Master Shenxiu: 'Whose doctrinal source is the dharma you transmit?' He answered: 'I have received the East Mountain Teaching of Qizhou.' She asked: 'Relying on what classical canon?' He answered: '**Relying on the single-practice samādhi of the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra***.' She said: 'Insofar as we speak of practicing the Way, there is nothing that exceeds the East Mountain Teaching.'" (translation mine)[^31]

Shenxiu here makes explicit that the East Mountain Teaching's canonical source is "the single-practice samādhi of the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra*." The reply does not mention the *Laṅkā*, because by Shenxiu's time the *Laṅkā* was already the default mind-sealing text of the East Mountain Teaching (i.e., the "teaching" in "attaching to the teaching and awakening to the source") and required no further mention; what required mention was the canonical source of the **practice method** — the single-practice samādhi. Shenxiu's reply therefore aligns fully with Daoxin's formula: the *Laṅkā* supplies the ontology of the object, the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra* supplies the method's operation, and the combination is the foundational structure of the East Mountain Teaching.

From Daoxin (first half of the seventh century) through Hongren (middle of the seventh century) to Shenxiu (end of the seventh to early eighth century), **the core formula of the East Mountain Teaching continues unbroken and consistent**: the *Laṅkā* as object-ontology, the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra*'s single-practice samādhi as method-operation, and "the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha" as the conclusion of their combination.[^32] This continuity is important for the corrections in §V: **the "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection" turn is not the incidental phenomenon of one period, not the isolated claim of one school, but the foundational structure of the mainstream early Chan tradition**.

### 4.4 Structural Differentiation from the Later Southern School

The relation between the Daoxin-Hongren-Shenxiu East Mountain Teaching and Huineng's (638–713) later Southern School is often described, within the standard Western sinological narrative, as an "opposition-and-replacement" structure — the Northern School (Shenxiu, gradual cultivation) versus the Southern School (Huineng, sudden awakening) — with the *Vajracchedikā-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra* replacing the *Laṅkā* as the canonical basis of mind-sealing in the Southern School.[^33] From the standpoint of P17's thesis, that narrative requires a key correction: **the "replacement of the *Laṅkā* by the *Vajracchedikā*" does not entail the repudiation of the "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection" structure** — to the contrary, the Southern School radicalized and simplified that structure into the more immediate "directly pointing at the root mind, seeing the nature and attaining buddhahood."

Guifeng Zongmi (780–841), in fascicle one of his *Preface to the Collected Essays on the Source of Chan*, offers a late-Tang retrospective:

> "The transmitting of the mind that does not set up words, displaying the source and breaking attachment, is why such utterances arose — not that liberation is spoken of apart from words. Thus the transmitting teachers who attained the meaning **frequently praised the *Vajracchedikā* and the *Laṅkā*, saying, 'These two sūtras are my heart-essence.'**… The *Laṅkā* says: '**What is quiescent is called one mind. One mind is the *tathāgatagarbha*. The *tathāgatagarbha* is also the Dharma-body in bonds**.'" (translation mine)[^34]

Zongmi places the *Vajracchedikā* and the *Laṅkā* side by side as "my heart-essence" — **not the *Vajracchedikā* replacing the *Laṅkā*, but the two juxtaposed**. This position, preserved in Zongmi's late-Tang vantage, makes clear that the Daoxin "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection" structure did not vanish with sectarian divergence but was integrated into the doctrinal frame of the Southern School. The Southern School's "seeing the nature and attaining buddhahood" (as in the *Blood-Vessel Treatise* of the *Six Gates of Shaoshi* — "**The Buddha is made by one's own mind; why would one seek the Buddha apart from this mind?**… **Mind is the Buddha, the Buddha is the mind; apart from the mind there is no Buddha, apart from the Buddha there is no mind**"[^35]) is structurally isomorphic with Daoxin's "to recollect the Buddha is to recollect the mind; to seek the mind is to seek the Buddha." The Southern School's formulation is more radical and more elided of intermediate steps; but its ontological structure — the object of recollection as the *hṛdaya* of one's own mind, the unity of buddha-recollection and seeing-the-mind at the level of that object — is common to both schools.

The significance for P17's thesis is this: **Daoxin's formula, as the foundational structure of the Chinese Chan tradition's "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection" turn, continues unbroken throughout the entire history of Chan (from the East Mountain Teaching to the Southern School)** — it is not a sudden event of one period but the *Laṅkā*'s sustained reading within the Chinese Chan tradition. This reading remains visible in contemporary Chinese-Buddhist practice — in the name-holding of the Taiwanese Pure Land, in the Chan *gōng'àn* "who is the one who recollects the Buddha?", in the Chinese Chan practice structure "the name as entry, one's own mind as return" — all of them modern transformations of the seventh-century foundational structure.

§IV's citation of Daoxin, serving as both the canonical culmination of §III's thesis and the historical interlocutor of §IV itself, completes the canonical construction of P17's reading of "absence of buddha-recollection as structural invitation." The paper now turns to Middle-Way Corrections.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section treats three principal misreadings: (i) reading "hearts of all buddhas" as *citta* rather than *hṛdaya*; (ii) collapsing *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna* into naive identity; and (iii) the "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" false friend. Of the three, the second has, within the Yogācāra tradition itself, a mature distancing move that serves as an internal textual anchor — no editorial voice from the present author need be imposed. This is particularly important.

### 5.1 ❌ Reading "Hearts of All Buddhas" as *Citta* rather than *Hṛdaya*

§2.1 and §3.1 established that T0670's interlinear gloss at p.481c01-03 — "like the solid heartwood of a tree, not a deliberating mind" — locks "hearts of all buddhas" onto *hṛdaya*; §3.1 further unfolded the structural consequences of that lock-in for the recollection verb. Yet in the actual operations of contemporary Chinese-and-English reading, this lock-in is frequently loosened: because the default semantic pull of "心" in modern Chinese, and of "mind" in modern English, is toward *citta* (deliberative activity) rather than *hṛdaya* (core essence), readers who do not actively perform a philological adjustment will automatically read "hearts of all buddhas first" under the *citta* key.

The structural consequence of this misreading is the **collapse of P17's thesis as a whole**. If "hearts of all buddhas" is the deliberative activity of the buddhas, then buddha-recollection is a chase after another's stream of mind, and the "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection" turn degenerates into a contrast between two external acts ("chasing the buddhas' deliberations" → "chasing one's own deliberations"), rather than a direct substance-to-substance linkage; Daoxin's formula "the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha" degenerates into the crude equation "the present deliberative activity of the practitioner's recollection is the deliberative activity of the Buddha," not the deep ontological thesis it actually is. Once this reading is set, the entire "seeing-the-mind" verb-structure argued for in P17 ceases to hold.

❌ **Correction.** Whenever the sentence "hearts of all buddhas first" is cited, **the interlinear gloss "like the solid heartwood of a tree, not a deliberating mind" must be cited with it at least once**, to lock the *hṛdaya* reading. This is the built-in anti-misreading mechanism of T0670 itself — the gloss is a philological cue inscribed by Guṇabhadra (or the translation team, or the early manuscript transmission) to forestall precisely this confusion.[^36] Contemporary readers inclined by Chinese linguistic instinct toward *citta*, or by English instinct toward mind-as-thinking, should take the gloss as their hermeneutic anchor.

The practical consequence for the path is that buddha-recollection is not the pursuit of the buddhas' deliberative activity with one's own — that is the "thinking-of-the-Buddha is also a samsaric mind" pathology that Zongmi warned against — but the linkage of one's own *hṛdaya* to the buddhas' *hṛdaya*: the direct ontological illumination in which seeing the core essence of one's own mind is seeing the core essence of all buddhas. This linkage is not a subject-object-objectifying mental act; it is a substance-to-substance direct seeing.

### 5.2 ⚠️ Collapsing *Tathāgatagarbha* and *Ālaya-Vijñāna* — the Dharmapāla-Xuanzang Distancing Move

§2.3 and §3.1 established the nonduality reading of *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna* within the *Laṅkā* — T0672's compound naming of "*tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*" and T0671's triune juxtaposition of "Tathāgata-mind / *ālaya* / *tathāgatagarbha*" identify them as a single referent. The structural risk of this identity is: **if simplified without qualification, the reader will read *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna* as two names of one substantive entity, losing the functional distinction** (*tathāgatagarbha* as pure face, *ālaya* as flow-face: these two faces of a single substance must be functionally distinguished, even as they are ontologically identical). Within the Yogācāra tradition itself, there exists a mature warning against this simplification — nowhere more clearly than in Dharmapāla's (c. 530–561) *Cheng Weishi Lun* (T1585).

Fascicle two of the Dharmapāla-Xuanzang *Cheng Weishi Lun* contains an extremely precise distancing move:

> "Yet the sūtras say: **'The nature of mind is pure' means the suchness disclosed by the emptiness-principle of mind. Suchness is the true nature of mind.** Or else: the mind's substance is not of the nature of defilement, hence it is called *naturally pure*; not that a defiled mind is itself undefiled in its nature, hence called 'naturally pure.'" (translation mine)[^37]

The structural logic here is extremely fine. For the thesis of "the nature of mind is intrinsically pure" in the sūtras (including the *Laṅkā*), Dharmapāla offers a double reading.

**First**: "'The nature of mind is pure' means the suchness disclosed by the emptiness-principle of mind" — "nature of mind is pure" does not refer to the existence of some substantive pure mind; it refers to "**the suchness disclosed by the emptiness-principle of the mind**"; that is, "intrinsic purity of mind" is reinterpreted as another name for "the suchness-nature of mind," with suchness existing as the principle disclosed by emptiness, not as some substantive pure mind. This is a Yogācāra-internal tightening of the *hṛdaya* reading: the "hearts of all buddhas first" (*hṛdaya*) is conceded to point to something existent at the ontological level, but this something is strictly delimited as "the suchness disclosed by emptiness" and not as a substantive pure mind.

**Second**: "Or else: the mind's substance is not of the nature of defilement, hence it is called 'naturally pure'; not that a defiled mind is itself undefiled in its nature, hence called 'naturally pure'" — or on another reading: "the mind's substance" (referring here to the *ālaya-vijñāna*'s own substance) is not of the nature of defilement, and so can be called "naturally pure"; but this "naturally pure" does not mean "a defiled mind's nature is itself undefiled" — the *ālaya*, as the ground for defiled seeds, is itself defiled in its proper operation and cannot be directly identified with a purely undefiled buddha-nature.

The combined force of the two readings is this: **Dharmapāla neither denies the scriptural thesis (that "the nature of mind is pure") nor accepts the substantive-pure-mind reading (*tathāgatagarbha* = substantive pure mind = substantive identification with *ālaya-vijñāna*)**. Yogācāra here acknowledges the nonduality reading of the *Laṅkā* as scriptural fact, while at the ontological level tightening the semantic register of that reading: the *tathāgatagarbha* is not a substance, the *ālaya* is not a substance, and their relation of "neither one nor different" must, within the emptiness framework of Yogācāra, be strictly delimited as "two faces of the same emptiness-principle" and not as "two names of one and the same substantive thing."

⚠️ **Correction.** Whenever P17 cites the *tathāgatagarbha* ↔ *ālaya* nonduality thesis, **it must cite, directly or by implication, the Dharmapāla-Xuanzang distancing move in T1585 as the anti-simplification anchor**. The key point of the anchor is this: **this warning is itself a Yogācāra-internal position, not an editorial tone imposed by the present author**. Were the warning issued in the voice of personal observation, its weight would fall only at the level of hermeneutic caution; once the T1585 anchor is established, the weight rises to that of a self-regulating norm internal to the Yogācāra tradition.

The structural bearing of this distancing move is especially relevant to twentieth-century Chinese debates over the integration of Yogācāra and the *tathāgatagarbha* system. From Ouyang Jingwu through Lü Cheng and Yinshun, Chinese Buddhist scholars have long debated whether "the *tathāgatagarbha* system belongs to the Yogācāra mainstream"; one core point of contention has been whether the *Laṅkā*'s nonduality reading of "*tathāgatagarbha* = *ālaya*" can be received into the orthodox Yogācāra lineage. The Dharmapāla-Xuanzang distancing move cited in P17 here supplies a precise doctrinal answer: Yogācāra adopts toward this nonduality reading a **spectrum** (the *Mahāyāna-sūtrālaṅkāra*, the *Madhyānta-vibhāga*, Paramārtha's translation of the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha-bhāṣya*, the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha-bhāṣya* glossed by Asvabhāva, and the *Awakening of Faith* on the adoptive end; the *Cheng Weishi Lun* on the distancing end; Kuiji's *Essentials in the Palm* on the South-Road-versus-North-Road controversy as middle reportage), rather than either wholesale acceptance or wholesale rejection.[^38] P17's stance is to accept this spectrum as fact, to acknowledge — when citing the *Laṅkā*'s nonduality reading — that it has limited standing within the Yogācāra tradition, and at the same time to cite T1585's distancing move as the anti-simplification anchor.

### 5.3 ⚠️ The "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" False Friend

§2.5 already flagged the "similar-in-form-divergent-in-meaning" problem of T0671's "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four": the title misleads, its content is in fact the *manomaya-kāya* (mind-made body) doctrine and has no direct bearing on the "hearts of all buddhas" *hṛdaya*; the correct correspondence in T0671 to T0670's "hearts of all buddhas first" is at fascicle two, p.520b12-15 (in the *Chapter on Questions and Answers, Chapter Two*), **not "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four."**

This correction has a protective function in practice. If a reader, drawing on the intuitive inference "P17 is about hearts of all buddhas first; T0671 has a 'Buddha-Mind Chapter,' so let me look there," directly consults "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" and cites its content in support of P17's thesis, that reader will be drawn into a category error — mistaking the teaching on the mind-made body (the operable body produced by the bodhisattva's mastered manifestation through the stream of mind) for the teaching on "the shared heart-essence of all buddhas." **The "mind" of the mind-made body is a concept within the *manas* / *citta* family (a body produced by thought), and is categorically distinct from the "heart" of "hearts of all buddhas" (*hṛdaya*, core essence)**: to confuse the two destroys the *hṛdaya* lock-in of §3.1.

⚠️ **Correction.** When citing T0671, **one must clearly distinguish the "hearts of all buddhas first" passage (fascicle two, p.520b12-15, in the *Chapter on Questions and Answers, Chapter Two*) from "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" (fascicle five, on the mind-made body)**. Both passages exist in T0671 and both involve the phrase "buddha-mind," but they belong to different ontological categories. P17's *hṛdaya* lock-in applies only to the first, not to the second.

The three Middle-Way Corrections together precisely delimit the thesis of P17: inwardly, they guard against simplification (the *hṛdaya* lock-in and the distancing move on nonduality); outwardly, they guard against category error (the "Buddha-Mind Chapter Four" false friend). Each correction has its own canonical-internal anchor — the T0670 interlinear gloss, the T1585 distancing move, and the self-distinction of T0671's chapter structure — rather than an editorial voice from the present author; this gives P17's corrective mechanism the character of **hermeneutic self-regulation**. The boundaries of its correction are set by the structure of the canon itself, not by the interpretive preferences of the author.

## VI. Conclusion

### Essentials

This paper has taken the three Chinese recensions of the *Laṅkāvatāra* (T0670 / T0671 / T0672) as canonical grounding, and argued that the *Laṅkā*, on which the Bodhidharma-line Chan relied, is not a sūtra of exoteric buddha-recollection but **the canonical scriptural ground on which the buddha-recollection verb is radically interiorized into a seeing-the-mind verb**. The philological fact that T0670 contains no direct occurrence of *niàn-fó*, *niàn-rúlái*, *guān-fó*, or *xiǎng-fó* is not a coverage failure; it is the *Laṅkā*'s built-in mechanism for the ontological reorganization of that verb. The interlinear gloss at T0670 p.481c01-03, "like the solid heartwood of a tree, not a deliberating mind," locks the object of "hearts of all buddhas first" on the *hṛdaya* sense and excludes the *citta* sense; T0672's *Kṣaṇika-parivarta* compound "*tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna*" settles this *hṛdaya* further onto the one mind that is the common ground of defiled and pure; T0671's triune identification of "Tathāgata-mind / *ālaya* / *tathāgatagarbha*" completes the same-substance subject-object structure. Under this ontological premise, the absence of buddha-recollection itself constitutes a structural invitation to "**the radical interiorization of buddha-recollection into seeing-the-mind**." The invitation is formally completed, by the mid-seventh century, in the five-link chain-formula of Daoxin's *Entry into the Path of Pacifying-the-Mind* (T2837) — "**Relying on the *Laṅkā*'s 'hearts of all buddhas first' + relying on the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā's* 'single-practice samādhi' → the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha**" — proving that the turn from "buddha-recollection" to "mind-recollection" was complete within the early Chan tradition before the Southern School emerged. The seventh layer of the verb-axis, **seeing-the-mind**, is thereby established: following the six layers of P11–P16 in their ontological-dimension extension of the object.

### Guidance for Practice

The guidance that may be distilled from this structure for contemporary Chinese-Buddhist practice is as follows.

**First, the object of buddha-recollection is the *hṛdaya* of all buddhas, not their *citta*.** In the present moment of invocation or contemplation, the practitioner's attention should actively shift the structure of the object from "the buddhas' deliberative activity" to "the buddhas' core essence." This shift does not require a change in the act's shape (one may still invoke, one may still contemplate); it requires only a change in the ontological register on which the object is understood — the name invoked points to the buddhas' *hṛdaya*, the form contemplated discloses the buddhas' *hṛdaya*, and that *hṛdaya* is of one substance with the *tathāgatagarbha* within the practitioner's own mind.

**Second, buddha-recollection is the seeing of one's own *hṛdaya*.** Under the *hṛdaya* lock-in, outward invocation and inward seeing-the-mind are not two paths but two faces of a single act. At the moment of invocation, the substance of the act is the seeing of one's own *hṛdaya*; at the moment of inward contemplation of the mind, the substance of the act is the recollection of the buddhas' *hṛdaya*. The two are one at the ontological level and mutually express one another at the level of act-shape. This is the practical landing of Daoxin's "to recollect the Buddha is to recollect the mind; to seek the mind is to seek the Buddha."

**Third, the twin operation of constant calling-to-mind and non-arising of clinging.** The closing line of Daoxin's passage — "constantly calling the Buddha to mind, while clinging does not arise, will dissolve into formlessness" — is the formal guide for this practice: the practitioner must sustain the act of calling-to-mind (constantly recollecting the Buddha) and must simultaneously guard the act against sliding into the clinging of subject-object objectification (clinging does not arise), steering the twin operation toward formlessness as its ontological target (dissolving into formlessness). Sustained without clinging is the correct shape of *hṛdaya*-linkage; sustained without guarding against clinging collapses into Zongmi's "thinking-of-the-Buddha is also a samsaric mind"; guarding against clinging without sustaining collapses into a view of extinguished emptiness. The twin operation, non-dual, is the complete practical landing of "buddha-recollection is mind-recollection."

**Fourth, the *Laṅkā* may be read as a canonical text on "the interiorization of buddha-recollection."** A practitioner who wishes to deepen her grasp of the "buddha-recollection → mind-recollection" turn can read Guṇabhadra's four-fascicle T0670 directly, paying attention to the fact that the text never speaks of buddha-recollection, and to such passages as "hearts of all buddhas first," "the *tathāgatagarbha*," "the *ālaya*," and "objective fields manifest from one's own mind." The reading-process is itself an exercise of the ontological reorganization of the recollection verb: the sūtra does not explicitly speak of buddha-recollection, and the reader completes the inward turn from outward invocation to inward seeing-the-mind of her own accord; completing that turn **is** the functioning of the *Laṅkā* as a "mind-sealing sūtra."

### Open Question — Forward Link to P18 (the *Ghanavyūha*)

P17's verb-axis of "seeing-the-mind" has completed the ontological reorganization of the recollection-object from external name to one's own *hṛdaya*. Yet one central question is left open: **if the object of buddha-recollection has been thoroughly interiorized into one's own *hṛdaya*, how does that interiorization operate within the structure of the *ālaya-vijñāna*? How does the seeing-the-mind act alter the structure of the practitioner's stream of consciousness? Where does the linkage of *hṛdaya* stand within the Yogācāra system of *ālaya-parāvṛtti* (the transmutation of the storehouse consciousness)?**

This question is the central open issue left by P17: the ontological lock-in of the *hṛdaya* object is complete, but the dynamic mechanism at the operational level of the stream of consciousness after the object is locked (i.e., how the *ālaya* is transmuted in the act of seeing-the-mind) has not been unfolded. That unfolding must be completed in P18: **P18 on the *Ghanavyūha-sūtra* (T0681 / T0682) will take "buddha-recollection as *ālaya*-transmutation" as its theme and unfold the central open question left by P17**.[^39] The *Ghanavyūha* is the extension of *Laṅkā*-system thought into the realm of the *ghana-vyūha* land (the dense-and-magnificent adornment of the *dharmadhātu*); its core teaching is that **buddha-recollection is the root-level transmutation mechanism of the *ālaya-vijñāna*** — landing the seeing-the-mind act of P17 (the seventh verb-layer) into the actual refashioning of the structure of the stream of consciousness (the eighth verb-layer: **transmutation**). Viewed with P17 together, the *Laṅkā*-system's verb-axis of buddha-recollection will have completed its double-axis construction across the ontology of the object (P17) and the dynamics of the stream of consciousness (P18). The verb-axis of the eight great Mahāyāna sūtras of Part V will then continue into P19 on the single-practice samādhi of the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra*, where Daoxin's method-in-combination, already structurally prepared by P17 §IV, will receive its own independent unfolding.

P17's argument is here concluded. The seventh layer of the verb-axis, seeing-the-mind, has taken its structural position along the NIAN vertical; the *Laṅkā*'s absence of buddha-recollection is re-understood, within the sūtra's overall ontological structure, as a structural invitation to the interiorization of that verb; Daoxin's seventh-century chain-formula serves as the canonical culmination of that invitation's completion; the Dharmapāla-Xuanzang distancing move serves as the Middle-Way anchor. The complete verb-axis of buddha-recollection — from P08's verb-coupling, through P11–P16's six-layer structural unfolding, to P17's ontological interiorization of the object — reaches here a significant milestone in the middle of Part V. P18 will continue the vertical.

[^26]: The concrete operation of the single-practice samādhi, at T0232, p.731b: "**To enter the single-practice samādhi, one should go to a quiet and secluded place, setting aside all distracting thoughts, not grasping at marks or features, tethering the mind to a single buddha, exclusively invoking the name**, turning one's body toward the direction of that buddha. If one can achieve continuity of thought upon a single buddha, then within this continuity one can see the buddhas of past, future, and present." This is the canonical textual basis of "invocation as method-operation." P19 will take up the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra*'s single-practice samādhi for independent treatment; this paper confines itself to citing it in structural combination with Daoxin's formula.

[^27]: On the historical location of the East Mountain Teaching, see Chappell, David W., "The Teachings of the Fourth Ch'an Patriarch Tao-hsin (580-651)," in *Early Ch'an in China and Tibet*, ed. Whalen Lai and Lewis R. Lancaster, 89-129 (Berkeley Buddhist Studies Series, 1983); McRae, *The Northern School* (1986); and Yanagida 1971, cited above.

[^28]: Jìngjué and the East Mountain Teaching transmission: Jìngjué is a disciple of Xuanze, who is the disciple of Shenxiu, and thus stands in the Northern School / East Mountain lineage; his *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkā* represents the Northern School's official self-historiography of the East Mountain Teaching. After the rise of the Southern School under Huineng in the mid-eighth century, this text was gradually overshadowed by Southern School histories (such as the *Lidai fabao ji*, T2075, and Huineng's lineage *Platform Sūtra*, T2008); but the twentieth-century discovery of Dunhuang manuscripts has provided direct textual evidence for this primary Northern School narrative.

[^29]: Two Chinese translations of the *Mañjuśrī-prajñā-sūtra* were in circulation by Daoxin's time: Maṇḍala's T0232 (502–557 CE) and Saṅghabhadra's T0233 (506–520 CE). Which recension Daoxin cited is not certain, but their handling of the single-practice samādhi passage is substantially the same.

[^30]: *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkā*, fascicle one, "Sixth Generation: Hongren," T85 no.2837, p.1289b. Original: "Sixth.… Ren transmitted the dharma.… He was taught the meaning of the *Laṅkā* as: 'This sūtra can only be realized through the mind; it is not something letter-commentaries can explain.'… 'Shenxiu and I have discussed the *Laṅkā* together; the profound principle came through keenly for us; it must be much of benefit.'" The passage closes with the note that Hongren's teaching to Xuanze is recorded in Xuanze's *Record of Persons and Dharmas of the Laṅkā*, indicating that the instruction was recorded by Xuanze and re-entered by Jìngjué as first-hand historical material.

[^31]: *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkā*, fascicle one, "Seventh Generation: Shenxiu," T85 no.2837, p.1289c–1290a. This passage records the exchange between the Great Sage Empress Wu of the Zhou (624–705) and Shenxiu on the canonical basis of the East Mountain Teaching, and serves as an official court confirmation of the Northern School's status in the Tang.

[^32]: On the Daoxin → Hongren → Shenxiu continuity of the East Mountain Teaching, see Yanagida, *Shoki no zenshi I*, chapter three; McRae, *The Northern School*, chapters three and four. The core doctrinal structure is continuous across the three generations; it remains unchanged under Shenxiu's disciples, Hui'an (Lao'an, 582–709), Xuanze, and Puji (651–739); it is only in the mid-eighth century, after Shenhui's (684–758) Southern-School northern campaign, that the structural position of the East Mountain Teaching / Northern School is displaced by the Southern School.

[^33]: The opposing narrative "Northern School = gradual cultivation vs. Southern School = sudden awakening," together with the narrative of "the *Vajracchedikā* replacing the *Laṅkā*" as canonical authority, was formed largely in Shenhui's "*Determination of the True and False of the Southern School of Bodhidharma*" (T2045) and in later Southern-School histories (the *Platform Sūtra*, T2008; the *Lidai fabao ji*, T2075). Twentieth-century studies by Hu Shih, Yampolsky, McRae, and others have shown that this narrative is a retrospective construction of the Southern School, not an account of early Chan as it was. See McRae, op. cit.; and Hu Shih, "Ch'an (Zen) Buddhism in China: Its History and Method," *Philosophy East and West* 3, no. 1 (1953): 3-24.

[^34]: Guifeng Zongmi (780–841), *Preface to the Collected Essays on the Source of Chan*, fascicle one, upper, T48 no.2015, p.400a–b and p.404a. Original: "The transmitting of the mind that does not set up words, displaying the source and breaking attachment, is why such utterances arose — not that liberation is spoken of apart from words. One mind is the *tathāgatagarbha*. The *tathāgatagarbha* is also the Dharma-body in bonds.'"

[^35]: *Blood-Vessel Treatise*, the sixth gate of the *Six Gates of Shaoshi* (attributed to Bodhidharma), T48 no.2009, p.375b. Original: "The Buddha is made by one's own mind; why would one seek the Buddha apart from this mind? Buddhas of the past and buddhas of the future speak only of the mind. Mind is the Buddha, the Buddha is the mind; apart from the mind there is no Buddha, apart from the Buddha there is no mind.… Beings are inverted; they do not know and do not perceive that their own mind is the Buddha. If one knows that one's own mind is the Buddha, one will not seek the Buddha outside the mind.… If one wishes to seek the Buddha, one must see the nature. The nature is the Buddha." The text's historical attribution is a later ascription (the actual text is a Chan manuscript-collection of the early-to-mid Tang), but its language circulated widely in early Chan transmission and stands as the standard articulation of the Bodhidharma-line "one's own mind is the Buddha" thesis.

[^36]: Philological status of T0670's interlinear gloss "like the solid heartwood of a tree, not a deliberating mind": in CBETA XML this gloss is tagged `<note place="inline">`, indicating that it is small-type marginalia internal to the sūtra and not a later editorial addition; its function is a *hṛdaya* / *citta* distinction-cue carried by Guṇabhadra (or his translation team, or the early manuscript transmission) itself.

[^37]: *Cheng Weishi Lun* (composed by Dharmapāla and others, translated by Xuanzang), T1585, fascicle two, p.8c. The passage is representative of Yogācāra's handling of the thesis "the nature of mind is intrinsically pure." See Lü Cheng, "Studies on the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (Part One) — General Introduction to the Treatise," *Selected Buddhist Writings of Lü Cheng*, vol. 3.

[^38]: The spectrum of Yogācāra positions on the nonduality reading of *tathāgatagarbha* ↔ *ālaya-vijñāna* is as follows. **Adoption end**: the *Mahāyāna-sūtrālaṅkāra* (T1604), fascicles three and six ("sentient beings are called the *tathāgatagarbha*," "the nature of mind is intrinsically pure, defiled through adventitious defilement"); the *Madhyānta-vibhāga* (T1600), fascicle one ("because the nature of mind is intrinsically pure, it is defiled by adventitious defilements"); Paramārtha's translation of the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha-bhāṣya* (T1595), fascicle one (citing three *Śrīmālā* *tathāgatagarbha* passages within the section on the *ālaya-vijñāna* as ground of the known); Asvabhāva's gloss of the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha-bhāṣya* (T1598), fascicle five ("in the sacred teaching it is said that all sentient beings have the *tathāgatagarbha*"); and the *Awakening of Faith in the Mahāyāna* (T1666) ("relying on the *tathāgatagarbha* there is the mind of arising-and-ceasing, which is called the *ālaya-vijñāna*"). **Distancing end**: the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (T1585), fascicle two ("'the nature of mind is pure' means the suchness disclosed by the emptiness-principle of mind"). **Middle-reporting end**: Kuiji's *Essentials in the Palm of the Cheng Weishi Lun* (T1831), fascicle one, end (recording the Southern-Road and Northern-Road controversy on the *tathāgatagarbha*); and Kuiji's *Grove of Meanings of the Mahāyāna Dharma Garden* (T1861), fascicle one, the *Chapter on Mind-Only* (citing the *Laṅkā*'s "eight and nine various consciousnesses, like the waves on water" verse).

[^39]: P18 *Ghanavyūha* theme forecast. The *Mahāyāna-ghanavyūha-sūtra* (T0681, trans. Divākara; T0682, trans. Amoghavajra) centers on the manifestation of the *ālaya-vijñāna* (the storehouse consciousness) in the *ghana-vyūha* land (the dense-and-magnificent adornment of the *dharmadhātu*). Its extension of the P17 thesis: how the "seeing-the-mind" act of buddha-recollection (P17's seventh layer) operates within the structure of the *ālaya-vijñāna*'s stream of consciousness, and how *ālaya-parāvṛtti* (the transmutation of the storehouse consciousness) is completed in substance. P18 will take "buddha-recollection as *ālaya*-transmutation" as the eighth layer of the verb-axis: **transmutation**.

- **P08 "From Calling-to-Mind to Name-Invocation — A Four-Step Analysis of *Niàn-Chēng* Verb-Coupling"**: the beginning of the verb-axis; the four-step coupling of recollection / invocation / remembrance / holding.
