## Abstract
This paper takes up the second member of Part V's Mahāyāna-Eight-Sūtras cluster — the *Lotus Sūtra* (*Miàofǎ liánhuā jīng* 妙法蓮華經, T0262) — with the Universal Gate Chapter (*Pǔmén pǐn* 普門品, Chapter 25, Fascicle 7) as the main axis, the Expedient Means Chapter (*Fāngbiàn pǐn* 方便品, Chapter 2, Fascicle 1) as supporting ground, and the Bodhisattva Never Disparaging Chapter (*Chángbùqīng púsà pǐn* 常不輕菩薩品, Chapter 20, Fascicle 6) as the distant axis. It argues three things. **First**, within T0262 itself, Lotus *name-invocation* (*chēngmíng* 稱名) is a three-karmic integrated practice — not a single, repetitive vocal act, but the simultaneous activation of vocal-karma (invocation of the name, centered in the Universal Gate), bodily-karma (reverence, centered in Never Disparaging), and mental-karma (deep respect, crystallized in Never Disparaging's "I deeply revere you all," *wǒ shēn jìng rǔděng* 我深敬汝等) — dismantling the Western flattened readings of Buddhist *chanting* as either *intercessory prayer* (Christian framework) or *mantra* (Indian sonic-ritual framework). **Second**, internal to the Universal Gate Chapter itself, the prose-section operates around the verb *chēng* 稱 ("to invoke by name") while the verse-section systematically shifts to the verb *niàn* 念 ("to recollect the power"), splitting the name-practice into outward vocal activation and inward return-to-merit-body across two literary registers. This split is the sūtra's own internal self-limitation on the verb *chēng* — the Lotus will not be reduced to sonic repetition, because the Lotus itself routes invocation back through the recollection of Avalokiteśvara's power (*niàn bǐ guānyīn lì* 念彼觀音力), twelve times in the closing verses. **Third**, T0262 internally unfolds a **single-sūtra dual-pole** structure — the Expedient Means Chapter's "a person with a scattered mind, stepping into a stūpa or shrine, invokes once 'Homage to the Buddha,' and has already completed the Buddha-path" (*ruò rén sànluàn xīn, rù yú tǎ-miào zhōng, yī chēng nāmó-fó, jiē yǐ chéng fódào* 若人散亂心、入於塔廟中、一稱南無佛、皆已成佛道) sets the lowest threshold with the highest fruition (already-completed Buddhahood), while the Universal Gate's "single-minded name-invocation" (*yīxīn chēngmíng* 一心稱名) sets the highest threshold with present-life liberation from suffering — the threshold-fruition cross-configuration reveals the Lotus's structural judgment that name-invocation is not a *tool for worldly efficacy* but rather *the embodied acknowledgment that all beings have already completed the Buddha-path*. Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words of the Lotus Sūtra* (*Fǎhuá Wénjù* 法華文句, T1718) reads Fascicle 10's Universal Gate exposition through a *perfect-one-is-singular, partial-ones-are-many* (*yuán rén wéi yī, piān rén zé duō* 圓人唯一、偏人則多) qualitative ranking — a late-Tang precursor to late-Ming Zhixu's "a single utterance contains faith, vow, and practice simultaneously" doctrine, forming a **Tiantai twin-axis** across P10 (the contemplation axis of the *Great Calming and Contemplation*) and P13 (the invocation axis of the *Commentary on the Words of the Lotus Sūtra*). §V corrects three popular misreadings (name-invocation as Christian intercessory prayer; as sonic mantra; as a sectarian token in the Lotus-vs-Pure-Land comparison game) plus one philological correction (the *phenomenal-principial* (*shì-chí / lǐ-chí* 事持／理持) partition of name-invocation is Ming-Qing Pure Land commentarial vocabulary, not Zhiyi's).

**Keywords:** Lotus name-invocation, T0262 *Lotus Sūtra*, Universal Gate Chapter, Bodhisattva Never Disparaging Chapter, Expedient Means Chapter, Avalokiteśvara, three-karmic architecture, vocal-karma name-invocation, bodily-karma reverence, mental-karma deep respect, prose-verse dual-track, recollection of Avalokiteśvara's power, scattered-mind single invocation, single-minded name-invocation, single-sūtra dual pole, "one invocation of Homage to the Buddha," "already completed the Buddha-path," "all attain liberation," mutual-reflecting subject-object, T0263 *Sūtra of the Correct Dharma-Flower*, Guangshiyin, holding-the-name, Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words of the Lotus Sūtra*, ten senses of "universal," "perfect-one is singular, partial-ones are many," Tiantai twin-axis, capacity-response structure, three-fold exposition of the seven perils, middle-way-reality, non-sectarian recollection, T0262 and T0366 in parallel.

## I. Introduction: When Name-Invocation Is Reduced to a Single Syllable

When Western English-language Buddhist readers first approach the Universal Gate Chapter of the *Lotus Sūtra* (T0262), the most common reaction is to choose between two familiar categories for receiving the text. The first is Christian *intercessory prayer* — invoking the name of a saint for rescue and protection, structurally parallel to the Catholic *Litany of the Saints*. The second is the Indian-Buddhist tradition of *mantra* recitation — in which sound itself is regarded as a sonic vibration possessed of salvific power. Within either category, invoking "*Nāmó Guānshìyīn Púsà*" ("Homage to Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva") becomes a single action: one sound, one object, one expected result (relief from suffering, or accumulation of merit).

Neither category is entirely wrong. The Universal Gate Chapter does contain rescue-language in the idiom "single-mindedly invoking the name, all attain liberation" (*yīxīn chēngmíng, jiē dé jiětuō* 一心稱名、皆得解脫), whose surface structure is indeed close to intercessory prayer; and its ritualized sonic repetition is indeed close to the mantra tradition. But both categories share a single flattened premise — that name-invocation is a **single-organ (the mouth), single-verb (*chēng*), single-object (the name), single-result (rescue)** simple action. Under this flattening, Lotus name-invocation becomes co-opted either as the East-Asian-Buddhist version of a religious rescue request, or as an Eastern-style sonic therapy.

This chapter rejects that flattening. Within T0262 itself, Lotus name-invocation is already structured as an **integrated action of all three karmas — body, speech, and mind — operating simultaneously**: vocal-karma name-invocation (the main axis in the Universal Gate Chapter), bodily-karma reverence (the distant axis in Never Disparaging, supported by "raising one hand, slightly bowing the head" in Expedient Means), and mental-karma deep respect (with Never Disparaging's "I deeply revere you all" as the point where the three karmas converge).[^1] More than this: the Universal Gate Chapter itself unfolds a second layer of structure — the prose-section (*chánghang* 長行) uses the verb *chēng* ("to invoke") to describe outward vocal-karma, while the verse-section (*jìsòng* 偈頌) uses the verb *niàn* ("power-recollection") to describe inward mental-karma returning to the merit-body.[^2] *Chēng* and *niàn* are not two different dharma-gates but two literary registers of the same practice.

This chapter proceeds along three layers of argument. **Layer One** (§3.1): the three-karmic architecture of Lotus buddha-recollection — Universal-Gate name-invocation (speech), Expedient Means's "raise one hand, slightly bow the head" together with Never Disparaging's reverence (body), and Never Disparaging's deep respect (mind) operating as a single integrated act — dismantles the single-organ model of name-invocation. **Layer Two** (§3.2): within the Universal Gate Chapter itself, a "prose-invokes / verse-recollects" dual track — the prose-level activates the name outward through vocalization, while the verse-level returns inward through power-recollection; their parallel operation within a single chapter is the Lotus's own structural self-limitation on the verb *chēng*. **Layer Three** (§3.3): T0262's internal "scattered-mind single-invocation / single-minded name-invocation" dual pole — Expedient Means's "a person with a scattered mind, stepping into a stūpa or shrine, invokes once 'Homage to the Buddha,' and has already completed the Buddha-path" sets the lowest threshold with the highest fruition for name-invocation; the Universal Gate's "single-minded name-invocation, and immediately attains liberation" sets the highest threshold with a present-life fruition. Both poles present within a single sūtra, the Lotus self-sufficiently displays both ends of the name-invocation spectrum.

§IV takes Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words of the Lotus Sūtra* (T1718) exposition of the Universal Gate Chapter as the primary material, unfolding the Tiantai tradition's detailed analysis of the capacity-response structure of name-invocation — in particular, Zhiyi's "ten senses of universal" (*pǔmén shí yì* 普門十義), his qualitative ranking by "the perfect-one is singular, partial-ones are many," and his middle-way-ultimate-reality reading of "*gate*" (*mén* 門) as "simultaneously empty, conventional, and middle." §IV also marks the **Tiantai twin-axis** between this chapter and P10's treatment of Zhiyi's *Great Calming and Contemplation* exposition of the Sixteen Contemplations — P10 handles the contemplation-method / stillness-cultivation axis, P13 handles the name-invocation / action axis; the two papers form an internal suture within Tiantai exegesis. §V's middle-way corrections treat three popular misreadings plus one philological correction. §VI's conclusion turns toward P14 (*Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*): when Lotus name-invocation has already self-sufficiently presented both poles (scattered / single-minded), how will the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* reassemble these two poles within its concrete structure of "capacity-and-response for sentient beings of the final age"?

This chapter continues the fourth layer of the NIAN verbal-grammar longitudinal axis: P08 on the four-step coupling of *niàn-chēng* (recollect / invoke / recall / hold) → P11 on *holding* (resolute grip + steady preservation) → P12 on *gathering in the sense-roots* (quiescence of the six faculties + uninterrupted pure recollection) → P13 on *name-invocation* (outward vocalization + inward power-recollection).[^3] But P13 does not continue P12's "cross-sūtra dual-axis" structural model (T0366 ↔ T0945); it turns instead to a "single-sūtra dual-pole" structural density — the Lotus as a single sūtra self-sufficiently presents the name-invocation spectrum, with no need to draw on cross-sūtra comparison. This structural choice is itself an embodiment of the design logic of Part V: from the opening declaration (P12) turning into the autonomous internal deepening of each Mahāyāna sūtra from P13 onward.

## II. Canonical Grounding

### 2.1 The Universal Gate Chapter (T0262, Fascicle 7)

The Universal Gate Chapter, Chapter 25 of Kumārajīva's (*Jiūmóluóshí* 鳩摩羅什) *Lotus Sūtra* (composed in 406 CE, eighth year of Hongshi of the Later Qin), stands in Fascicle 7 as the twenty-fifth chapter of the sūtra — after the Chapter on the Merits of the Dharma-Teacher and the Bodhisattva Never Disparaging Chapter of Fascicle 6, and before the *Dhāraṇī* Chapter, the Chapter on King Śubhavyūha of Old, and the Chapter on the Encouragement of Samantabhadra of Fascicle 7.[^4] The chapter opens with Bodhisattva Akṣayamati (*Wújìnyì* 無盡意) posing the question as a narrative hook:

> "World-Honored One! For what causes and conditions is the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara (*Guānshìyīn* 觀世音) so named?" (T0262, Fascicle 7, Universal Gate Chapter; translation mine)

The Buddha's answer to Akṣayamati forms the argumentative backbone of the chapter:

> "Good son! Suppose there are immeasurable hundreds of thousands of myriads of millions of beings undergoing various sufferings and distress; if they hear of the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara and single-mindedly invoke his name, Avalokiteśvara will instantly contemplate their voices, and all will attain liberation." (Ibid.; translation mine)

This answer establishes the two structural verbs of the chapter: *yīxīn chēngmíng* 一心稱名 (the sentient being's action — "single-mindedly invoke the name") and *guān qí yīnshēng* 觀其音聲 (the bodhisattva's action — "contemplate their voices"). The two form a mutual pair: the being invokes, the bodhisattva contemplates; what is invoked is the *name*, what is contemplated is the *sound*. This paired structure signals that "Avalokiteśvara" as a name is not a unidirectional object-of-appeal, but a bidirectional verb-relation — the being's invocation and the bodhisattva's hearing form, in a single instant, a mutual subject-object reflection (*néng-suǒ xiāng-zhào* 能所相照). The structure bypasses intercessory prayer's "caller → called → response" unidirectional chain and substitutes a "the invoker and the contemplator meet within the instant of sound" bidirectional structure.

The body of the chapter consists of the *seven perils* section. In seven hazardous situations — fire peril, water peril, *rākṣasa* peril, sword-and-staff peril, demon peril, cangue-and-shackles peril, and enemy-bandit peril — *invoking the name* (*chēng qí míng-hào* 稱其名號) is in every case the shared rescue-action.[^5] The structural pattern of the seven-peril template is: being placed in a concrete peril → "one who invokes the name of the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara" or "one who hears the invocation of the name of the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara" → the peril "immediately obtains liberation" / "is all severed and destroyed" / "will attain liberation." The seven sections repeat the same structure, establishing the "name-invocation → responsive manifestation" capacity-response pattern as the skeletal backbone of the chapter's exposition.

Beyond the seven perils, there is the section on the *three poisons* (lust, hatred, and delusion):

> "If there are beings greatly given to lust, who constantly recollect and revere the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, they will attain freedom from lust. If greatly given to hatred, who constantly recollect and revere Avalokiteśvara, they will attain freedom from hatred. If greatly given to delusion, who constantly recollect and revere Avalokiteśvara, they will attain freedom from delusion." (Ibid.; translation mine)

Note that in this three-poisons section, the verb has shifted from the seven perils' "invoking the name" (*chēngmíng* 稱名) to "constantly recollect and revere" (*chángniàn gōngjìng* 常念恭敬) — *niàn* and *jìng* replace *chēng* as the main verbs. This is internal evidence, within the prose itself, for §3.2's prose/verse dual-track argument: even within the prose, "name-invocation" and "recollection/reverence" are *not* fully interchangeable synonyms — the seven perils are external rescue (use *chēng*), the three poisons are inward transformation of afflictions (use *niàn* and *jìng*).

The chapter's merit-field quantification section establishes an equivalence-judgment: "one name equals six billion two hundred million bodhisattva names":

> "Suppose someone upheld the names of as many bodhisattvas as the sands of sixty-two billion Ganges rivers, and for the full course of their life offered food, clothing, bedding, and medicine to them… and suppose another person upheld the name of the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, bowing and making offerings even for a single moment — the merits of these two persons are exactly equal, without difference, and will not be exhausted in hundreds of thousands of myriads of millions of kalpas." (Ibid.; translation mine)

This passage serves as the direct sūtra-basis for Zhiyi's "the perfect-one is singular, partial-ones are many" quantitative ranking in §4.3 — the Universal Gate itself already establishes a "one name = six billion two hundred million names" equivalence-judgment, and Zhiyi in his *Commentary on the Words* reads this equivalence through a perfect/partial hierarchical framework.

The *thirty-three responsive bodies* section enumerates the forms Avalokiteśvara manifests according to the beings ripe for liberation — Buddha-body, *pratyekabuddha*-body, *śrāvaka*-body, Brahmā-king body, Śakra body, Sovereign-Heaven body, Great-Sovereign-Heaven body, Heavenly-General body, Vaiśravaṇa body, petty-king body, elder body, householder body, official body, *brāhmaṇa* body, bodies of *bhikṣu / bhikṣuṇī / upāsaka / upāsikā*, the wife-bodies of the elder/householder/official/*brāhmaṇa* classes, boy and girl bodies, bodies of deva / *nāga* / *yakṣa* / *gandharva* / *asura* / *garuḍa* / *kiṃnara* / *mahoraga* / human / non-human, and the Vajrapāṇi body.[^6] Strictly counted, Kumārajīva's version actually lists nineteen body-forms; the traditional "thirty-three" derives from later compilers' alternative combining. In this chapter's §3.1 three-karmic architecture argument, the section serves as structural reinforcement for "the responsive-body structure mirrors the reverence-subject structure": the bodhisattva manifests downward in nineteen or thirty-three bodies (undifferentiated response), while Never Disparaging bows upward to beings of the six realms (undifferentiated reverence) — both directions are structural operations of the Lotus's one-vehicle equality doctrine.

The verses at the end of the chapter are the core material for §3.2's argument. The verses take "*niàn bǐ guānyīn lì*" 念彼觀音力 ("recollect the power of that Avalokita") as the skeletal phrase: across twelve hazards (fire-pit, vast ocean, Mount Sumeru, the *vajra* mountain, enemy bandits, royal peril, cangue-and-shackles, curses, evil *rākṣasas*, savage beasts, vipers and serpents, thunderclouds), each time the same phrase "recollect the power of that Avalokita" is the hinge of liberation — "recollect the power of that Avalokita, and the fire-pit turns into a pool"; "recollect the power of that Avalokita, and the waves cannot submerge"; "recollect the power of that Avalokita, and one rests like the sun in empty space"; "recollect the power of that Avalokita, and a single hair cannot be injured"…[^7] The closing verse reads:

> "The wondrous sound of Avalokita, the Brahma-sound, the tide-sound of the ocean, / surpassing all sounds of the world — therefore let one constantly recollect, / recollection upon recollection, let no doubt arise. / Avalokiteśvara the pure and holy, amid suffering, agony, and the pangs of death, / is able to be the refuge and support." (Ibid.; translation mine)

The main verb of the chapter's prose is *chēng* 稱 (single-mindedly invoke the name, invoke the name-title), while the main verb of the verses is *niàn* 念 (recollect that Avalokita's power, therefore constantly recollect, recollection upon recollection let no doubt arise). This prose/verse verbal differentiation is the canonical basis of the §3.2 dual-track argument.

### 2.2 The Expedient Means Chapter (T0262, Fascicle 1)

The Expedient Means Chapter, Chapter 2, stands in Fascicle 1 as the first narrative chapter following the opening Introductory Chapter. Its core doctrinal content consists of the *ultimate-reality* (*zhūfǎ shí-xiàng* 諸法實相) teaching of the "ten suchnesses" (appearance, nature, substance, power, function, cause, condition, effect, retribution, ultimate coherence of beginning and end) and the "opening the three to reveal the one" (the ten-direction buddha-realms contain only a one-vehicle dharma, there being neither two nor three except as the Buddha's expedient teaching).[^8] This chapter does not treat the doctrinal content of Expedient Means as a whole — that belongs to the theme of Lotus *teaching-classification*, not to the scope of name-invocation verbal grammar. This chapter extracts only the *bodily-karma verses* and the *scattered-mind single-invocation verses* of the Expedient Means verse-section, as sūtra-evidence for §3.1's bodily-karma architecture and §3.3's scattered-mind / single-minded dual pole.

**The bodily-karma verses**: the Expedient Means verses enumerate the causes and conditions of completing the Buddha-path. Among many external actions — building stūpas, building images, making paintings, singing praises, piling sand — every such act is recognized as a cause of Buddhahood. The most minimal form of bodily-karma reads:

> "Some people bow in worship, / or merely press their palms together, / or raise even one hand, / or slightly bow the head — / using these to make offerings to images, / they will gradually behold immeasurable Buddhas." (T0262, Fascicle 1, Expedient Means Chapter; translation mine)

"Raising even one hand" and "slightly bowing the head" are the lowest thresholds of bodily-karma — bodily acts that can hardly be reduced any further. The structural significance of this passage: the Lotus accepts the most minimal bodily-karma as already an avenue to the Buddha-path, forming — together with Never Disparaging's "only performing bows" complete-reverence model in §2.3 — the two poles of the bodily-karma spectrum (minimal / complete).

The *children-at-play piling sand* passage pushes the bodily-karma threshold still further down, to the level of children's games:

> "Even if only children at play / pile sand to form a Buddha-stūpa — / such persons, one and all, / have already completed the Buddha-path." (Ibid.; translation mine)

The structural significance of this passage: Expedient Means accepts that **non-conscious bodily-karma** (children at play) also enters the Buddha-path — the establishment of bodily-karma does not require the full activation of mental-karma. This runs in parallel with §3.3's scattered-mind single-invocation argument: scattered-mind works (one need not be single-minded) / children's play works (one need not be self-aware) / a single invocation works (one need not hold the name for long) — these three "works" set Expedient Means's lowest thresholds for invocation, offering, and reverence.

**The scattered-mind single-invocation verses** (the core sūtra-evidence for §3.3):

> "If a person with a scattered mind / enters a stūpa or shrine, / and once invokes 'Homage to the Buddha' — / such persons, one and all, have already completed the Buddha-path." (Ibid.; translation mine)

These four lines constitute the Lotus's lowest threshold for name-invocation: scattered mind (not requiring single-mindedness), entering a stūpa or shrine (not requiring meditation practice), a single invocation (not requiring sustained holding), "Homage to the Buddha" (not requiring a designated Buddha-name) — four negating conditions, each pushing the threshold to its minimum — while the fruition is "have already completed the Buddha-path," the highest fruition. This is one end of the §3.3 single-sūtra dual-pole argument.

### 2.3 The Bodhisattva Never Disparaging Chapter (T0262, Fascicle 6)

The Bodhisattva Never Disparaging Chapter, Chapter 20, stands in Fascicle 6, after the Chapter on the Merits of the Dharma-Teacher and before the Chapter on the Tathāgata's Spiritual Power. The chapter recounts Bodhisattva Never Disparaging's reverence-practice during the semblance-dharma final age of Buddha King of Awesome Sound (*Wēiyīn-wáng fó* 威音王佛):[^9]

> "Mahāsthāmaprāpta! For what causes and conditions is this bhikṣu called Never Disparaging? Whenever this bhikṣu saw anyone — whether *bhikṣu*, *bhikṣuṇī*, *upāsaka*, or *upāsikā* — he would bow in reverence and praise them, saying: 'I deeply revere you all, and dare not treat you with contempt. Why? Because you all practice the bodhisattva path, and shall all become buddhas.'" (T0262, Fascicle 6, Bodhisattva Never Disparaging Chapter; translation mine)

Never Disparaging's practice has three distinctive features: **First**, **undifferentiated attention to his object** — he bows equally to any member of the four assemblies; **Second**, **three-karmic integration of the action** — bowing (bodily-karma), uttering praise (vocal-karma), and deep respect (mental-karma) arise simultaneously; **Third**, **extreme minimalism of method** — "he did not specialize in reciting scriptures, but only performed bows," his entire practice being this one reverence-and-utterance act.

The structure of this chapter provides §3.1's three-karmic argument with its clearest canonical skeleton: Never Disparaging gathers body, speech, and mind karmas into a single reverence act, and the three karmas are not decomposable into sequence or hierarchy — the bodily act of bowing, the mental motion of deep respect, and the verbal act of saying "I dare not treat you with contempt, for you all shall become buddhas" arise together in every instance of reverence.

The chapter's closing section forms a cross-sūtra mirror with P12:[^10]

> "When this bhikṣu was approaching death, from the open sky he heard, in its entirety, the *Lotus Sūtra*'s twenty thousand million verses previously spoken by Buddha King of Awesome Sound, and was able to uphold them in full. Thereupon he attained, as stated above, the purification of the eye-faculty, and of the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind faculties." (Ibid.; translation mine)

Never Disparaging's *final-moment purification of the six sense-faculties* — this is the P12 *Śūraṅgama* "gather in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted" teaching presented in another vector. P12 is **gathering the roots inward** (the six faculties quiescent and integrated in a single buddha-recollecting mind); Never Disparaging is **gathering the roots outward** (the six faculties purified at the terminus of reverence toward beings). The former's fruit is certified through stillness-cultivation; the latter's fruit is evoked through reverence. Both return to the purification of the six sense-faculties, but in opposite vectors — §3.1 takes this cross-sūtra mirror as structural evidence that "bodily-karma reverence is itself a complete form of buddha-recollection," without needing to import external argument from other buddha-recollection traditions.

### 2.4 The T0263 Guangshiyin Parallel and an Overview of Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words*

The *Sūtra of the Correct Dharma-Flower* (*Zhèng Fǎhuá Jīng* 正法華經, T0263) was translated by Dharmarakṣa (*Zhú Fǎhù* 竺法護) in the seventh year of Taikang (286 CE) of the Western Jin — the earliest extant complete Chinese translation of the *Lotus Sūtra*, predating Kumārajīva's T0262 by 120 years.[^11] The corresponding chapter in T0263 stands in Fascicle 10 as Chapter 23 (T0263 contains 27 chapters total, Kumārajīva's T0262 has 28; the chapter divisions differ), with the translated title "Universal Gate Chapter on the Light of the World's Sounds" (*Guāngshìyīn pǔmén pǐn* 光世音普門品), rather than "Universal Gate Chapter on the Contemplator of the World's Sounds." The key verbal difference between the two translations: T0262 uses *yīxīn chēngmíng* 一心稱名 ("single-mindedly invoke the name"), while T0263 uses *yīxīn zì-guī* 一心自歸, *jīshǒu guī-mìng* 稽首歸命, and *shēn-mìng zì-guī* 身命自歸 — Kumārajīva's *chēng* is vocal-karma vocalization, whereas Dharmarakṣa's *guī* ("turn toward, entrust") is body-mind orientation. More crucially, T0263's name-invocation merit-comparison section contains explicitly the four characters *zhíchí míng-hào* 執持名號 ("holding the name"):

> "Even if one made offerings to these immeasurable bodhisattvas, that would not equal once turning to Guangshiyin — bowing the head, making salutation, and holding the name — whose merit surpasses the former." (T0263, Fascicle 10; translation mine)

This is among the earliest Chinese-translation attestations of the "holding the name" verbal grammar — roughly 114 years earlier than Kumārajīva's T0366 *Amitābha Sūtra* (402 CE).[^12] This chronological fact supplies external corroboration for the "three-sūtra parallel" argument of P11 §3.3: the Lotus name-invocation tradition (T0263, 286 CE) and the Pure Land name-layer (T0366, 402 CE) are *parallel crystallizations* in Chinese of the Indian prototype of name-invocation — both predating the formal establishment of Chinese Pure Land or Chan schools by several centuries. §V's Misreading Three will use this chronological fact to dispatch the "Lotus buddha-recollection vs. Pure Land buddha-recollection" sectarian-comparativist framework.

Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words of the Lotus Sūtra* (*Fǎhuá Wénjù* 法華文句, T1718), the authoritative Sui-dynasty commentary on the *Lotus Sūtra*, was orally delivered by Zhiyi and recorded by Guanding — ten fascicles in forty major sections, collected as Taishō Volume 34.[^13] §IV of this chapter will fully unfold the three-layered structure of T1718's Fascicle 10 Universal Gate exposition (the ten-fold pairings, the explication of the ten senses of "universal," and the subdivision of name-invocation under capacity-response). Two points mark the ground here: **First**, T1718 uses the four exegetical methods (exegesis by *causes-and-conditions*, by *teachings*, by *origin-and-trace*, and by *contemplation of mind*) as its interpretive backbone, sectioning each chapter accordingly — standard Tiantai commentarial method. **Second**, T1718 opens its Universal Gate commentary with the remark that "this chapter is, along the road, a royal sūtra; those who have lectured on it are many… there are besides two fascicles of *Private Notes*" — the *Private Notes* being the *Profound Meaning of Avalokiteśvara* (*Guānyīn Xuányì* 觀音玄義, T1726) and the *Commentary on the Meaning of Avalokiteśvara* (*Guānyīn Yìshū* 觀音義疏, T1728). Together with the Universal Gate exposition of the *Words*, the three constitute Tiantai's Avalokiteśvara / Universal-Gate exegetical cycle. This chapter's §IV focuses on the structural skeleton of the *Words*.

## III. Core Argument: The Lotus's Three-Karmic Architecture, Dual Track, and Dual Pole

### 3.1 Three-Karmic Architecture: Vocal-Karma Name-Invocation, Bodily-Karma Reverence, Mental-Karma Deep Respect

The first entry-point for correcting the Western flattened reading of name-invocation is the **three-karmic architecture**. Within T0262 itself, Lotus name-invocation is not a single-karma vocal action but an integrated practice of all three karmas operating at once.

**Vocal-karma name-invocation** centers on the Universal Gate Chapter. The paired structure of "single-mindedly invoke the name" and "contemplate the voices" (§2.1) already establishes the action-grammar of vocal-karma: in a single instant, the invocation meets the bodhisattva's hearing — this is the structural substance of vocal-karma name-invocation, not a unidirectional message from an invoker to an addressee. The seven-perils section, the three-poisons section, the merit-field section, and the thirty-three-responsive-bodies section all take vocal-karma name-invocation as the main action, but each embeds that action within a larger structure (seven perils in external rescue, three poisons in the transmutation of internal afflictions, the merit-field in merit-quantification, the thirty-three responses in downward manifestation). Vocal-karma name-invocation is therefore not an isolable single action but an action-node operating within a complete structural network.

**Bodily-karma reverence** centers on Never Disparaging. Never Disparaging's "only performing bows" (§2.3) practice elevates bodily-karma into a complete form of buddha-recollection — no recitation, no contemplation, no seated meditation, no *mantra*-holding, the entire practice consisting of this one reverence-action. Note especially that Never Disparaging's object of reverence is **not a Buddha-image, not a bodhisattva, not a chosen deity** — it is **the four assemblies who have not yet become buddhas**. This is the structural distinctiveness of Lotus bodily-karma buddha-recollection: the object of reverence is not an already-accomplished buddha but a future buddha — every sentient being is a future buddha, and reverence is the embodied acknowledgment of the fact that "they all shall become buddhas." This structure wholly sidesteps the intercessory-prayer model of "appealing to an already-accomplished holy object" and also sidesteps the mantra-recitation model of "syncing one's own vibration with a sonic structure" — both are structurally untenable within Never Disparaging's bodily-karma buddha-recollection.

The bodily-karma verses of Expedient Means (§2.2) supply the lowest end of the bodily-karma threshold — "raise one hand, slightly bow the head" and "children at play, piling sand into a Buddha-stūpa." Together with Never Disparaging's full-reverence, they form the two poles of the bodily-karma spectrum. From the lowest threshold (raising one hand) to the full action (only performing bows), with pressing palms together, offering flowers, chanting praises, painting images, and building stūpas and shrines in between — Lotus bodily-karma buddha-recollection is spectrum-rich; any bodily act can serve as an entry point.

**Mental-karma deep respect** centers on Never Disparaging's "I deeply revere you all, and dare not treat you with contempt." In the phrase "deep respect" (*shēn jìng* 深敬), the character *shēn* 深 signals the *depth* of the mental-karma (not surface politeness but a deep-layered acknowledgment of the other's future buddhahood), and the character *jìng* 敬 signals the *texture* of the mental-karma (not the trembling of awe, not the gaze of admiration, but the recognition of an equal as a future buddha). Deep respect is not an externally observable action, but it threads through the bodily act of bowing and the verbal act of uttering praise — a bow without deep respect is form, an utterance without deep respect is lip-service. The structural significance of Never Disparaging's three-karmic integration lies exactly here: the three karmas are not three independent actions but rather the mental-karma deep respect serving as inner motive force, with the bodily-karma bow and vocal-karma utterance as outer expressions — the three arise simultaneously in a single event, and the absence of any one voids the whole.

The significance of the three-karmic architecture: within T0262 itself, Lotus name-invocation has already been expanded from a single vocal-karma into an integration of all three karmas. Universal-Gate name-invocation (speech) + Never Disparaging's reverence (body) + Never Disparaging's deep respect (mind) mutually support one another within T0262; no single karma can carry the full meaning of Lotus buddha-recollection alone. If a Western reader takes a model of name-invocation solely from the Universal Gate Chapter, they will mistake Lotus buddha-recollection as equivalent to vocal-karma vocalization. But once Never Disparaging's bodily-karma and deep respect are brought into the same architecture, the three-karmic nature of Lotus buddha-recollection becomes self-evident.

This three-karmic architecture is not an external interpretive framework imposed on the text; it is the Lotus's own three-chapter configuration — the Universal Gate Chapter (vocal-karma central stage), Never Disparaging (bodily-karma + mental-karma central stage), and Expedient Means (bodily-karma threshold + scattered-mind single-invocation support) — the three chapters within T0262 each playing their role and together constituting the complete narrative of Lotus buddha-recollection.

### 3.2 Prose/Verse Dual Track: *Chēng* Going Outward, *Niàn* Returning Inward

The second layer of argument enters a more precise structure: within the Universal Gate Chapter itself, the prose (*chánghang* 長行) and verses (*jìsòng* 偈頌) display a systematic differentiation between the two main verbs *chēng* 稱 and *niàn* 念.

The main verb of the prose is *chēng*: "single-mindedly invoke the name," "invoke his name-title," "one who invokes the name of the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara," "one who hears another invoking the name of the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara" (here *chēng* refers still to another's invocation), "all together utter 'Homage to Avalokiteśvara,' because they invoke his name" — the verbal structure of the seven-perils section runs entirely on *chēng*. Although the three-poisons section substitutes "constantly recollect and revere" for "invoke the name," its context remains outward (freedom from lust, hatred, and delusion are all an outward casting-off). The prose's structural concern is **the liberation of external perils** — fire, water, *rākṣasa*, sword-and-staff, demon, cangue-and-shackles, and enemy-bandit are all external, liberation is escape from external conditions, and the action is outward vocalization, *chēng*.

The main verb of the verses shifts to *niàn*: "recollect the power of that Avalokita, the fire-pit turns into a pool"; "recollect the power of that Avalokita, the waves cannot submerge"; "recollect the power of that Avalokita, one rests like the sun in empty space"… — across twelve hazards, every one uses "recollect the power of that Avalokita" as the hinge of liberation. The closing is still clearer: "therefore let one constantly recollect, recollection upon recollection let no doubt arise" — *niàn* and *niàn-niàn* have replaced the prose's *chēng* and *chēngmíng*.

This prose/verse verbal differentiation is not a literary accident of the chapter; it is a structural division of verbal labor. *Chēng*'s action is outward vocalization (vocal-karma activation, sound issued forth, the mutual subject-object reflection constituted in the instant of sound). *Niàn*'s action is inward power-holding (mental-karma preservation, the object being not the Buddha-*name* but the Buddha-*power*, power-holding sustained along the flow of consciousness). The prose handles the outward dimension of *chēng* — beings invoke amid peril, the bodhisattva instantly contemplates the voices and responds; the verses handle the inward dimension of *niàn* — after the invocation-sound, beings hold the bodhisattva's power in mind, and as that power manifests, the peril is transformed.

The character *lì* 力 ("power") in "recollect the power of that Avalokita" is the key to this dual track. The object of *chēng* is the *name* (Avalokiteśvara's name-title), while the object of *niàn* is the *power* (Avalokiteśvara's rescue-merit). *Name* is verbal sign; *power* is the merit-body itself — name-invocation is the action at the outward-vocalization level, power-recollection is the action that returns to the merit-body at the inward level. The two operate bidirectionally within a single practice: first invoke the name, then recollect the power; *chēng* activates, *niàn* preserves; *chēng* brings subject and object into mutual reflection, *niàn* brings merit into manifest presence. This dual-track structure wholly dismantles the flattened mantra reading of name-invocation as simple sonic repetition — *chēng* is only one outward face of the dual track, and the inward return of *niàn* is where rescue actually occurs.

The dual track resonates with the NIAN verbal-grammar longitudinal axis: P12's gathering-roots recollection handles the inward integration (the six faculties quiescent within a single buddha-recollecting mind), while P13's name-invocation recollection handles both outward and inward (outward invocation activation + inward power-recollection return). P13 does not repeat P12's inward structure but makes P12's inward structure the inward one track of a dual track, and adds the outward track of name-invocation to form the dual-track whole. From the longitudinal axis, P12's unidirectional structure expands in P13 into a bidirectional structure — the development of verbal grammar is not a single-line deepening but a bidirectional unfolding.

### 3.3 Scattered-Mind Single Invocation / Single-Minded Name-Invocation: Single-Sūtra Dual Pole

The third layer of argument addresses the most taut structure within T0262: the dual-pole opposition between Expedient Means's "a person with a scattered mind invokes once 'Homage to the Buddha,' and has already completed the Buddha-path" and the Universal Gate's "single-mindedly invoke the name, and immediately attain liberation."

Expedient Means's *scattered-mind single-invocation verses* (§2.2) drive the threshold of name-invocation to its minimum: scattered mind (not requiring single-mindedness), a single invocation (not requiring sustained holding), inside a stūpa or shrine (not requiring meditation practice) — four negating conditions, each pushing the threshold to the lowest conceivable point. Yet the fruition is "have already completed the Buddha-path" — not a minor fruit (*arhat*, *pratyekabuddha*), not a middle fruit (arousing *bodhicitta*, attaining forbearance), but the highest fruit (Buddhahood already complete).

The Universal Gate's *single-minded name-invocation* section (§2.1) drives the threshold of name-invocation to its maximum: single-minded (not scattered), name-invocation (clear vocal activation), and Avalokiteśvara's immediate contemplation of the voices (the bodhisattva's instantaneous response) — three conditions requiring the mental-karma to be fully concentrated, the vocal-karma to be distinctly activated, and subject-object to be mutually reflected in a single instant. The fruition is "all attain liberation" — not completing the Buddha-path, but the liberation-from-suffering *at that very moment* (fire cannot burn, water cannot drown, sword and staff are shattered…).

The structural key-points of this dual pole:

**First, the cross-configuration of threshold and fruition**. Scattered-mind invocation has the lowest threshold with the highest fruit (Buddhahood); single-minded invocation has the highest threshold with a relatively concrete fruit (present-life liberation from suffering). This cross-configuration is not a simple "easy vs. difficult" ordering — if ordered by linear difficulty, a scattered-mind single invocation producing Buddhahood should be harder to achieve than single-minded name-invocation producing present-life rescue (one yields Buddhahood, the other yields mere rescue). But the Lotus trades scattered mind *for* Buddhahood, and single-mindedness *for* present-life rescue — a non-linear structural configuration reflecting the Lotus's doctrinal judgment on "what name-invocation is actually doing": the deepest meaning of name-invocation is not the acquisition of worldly efficacy, but the *minimal-threshold acknowledgment of the fact that all beings have already completed the Buddha-path*; under scattered mind, still being able to issue even a single invocation is itself the proof that this acknowledgment has occurred. Once the acknowledgment has occurred, the Buddha-path is already established — note the completion-aspect of "have already (*yǐ* 已) completed." Present-life rescue is, by contrast, more local, more specific response-to-capacity — amid peril, with single-mindedness, with invocation, Avalokiteśvara responds; three concrete conditions must simultaneously be in place.

**Second, the temporal structure of the dual pole**. The scattered-mind single-invocation passage uses "have already completed the Buddha-path" — the completion-aspect (*yǐ* 已); the single-minded name-invocation passage uses "will instantly contemplate their voices, all attain liberation" — the immediate-aspect (*jí* 即). The Lotus's two name-invocation fruits employ both completion-aspect and immediate-aspect tenses at once: the completion-aspect is time-retrospective (the invocation event has already occurred, the Buddha-path has already been completed, this is located in the three-temporal continuum); the immediate-aspect is time-simultaneous (invocation and contemplation of the voices arise within the same instant). The dual-pole of temporal structure mutually supports the dual-pole of threshold and fruition — the retrospective aspect suits the extremely low threshold (one fleeting scattered-mind invocation is enough); the simultaneous aspect suits the concrete threshold (the immediate response to single-minded name-invocation).

**Third, the capacity-response division of labor in the dual pole**. Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words* divides the text under a "good capacity / bad capacity / response" capacity-response structure (discussed in detail in §4.2). The capacity of scattered-mind single-invocation is the *distant-cause capacity* — this lifetime's single invocation is the seed of distant-cause Buddhahood; the capacity of single-minded name-invocation is the *near-fruit capacity* — this moment's invocation is the trigger for present-life rescue. All beings possess the distant-cause capacity (because at some instant every being has, or will, invoke once with scattered mind); specific beings possess the near-fruit capacity (because only some beings, in specific perils, can invoke with single-mindedness). The Lotus uses its dual pole to cover both types of capacity — within a single sūtra self-sufficiently addressing the full spectrum from the universal lowest-level capacity-response to the specific highest-level capacity-response.

**Fourth, the dual pole is not a cross-sūtra construction**. The argumentation of this dual-pole structure is completed entirely within T0262 — Expedient Means in Fascicle 1, the Universal Gate in Fascicle 7, Never Disparaging in Fascicle 6, three chapters within the same sūtra mutually supporting one another. P13 therefore does not require, as P12 did, the T0366 / T0945 cross-sūtra dual-axis (holding-the-name ↔ gathering-the-roots / one-mind ↔ pure-recollection) as its principal argument — T0262's internal dual pole is self-sufficient. This structural choice reflects the design logic of Part V after its opening (P12 T0945): at the opening declaration, cross-sūtra comparison is required in order to establish the overall proposition of the Mahāyāna sūtras as a collective deepening layer; after the opening, each sūtra unfolds its distinctive deepening path through its own internal structure. The Lotus displays this through its single-sūtra dual pole; the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* through its final-age capacity-response; the *Brahmā Net Sūtra* through its protection of the precept-forms; the *Golden Light Sūtra* through its pre-practice repentance — each sūtra possesses its own internal structural density, requiring no external prop.

The three layers of the argument close on one another: the three-karmic architecture (body / speech / mind operating together), the prose/verse dual track (outward *chēng* / inward *niàn*), and the scattered-mind / single-minded dual pole (distant-cause capacity / near-fruit capacity). All three are completed within T0262 itself, drawing on no external interpretive framework from Pure Land, Chan, or Tiantai. This is the practice of NIAN-C's *cross-sectarian material discipline* in the second paper of Part V — the Lotus presents its own buddha-recollection face through its own text, and it is only from Part VI onward that sectarian divergences and their differing interpretive frameworks will be addressed.

## IV. Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words of the Lotus Sūtra* on the Universal Gate Chapter: The Tiantai Twin-Axis and a Thousand-Year Precursor

P10 §IV took Zhiyi's *Great Calming and Contemplation* exposition of the Sixteen Contemplations as its primary Tiantai material, treating the contemplation / stillness-cultivation axis. P13 §IV turns to Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words of the Lotus Sūtra* exposition of the Universal Gate Chapter, treating the name-invocation / action axis. The two papers establish within Tiantai exegesis a **Tiantai twin-axis** — the same commentator unfolding his exegetical skeleton across two different canonical works, stillness and action, contemplation and invocation, the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus* and the *Lotus Sūtra* — the two axes mutually supporting one another within Zhiyi's own system.

### 4.1 Ten-fold Pairings and the Explication of the Ten Senses of "Universal"

Zhiyi opens the Universal Gate commentary with a general outline: "comprehensive exegesis under ten pairs, specific exegesis under five words" (*tōng shì shí shuāng, bié shì wǔ zhī* 通釋十雙、別釋五隻).[^14] The ten pairs are **paired expositions** (two meanings each), placing "Avalokiteśvara" and "Universal Gate" at the two ends of each pair: person / dharma (Avalokiteśvara = person, Universal Gate = dharma); compassion-wisdom / loving-kindness-compassion (Avalokiteśvara = great compassion extracting suffering, Universal Gate = great loving-kindness bestowing joy); the two adornments (Avalokiteśvara = wisdom adornment, Universal Gate = merit adornment); *dharmakāya* / responsive-body; King of Medicines / wish-fulfilling-jewel king; hidden response / manifest response; self-oriented real-wisdom / other-oriented provisional-wisdom; origin-and-trace; the two causal seeds (realization-cause seed / condition-cause seed); the two virtues (wisdom-virtue / severance-virtue).

The structural significance of the ten pairs: Avalokiteśvara and Universal Gate are not two concepts but the two faces of a single concept — Avalokiteśvara is substance, Universal Gate is function; Avalokiteśvara is origin, Universal Gate is trace; Avalokiteśvara is the causal position, Universal Gate is the fruitional function. This paired structure echoes directly in §3.2's prose/verse dual-track: outward *chēng* and inward *niàn* are also two faces of a single matter, *chēng* activating mutual reflection, *niàn* returning the merit into manifest presence; the two are mutually substance-and-function, and removing one voids the whole. Zhiyi sets the root structure of "Avalokiteśvara and Universal Gate" as paired-bidirectional; this chapter sets the root structure of name-invocation buddha-recollection as outward-inward dual-track — the two resonate in structural isomorphism, though this chapter's dual track is not a simple application of Zhiyi's ten pairs but an independent verbal differentiation discovered within the prose/verse textual distinction itself.

After the ten pairs, Zhiyi's "specific exegesis under five words" analyzes character-by-character the five-character phrase "*Guān / Shì / Yīn / Pǔ / Mén*" 觀、世、音、普、門. In unfolding the "*Pǔ*" 普 character, he lists the **formal ten senses**: universality of loving-kindness-compassion, of the great vow, of practice, of severing afflictions, of entering the dharma-gates, of spiritual powers, of expedient means, of dharma-exposition, of bringing beings to full maturation, and of making offerings to all buddhas.[^15] A lexical note on these ten senses: Zhiyi names them with "*suǒwèi* 所謂" ("these are…") followed by the ten items as a comma-separated list; in the detailed exposition that follows, the fourth — "universality of severing afflictions" (*lí-huò pǔ* 離惑普) — is called "universality of cutting off afflictions" (*duàn-huò pǔ* 斷惑普). The heading and the detailed section use slightly different phrasings for the same meaning. This is the literal state of the CBETA text and not an extraction error.

The structural features of the ten senses: Zhiyi closes each sense with a *"only… may be called universal"* verdict — "only unconditioned compassion (not compassion arising from beings or from dharmas) may be called universal compassion"; "only the four noble truths understood as uncreated (not conditioned, not unarising, not incalculable) may be called universal great vow"; "only principle-wisdom traversing dharmas, where event *is itself* principle, may be called universal practice"; "only omniscient wisdom severing primal ignorance may be called universal cutting-off of afflictions"; "only the samādhi-of-all-samādhis that embraces every samādhi may be called universal entry into dharma-gates"; "only what is oriented to the ultimate-reality may be called universal spiritual power"; "only post-path transformation, perfectly pervasive in a single instant, may be called universal expedient means"; "only the single wondrous sound that calls the capacities of the ten dharma-realms may be called universal dharma-exposition"; "only showing-teaching-benefiting-delighting and leading beings to the three bodhis may be called universal maturation of beings"; "only the many practices resourcing the perfect wisdom may be called universal offering to the buddhas."[^16] This "*only… may be called universal*" verdict-structure means: "universal" is not spatial breadth of distribution but the *perfectness of action-mode*. Under this structure, the "universal" of name-invocation is not "invoking everywhere, broadly, at any place" — a spatial diffusion — but "the perfectness of the invocation itself: the act of invocation simultaneously possessing the complete perfection of the ten senses" — a quality of the action.

This verdict-structure directly reinforces §3.1's three-karmic argument: Lotus name-invocation's "perfect" (*yuán* 圓) is not expanded scope (from invoking one Buddha expanded to invoking all Buddhas) but deepened quality (in a single utterance of the name, simultaneously possessing the three karmas of body, speech, and mind; the *chēng-niàn* dual track; and the scattered-mind / single-minded dual pole in full perfection). Zhiyi's exegesis of the ten senses supplies authoritative late-Tang precedent for "perfect = deep, not broad."

### 4.2 The Capacity-Response Structure and the Three-fold Exposition of the Seven Perils

Zhiyi expounds the name-invocation section under a *capacity-and-response* (*jī-yìng* 機應) structure.[^17] His general answer is divided into four layers — number of persons, encountered suffering, hearing and invoking the name, and attaining liberation — and he partitions them into capacity and response: encountered suffering is the *bad capacity*, name-invocation is the *good capacity*; good and bad together constitute "capacity." "Attaining liberation" is the "response." This capacity-response structure provides pre–late-Tang authoritative support for §3.3's dual-pole argument: scattered-mind single-invocation is "the minimum threshold of good-capacity" (a fleeting scattered-mind moment is already the arising of good-capacity); single-minded name-invocation is "the full activation of good-capacity" (the concentrated state of single-mindedness is already the saturation of the good-capacity's activation); both operate within the capacity-response structure, but their qualities of capacity differ. The Lotus covers both ends with the scattered-mind low threshold and the single-minded high threshold; Zhiyi's capacity-response structure gives doctrinal foundation to both ends — the activation of capacity can take place at any threshold, the difference lying in the quality of that capacity, and thereby in the speed and depth of the response it summons.

Zhiyi further unfolds the capacity-response content of each peril through a *three-fold exposition*: each peril has three layers — the retribution-layer (from the hells up to the first *dhyāna* heaven, capacity-response is discussed), the karmic-layer (from the hells up to the "neither perception nor non-perception" heaven, capacity-response is discussed), and the affliction-layer (from the hells up to the *pratyekabuddha* stage, capacity-response is discussed). The seven perils, three poisons, and two wishes each unfold under this three-fold exposition. This three-fold exposition means that every name-invocation act can activate capacity-response across three levels simultaneously: the invocation relieves the present peril (retribution-layer), the invocation relieves the karma that produced that peril (karmic-layer), and the invocation relieves the root afflictions that produced that karma (affliction-layer). *A single invocation, three layers of capacity-response activated at once* — this is Zhiyi's pre–late-Tang exposition of "density-saturation in a single utterance of the name."

### 4.3 "The Perfect-One Is Singular, Partial-Ones Are Many": Zhiyi's Quantitative Ranking by Hierarchical Position

In his three-section treatment of "exhorting to hold the name" (*quàn-chí / gé-liàng / jié-tàn* 勸持／格量／結歎 — exhort, rank, and conclude-with-praise), Zhiyi cites his own *perfect / partial* ranking:[^18]

> "The wise one says: 'The perfect-one is singular, partial-ones are many' — to equate six billion two hundred million partial bodhisattvas with a single perfect bodhisattva." (T1718, Fascicle 10; translation mine)

This verdict answers directly to the merit-field quantification section of the Universal Gate Chapter (§2.1) — "upholding the names of as many bodhisattvas as the sands of sixty-two billion Ganges rivers… and another person upholding the name of Avalokiteśvara alone… the merits of these two persons are exactly equal." Zhiyi does not read this equivalence as a non-discriminating "one name = many names" leveling; he reads it as a quantitative-ranking judgment of "one perfect bodhisattva = six billion two hundred million partial bodhisattvas." In Zhiyi's reading, Avalokiteśvara is a perfect-position bodhisattva (subsumed under the Perfect Teaching), while the six billion two hundred million sands-of-the-Ganges bodhisattvas are partial-position bodhisattvas (subsumed under the Tripiṭaka, Common, and Distinct teachings); the merit of one perfect bodhisattva equals the merit of six billion two hundred million partial bodhisattvas — the difference in position offsets the disparity in numerical count.

The structural significance of this verdict: **Zhiyi replaces "phenomenal / principial" mental-cultivation partitioning with "perfect / partial" hierarchical quantitative ranking**. Late-Ming Pure Land studies (Ouyi Zhixu's *Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra*, etc.) take "phenomenal-holding / principial-holding" and "phenomenal one-mindedness / principial one-mindedness" as the primary axis for differentiating name-invocation,[^19] but Zhiyi's own interpretive vocabulary is "perfect / partial" *hierarchical position* rather than "phenomenal / principial" *mental cultivation*. P11 §2.5 has already discussed Zhixu's use of the six faiths (self / other / cause / effect / phenomenon / principle) plus the three provisions of faith, vow, and practice as "not sequenced and not rigidly three" to prove "a single utterance of the name simultaneously possesses the three provisions in full"[^20] — this is the late-Ming doctrine of name-invocation density saturation. In Zhiyi we find its thousand-year precursor: Zhiyi's quantitative ranking by "the perfect-one is singular, partial-ones are many" and Zhixu's cognitive premise of "faith-vow-practice simultaneously complete in a single utterance" share the same isomorphic thesis — they resonate on the unified claim that "the density of a single utterance of the name cannot be counted in numbers," though along different axes. Zhiyi's axis is hierarchical (perfect vs. partial); Zhixu's axis is cognitive (faith-vow-practice vs. sequenced/rigid-three). Both axes land on the same conclusion: the density of name-invocation cannot be quantified.

This thousand-year echo supplies structural significance for §IV of this chapter: the Tiantai twin-axis (P10's contemplation-method axis ↔ P13's name-invocation axis) is not merely Zhiyi's two commentarial works but, more broadly, Zhiyi as the thousand-year precursor of the name-invocation density-saturation doctrine, *cross-temporally continuous with late-Ming Zhixu on the verbal grammar of name-invocation*. Parts IV and V are joined within the Tiantai exegetical tradition — P11 takes late-Ming Zhixu as its representative commentator, P13 takes late-Tang Zhiyi as its representative commentator, and the two ends form a late-Ming → late-Tang reverse-chronological mirror on the shared thesis of "density-saturation in a single utterance of the name."

### 4.4 The "Gate" Character: The Middle-Way Ultimate-Reality Reading

Zhiyi's exegesis of the character *mén* 門 ("gate") reads:[^21]

> "The gate: entering emptiness from the conventional means emptiness is open but the conventional is blocked; entering the conventional from emptiness means the conventional is open but emptiness is blocked; if either is partially open, it is not universal; being blocked, it is not a gate. The middle way — neither empty nor conventional — is the correct openness into ultimate-reality, illuminating the two truths on both sides, and therefore is called universal; being correctly open, it is called a gate." (T1718, Fascicle 10; translation mine)

This is Zhiyi's final coordinate for "Universal Gate": **Universal Gate = middle-way ultimate-reality = simultaneously empty, conventional, and middle** (*jí kōng jí jiǎ jí zhōng* 即空即假即中). When the emptiness-contemplation is partially open, the conventional is blocked; when the conventional-contemplation is partially open, emptiness is blocked; either one-sided openness fails to be universal. Only the middle-way ultimate-reality, illuminating the two truths on both sides, is *correctly open* and therefore *universal-gate*.

This "simultaneously empty, conventional, and middle" middle-way ultimate-reality reading supplies a subtle but crucial reinforcement to this chapter's structure: it explains *why* Lotus name-invocation can cover both poles — scattered-mind single-invocation and single-minded name-invocation — simultaneously. If the nature of name-invocation leaned toward emptiness-contemplation (name-invocation as expedient of dependent-arising-emptiness), it would be hard to explain how scattered-mind invocation can yield Buddhahood (how can scattered mind enter emptiness?). If the nature of name-invocation leaned toward conventional-contemplation (name-invocation as the accumulation of truly-existent merit), single-minded name-invocation's present-life rescue would still only be merit cashed in (falling short of emptiness-liberation). Only middle-way ultimate-reality — in which name-invocation is simultaneously empty (in empty nature, no self-grasping, scattered mind no obstacle to Buddhahood), conventional (conventional existence, merit manifest, single-minded invocation attains rescue), and middle (the two truths illumined on both sides, substance and function non-dual) — can contain both poles without tilting to either side.

This middle-way ultimate-reality reading therefore provides the deepest doctrinal foundation for §3.3's dual-pole argument: the Lotus can cover the full spectrum of name-invocation with its scattered-mind / single-minded dual pole because the very substance of name-invocation *is* middle-way ultimate-reality — simultaneously empty, conventional, and middle. With a single-character exegesis of the "gate" in "Universal Gate," Zhiyi sets this doctrinal coordinate.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading One: Name-Invocation as the Buddhist Equivalent of Christian Intercessory Prayer

The popular reading — especially in Western comparative-religion contexts and in the first encounter of Christian-background readers with the Universal Gate Chapter — reads "single-mindedly invoke the name, all attain liberation" as structurally analogous to the Catholic intercessory-prayer structure of the *Litany of the Saints*. Under this reading, Avalokiteśvara is understood as analogous to Mary or to a canonized saint in the role of rescuer from peril; name-invocation is understood as an appeal to a transcendent rescuing agent; liberation is understood as the result of the rescuer's response to the prayer.

Correction: the Universal Gate structure — "single-mindedly invoke the name, Avalokiteśvara instantly contemplates the voices" — is *not* a unidirectional request → response chain but a bidirectional subject-object mutual-reflection meeting in a single instant. *Chēng* is the being's action; "contemplating the voices" is the bodhisattva's action; both arise in the same instant, not in causal succession. Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words*, with its capacity-response structure — "good capacity + bad capacity = capacity," "attainment of liberation = response" — further clarifies: the activation of capacity (invocation + peril) and the manifestation of response (the bodhisattva's contemplation of the voices and rescue) are two faces of a single event, not two temporally successive events. This structure is categorically different from the unidirectional chain of intercessory prayer — the former is a simultaneous mutual-reflection structure, the latter is a request → response temporally successive structure. If a Western reader imports the intercessory-prayer framework to receive Lotus name-invocation, they will mistake Avalokiteśvara for the Lotus version of a Catholic saint — a misreading that fails at the structural level.

### ❌ Misreading Two: Name-Invocation as Mechanical Sonic Mantra Recitation

The popular reading — common in comparative Indic-Buddhist studies and in yoga-Buddhism integration contexts — reads Universal-Gate name-invocation as a sonic-vibration practice analogous to *mantra* recitation. Under this reading, the sound "*Nāmó Guānshìyīn Púsà*" is taken as a sonic vibration that is itself possessed of salvific power, and the core of name-invocation is taken to be the repeated issuing of this sound.

Correction: the closing verses of the Universal Gate Chapter explicitly fold the verb *chēng* into the verb *niàn* — "therefore let one constantly recollect, recollection upon recollection let no doubt arise" — and the object of *niàn* is the *power* of Avalokiteśvara (the bodhisattva's merit-function), not the sound of the name itself (§3.2's dual-track argument). The verses' "recollect the power of that Avalokita" is repeated twelve times; each accomplishment is not an accumulation of sonic vibration but a concrete liberation-event in a specific peril, manifested through the bodhisattva's merit-power. Within the chapter itself, the prose-layer activates name-invocation and the verse-layer folds it into power-recollection — only reading both layers together yields the complete structure of Lotus buddha-recollection. Taking only the prose-layer's *chēng* and omitting the verse-layer's *niàn* narrows name-invocation to a sonic layer. This narrowing is structurally untenable from the text itself — the chapter has already, internally, folded *chēng* into *niàn*, without needing later commentators to do it.

### ❌ Misreading Three: Lotus Buddha-Recollection vs. Pure-Land Buddha-Recollection — Sectarian Comparativism

The popular reading — common in modern Chinese Buddhist historiography and in Western Pure-Land / Tiantai specialist studies — places Lotus name-invocation and Pure Land name-invocation within a sectarian-comparative framework, asking: "whose buddha-recollection is superior, Lotus's Avalokiteśvara-invocation or Pure Land's Amitābha-invocation?" Under this reading, the two traditions are taken as parallel sectarian practices whose comparative evaluation is a matter of sectarian scholarship.

Correction: the methodological premise of this reading does not hold. T0263 (Dharmarakṣa, 286 CE), in its "Universal Gate Chapter on the Light of the World's Sounds," explicitly contains the four characters *zhíchí míng-hào* 執持名號 ("holding the name") — 114 years earlier than T0366 (Kumārajīva, 402 CE) and several centuries earlier than the Chinese Pure Land patriarchs (Tanluan, c. 476–542; Daochuo, 562–645; Shandao, 613–681).[^22] The Lotus name-invocation tradition and the Pure Land name-layer were, at the Indic prototype level, parallel existents — not later sectarian inventions. The "Lotus vs. Pure Land" comparative framework is a construction of modern Chinese Buddhist historiography (especially the Republican-era sectarian-scholarship formation), not a structure internal to the Lotus or Pure Land canonical texts themselves. P11 §3.3 already established the "three-sūtra parallel" argument — T0360's vow-layer, T0365's contemplation-layer, and T0366's name-layer as three channels of parallel crystallization within Indic Pure Land thought-space.[^23] A fourth point can now be added: T0263's Lotus name-invocation and T0366's Pure Land name-invocation are two parallel Chinese crystallizations of the Indic name-invocation tradition — both pre-dating the formation of sectarian schools by several centuries. Name-invocation does not belong to any sect, and cannot be adequately understood within a sectarian-comparative framework.

### ⚠️ Philological Correction: Phenomenal/Principial Name-Invocation Is Not Zhiyi's Vocabulary

Under the influence of late-Ming and modern Pure Land studies, the partition of *phenomenal-holding / principial-holding* (*shì-chí / lǐ-chí*) and *phenomenal one-mindedness / principial one-mindedness* (*shì-yīxīn / lǐ-yīxīn*) is often retroactively attributed to Zhiyi — as if Zhiyi were the pre–late-Tang originator of this partition. This attribution is philologically untenable.

CBETA entity-verification of T1718 Fascicle 10's Universal Gate exegesis[^24]: Zhiyi in this chapter's exegesis **makes no phenomenal/principial partitioning of "name-invocation" itself**. The passage closest to this partition is the "universality of offerings to all buddhas" section's external-event / internal-contemplation partition — "if one performs outward offerings, then with a single meal or a single flower or a single incense one makes universal offering to all buddhas… if one performs inner contemplation, then perfect wisdom leads many practices, perfect wisdom is called *buddha*, and the many practices resourcing perfect wisdom are themselves offerings to the Buddha." This is indeed an event/principle-type partition, but **the object is *offering*, not name-invocation**. Zhiyi's own judgment-language on name-invocation is *hierarchical-positional quantitative ranking* (the perfect-one is singular, partial-ones are many, §4.3), not *mental-cultivation partitioning* (phenomenal-holding / principial-holding). The phenomenal/principial partition of name-invocation stems from Ming-Qing Pure Land studies (Ouyi Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* and other late-Ming Pure Land texts crystallize the usage), not from Zhiyi's vocabulary.[^25]

This correction continues the philological-discipline mirror-chain of P10 §V ("Zhiyi's 'four buddha-recollections' — name-invocation / image-contemplation / visualization / ultimate-reality — is not the original wording of T1750") and P11 §V ("Yinguang's 'even if an ancient Buddha returned' praise comes from the main text of T1762").[^26] Parts IV through V share a single set of CBETA entity-verification standards. When citing patriarchal interpretations, one should distinguish whether the interpretation is original to the CBETA source or is a later commentator's / editor's compilation. This distinction is not pedantic error-hunting but a safeguard against flattening the historical layers within the exegetical tradition — Zhiyi has Zhiyi's vocabulary, Zhixu has Zhixu's vocabulary, and the phenomenal/principial partition has its Ming-Qing context; each occupies its own position, and mixed retroactive attribution costs historical precision.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Essential Restatement

Within T0262 itself, Lotus name-invocation is an integrated three-karmic practice — vocal-karma name-invocation centers on the Universal Gate Chapter, bodily-karma reverence centers on Never Disparaging, and mental-karma deep respect converges at Never Disparaging's "I deeply revere you all." This three-karmic architecture dismantles the Western reader's flattening of the Universal Gate Chapter under either the Christian intercessory-prayer framework or the Indic mantra-recitation framework. Within the Universal Gate Chapter itself, the prose's verb *chēng* and the verses' verb *niàn* display a systematic differentiation — *chēng*'s outward vocalization activates subject-object mutual reflection, while *niàn*'s inward power-holding returns to the merit-body; the two operate in parallel within a single chapter as the Lotus's own structural self-limitation on the verb *chēng*. T0262 further unfolds the scattered-mind single-invocation (Expedient Means) / single-minded name-invocation (Universal Gate) single-sūtra dual pole — scattered mind's lowest threshold closing with "have already completed the Buddha-path" as the highest fruit; single-mindedness's highest threshold closing with "all attain liberation" as the present-life fruit; the cross-configuration of threshold and fruition reflects the Lotus's doctrinal judgment on name-invocation — its deepest meaning is not the acquisition of worldly efficacy but the acknowledgment of the fact that all beings have already completed the Buddha-path. Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Words* on the Universal Gate Chapter, through its qualitative "the perfect-one is singular, partial-ones are many" ranking, establishes a pre–late-Tang precursor to the name-invocation density-saturation doctrine; this resonates with late-Ming Zhixu's "faith-vow-practice simultaneously complete in a single utterance" cognitive-premise doctrine, forming within the Tiantai exegetical tradition a cross-temporal longitudinal axis of "the density of name-invocation cannot be counted in numbers."

### 6.2 Practice Guidance

Three concrete implications of this architecture for practitioners.

**First, take Never Disparaging's bodily-karma reverence as the entry point**. Western readers trapped between the twin flattenings of *mantra* and *intercessory prayer* will find in Never Disparaging's reverence model a third entry-point — bodily-karma is visible and palpable, its threshold is low, and its structure is complete. In a single bow, bodily, vocal, and mental actions arise simultaneously; the object of reverence is not a holy object but a sentient being who has not yet become a buddha; the structure is executable in daily life (every silent inner reverence offered to family, colleagues, or strangers is a micro-instantiation of Never Disparaging's practice). Western readers' bias toward embodiment and action finds its application here.

**Second, scattered-mind single-invocation as the minimum maintenance dose of daily practice**. Expedient Means's "if a person with a scattered mind enters a stūpa or shrine, and once invokes 'Homage to the Buddha,' all have already completed the Buddha-path" supplies the minimum threshold of name-invocation. Practitioners need not reach one-pointed concentration at every invocation — a single utterance under scattered-mind conditions is sufficient to constitute the arising of wholesome capacity, sufficient to locate the distant-cause of Buddha-path within the three-temporal continuum. This is the "lower-bound guarantee" of name-invocation practice: no matter how scattered the day's mental state, a single invocation keeps the continuity of practice intact. The existence of this lower bound prevents practice from breaking off because of one day's scatteredness — even on a scattered day, one can still invoke once, still remain within practice.

**Third, the object of name-invocation is power, not sound**. The verses' "recollect the power of that Avalokita" points to the core of name-invocation as *recollection of the bodhisattva's merit-power*, not as sonic vibration. Practitioners can observe: at the moment of a single utterance, is the mind on the syllables of the name or on the bodhisattva's merit-power? If the former, the practice easily slides into mechanized sonic mantra; if the latter, the content of the name-invocation is the bodhisattva's vow-power, rescue-power, and undifferentiated responsive-manifestation-power — and the recollection of these powers turns name-invocation into a mental channel opening onto the merit-body itself, rather than a physiological repetition of sound. The Lotus's dual-track design of "activate with *chēng*, fold with *niàn*" is precisely to supply the practitioner with a natural transition channel from the sonic level to the merit-level.

### 6.3 Open Questions: Turning Toward P14 on the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*

The Lotus self-sufficiently presents the name-invocation spectrum through its single-sūtra dual pole; but its dual-pole configuration is a symmetric structure of low (scattered mind) vs. high (single-mindedness). Entering P14 on the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* (*Yuánjué Jīng* 圓覺經), we encounter a different structural challenge — the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* takes the *beings of the final age* (*mò-shì zhòngshēng* 末世眾生) as its explicit capacity-response object, and the *twenty-five pure *samādhi*-wheels* (*èrshíwǔ qīngjìng dìnglún* 二十五清淨定輪) as its cultivation-configuration framework. If the Lotus's dual pole is a "universally accessible" threshold-spectrum, is the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*'s pure-*samādhi*-wheel a cultivation-map "specifically usable by beings of the final age"? In the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*'s final-age capacity-response architecture, how will the two ends — scattered-mind single invocation and single-minded name-invocation — be re-located? And how will the sudden-enlightenment / gradual-cultivation dialectic, within the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*'s pure-*samādhi*-wheels, interact with buddha-recollection practice? P14 awaits.
