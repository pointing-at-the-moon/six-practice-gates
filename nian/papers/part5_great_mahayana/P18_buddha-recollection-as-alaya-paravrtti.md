## Abstract
The *Ghanavyūha-sūtra* (*Dàshèng mìyán jīng* 大乘密嚴經; T0681, trans. Divākara; T0682, trans. Amoghavajra) deposits, respectively in its third and second fascicles, two key ontological statements — "**The Buddha declares the *tathāgatagarbha* and thereby sets it forth as the *ālaya*; as gold and its ring, they unfold without difference**" (T0682, fascicle 3, *Chapter 8: The Ālaya is Ghanavyūha* 阿賴耶即密嚴品第八), and "**The subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted**" (T0682, fascicle 2, *Chapter 4: The Self-Wrought Objective Field* 自作境界品第四). The first anchor, through its simile of gold and gold ring, locks in the ontological identity of *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna*; the second defines the very substance of the Ghanavyūha buddha-field as the consciousness that has already undergone *āśraya-parāvṛtti* (the transmutation of the base). Each anchor, however, has its limit. The first is a static statement of identity — it says *what a thing is*, not *how it transforms*. The second presupposes a transmutation-event but does not unfold its internal mechanism. Taking as its textual foothold a third scriptural line from the same sūtra — "**This is the field of the contemplative practice of buddha-recollection, and it is called the *tathāgatagarbha*"** (T0682, fascicle 1, *Chapter 1: The Bodhimaṇḍa of Ghanavyūha* 密嚴道場品第一) — this paper argues that *buddha-recollection* (*niàn-fó* 念佛, *buddhānusmṛti*) can function as the constructive bridge that carries the two anchors from static ontological identity into dynamic soteriological mechanism: buddha-recollection as the contemplative entry-point to the pure-reliance face of the storehouse-consciousness, propelled along twin axes of the Buddhas' *adhiṣṭhāna* (sustaining power) and the practitioner's *śraddhā* (faith), continuously rotating the functional weight from defiled-reliance toward pure-reliance — the gradual unfolding of *parāvṛtti*. This bridge is explicitly declared as constructive interpretation, not exegetical assertion. The Yogācāra śāstras (*Cheng Weishi Lun* 成唯識論, T1585; *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* 瑜伽師地論, T1579; *Āryaśāsana-prakaraṇa* 顯揚聖教論, T1602) are entirely silent on "buddha-recollection as a trigger of the transmutation-of-the-base," and the *Ghanavyūha* itself does not textually weld the two together either. §IV stages Waldron's *The Buddhist Unconscious* as primary interlocutor, Lusthaus's *Buddhist Phenomenology* as phenomenological guardrail, and Schmithausen's *Ālayavijñāna* as conceptual-historical backing, to prevent the constructive thesis from sliding into Western frameworks of the unconscious. §V handles four typical Western misreadings — the transmutation-of-the-base is *not* neural plasticity, buddha-recollection is *not* a mantra-conditioned response, the Ghanavyūha buddha-field is *not* a collective-unconscious projection, and *ālaya* is *not* the Freudian unconscious — using Waldron's rigorous discipline as anchoring corrective. The eighth verb-axis layer, the transmutation-of-the-base, follows upon the seventh (*seeing-the-mind*) established in P17 on the *Laṅkāvatāra*; P19 on the *Mañjuśrī-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra*'s single-practice samādhi (*eka-vyūha-samādhi*) will carry the argument further into the precise methodological form of buddha-recollection.

## I. Problem-Statement — The Inertia of Western Readings of *Ālaya* and the Ghanavyūha's Two Anchor-Points

When Anglophone Western readers enter Yogācāra (*yújiā xíng pài* 瑜伽行派, consciousness-only) philosophy, the first core term they usually meet is *ālaya-vijñāna* — commonly rendered as "storehouse consciousness" (*zàng-shì* 藏識), and continually juxtaposed in popular-Buddhist writing with the Western traditions of the unconscious: the Freudian unconscious, Jung's collective unconscious, and cognitive science's cognitive unconscious. This juxtaposition seems to offer Western readers a convenient entry-point into the *ālaya*, but it hides a structural trap — it mis-maps a technical category of karmic-causal continuity (*cittasantāna* 心相續, the mind-continuum) onto a psychological model of dynamic repression or archetypal projection. William Waldron's *The Buddhist Unconscious* (2003) has already made the differentiation precisely: the *ālaya* and the Western unconscious are "structurally similar, but fundamentally asymmetric in their temporal frameworks." His rigorous analysis, however, has had limited effect outside academic circles; general readers still habitually grasp the *ālaya* through rules-of-thumb like "the Buddhist version of the unconscious."[^1]

The problem is acutely practical in contemporary buddha-recollection practice. When Western Buddhist discussion asks how buddha-recollection changes the person, two readings circulate. First: that buddha-recollection is a Buddhist version of neural plasticity training — repeated recollection drives neural-circuit rewiring. Second: that buddha-recollection is mantra-based conditioning — sustained sonic stimulus produces a conditioned response. Both readings share a presupposition: buddha-recollection works because it operates at the level of brain or nervous system. This presupposition is structurally homologous with the secularizing reading at the heart of the *McMindfulness* phenomenon — reducing a traditional contemplative practice to a psychological technique, erasing its ontological orientation.[^2]

At precisely this juncture the *Ghanavyūha-sūtra* offers two unusually direct ontological statements, the close reading of which is sufficient to dissolve the ground of these readings. The first (T0682, fascicle 3, *Chapter 8: The Ālaya is Ghanavyūha* 阿賴耶即密嚴品第八): "**The Buddha declares the *tathāgatagarbha* and thereby sets it forth as the *ālaya*; as gold and its ring, they unfold without difference**" (translation mine).[^3] This line directly identifies the *tathāgatagarbha* with the *ālaya-vijñāna*, and with the simile of gold and gold ring locks in their ontological identity — one and the same gold, two forms; one substance, two names. The second (T0682, fascicle 2, *Chapter 4: The Self-Wrought Objective Field* 自作境界品第四): "**The subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted**" (translation mine).[^4] This line defines the very *substance* (*tǐ* 體) of the Ghanavyūha buddha-field as the "transmuted consciousness" — that is, as the *ālaya* itself, having already undergone *āśraya-parāvṛtti*. Ghanavyūha is not an other-worldly Pure Land — it is the field of manifestation of the *ālaya* after its transmutation.

These two lines, however, are both static statements of identity. They tell the reader *what* a thing is, not *how* it transforms. The first locks in the *tathāgatagarbha* as another name for the *ālaya*, but does not explain how the worldling's defiled-reliance is able to rotate into the saint's pure-reliance. The second presupposes that a transmutation-event has occurred, but does not unfold its triggering mechanism. The Yogācāra śāstras — the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (T1585), the *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (T1579), and the *Āryaśāsana-prakaraṇa* (T1602) — do give systematic analysis of the transmutation (the six positions of transmutation, the four aspects of transmutation, the eradication of the gross obstructions across the ten grounds), but are entirely silent on "buddha-recollection as a trigger of the transmutation." The *Ghanavyūha* itself, though it does carry an original-scripture line on buddha-recollection (T0682, fascicle 1, *Chapter 1: The Bodhimaṇḍa of Ghanavyūha* 密嚴道場品第一, "this is the field of the contemplative practice of buddha-recollection, and it is called the *tathāgatagarbha*"),[^5] does not textually weld buddha-recollection to the transmutation.

From this situation the problem-statement of this paper takes shape: the *Ghanavyūha* supplies two ontological anchor-points (the double-anchor) but leaves a gap — how does one move from static ontological identity into dynamic soteriological mechanism? The thesis of this paper is that **buddha-recollection functions as the constructive bridge that strings the two anchors into a dynamic process**: buddha-recollection as the contemplative entry-point to the pure-reliance face of the storehouse-consciousness, activating the conditional ground for the defiled-reliance-to-pure-reliance reversal. This bridge is openly declared to be constructive interpretation — it is not an exegetical unpacking of scripture and śāstra but this paper's hermeneutic cashing-out.

P17 on the *Laṅkāvatāra* has already laid down the epistemological face of this same Yogācāra horizon: via Guṇabhadra's four-fascicle T0670 containing no *niàn-fó* / *guān-fó* language as a structural invitation, and via Daoxin's chain-formula "**the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha**," the paper argued that the object of buddha-recollection turns from the external name inward into the hearts-of-all-buddhas (*hṛdaya*, the essential heart-substance) already present as one's own. P18 takes up this epistemological groundwork and advances it onto the ontological-and-mechanical face: if the verb *seeing the mind* has had its object-structure established in P17, then how does this verb operate in the stream of consciousness? The *Ghanavyūha*'s double-anchor supplies the textual ground, and the constructive position of buddha-recollection within this architecture is the core work of this paper. P17 and P18 jointly constitute the twin axes of buddha-recollection on the Yogācāra horizon — the former handles "what is recollected" (the ontology of the object), the latter handles "how the stream-of-consciousness operates in the moment of recollection" (the ontological-mechanical face).

The paper proceeds in six sections. §II establishes the three original-scripture anchors (two ontological anchors plus the buddha-recollection anchor) and the transmutation-architecture of the Yogācāra śāstras as scriptural-treatise foundation. §III unfolds the double-anchor bridging thesis as core argument, declaring its constructive status openly and giving three levels of textual support. §IV stages Waldron as primary interlocutor, Lusthaus as phenomenological guardrail, and Schmithausen as conceptual-historical backing. §V uses four typical Western misreadings (neural plasticity / mantra conditioning / collective-unconscious projection / Freudian unconscious) as corrective anchors. §VI gathers the result into "transmutation-of-the-base as the eighth verb-axis layer" and hands the baton to P19 on the *Mañjuśrī-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra*'s single-practice samādhi.

⚠️ **Philological note.** T0681 and T0682 are two separate Chinese translations of the same Sanskrit textual system, not a translator's own revised edition. T0681 was translated by Divākara (*Dìpóhēluó* 地婆訶羅) during the Chuigong era of Wu Zetian's Tang (c. 685–688 CE), in three fascicles. T0682 was translated by Amoghavajra (*Bùkōng* 不空) under imperial commission during the Dali era of Tang Daizong (c. 766–774 CE), also in three fascicles. The two translations are separated by some eighty years and belong to different translational lineages (Divākara representing the central-Indian Nālandā system, Amoghavajra the southern-Indian and Sinhalese tantric system); the differences in their diction carry independent editorial-epistemological weight, and are not the result of one hand revising another. This paper treats the two recensions as peer witnesses and parallels them at citation. Their near word-for-word parallels at the key anchor-lines provide cross-recension proof of the stability of the *Ghanavyūha*'s thesis.[^6]

## II. Canonical Grounding — The Three-Anchor Architecture and the Yogācāra-Śāstra Framework of Transmutation

This section establishes the canonical foundation of P18. The double recensions T0681 and T0682 supply three original-scripture anchors. The first anchor is "**The Buddha declares the *tathāgatagarbha* and thereby sets it forth as the *ālaya***" — the ontological identity of *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya*. The second is "**The subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted**" — the ontological pivot between the Ghanavyūha buddha-field and the transmuted consciousness. The third is "**this is the field of the contemplative practice of buddha-recollection, and it is called the *tathāgatagarbha***" — the welding of the buddha-recollection object to the *tathāgatagarbha*. Each anchor stands textually on its own; taken together they form the textual support for the constructive thesis of §III. The Yogācāra śāstras (T1585, T1579, T1602) supply the systematic framework of the concept of transmutation — the six positions, the four aspects, the eradication of the gross obstructions across the ten grounds — but are entirely silent on "buddha-recollection as trigger of transmutation." This silence is precisely the legitimate space within which the constructive work of §III operates.

### 2.1 The Basic Bibliographic Situation of the Two Recensions

T0681 *Dàshèng mìyán jīng* was translated by Divākara during the Chuigong era of Wu Zetian's Tang, in three fascicles and thirteen chapters (four chapters in fasc. 1, four in fasc. 2, five in fasc. 3). T0682 *Dàshèng mìyán jīng* was translated by Amoghavajra under imperial commission during the Dali era of Tang Daizong, also in three fascicles but eight chapters (three in fasc. 1, three in fasc. 2, two in fasc. 3). The two recensions belong to different translational traditions — Divākara representing the central-Indian Nālandā system, Amoghavajra the southern-Indian and Sinhalese tantric system — and are separated by some eighty years, with no mutual inheritance. Their Sanskrit witnesses represent two transmitted states of a shared archetype; but each recension had its own editorial calibration of diction, so parallels between them at the line of an original-scripture phrase acquire cross-recension epistemological weight.

On the level of chapter-titles, T0682 fasc. 3 *Chapter 8: The Ālaya is Ghanavyūha* (阿賴耶即密嚴品第八) uses a statement-of-identity as its chapter-title, directly declaring "*ālaya* = Ghanavyūha." The T0681 parallel stands as *Chapter 8: The Subtle Secret of the Ālaya* (阿賴耶微密品第八), a looser formulation. The difference reflects Amoghavajra's more identity-driven grasp of the sūtra's thesis.

### 2.2 The First Anchor — The Ontological Identity of *Tathāgatagarbha* and *Ālaya*

Near the close of T0682's *Chapter 8: The Ālaya is Ghanavyūha*, Bodhisattva Vajragarbha 金剛藏 speaks to the assembly of Paranirmita-vaśa-vartin devas on "the samādhi that perfects the virtues of the Great Vehicle":

> The Tathāgata's pure treasury is also called the undefiled wisdom; it abides everlastingly, without beginning or end, and is free of the four-alternatives mode of speech. The Buddha declares the *tathāgatagarbha* and thereby sets it forth as the *ālaya*; those of poor understanding cannot know this — the treasury is the *ālaya*-consciousness itself. The Tathāgata's pure treasury, and the worldly *ālaya*, are like gold and its ring — unfolding without difference. (translation mine)[^7]

The T0681 parallel reads:

> The Buddha declares the *tathāgatagarbha* as the *ālaya*-consciousness; those of poor understanding cannot know this — the treasury is the *ālaya*. The Tathāgata's pure treasury, and the worldly *ālaya*, are like gold and its ring — unfolding without difference. (translation mine)[^8]

The two recensions diverge by a single character: T0682 has "*yǐ-wéi* 以為" ("thereby sets forth as"), where T0681 has "*wéi* 為" ("is as"). Amoghavajra's additional character *yǐ* 以 strengthens the sense of active *prajñapti* (designation, positing): the Buddha *posits* the *tathāgatagarbha* as the *ālaya*, rather than being bluntly identical with it. The semantic shift, though small, aligns with Amoghavajra's habitual tantric translational idiom, which favours *prajñapti*-syntax. On the other hand, the two recensions are nearly identical at the gold-and-ring simile, which locks in the ontological identity of *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya* — one and the same gold, one and the same substance, differing only in form and in name.

The doctrinal position of this passage is among the key Chinese-Buddhist sources for the confluence of *tathāgatagarbha* thought and Yogācāra. It pairs with the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s compound *tathāgatagarbhālayavijñāna* (already treated in P17 §2.2) to form a cross-sūtra resonance. Both sūtras identify *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna*, but with different emphases — the *Laṅkā* emphasizes the identity at the epistemological level of "the shared support of defilement and purity" (P17's home territory); the *Ghanavyūha* emphasizes the identity at the ontological level of "gold and its ring" (P18's home territory).

The limit of this anchor: the line describes identity, not mechanism. The *tathāgatagarbha* and the *ālaya* are two faces of the same substance, but this identity is static — it says *what* a thing is, not *how* it transforms. To stop at this anchor is to fall into one of two sicknesses. Reading A (the wild-Chan type): "if the *tathāgatagarbha* is the *ālaya*, there is no need for cultivation" — ignoring the warning "those of poor understanding cannot know this." Reading B (the dualistic type): "*tathāgatagarbha* is the pure one, *ālaya* is the defiled one, one must turn the defiled into the pure" — ignoring the fundamental unity of "unfolding without difference." Both readings fall ill because they are not yet conjoined with dynamic mechanism. The detailed treatment is given in §3.1.

### 2.3 The Second Anchor — The Ghanavyūha Buddha-Field and the Ontological Pivot of the Transmuted Consciousness

In T0682's *Chapter 4: The Self-Wrought Objective Field*, Vajragarbha answers in verse the question of Bodhisattva Ratnacūḍa 寶髻 concerning the intrinsic nature of the Ghanavyūha buddha-field:

> The subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted; it transcends the discriminating mind, it is not the field of deluded feeling. The Tathāgata's Ghanavyūha realm has no end and no beginning; it is not the intrinsic nature of subtle particles, it is not produced by desire, it is not made by the Great Sovereign, it is not the karma of ignorance and craving — it is born only of the effortless subtle wisdom. (translation mine)[^9]

The parallel at T0681 fasc. 2, *Chapter 4: Revealing Self-Wrought* (顯示自作品第四):

> The subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted; it transcends the objective field of the discriminating mind, it is not where deluded feeling courses. (translation mine)[^10]

The two recensions at the core line "the subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted" are nearly word-identical, differing only in a single character between "*miào* 妙" / "*wēimiào* 微妙" ("subtly-wrought" / "subtly-and-wondrously-wrought") and an interchangeable synonym between "*tǔ* 土" and "*shà* 剎" ("realm"). Their parallel stability at this line supplies cross-recension stability for the core thesis of P18 §III.

The structural significance: the line defines the *substance* (*tǐ* 體, ontological essence) of Ghanavyūha directly as "the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted" — that is, as the *ālaya* itself, after *āśraya-parāvṛtti*. Ghanavyūha is not "an objectively existing Pure Land somewhere," not "a field of psychological imagination," but the manifesting field of the storehouse-consciousness once it has undergone transmutation. The concluding verses of T0682's *Chapter 8: The Ālaya is Ghanavyūha* further consolidate the point:

> Just as the stars and moon revolve in dependence on Mount Sumeru, so too the consciousnesses turn constantly in dependence on the *ālaya*. The *ālaya* itself is the Ghanavyūha — the wondrous substance, in itself pure. (translation mine)[^11]

"*The ālaya itself is the Ghanavyūha*" is the six-character concluding verse of the entire sūtra — Ghanavyūha at the ontological level fully coincides with the pure face of the *ālaya*.

The advance of this anchor over the first: it contains a dynamic verb — "transmuted" is already a nominalized perfective of a dynamic verb ("the consciousness *which has been* transmuted"). It therefore links from static identity toward soteriological dynamic. But the linkage leaves a gap — between (the original state of the *ālaya*) and (Ghanavyūha = the transmuted *ālaya*), what is the internal mechanism of the transmutation-event itself? The *Ghanavyūha* does not unfold it. That gap is the entry-point of the constructive work of §III.

### 2.4 The Third Anchor — The Welding of the Buddha-Recollection Object to the *Tathāgatagarbha*

In T0682 fasc. 1, *Chapter 1: The Bodhimaṇḍa of Ghanavyūha*, the Buddha answers Bodhisattva Vajragarbha's question "Bhagavan, what is the meaning of the term 'Buddha'?":

> Vajragarbha! The Tathāgata abides eternally and does not change — this is the field of the contemplative practice of buddha-recollection, and it is called the *tathāgatagarbha*. Like space, which cannot be destroyed, it is called also the realm of *nirvāṇa*, and also the *dharmadhātu*, the *dharma*-abidingness-nature, and the *dharma*-*niyāma*-nature. (translation mine)[^12]

The T0681 parallel, in fasc. 1, reads:

> This is the objective field contemplated by one practicing contemplation. (translation mine)[^13]

At this line the two recensions diverge markedly. T0682 explicitly uses the four characters *niàn-fó guān-xíng* 念佛觀行 ("buddha-recollection contemplative-practice"); T0681 writes only *guān-xíng* 觀行 ("contemplative-practice"), with no "buddha-recollection" on the surface. Here it is Amoghavajra's recension that preserves the term *buddhānusmṛti* on the textual surface and directly equates "the field of buddha-recollection contemplative-practice" with the *tathāgatagarbha*. Amoghavajra did not avoid the term — on the contrary, he welded it to the *tathāgatagarbha* as object. This single datum overturns the general impression that "Amoghavajra suppresses *niàn-fó* wording in order to align with tantric idiom."

The doctrinal weight of this passage: it welds, at the textual surface, buddha-recollection (as act) with *tathāgatagarbha* (as object-substance) — the field of buddha-recollection *is* the *tathāgatagarbha*, which at the first anchor is in turn identical with the pure face of the *ālaya*. By transitive inference, the object of buddha-recollection arrives at the pure face of the *ālaya*. This is the third anchor the *Ghanavyūha* provides textually — buddha-recollection is already, on the scriptural surface, strung together with the *tathāgatagarbha* and the *ālaya*. But the linkage is still descriptive. "The field of buddha-recollection contemplative-practice is called the *tathāgatagarbha*" tells the reader the ontology of the object; it does not tell the reader *how* buddha-recollection triggers the transmutation-process.

A supplementary text for this third anchor: in T0682 fasc. 3, at the close of *Chapter 7: The Field of Self-and-Consciousness* (我識境界品第七), the Buddha addresses Bodhisattva Yathābhūta-darśana 如實見 ("Beholder-of-Reality") in a rare long-prose (rather than verse) pronouncement, concluding the chapter in his own voice:

> Lord, all who practice the deep samādhis cleanse self-view from upon this very consciousness. You and the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas should likewise do so — cultivating diligently yourselves and expounding it for others, so that they may swiftly enter the Ghanavyūha buddha-field. (translation mine)[^14]

This is a rare long-prose direct instruction in the Buddha's own voice, and carries higher probative weight than verse sections. "Cleansing self-view upon this very consciousness (the *ālaya*)" is defined here as the practice of "entering the Ghanavyūha buddha-field." "Cleansing self-view" is not the same lexical item as buddha-recollection, but is intimately linked with it at the soteriological level — if buddha-recollection genuinely touches the breaking of *manas*-grasping-at-self, then it is the concrete operation of "cleansing self-view upon the *ālaya*." The passage functions as supplementary textual support for the argument of §III.

### 2.5 The Yogācāra-Śāstra Framework of Transmutation

The Yogācāra śāstras supply a systematic analysis of *āśraya-parāvṛtti*. The *Cheng Weishi Lun* (T1585), fascicle 10, at the section on the stage of cultivation, lists six "positions of transmutation" and four "aspects of transmutation":

> The positions of transmutation are, briefly, six: first, the transmutation of "weakening the power and strengthening the capacity"; second, the transmutation of direct comprehension; third, the transmutation of cultivation; fourth, the transmutation of the perfection of fruition; fifth, the transmutation of the low and inferior; sixth, the transmutation of greatness and vastness. ... The aspects of transmutation are, briefly, four: first, the enabling path of transmutation; second, that upon which transmutation operates; third, that which is cast off in transmutation; fourth, that which is attained in transmutation. (translation mine)[^15]

The six positions display the transmutation not as a single momentary event but as a process that unfolds progressively along the bodhisattva path — the five stages of accumulation, preparatory practice, direct comprehension, cultivation, and ultimacy all have their corresponding forms of transmutation. The four aspects break the transmutation into four conditional elements: the enabling path (root non-conceptual wisdom and subsequent wisdom), that upon which transmutation operates (the *ālaya* as seed-bearer and suchness as deluded-or-awakened support), that which is cast off (the gross obstructions of the afflictive and cognitive hindrances), and that which is attained (great *nirvāṇa* and great *bodhi*). The entire system takes the maturing of non-conceptual wisdom (*nirvikalpa-jñāna*) as principal driver, and the three incalculable aeons of gradual cultivation as its temporal frame.

The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (T1579), fascicles 96 and 98, at the Section of Decisive Ascertainment, enumerates in detail the "ignorances" (*avidyā*) and "gross obstructions" (*dauṣṭhulya*) to be eliminated at each of the ten bodhisattva-grounds, treating the progressive severing of the gross obstructions as the concrete operation of the transmutation. The *Āryaśāsana-prakaraṇa* (T1602) further distinguishes the distinct transmutation-fruits of the three vehicles — śrāvakayāna, pratyekabuddhayāna, and mahāyāna each have their own attained quietude and path.[^16]

In the systematic treatment of the transmutation across all three śāstras, however, buddha-recollection is not listed as a trigger of the transmutation. The *Cheng Weishi Lun* has no passage welding buddha-recollection with transmutation; the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s detailed treatment of the ten pāramitās does not centre on buddha-recollection; the *Āryaśāsana*'s three-vehicle doctrine of the transmutation-fruits likewise does not mention buddha-recollection. The śāstras take the maturing of non-conceptual wisdom and the gradual completion of the ten pāramitās as the principal drivers of the transmutation. If buddha-recollection figures at all, it figures as an auxiliary practice at the stage of accumulation, not as a core trigger at or above the stage of direct comprehension.

This silence is not an oversight but a genre-selection of the śāstra. As systematic analytic philosophy, the Yogācāra śāstras address the formal architecture of the transmutation and the general form of its mechanism; specific triggering practices belong to the soteriological-practical stratum, which is not within the general analytic scope of the śāstras. The *Ghanavyūha*, as a scripture of practice-inspired content rather than as a systematic treatise, *can* weld buddha-recollection to the transmutation; the śāstras do not speak this linkage directly.

This is the constructive space of §III: the śāstras are silent on "buddha-recollection = transmutation-trigger," but this silence is not a refutation; it is the legitimate constructive field for P18's thesis. On the scriptural surface the *Ghanavyūha* provides three anchors (*tathāgatagarbha*-*ālaya* identity; Ghanavyūha = transmuted consciousness; buddha-recollection-object = *tathāgatagarbha*), and these three anchors strung together already supply the necessary textual foothold. The work of P18 is to weld the three with a dynamic mechanism — a welding explicitly declared as hermeneutic cashing-out, not pretending to be a direct statement of scripture or śāstra.

## III. Core Argument — The Double-Anchor Bridging Thesis and Buddha-Recollection as Constructive Bridge

**The argument of this section is constructive interpretation, not exegetical assertion.** Its textual foothold is the three original-scripture anchors established in §§2.2–2.4 and the Yogācāra śāstras' transmutation-architecture of §2.5, but its welding of buddha-recollection and transmutation as dynamic mechanism is the hermeneutic work of P18, not a direct statement of the scriptures or śāstras. The declaration is not a retreat — it is the evolution of the anchoring mode used in P17 on the *Laṅkāvatāra*. P17 used two anchor-points — the original-scripture interlinear gloss and Daoxin's chain-formula "the mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha" — to establish the epistemological structure of the object of buddha-recollection: the hearts-of-all-buddhas as object-substance. P18 takes up this epistemological groundwork and, using the three *Ghanavyūha* anchors as textual foothold, welds buddha-recollection to transmutation as dynamic mechanism — advancing from the epistemological to the ontological-mechanical face. The legitimacy of this advance lies in this: the object-substance established in P17 (hearts-of-all-buddhas, *hṛdaya*) and the soteriological mechanism established in P18 (buddha-recollection → transmutation) are co-axial on the Yogācāra horizon — the former answers "what is to be recollected," the latter answers "how the stream-of-consciousness operates at the moment of recollecting." The two do not contradict and do not duplicate. Together they constitute the twin-axis structure of buddha-recollection.

### 3.1 The Double-Anchor Bridging Thesis — From Static Identity to Dynamic Mechanism

The first anchor (§2.2) is a static statement of identity: "*The Buddha declares the tathāgatagarbha and thereby sets it forth as the ālaya; as gold and its ring, they unfold without difference.*" The line tells the reader *what* a thing is — *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya-vijñāna* are one at the level of substance. The second anchor (§2.3) contains an implicit dynamic: "*The subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted.*" The line presupposes that an *āśraya-parāvṛtti* event has occurred — Ghanavyūha is the *ālaya* having been transmuted. Taken together, the two anchors leave a gap: if *tathāgatagarbha* and *ālaya* are ontologically identical (the first anchor), why does the worldling's present *ālaya* require transmutation (the second anchor)? The worldling's present *ālaya* and the post-transmutation *ālaya* (Ghanavyūha), given ontological identity, differ at what stratum?

The *Ghanavyūha*'s textual hint toward this gap lies in this: the two anchors describe two *functional-stratum states* of the same substance. The *tathāgatagarbha* describes this substance's pure-reliance face — eternally pure, intrinsically pure from the outset, the "beyond-language field that those of poor understanding cannot know." The *ālaya* describes this substance's defiled-reliance face — bearing karmic seeds, serving as the reliance of all the consciousnesses, and manifesting in the worldling's condition as the grasping of self-view. The two faces are one substance — "as gold and its ring, unfolding without difference" — but at the functional stratum, defiled-reliance and pure-reliance each have their manifesting mechanism. *Transmutation* (*parāvṛtti*) is the gradual process by which this substance's functional configuration rotates from defiled-reliance-dominance into pure-reliance-dominance. It is not the arising of a separate "new consciousness," not a fabrication-from-nothing.

Buddha-recollection functions as the constructive bridge of this process in this way: with the Buddhas' *adhiṣṭhāna* and the practitioner's *śraddhā* as twin-axis pivot, it continuously projects contemplative attention onto the pure-reliance face of the storehouse-consciousness. In Yogācāra vocabulary this projection is an *ālambana-pratyaya* (the objective condition) — the Buddha, as object, is projected as a seed-image onto the *ālaya*; the ongoing presencing of the Buddhas' virtue corresponds to the *adhipati-pratyaya* (the contributory condition) — not a direct causal condition (*hetu-pratyaya*) but a creator of the peripheral conditions that allow the transmutation to arise. A single act of buddha-recollection does not directly *produce* transmutation, but every act of buddha-recollection weakens the dominance of defiled-reliance and supplies the conditions for the emergence of pure-reliance. Across thousands upon thousands of acts of recollection, the dominant tension at the functional stratum rotates from defiled-reliance toward pure-reliance — this is one possible configuration of the internal dynamic that the *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s six positions of transmutation describe as the "transmutation of weakening the power and strengthening the capacity" and the "transmutation of direct comprehension."

This transitivity of functional-stratum reversal can be further specified in Yogācāra vocabulary. When defiled-reliance dominates, *manas* 末那 grasps the *ālaya*'s appearance-portion as "self" through self-view; the other consciousnesses operate as pivots around that grasping. When pure-reliance emerges, the self-view-grasping of *manas* gradually loosens; the other consciousnesses come to operate in dependence on the intrinsically pure appearance-portion of the *ālaya*. The function of buddha-recollection is to supply *manas* with a continuous contemplative object that is *not-self* (the Buddha's virtue, name, body), so that the habitual pivot of self-grasping is displaced by the other-pivot (the Buddha as not-self object). This displacement is not an instantaneous severing but a gradual loosening — what the *Cheng Weishi Lun* calls the "transmutation of weakening the power and strengthening the capacity": the "power" of defiled-reliance is weakened, the "capacity" of pure-reliance is strengthened.

### 3.2 Three Levels of Textual Support

The textual foothold of this constructive bridge is not arbitrary; it has three progressively deeper levels of support.

**The first level** is the stringing of the three original-scripture anchors of §II. T0682 fasc. 1 *Chapter 1: The Bodhimaṇḍa of Ghanavyūha* declares "this is the field of the contemplative practice of buddha-recollection, and it is called the *tathāgatagarbha*" — the field of buddha-recollection *is* the *tathāgatagarbha*. T0682 fasc. 3 *Chapter 8: The Ālaya is Ghanavyūha* declares "the Buddha declares the *tathāgatagarbha* and thereby sets it forth as the *ālaya*; as gold and its ring, they unfold without difference" — the *tathāgatagarbha* *is* the *ālaya*. T0682 fasc. 2 *Chapter 4: The Self-Wrought Objective Field* declares "the subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted" — Ghanavyūha *is* the transmuted *ālaya*. By the direct transitivity of these three anchors, the scriptural surface has already connected the object of buddha-recollection with the transmuted *ālaya*: the field of buddha-recollection is called the *tathāgatagarbha* → the *tathāgatagarbha* is the *ālaya* → Ghanavyūha is the already-transmuted *ālaya* → the field of buddha-recollection, at the level of ontological identity, coincides with Ghanavyūha. This first level of support comes entirely from the surface sequence of scriptural sentences; it involves no construction.

**The second level** is the systematic description of the transmutation-mechanism in the Yogācāra śāstras. The six positions and four aspects of the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (cited in §2.5) supply the formal architecture of the transmutation: the enabling path, that upon which transmutation operates, that which is cast off, that which is attained. The śāstras leave an open space for "what kind of mental practice triggers the transmutation" — non-conceptual wisdom's maturation is the principal driver, but the "four searches" and "four knowings-as-such" of the preparatory stage and the various auxiliary practices of the accumulation stage are all listed by the śāstras as peripheral conditions of the transmutation. Buddha-recollection, as a contemplative practice with an object, when understood (under the scriptural grounding of §2.4) as "contemplation upon the pure-reliance face of the *ālaya*," may be placed within this open space — not as a śāstric direct statement, but as a content coherent with the śāstras' formal architecture.

**The third level** is the Chinese-Buddhist tradition of the confluence of *tathāgatagarbha* thought and Yogācāra. The *Laṅkāvatāra* (P17's home territory) and the *Ghanavyūha* are paired in the Chinese tradition as the twin-scripture foundation of this confluence; the Dilun-school, the Shelun-school, and the Faxiang-school all situate their soteriologies within this confluential lineage. This tradition, though it does not textually weld buddha-recollection to transmutation (as noted in §2.5, the Yogācāra śāstras are silent on this), does at the practical level provide later soteriological accounts of the efficacy of buddha-recollection with a Yogācāra-grounded framework. The Pure Land patriarchs of the Tang and thereafter do not cite the *Ghanavyūha* as their metaphysical backing (see the discussion in §VI), but their descriptions of "getting traction in buddha-recollection" (e.g., Shandao's phrase "the karmic business accomplished") can, at the ontological level, receive a Yogācāra-framed interpretation under the *Ghanavyūha*'s double-anchor architecture. This third level is not the principal support of the argument — §VI will declare openly that the line of inference here is constructive, not historical — but it provides a reasonable Chinese-Buddhist-traditional background for P18's constructive bridge.

### 3.3 Defence of the Constructive Thesis — A Three-Party Dialogue

The principal objection this thesis must face can be staged as a question from a Yogācāra śāstric master: neither the *Cheng Weishi Lun* nor the *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* places buddha-recollection on the list of triggers of the transmutation. The śāstras take the maturing of non-conceptual wisdom and the gradual completion of the ten pāramitās as the principal drivers of the transmutation. If buddha-recollection truly could trigger transmutation, why are the śāstras silent?

*Reply one (genre-selection).* The genre of the śāstra is systematic analytic philosophy; its responsibility is to describe the formal architecture and general mechanism of the transmutation, not to exhaustively enumerate every possible specific triggering practice. Buddha-recollection as a concrete method belongs to the practical stratum, and is outside the general analytic scope of the śāstra. The śāstras' silence on buddha-recollection is not a denial — just as the śāstras do not enumerate breath-contemplation, loving-kindness contemplation, or the four establishments of mindfulness one by one, yet such practices are widely accepted in the practice literature as peripheral conditions of the transmutation.

*Reply two (scripture–śāstra complementarity).* The *Ghanavyūha*, as a scripture of practice-inspired content (not a systematic treatise), has a different responsibility — to supply textual grounding. Its three original-scripture anchors supply the textual welding of buddha-recollection with *tathāgatagarbha* / *ālaya* / transmutation. The śāstras' silence and the scripture's hint are not contradictory but complementary — the śāstra gives the formal skeleton of the mechanism, the scripture gives the directional indication of the practice. P18's constructive-bridge work is precisely the welding of the two, a hermeneutic cashing-out, declared openly as constructive.

*Reply three (precise formulation).* This thesis does *not* claim that buddha-recollection is the unique trigger of the transmutation, nor that buddha-recollection is sufficient to complete the transmutation on its own. The precise form of the thesis is this: within the Yogācāra formal architecture, buddha-recollection can be understood as a twin-axis pivot of contributory-condition and objective-condition, supplying continuous conditional resources to the transmutation-process. This is not in contradiction with the śāstras' placement of non-conceptual wisdom as principal driver — buddha-recollection's auxiliary function can unfold at the stages of accumulation, preparatory practice, and cultivation, but the transmutation of the stage of direct comprehension still arises from the triggering of non-conceptual wisdom. The constructive bridge does not replace non-conceptual wisdom — it offers one soteriological entry-point for the maturing of non-conceptual wisdom.

The outcome of this three-party dialogue is this: the Yogācāra master's objection does not overturn P18's thesis; rather, it brings the precise form of the thesis into relief — buddha-recollection as auxiliary pivot of contributory-and-objective condition, not as principal driver. The precisification is itself one of the outputs of P18's constructive work.

### 3.4 Cross-Recension Diction Differences as Argumentative Resource

§II has already noted the two recensions' near-identical parallels at the key anchor-lines ("as gold and its ring, they unfold without difference" / "the subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted"). The parallelism itself supplies the *Ghanavyūha*'s thesis with a cross-recension stability that doubles its epistemological weight — the two recensions belong to different translational traditions, are separated by eighty years, and have no mutual inheritance; yet at the core anchor-lines they have been independently calibrated into near-identical wording. The parallelism cannot be coincidental; it reflects the stable textual form of the Sanskrit archetype.

At the points of divergence ("*yǐ-wéi* 以為" vs "*wéi* 為"; "*niàn-fó guān-xíng* 念佛觀行" vs "*guān-xíng* 觀行"), the two recensions supply a further level of argumentative resource — the divergence itself can serve as hermeneutic clue. Amoghavajra's "*yǐ-wéi*" strengthens the sense of active *prajñapti* (positing, designation) — the Buddha *posits* the *tathāgatagarbha* as the *ālaya*, not that the two are mechanically identical. This diction-choice is consistent with Amoghavajra's tantric translational idiom (which favours *prajñapti*-syntax) and suggests an epistemological distinction between construction (*prajñapti*) and direct description (*svabhāva-lakṣaṇa*). The distinction is structurally homologous with the defence of the constructive thesis in §3.3 — the *Ghanavyūha*'s *prajñapti* of the *tathāgatagarbha* as the *ālaya*, and P18's constructive positing of buddha-recollection as transmutation-bridge, share a common epistemological discipline: the open declaration of hermeneutic status.

The divergence "*niàn-fó guān-xíng*" vs "*guān-xíng*" is even more decisive: Amoghavajra's recension preserves the surface term *buddhānusmṛti* and directly equates "the field of buddha-recollection contemplative-practice" with the *tathāgatagarbha*. The diction-choice overturns the prior assumption that "Amoghavajra suppresses *niàn-fó* wording to align with tantric idiom"; rather, it supplies P18's "buddha-recollection as the object-entry-point of transmutation" with direct textual attestation at the T0682 layer. The two recensions' diction-difference is not a difficulty for P18's thesis but its fourth level of textual support — Amoghavajra's surface preservation of the term at the key anchor-line directly backs the core argument of §III.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence — Waldron as Primary Interlocutor, Lusthaus as Phenomenological Guardrail, Schmithausen as Conceptual-Historical Backing

This section takes three principal figures of modern Western *ālaya* scholarship as interlocutors, supplying three layers of protection for the constructive thesis of §III. Waldron's *The Buddhist Unconscious* (2003) serves as primary interlocutor: his rigorous analysis of the *ālaya* against the Western traditions of the unconscious supplies disciplined safeguards against the misreadings flagged in §I. Lusthaus's *Buddhist Phenomenology* (2002) serves as phenomenological guardrail: his reading of Yogācāra as *vijñapti-mātra* (representation-only) rather than as Western idealism prevents the "Ghanavyūha-ontology" thesis of §III from sliding into a Western metaphysical framework. Schmithausen's *Ālayavijñāna* (1987) serves as conceptual-historical backing: the Initial Passage Hypothesis, which displays the *ālaya*'s development from a specific passage on the continuity of mind across the attainment of mindlessness into a general substrate consciousness, is structurally compatible with P18's functional-stratum reading of defiled-reliance and pure-reliance. None of the three directly addresses "buddha-recollection as trigger of transmutation"; but taken together they supply P18's constructive work with three layers of modern-scholarly protection. §4.4 lays out the limits of the three, preserving structural clarity.

### 4.1 Waldron as Primary Interlocutor — The Structural Differentiation of the *Ālaya* from the Western Unconscious

Waldron's *The Buddhist Unconscious: The Ālaya-vijñāna in the Context of Indian Buddhist Thought* (2003) systematically compares the *ālaya* with the Western traditions of the unconscious. The book's core thesis: the *ālaya* is structurally similar to the Freudian unconscious and to Jung's collective unconscious (all handle mind-streams operating outside conscious access and address the accumulation of personal experience as influence upon present consciousness, and all posit mental strata not directly introspected), but is fundamentally asymmetric to them in temporal framework. The Freudian unconscious takes individual life-history as its temporal unit and childhood-trauma-repression as its core dynamic; the *ālaya* takes *anādi-kālika* (beginningless) karmic continuity as its temporal unit and the seed-perfumation-and-active-manifestation cycle (*bīja*–*vāsanā*–*pravṛtti*) of the *cittasantāna* as its core dynamic. The first has an ontological scope bounded by a single lifetime; the second extends across lifetimes.[^17]

Waldron further differentiates: the "storehouse" metaphor applied to the *ālaya* must not be read as a Freudian-style warehouse of *repressed contents*. What the *ālaya* "stores" is the *bīja* — functional causal potentials, not suppressed memories or affective residues. The relation between seeds' perfumation and their active manifestation (*pravṛtti*) is a causal-sequential structure, not a dynamical repression-and-unrepression structure. This point has particular relevance for *Ghanavyūha* scholarship: what the *Ghanavyūha* calls "treasury" (*zàng* 藏) is precisely the *bīja*-sense of storing, not the Freudian sense. To read the *Ghanavyūha*'s "Tathāgata's pure treasury" as a "collective subconscious stratum" or "archetypal substrate" of the Western unconscious tradition is to mis-locate an ontological statement of the *Ghanavyūha* within a Western-psychological category, violating the temporal asymmetry Waldron has carefully drawn.

P18 can directly inherit Waldron's rigorous discipline: the "defiled-reliance / pure-reliance functional-stratum reversal" of §III is *not* a Western-style "rising of unconscious content into consciousness" but a reconfiguration of the *ālaya*'s own functional disposition — from self-view-dominance into self-view-loosening. The mechanism of the transmutation does not need to borrow any concept from the Western traditions of the unconscious — the *bīja*-dynamic and the concept of *parāvṛtti* within Yogācāra are already sufficient to describe the process. Waldron's precise differentiation supplies P18 with the legitimacy of "no cross-cultural conceptual borrowing" — the constructive work of §III unfolds entirely within Yogācāra internal vocabulary, without need of cross-cultural lexical importation.

### 4.2 Lusthaus's Phenomenological Guardrail — Yogācāra as Phenomenology Rather Than Realism

Dan Lusthaus, in *Buddhist Phenomenology: A Philosophical Investigation of Yogācāra Buddhism and the Ch'eng Wei-shih lun* (2002), proposes a key differentiation: Yogācāra is not Western-style idealism but a phenomenology of *vijñapti-mātra* (representation-only). The crux of the differentiation: the Yogācāra formulation "*consciousness-only*" is not the idealist proposition "the world is only mind," but the phenomenological proposition "the world we encounter is always presented through the representations of consciousness." The *ālaya* is not "ultimate reality" but the functional substrate on which representation rests.[^18]

This differentiation supplies an important guardrail for the "Ghanavyūha-ontology" thesis of §III. §2.3 cites "the subtly-wrought realm of Ghanavyūha — its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted" as the second anchor. If a reader reads this line with the default Western-metaphysical presupposition of a realist ontology, they arrive at the misreading "Ghanavyūha is some objectively existing ultimate world" — exactly one of the misreadings §V will correct. Lusthaus's phenomenological reading supplies the correct frame: Ghanavyūha, as "the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted itself," is a phenomenological "lived field" (*Lebenswelt*) — the representational field of the *ālaya* under its transmuted functional disposition, not an objective world in the realist sense.

The importance of the guardrail for P18 is this: it preserves the ontological weight of the *Ghanavyūha*'s two anchor-lines without letting that weight slide into Western realist metaphysics. The *Ghanavyūha*'s "its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted" is a statement of phenomenological ontology — a statement about how consciousness constitutes the arising field — not a metaphysical assertion. Lusthaus's guardrail enables §III to speak of "substance" without being misread by Western-philosophical readers as realist metaphysics. The guardrail also supplies a modern-scholarly correlate for the *prajñapti*-syntax of Amoghavajra's translation noted in §3.4 — *prajñapti* syntax is itself textual-level evidence of the refusal of realist reading, structurally homologous with Lusthaus's phenomenological reading.

### 4.3 Schmithausen's Conceptual History — The Initial Passage Hypothesis as Backing

Lambert Schmithausen, in *Ālayavijñāna: On the Origin and the Early Development of a Central Concept of Yogācāra Philosophy* (1987, two volumes), proposes the celebrated Initial Passage Hypothesis: the concept of the *ālaya-vijñāna* originates in a specific passage of the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Section of Decisive Ascertainment — a passage addressing the technical question of how the mind-stream continues after the attainment of the state of mindlessness — as a stream-of-mind segment posited in the first instance for the solution of that specific problem, and later refined through successive masters of Yogācāra into a general substrate consciousness.[^19]

The trajectory of this conceptual-historical development is: from a specific technical problem to a general substrate function; from a hypothetical passage to a general structural stratum; from descriptive (explaining the continuity across the mindless state) to explanatory (explaining all karmic continuity). The development was not instantaneous but passed through several generations of Yogācāra masters' refinement — the root text of the *Yogācārabhūmi*, the Section of Decisive Ascertainment, the *Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra*, the *Āryaśāsana-prakaraṇa*, the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha*, and the *Cheng Weishi Lun* each supplemented and systematized the *ālaya*'s structure and function at its own stage.

The structural compatibility of this development-history with P18: the reading of "defiled-reliance / pure-reliance functional strata" proposed in §3.1 is not a new ontological posit about the *ālaya*, but a differentiation of functional configurations within the *ālaya*. As Schmithausen shows, the development of the *ālaya*-concept itself moves from specific function toward general structural capacity; under this developmental logic, P18's further differentiation within the general substrate — defiled-reliance and pure-reliance as two functional strata — extends the Yogācāra internal methodology of "using functional configuration to explain soteriological dynamics." Schmithausen's conceptual history does not endorse the specific buddha-recollection–transmutation weld of P18, but it does endorse the overall research direction of "identifying functional strata within the *ālaya* as a way to describe soteriological dynamics." The constructive work of P18 sits on the logical extension of the already established pattern of Yogācāra's conceptual development, not as an external imposition.

### 4.4 The Limits of the Three — Buddha-Recollection Not Within Their Field of View

The three supply P18 with dialogue, guardrail, and conceptual-historical backing; the limits of the three must also be declared. Waldron, Lusthaus, and Schmithausen do not take buddha-recollection (*buddhānusmṛti*) as a research theme. Waldron's focus is the systematic structure of the *ālaya* and its contrast with the Western unconscious; Lusthaus's focus is the comprehensive phenomenological-methodological interpretation of Yogācāra; Schmithausen's focus is the historical genesis of the *ālaya*-concept. All three focus on the internal theoretical clarification of the Yogācāra system, not on the welding of specific practices with that system.

The limit has a double significance for P18. First, P18's constructive work — buddha-recollection as contributory-and-objective auxiliary-pivot of the transmutation — lies outside the field of view of modern Western *ālaya* scholarship; P18 cannot cite the authority of the three as backing for the construction, and must not pretend that their discussions already contain the implication. Second, the constructive work does not conflict with the three: Waldron's structural differentiation permits P18 to speak of functional-stratum reversal (without touching the Western traditions of the unconscious); Lusthaus's phenomenological guardrail permits P18 to speak of "substance" (as long as it does not slide into realism); Schmithausen's conceptual history permits P18 to identify functional strata within the *ālaya* (since such identification extends the logic of Yogācāra's conceptual development). What the three supply P18 is a space of *not endorsing but not prohibiting* — and that space is P18's modern-scholarly foothold for the constructive bridge.

§V turns into middle-way correction, handling four typical Western-reader misreadings with the discipline of the three as anchoring corrective.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections — Four Typical Western-Reader Misreadings

This section uses the discipline of Waldron / Lusthaus / Schmithausen as anchoring corrective to handle four typical misreadings in which Western readers slide the thesis of §III out of Yogācāra territory. The first three are treated in full; the fourth (the Freudian unconscious), because it has been amply handled in §4.1, is treated briefly.

### 5.1 ❌ The Transmutation of the *Ālaya* is Not Neural-Plasticity Training

Western readers are most easily tempted to read the "defiled-reliance → pure-reliance functional-stratum reversal" of §III as a Buddhist version of neural plasticity — analogizing the storehouse-consciousness to a plastically configurable brain-structure, and reading buddha-recollection as a training regime for neural-circuit rewiring. The reading is mistaken at two levels.

First, category-mislocation. Neural plasticity is an experimental-neuroscientific concept describing the structural variability of biological nervous systems; its operational range is restricted to the physiological nervous tissue of a single lifetime. The *ālaya* is a cross-lifetime karmic-causal architecture; its operational range exceeds a single biological substrate. The temporal-framework asymmetry Waldron analysed in §4.1 applies directly — neural plasticity takes individual physiological lifespan as its temporal unit, the *ālaya* takes *anādi-kālika* (beginningless) *cittasantāna* (mind-continuum) as its temporal unit; the two have ontological-categorical strata of different levels.

Second, causal-structure mislocation. Neural plasticity operates by the strengthening and weakening of synaptic connections between neurons; its causal structure is direct physiological causation (comparable to *hetu-pratyaya* in its physiological sense). The *ālaya*'s functional-stratum reversal is the contributory-and-objective relation between seed-perfumation and active manifestation (*adhipati-pratyaya*, *ālambana-pratyaya*); its causal structure is conditional causation in the karmic stratum. To reduce the transmutation to neural plasticity is to reduce the karmic architecture to the physiological architecture, losing the cross-lifetime dimension of the *ālaya*.

### 5.2 ❌ Buddha-Recollection is Not a Mantra-Conditioned Response

Another reading takes buddha-recollection (especially the repetition of name-invocation) to be a conditioned response in the sense of classical or operant conditioning — the repetition of buddha-recollection forms a stimulus-response neural circuit, and transmutation becomes a conditioned behavioural change. The reading is likewise mistaken at two levels.

First, object-structure mislocation. Conditioned response is triggered by an arbitrary stimulus producing a physiological or behavioural reaction; no ontological object is necessary. Buddha-recollection takes the Buddha's virtue (*adhiṣṭhāna*) as ontological object, and T0682 *Chapter 1: The Bodhimaṇḍa of Ghanavyūha* directly declares "the field of buddha-recollection contemplative-practice is called the *tathāgatagarbha*" — the object itself is an ontological item, not an interchangeable sonic marker. To evacuate the object of buddha-recollection into an arbitrary sound is to cancel its ontological orientation — and this is precisely the secularizing structure of the *McMindfulness* phenomenon noted in §I.

Second, motivational-structure mislocation. Conditioned response does not require *śraddhā* (faith) or *prasāda* (pure faith) in the Yogācāra sense — Pavlov's dog needs no "belief" in the bell to salivate. Buddha-recollection rests on faith as its motivational ground — without faith, recollection is merely empty sound, the Buddhas' *adhiṣṭhāna* has nothing to land upon. Faith, as *adhipati-pratyaya*, is a necessary condition for the transmutative efficacy of buddha-recollection. The difference is sharpened in Yogācāra's five object-specific mental factors (desire, resolute conviction, recollection, concentration, insight) — buddha-recollection involves the operation of *adhimokṣa* (resolute conviction) and *smṛti* (recollection) as mental factors, and cannot be replaced by the mechanical linkage of a conditioned response.

### 5.3 ❌ The Ghanavyūha Buddha-Field is Not a Collective-Unconscious Projection

The Jungian tradition carries wide influence in Western Buddhist circles; readers easily read the Ghanavyūha buddha-field as a Jungian-style projection of the collective unconscious — Ghanavyūha as an archetypal collective manifestation in the stratum of group-consciousness, akin to the psychological root of mythological imagery. The reading is mistaken at two ontological-structural levels.

First, subject-mislocation. Jung's collective unconscious is an inherited structure of human collective psychology; its ontology is at the psychological stratum. The *Ghanavyūha* buddha-field's subject is "the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted" (§2.3: "its very substance is the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted") — that is, the manifesting field of a *single* practitioner's storehouse-consciousness after transmutation. Ghanavyūha is not a shared substrate of collective psychology but the ontological fruition-level of the individual practitioner's transmutation-process. Although Yogācāra does have the concept of "common-share seeds" (*sādhāraṇa-bīja*) describing the shared dimension of sentient beings' karma, Ghanavyūha is still not the manifestation of the common-share seeds; it is the full unfolding of the pure-reliance face of the individual storehouse-consciousness.

Second, the phenomenological guardrail. Lusthaus's phenomenological reading (§4.2) applies again — Ghanavyūha, as "the consciousness-that-has-been-transmuted itself," is a phenomenological lived field (*Lebenswelt*), not the content of a psychological projection. The collective-unconscious-projection reading reads Ghanavyūha as "some psychological content," and presupposes an observer-observed subject-object structure; the phenomenological reading reads Ghanavyūha as "the very functional configuration of consciousness," and its structure is constitutive, not contentful.

### 5.4 ❌ The *Ālaya* is Not the Freudian Unconscious (Briefly)

The fourth misreading — directly analogizing the *ālaya* to the Freudian dynamic unconscious — will not be unfolded here, as §4.1 has amply handled its structural asymmetry. In brief: the Freudian unconscious takes the childhood-trauma-repression dynamic of the individual as its core; the *ālaya* takes *anādi-kālika* karmic continuity as its core. The two have fundamentally asymmetric temporal frameworks and dynamical structures. See Waldron, *The Buddhist Unconscious*, chapter three, for the full argument. The misreading shares a common structural feature with §§5.1–5.3: all of them reduce the *ālaya* / transmutation / Ghanavyūha into Western psychological or neuroscientific categories, losing the three-layered Yogācāra structure of karmic architecture, ontology, and phenomenology.

**Gathering.** The four misreadings all stem from the same underlying tendency — to substitute Yogācāra categories with Western psychological or neuroscientific ones, extracting the *Ghanavyūha*'s double-anchor thesis from its karmic architecture and reinstalling it within Western reductionism. The discipline of Waldron (§4.1), Lusthaus (§4.2), and Schmithausen (§4.3) supplies modern-scholarly lines of defense against these misreadings. The concepts used throughout the constructive work of §III — transmutation, defiled-reliance / pure-reliance, the twin axes of contributory-and-objective condition, *adhiṣṭhāna*, *śraddhā* — all operate in Yogācāra internal vocabulary, without borrowing Western concepts; this is the structural pre-emption of the four misreadings.

§VI builds on this gathering to give the conclusion and hand the baton to P19 on the *Mañjuśrī-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra*'s single-practice samādhi.

## VI. Conclusion — The Eighth Verb-Axis Layer of Transmutation and the Handoff to P19's Single-Practice Samādhi

P18 has taken the *Ghanavyūha*'s two anchors (*tathāgatagarbha* = *ālaya*; Ghanavyūha = the transmuted consciousness) and its third anchor (the field of buddha-recollection contemplative-practice is called the *tathāgatagarbha*) as textual foothold, and in the status of a constructive interpretation has welded buddha-recollection to the transmutation-process as an auxiliary pivot along the twin axes of contributory-and-objective condition. This is the placement of the eighth verb-axis layer, *transmutation-of-the-base*, within the Part V series on the Eight Mahāyāna Sūtras: the object of recollection has been laid down in P17 on the *Laṅkāvatāra* as the hearts-of-all-buddhas (epistemological face); the mechanism of recollection has been laid down in P18 on the *Ghanavyūha* as functional-stratum reversal (ontological-mechanical face). The two axes, on the Yogācāra horizon, co-constitute the P17–P18 twin-axis structure of buddha-recollection.

### 6.1 Practice-Guidance — Buddha-Recollection as the Daily Entry-Point of Transmutation

For the actual practitioner, P18's constructive thesis translates into a concrete instruction: buddha-recollection does not need to presuppose a psychological goal of "changing the brain" or "producing a psychological effect." Rather, in every moment of recollection it supplies *manas* with a not-self object (the Buddha's virtue, the Buddha's name, the Buddha's body), continuously loosening the habitual pivot of self-grasping. This loosening is not to be measured by count, not to be verified by emotional effect, but to be examined internally through the question: "*Is the tension of my self-view-grasping gradually diminishing?*" If, during recollection, a comparing-mind arises — "I am not reciting well enough, my merit is not enough, the response has not yet come" — this is *manas* again seizing self-view as pivot; the recollection has slid back into the operation of defiled-reliance. If, at that moment, comparison is set down and one is only in continuity with the Buddha's virtue without grasping at a result, this is the supplying of conditions for the emergence of pure-reliance. This internal examination corresponds directly with the *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s "transmutation of weakening the power and strengthening the capacity" as a gradual mechanism — not a one-time transmutation-event, but the supplying of conditions at every moment of recollection. The efficacy of buddha-recollection lies neither in measurable change at the neural stratum nor in measurable change at the psychological stratum, but in the continuous loosening of *manas*-grasping within the karmic architecture; its temporal framework crosses lifetimes, and its causal structure is conditional *adhipati-pratyaya*, not mechanical direct *hetu-pratyaya*. This is the positive reverse of the four misreadings of §V — the Yogācāra significance of buddha-recollection emerges on its own, requiring no Western conceptual backing and not sliding into Western psychological categories.

### 6.2 Handoff to P19 — From Transmutation-Ontology to the Precise Method of the Single-Practice Samādhi

P18 has established the welding of buddha-recollection with the ontological-and-mechanical face of the transmutation, but leaves a key question open: what is the precise methodological form of buddha-recollection? P17 on the *Laṅkāvatāra* established the object-substance (the hearts-of-all-buddhas); P18 on the *Ghanavyūha* establishes the functional mechanism (the defiled-reliance / pure-reliance functional-stratum reversal); but "the precise form of the mental-factor operation at the moment of recollecting" is still to be specified. The question will be answered head-on by P19 on the *Mañjuśrī-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra* (T0232) and its single-practice samādhi (*eka-vyūha-samādhi*) — as a cross-sectarian scriptural witness for buddha-recollection, the single-practice samādhi specifies the precise method of buddha-recollection as "fixing the mind on one Buddha, invoking exclusively the name," a continuity of one mind, supplying the concrete praxis-form for the twin-axis auxiliary pivot of P18. P17 → P18 → P19 thereby constitute the complete tri-axis of buddha-recollection: object-substance → functional mechanism → method-form, co-axial on the Yogācāra horizon.

And concerning §3.2's third level — the fact that the Chinese Pure Land patriarchs do not cite the *Ghanavyūha* as metaphysical backing (the Pure Land exegetical tradition from the Tang and Song onward makes no contact with the *Ghanavyūha* at all) — this is not a difficulty for this paper. Rather, it highlights the distinctive position of P18 as a constructive-bridge proposal: the *Ghanavyūha*'s double-anchor architecture is, for the Pure Land soteriology of the Tang and after, an unwelded Yogācāra-metaphysical foundation; the historical absence of this welding is precisely the space that P18's constructive work fills. The proposal does not pretend to be historical inquiry, nor does it replace the doctrinal establishments of the Pure Land patriarchs in their own registers. It offers a contemporary reader (in particular, the Western reader) a supplementary path to the understanding of buddha-recollection-soteriology under a Yogācāra framework. The path is parallel to, and complementary with, the paths of the Pure Land patriarchs — supplying, in a cross-cultural setting, a Yogācāra-internal-vocabulary ontological ground for contemporary buddha-recollection practice. This is the final placement of P18's constructive bridge.

Waldron explicitly states that the "storehouse" (*zàng* 藏) metaphor of the *ālaya* must not be read as a Freudian warehouse of *repressed contents* — what is "stored" are the causal potentials of *bīja*, not repressed memories.

[^18]: Lusthaus, Dan, *Buddhist Phenomenology: A Philosophical Investigation of Yogācāra Buddhism and the Ch'eng Wei-shih lun* (London: RoutledgeCurzon, 2002). The book's core thesis is that Yogācāra should be understood as phenomenology (*phenomenology*) rather than as Western idealism (*idealism*); the correct rendering of *vijñapti-mātra* is "representation-only" rather than "mind-only." The introduction (pp. 1–32) and chapter 6 (pp. 343–406) present the *ālaya* as phenomenological substrate and are the direct source for §4.2. Lusthaus further treats Xuanzang's *Cheng Weishi Lun* in a phenomenological reading (chapter 10 and after); his precision on the concept *vijñapti* provides the terminological guardrail for §III's formulation "buddha-recollection as representational projection onto the storehouse-consciousness."

[^19]: Schmithausen, Lambert, *Ālayavijñāna: On the Origin and the Early Development of a Central Concept of Yogācāra Philosophy*, 2 vols. (Tokyo: International Institute for Buddhist Studies, 1987). Schmithausen's Initial Passage Hypothesis: the *ālaya-vijñāna*-concept originates in a passage of the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Section of Decisive Ascertainment addressing the technical problem of how the mind-stream continues after the attainment of the state of mindlessness; the *ālaya* was first posited as a specific mind-stream segment for that particular problem, and later refined through successive Yogācāra masters into a general substrate consciousness. Volume 1 (pp. 12–88) establishes the textual location of the initial passage; volume 2 (pp. 245–310), in the notes, traces the developmental trajectory across later commentators. The Initial Passage Hypothesis has subsequently been partially revised by Buescher (*The Inception of Yogācāra-Vijñānavāda*, 2008) and Yamabe Nobuyoshi and others, but the core judgement — "the *ālaya*-concept is the mature crystallization of Yogācāra internal interpretative development" — remains scholarly consensus. Direct backing for §4.3; Schmithausen's conceptual history endorses the extended methodological line of "identifying functional strata within the *ālaya* to describe soteriological dynamics."

### IV. Cross-Reference to Series Papers

- **P12 — *Gathering the Six Faculties: The Twin-Axis Deepening of the Buddha-Recollection Verb* (*Śūraṃgama*):** the Śūraṃgama "gathering of the six" — spatial integration as inward concentration; the description of "*manas*-grasping-loosened" in P18 §6.1 is homologous in internal structure with P12's inward-concentration architecture.
- **P13 — *Universal-Gate Name-Invocation: The Three-Karmas Architecture of the Lotus's Buddha-Recollection*:** the three-karmas architecture as outward unfolding; pairs with P18 §6.1's "supplying *manas* with a not-self object at the moment of recollection" to form the inner-outer twin wings of the verb-axis.
- **P14 — *Binding the Three Periods: The Time-Dependent Verb-Axis of the Final-Age Buddha-Recollection in the Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*:** time-segment rhythm; homologous in structure with P18 §6.1's internal-examination discipline of "not measured by count, not verified by emotion."
- **P15 — *Seeing the Form and Attaining the Precepts: The Precept-Dependent Verb-Axis of the Brahma-Net Bodhisattva Precepts and Continuous Buddha-Recollection*:** the boundaries of precept; pairs with the continuity requirement of P18 §6.1's "conditional supplying for the emergence of pure-reliance."
- **P16 — *Confession as Buddha-Recollection: The Dream-Sighted Golden Drum of the Suvarṇaprabhāsa-sūtra as the Activating Device of the Dispersed Subject*:** event-chain of dispersed subject; P18 §6.1's single-practitioner internal-examination path forms, with P16's dispersed-subject activation, the inner-outer twin routes.
- **P17 — *Hearts of All Buddhas First: The Laṅkā's Absence of Buddha-Recollection as the Scriptural Ground of Bodhidharma's Mind-Seal*:** object-substance (hearts-of-all-buddhas, *hṛdaya*); §I and §III of the present paper repeatedly mark the forward handoff P17 → P18 — P17 establishes the epistemological face of the object-substance; P18 establishes the ontological-mechanical face of the functional-stratum reversal; the two, on the Yogācāra horizon, co-constitute the twin-axis structure of buddha-recollection.
- **P19 — *The Mañjuśrī-Prajñāpāramitā Single-Practice Samādhi*:** method-form (*eka-vyūha-samādhi*); P18 §6.2 marks the explicit forward handoff to P19's home territory — the complete tri-axis P17 → P18 → P19: object-substance → functional mechanism → method-form.
- **P20–P24 — *The Two-Line Interweaving of Pure Land and the Chinese Confluence*:** P18 §6.2 notes the fact that the Chinese Pure Land patriarchs do not engage the *Ghanavyūha*, and marks P18 as a constructive-bridge proposal rather than historical inquiry; Part VI treats the historical and doctrinal interweaving of the Pure Land and Chan lines head-on.
