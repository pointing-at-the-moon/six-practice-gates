## Abstract
This paper takes up the third member of Part V's Mahāyāna-Eight-Sūtras cluster — the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* (*Yuánjué jīng* 圓覺經, T0842) — arguing three things. **First**, the three final-age chapters (Maitreya / Universal-Awakening / Perfect-Enlightenment) progressively stage the verb-structure of final-age buddha-recollection: Maitreya's **severing** (*duàn* 斷) the root of cyclic craving as inward origin, Universal-Awakening's **leaving** (*lí* 離) the four meditative diseases (fabrication / passivity / cessation / annihilation) as outward safeguarding, and Perfect-Enlightenment's **binding** (*jié* 結) the three periods (120 / 100 / 80 days) as temporal structuring — three axes operating simultaneously in every utterance of the Buddha's name, not as sequential stages. **Second**, Guifeng Zongmi's *Subcommentary on the Abridged Commentary to the Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* (X09n0248) establishes a dual-layer verb grammar through the tension between "thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind" (*sīfó yì lúnhuí xīn* 思佛亦輪迴心) and "bowing to the names of the ten-direction Buddhas" (*jīshǒu shífāng fómíng* 稽首十方佛名) — phenomenal recollection (*shìniàn* 事念) is admissible for final-age entry, while noumenal recollection (*lǐniàn* 理念) must dissolve the subject-object structure; the four diseases mapped onto four misfires of buddha-recollection form the metronome of the verb. This parallels Jiaoguang's distinction in P12 between *faculty-gathering recollection* and *recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness*, forming Part V's second commentarial axis. **Third**, the Bhadrasvara chapter's Twenty-Five Pure Cultivation Wheels — the apex of *chán* meditation theory as a permutational exhaustion of *śamatha* / *samāpatti* / *dhyāna* — conclude with the Buddha's own self-limitation: "excepting those of sudden awakening and those whom the dharma does not accord with" (*wéichú dùnjué-rén, bìng fǎ bù suíshùn* 唯除頓覺人，并法不隨順). This self-limitation, rarely foregrounded in the Chinese commentarial tradition, is the sūtra's own textual warrant for the gate of final-age buddha-recollection — a gate not externally imported, but opened by the Buddha himself in the verse's final line.

**Keywords:** *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*, T0842, final-age buddha-recollection, time-dependent verbal grammar, severing-leaving-binding, three final-age chapters, Maitreya chapter, Universal-Awakening chapter, Perfect-Enlightenment chapter, root of cyclic craving, four meditative diseases, fabrication, passivity, cessation, annihilation, three periods of cultivation, "binding the three periods," twenty-one-day repentance, three pure contemplations, *śamatha*, *samāpatti*, *dhyāna*, Bhadrasvara chapter, Twenty-Five Pure Cultivation Wheels, "excepting those of sudden awakening," "those whom the dharma does not accord with," dice-wheel method, "relying on the Buddha's great compassionate power," Guifeng Zongmi, *Subcommentary on the Abridged Commentary*, X09n0248, "thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind," "bowing to the names of the ten-direction Buddhas," phenomenal and noumenal recollection, regulated-recollection and gathered-recollection, recollection of the Buddha's grace, phenomenal and noumenal repentance, "knowing-illusion is leaving-illusion," Universal-Worthy chapter, Great Bright Treasure of Spiritual Penetration, "the unsurpassed Dharma King possesses a great dhāraṇī-gate," empty-flower metaphor, final-age alternative gate, verb-metronome, four misfires of buddha-recollection.

## I. Introduction — The Verbal Rhythm of the Final-Age Time-Segment

Of the several temporal grammars of practice available to Western anglophone Buddhist readers, the most readily assimilable is the "present-moment-ism" bequeathed by secularized mindfulness traditions — every moment an equally cultivable point of entry, with no need to distinguish epochs or partition time-segments. The structural strength of this time-grammar is the dissolution of religious-frame thresholds on eligibility (one need not be born in a particular era, need not possess a particular status); its structural cost is that the rhythm of practice is severed from historical time — all moments flattened onto a single unstructured plane.

Yet within the Chinese-Buddhist tradition there is a sūtra that expressly refuses this time-grammar — not by metaphysical proposition, but by verb-structure. The sūtra is *Dà fāngguǎng yuánjué xiūduōluó liǎoyì jīng* 大方廣圓覺修多羅了義經 (hereafter *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*; T0842), one fascicle, twelve chapters, traditionally ascribed to the Tang-era translator Buddhatrāta (*Fótuóduōluó* 佛陀多羅).[^1] The *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* takes the expression "sentient beings of the final age" (*mòshì zhòngshēng* 末世眾生) as the pivot term running through all twelve chapters of question-and-answer — the phrase is flagged explicitly some thirty times across the text — and structurally places the three-clause diagnosis "the Buddha grows ever more distant, the sages and worthies fall hidden, heterodox dharmas grow ever more ablaze" (*qù fó jiàn yuǎn, xiánshèng yǐn fú, xié fǎ zēngchì* 去佛漸遠、賢聖隱伏、邪法增熾)[^2] into the very site of practice — not as historical description but as a determining term of the rhythm of action. Timing determines action; action is embedded in the time-segment. By this bidirectional structure, the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* converts buddha-recollection from "a single act performable at any moment" into "a structured act possessing a specific rhythm within a specific time-segment."

This paper takes up the fifth layer of the NIAN longitudinal verbal-grammar axis: P08 on the *niàn-chēng* four-step coupling → P11 on *holding the name* (resolute severing + steady preservation) → P12 on *gathering in the six sense-roots* (spatial integration) → P13 on *universal-gate name-invocation* (three-karmic architecture + prose-verse dual-track) → P14 on **time-dependency** (time-segment rhythm).[^3] The first four layers treated the internal structure of buddha-recollection as action (verb, cognition, space, three karmas); P14 enters temporal structure — buddha-recollection is not only a question of "how to perform it," but also of "when to perform it, for how long, and in what rhythm."

The argument proceeds on three layers. **Layer One** (§III): the three final-age chapters — Maitreya, Universal-Awakening (*Pǔjué* 普覺), and Perfect-Enlightenment (*Yuánjué* 圓覺) — constitute, through their action-progression of "severing → leaving → binding," the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*'s complete response to the question of how sentient beings should practice buddha-recollection in the final age: severing the root of cyclic craving (inward origin), leaving the four meditative diseases (outward safeguarding), and binding the three periods of cultivation (temporal structuring). These three chapters are not juxtaposed accounts but the sūtra's own graded design of action. **Layer Two** (§IV): Guifeng Zongmi's (*Guīfēng Zōngmì* 圭峰宗密, 780–841) *Subcommentary on the Abridged Commentary to the Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* (*Yuánjué jīng lüèshū zhī chāo* 圓覺經略疏之鈔, X09n0248), through the dual-layer structure of "thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind" (*sīfó yì lúnhuí xīn* 思佛亦輪迴心) set against "bowing to the names of the ten-direction Buddhas" (*jīshǒu shífāng fómíng* 稽首十方佛名), clarifies the phenomenal-noumenal stratification of final-age buddha-recollection — phenomenal recollection is usable for entering practice; noumenal recollection, if left arrested in a subject-object structure, is itself cyclic mind. The four diseases (fabrication / passivity / cessation / annihilation) mapped onto four misfires of buddha-recollection serve as the verb-metronome. This dual-layer scheme is structurally isomorphic with P12's Jiaoguang distinction between "faculty-gathering recollection" and "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness," and so constitutes the second node of Part V's cross-paper commentarial axis. **Layer Three** (§V): the Bhadrasvara (*Biànyīn púsà* 辯音菩薩) chapter's Twenty-Five Pure Cultivation Wheels are the apex expression of *chán* meditation theory; the final line of its summary verse — "excepting those of sudden awakening, and those whom the dharma does not accord with" (*wéichú dùnjué rén, bìng fǎ bù suíshùn* 唯除頓覺人、并法不隨順) — is, immediately after the Buddha's exhortation on the Twenty-Five Wheels, his own active self-limitation of the Wheels' scope of capacity-response: those of sudden awakening and those who are in accord with the dharma can use them; other final-age capacities must be given an alternative expedient. Final-age buddha-recollection is seated precisely within this "alternative expedient"; the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* itself supplies, in the closing line of the verse, the sūtric warrant for the final-age alternate gate.

§VI closes with a vertical foreshadowing of P15 on the *Brahma Net Sūtra* (*Fànwǎng jīng* 梵網經) — when the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* handles final-age buddha-recollection through the time-segment structure of "binding the three periods," the *Brahma Net* takes the precept-body (*jiètǐ* 戒體) of the bodhisattva precepts as its axis and unfolds a verb-structure of "precept-dependency." Time-segment (P14) and precept (P15) are two modes of structuring final-age practice; together, the two papers construct the time-and-space twin-axis of the middle stretch of Part V.

## II. The Prior Seating of the Body of Perfect Enlightenment

### 2.1 The Opening and the Mañjuśrī Chapter — The Great Bright Treasure of Spiritual Penetration and the *Dhāraṇī* Gate Named Perfect Enlightenment

The *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* does not open from Vulture Peak, nor from Jetavana Grove, but from the Tathāgata's own self-realized state:[^4]

> "Thus have I heard. At one time, the Bhagavān entered the Great Bright Treasure of Spiritual Penetration (*shéntōng dà guāngmíng zàng* 神通大光明藏), the right reception of *samādhi*; the light-adornment abiding of all Tathāgatas; the ground of pure enlightenment of all sentient beings. Body and mind extinct, the equal primordial edge; perfectly filling the ten directions, non-dual conformity; within the non-dual realm manifesting all pure lands." (T0842, Fascicle 1; translation mine)

"The Great Bright Treasure of Spiritual Penetration" is not a geographical location but the Tathāgata's self-realized *samādhi*-realm. This *samādhi* is simultaneously **that which abides** (the Buddha's realized state) and **that in which one abides** (the ground of enlightenment inherent in sentient beings) — subject and object non-dual, the ordinary and the saintly sharing one source. This is the first keynote of the entire sūtra. All twelve chapters of dialogue unfold beneath this keynote. That final-age sentient beings can still practice in the final age is not because some special expedient has been specifically added for them, but because final-age sentient beings still share this equal primordial edge. Time-segment differences (the age of decline) do not alter the shared dwelling of the ground of enlightenment. This is, at the deepest ontological level, the warrant of the whole P14 argument on final-age buddha-recollection.

The twelve leading bodhisattvas of the assembly gather in this site; Mañjuśrī (*Wénshū-shīlì* 文殊師利) is first to request. The core concern of Mañjuśrī's question is ontological: what is the pure causal ground from which all Tathāgatas originally arise? The Buddha's reply contains the densest doctrinal passage in the sūtra; its key line is:[^5]

> "Good sons! The unsurpassed Dharma King possesses a great *dhāraṇī* gate, named Perfect Enlightenment, from which flow forth all pure *tathatā*, *bodhi*, *nirvāṇa*, and the *pāramitā*-teachings that instruct bodhisattvas. The original causal ground from which all Tathāgatas first arise is nothing other than reliance on the perfectly-illumining mark of pure enlightenment, severing ignorance forever, and thereby completing the Buddha-path." (Ibid.; translation mine)

"The unsurpassed Dharma King possesses a great *dhāraṇī* gate, named Perfect Enlightenment" — this sentence is, structurally, the highest-ranking definition that the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* offers for the buddha-recollection dharma-gate. *Dhāraṇī* means total retention; Perfect Enlightenment means the perfectly-filled mark of enlightenment. The two terms joined designate "the meta-gate that retains all gates." If buddha-recollection is read as the act of totalizing the mark of enlightenment within a single moment of mind, then buddha-recollection is the concrete operation of this *dhāraṇī* gate. Buddha-recollection is not one gate among the eighty-four thousand, but the common structure within each gate. For the whole book *Mindful of the Buddha*, which positions *niàn* as the foundational ground among the four practice gates, this line of T0842 supplies sūtric warrant at the highest structural grade.

The Mañjuśrī chapter then unfolds the empty-flower metaphor (*kōng-huā yù* 空花喻) to show that ignorance has no substance:[^6]

> "All sentient beings, from beginningless time, have undergone every kind of inversion: like a deluded person who mistakes the four cardinal directions, they mistakenly identify the four great elements as the mark of their own body, and the shadow-images of the six dust-objects as the mark of their own mind. It is as when diseased eyes see flowers in the sky and a second moon. Good sons! The sky is in truth without any flower; it is the sick one who mistakes it. By this mistake, not only is the nature of the empty sky confused, but the very place of the arising of a real flower is also lost. By this mistaken arising there is the turning of birth-and-death — this is called ignorance." (Ibid.; translation mine)

The structural keystone of the empty-flower metaphor is **substancelessness**. Ignorance is not a real entity that must be annihilated — "like a person in a dream: at the time of the dream it is not non-existent, but at the moment of waking nothing at all is obtained." Dispelling ignorance is not shattering an existent but waking up. This structure is crucial for P14's handling of final-age buddha-recollection: if final-age sentient beings are presumed to be "heavily-veiled and must be dragged out by an external force," then buddha-recollection is read as a remedial expedient. But read through the empty-flower metaphor, final-age beings share the very same ground of enlightenment as the original causal ground of all Tathāgatas — the only difference is between "acknowledgment" and "non-acknowledgment." Under this reading, buddha-recollection becomes "the act of waking up," not "a remedial expedient."

### 2.2 The Universal-Worthy Chapter's "Knowing-Illusion is Leaving-Illusion" — The Pivotal Bridge from §II to §III

After the Mañjuśrī chapter fixes the view (sentient beings are originally empty), the Universal-Worthy (*Pǔxián púsà* 普賢菩薩) chapter immediately takes up the next question: under this view, how does one take up practice? The question is sharp. If body and mind are both illusory, who practices? If illusion is used to cultivate illusion, how is that possible? If all illusory natures are utterly extinguished, then there is no mind — who, then, is the practitioner? The Buddha's answer in the Universal-Worthy chapter does not solve this paradox; it dismantles its premise. The long-prose reply concludes with sixteen characters in three clauses — the most condensed expression of the sudden dharma in the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*:[^7]

> "Good sons! Knowing illusion is itself leaving illusion — no expedient is enacted; leaving illusion is itself enlightenment — nor are there gradations. All bodhisattvas and sentient beings of the final age who practice in accordance with this are in this very way able to leave all illusions forever." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Universal-Worthy Chapter*, p.914a20–a21; translation mine)

These sixteen characters in three clauses **belong to the Universal-Worthy chapter** (p.914a20–a21), not the Mañjuśrī chapter; this is an attribution correction after CBETA entity verification. Some Chinese commentators on the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* have occasionally attached this passage to the Mañjuśrī chapter; but in fact the Mañjuśrī chapter ends at "sentient beings of the final age practicing in this way will not fall into deviant views" (p.913c11), while the Universal-Worthy chapter opens at p.913c23 and closes at p.914b04 (the last line of its verse). The precision of this attribution is not merely a philological detail — the passage's location within the Universal-Worthy chapter is itself structurally significant: Mañjuśrī fixes the view, Universal-Worthy steps down into action; the two cannot be interchanged.

The structural unpacking of these three clauses is critical for the argument of §III on the three final-age chapters:

**"Knowing illusion is itself leaving illusion — no expedient is enacted."** The instant of knowing is already the instant of leaving; the two are not sequential. This is the verb-structure of the sudden dharma: no prior cultivation of some expedient (breath-regulation, body-contemplation, *śamatha*-*vipaśyanā*, mantra-retention) is required before one can leave illusion. The constitution of cognition and the occurrence of release happen in the same *kṣaṇa*. Applied to buddha-recollection: each utterance of the name is itself one concrete instance of "knowing-illusion is leaving-illusion" — knowing that this moment's delusive thought is illusion is itself non-grasping; the occurrence of leaving is precisely within the rise and close of one utterance of the Buddha's name.

**"Leaving illusion is itself enlightenment — nor are there gradations."** Leaving is not a target-state that must be attained; rather, it is the present manifestation of enlightenment. It is not that enlightenment comes after leaving; enlightenment is the very present instance of leaving. Nor is there any gradient of small-leaving-small-enlightenment and great-leaving-great-enlightenment; each leaving is itself one full instance of enlightenment. Applied to final-age buddha-recollection: the final-age ordinary person and the ten-recollections-at-death share one dharma-gate; the deathbed ten-recollections are neither "the simplified version" nor "the discounted version" — under the structure "leaving-illusion is enlightenment," the ten recollections at death and the lifelong million recollections are both concrete instances of the same action-structure.

The three clauses of the Universal-Worthy chapter supply the methodological ground of the §III argument on the three final-age chapters: final-age sentient beings are not disqualified by the age, nor disqualified by capacity. The act of "knowing-illusion is leaving-illusion" can be aroused at any moment; the sudden entry expressed by "nor are there gradations" presupposes no prior accumulation of resources. In the next section, as we enter the three final-age chapters, these three clauses will repeatedly show themselves as the **underlying progressive structure**: severing (Maitreya) → leaving (Universal-Awakening) → binding (Perfect-Enlightenment) — each of these progressions is a specific temporal unfolding of the same "knowing-illusion is leaving-illusion."

## III. The Three Final-Age Chapters — The Action-Progression of Severing → Leaving → Binding

Of the twelve chapters of the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*, three take "sentient beings of the final age" as the explicit interlocutor: the Maitreya chapter (fifth), the Universal-Awakening chapter (tenth), and the Perfect-Enlightenment chapter (eleventh). These three chapters are not parallel and independent; they form an action-progression. **The Maitreya chapter handles the inward origin of final-age practice (severing the root of cyclic craving); the Universal-Awakening chapter handles outward safeguarding (leaving the four meditative diseases); the Perfect-Enlightenment chapter handles temporal structuring (binding the period of days for the three retreats).** The three characters "severing → leaving → binding" (*duàn → lí → jié* 斷→離→結) are the verb-skeleton of this progression.

### 3.1 The Maitreya Chapter — Severing the Root of Cyclic Craving as the Inward Origin

Maitreya opens the question-segment by juxtaposing "all final-age sentient beings" with "bodhisattvas" as co-inquirers:[^8]

> "World-Honored One! If bodhisattvas and sentient beings of the final age wish to roam the great ocean of the Tathāgata's calm extinction, how shall they sever the root of cyclic craving? … We only pray the Great Compassion that saves the world not withhold, but cause all bodhisattvas and sentient beings of the final age among the practitioners to have eyes of wisdom that are clear and bright, mind-mirrors that radiate, and perfect realization of the Tathāgata's unsurpassed knowing and seeing." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Maitreya Chapter*, p.916a15–a26; translation mine)

The structural significance of this request is this: in the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*, final-age sentient beings are not subsidiary objects attached to bodhisattvas; they are co-equal recipients of the teaching. The two phrases "the fearless dharma-eye" and "decisive faith in Great *nirvāṇa*" are the baseline markings of the chapter's position on final-age practice: the legitimacy of final-age practice does not require special argument, because the Buddha himself has already placed final-age sentient beings in the same receiving position as bodhisattvas.

The Buddha's reply forms the core of the Maitreya chapter:[^9]

> "Good sons! All sentient beings, from beginningless edge, by reason of all kinds of affection-and-craving and desire, thus undergo cyclic existence. … You should know that cyclic existence has craving as its root. By the existence of desires, the nature of craving is aided and aroused; therefore birth-and-death is made to continue. … Thus sentient beings wishing to escape birth-and-death, to avoid all cyclic existence, must first sever desire and remove the thirst of craving.
>
> If all sentient beings of the final age can relinquish the desires, remove hatred and love, and forever sever cyclic existence, and diligently seek the realm of the Tathāgata's perfect enlightenment, then within a pure mind they will at once awaken." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Maitreya Chapter*, p.916b04–b19; translation mine)

The Maitreya chapter sets the first beat of final-age practice as **severing** — severing the root of cyclic craving. The action-structure is a four-beat sequence: **relinquish desire · remove hatred-and-love · diligently seek · awaken**. These four beats form a single complete verbal sequence, not a staged task-list. Relinquishing desire and removing hatred-and-love are inward loosening (loosening the structure of grasping what-is-mine); diligent seeking is the establishment of the verbal dimension (a sustained direction of action); awakening is the spontaneous self-occurrence of a pure mind. If buddha-recollection is placed within this four-beat structure, then name-invocation becomes the concrete action-form of "diligently seeking the realm of Perfect Enlightenment" — the object of seeking is not an external realm but the process of loosening initiated by name-invocation itself.

The Maitreya chapter's final section supplies a vow-template for final-age sentient beings:[^10]

> "If all sentient beings of the final age arouse a superior mind toward Great Perfect Enlightenment, they should utter the pure great vow of the bodhisattva, saying: 'May I now abide in the Buddha's Perfect Enlightenment, seek good spiritual friends, and never meet those of heterodox paths or of the two vehicles.' Practicing in accordance with this vow, gradually severing all obstacles, when obstacles are exhausted and the vow is fulfilled, one ascends the pure dharma-hall of liberation and realizes the wondrous adorned realm of Great Perfect Enlightenment." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Maitreya Chapter*, p.916c07–c14; translation mine)

This is the **only** passage in the entire *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* to supply an explicit vow-template for final-age sentient beings. The three clauses — "May I now abide in the Buddha's Perfect Enlightenment, seek good spiritual friends, and never meet those of heterodox paths or of the two vehicles" — form the *Yuánjué* prototype of the vow of buddha-recollection. The first clause ("abide in Buddha's Perfect Enlightenment") is *that-which-is-abided-in*; the second ("seek good spiritual friends") is the *external condition*; the third ("never meet heterodox paths or the two vehicles") is *risk-avoidance*. The three clauses together form a micro-vow-structure that can serve as the inward coordinate at the beginning of each utterance of the name for the final-age practitioner. The Maitreya chapter's "practicing in accordance with the vow, gradually severing all obstacles" explicitly lays out the full structure of final-age action-logic: **vow (vow-axis) + name-invocation / recollection (action-axis) + gradual severing (fruit-axis)** — three axes operating simultaneously, all indispensable.

### 3.2 The Universal-Awakening Chapter — Leaving the Four Diseases as Outward Safeguarding

Once the Maitreya chapter has established the inward origin, the Universal-Awakening chapter handles outward safeguarding: how are final-age sentient beings to avoid being led astray by deviant teachers and deviant dharmas? The verb shifts from "severing" to "leaving."

Universal-Awakening's request consists of five questions:[^11]

> "World-Honored One! The sentient beings of the final age are ever more distant from the Buddha; the sages and worthies fall hidden; heterodox dharmas grow ever more ablaze. What sort of person should such beings seek? On what sort of dharma should they rely? What sort of practice should they perform? What illness should they remove? How should they arouse the mind, so that the blind multitude will not fall into deviant views?" (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Universal-Awakening Chapter*, p.920a25–b03; translation mine)

The twelve characters "the Buddha grows ever more distant, the sages and worthies fall hidden, heterodox dharmas grow ever more ablaze" are one of the most compact Chinese declarations on the phase-mark of the final age — more present on the ground than the doctrinal framework of Japanese Pure Land's three-period teaching (true, semblance, final). The three clauses form a progression: Buddha-distance (temporal remove) → sages-hidden (absence of exemplars) → heterodoxy-ablaze (intensification of interference). Temporal factor, exemplary factor, and environmental factor deteriorate simultaneously. These three constitute the problematic basis of the chapter's teacher-selection argument.

The five-fold question — "what sort of person / what sort of dharma / what sort of practice / what illness to remove / how to arouse the mind" — is the **five-slot architecture** of final-age buddha-recollection action-logic: the object-slot (the teacher and dharma to be relied upon), the teaching-slot (the sūtras and treatises relied upon), the action-slot (the form of action cultivated), the illness-slot (the four meditative diseases to be avoided), and the vow-slot (the direction of aroused mind). Only when all five slots are simultaneously filled can final-age buddha-recollection be established; any empty slot falls into deviant practice.

The core of the Buddha's reply is the discourse on the four diseases, the central citation of §III:[^12]

> "Good sons! The wondrous dharma realized by that good spiritual friend ought to be free of four diseases. What are the four?
>
> **First, the disease of fabrication**: If some person says, 'I apply various practices on my original mind in order to seek Perfect Enlightenment,' — yet the nature of Perfect Enlightenment cannot be attained by fabrication; hence this is called a disease.
>
> **Second, the disease of passivity**: If some person says, 'We now neither sever birth-and-death nor seek *nirvāṇa*; there is no arising-and-ceasing recollection of *nirvāṇa* and birth-and-death; we yield passively to everything, conforming to the nature of dharmas, and thus seek Perfect Enlightenment,' — yet the nature of Perfect Enlightenment does not exist by passive letting-be; hence this is called a disease.
>
> **Third, the disease of cessation**: If some person says, 'I now make my own mind forever cease all recollections; the nature of all things becomes still and uniform; I seek Perfect Enlightenment thereby,' — yet the nature of Perfect Enlightenment does not accord with cessation; hence this is called a disease.
>
> **Fourth, the disease of annihilation**: If some person says, 'I now forever sever all defilements; body and mind are utterly empty and without anything; still less are there the false realms of faculties and dust-objects; all is forever still — I seek Perfect Enlightenment thereby,' — yet the nature of Perfect Enlightenment is not of the mark of stillness; hence this is called a disease.
>
> One who is free of the four diseases then knows purity; such observation is called right observation; any other observation is called deviant observation." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Universal-Awakening Chapter*, p.920b19–c03; translation mine)

The four-diseases framework is **the most independently profound contribution** of the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*'s account of final-age buddha-recollection. The structural signature is that each disease closes with "because the nature of Perfect Enlightenment is not attained / accorded / existent-in / still" — the nature of Perfect Enlightenment cannot be acquired through any of the four action-types (fabrication / passivity / cessation / annihilation). This is the sūtra's structural verdict on the essence of practice: cultivation is not achieved by the selection of an action-type; any action-type can devolve into a propositional hardening of the form "says as follows."

The four diseases map directly onto four common misreadings of buddha-recollection, familiar in both Western-anglophone and Chinese-Buddhist settings: **fabrication-disease** corresponds to "over-effortful recollection" — the mistake of supposing that buddha-recollection attains Perfect Enlightenment by one's own fabrication; **passivity-disease** corresponds to "let-go passive recollection" — the mistake of supposing that because mind is itself Buddha, no recollection is necessary (the error of the later "no-thing *chán*," and the modern variant that dismisses scattered-mind recollection as useless); **cessation-disease** corresponds to "thought-suppression recollection" — mistaking buddha-recollection for the annihilation of all thoughts (this cuts especially close to the common error in dual *chán*-Pure Land practice of treating recollection as a tool of *śamatha*-*vipaśyanā*); **annihilation-disease** corresponds to "annihilationist recollection" — mistaking buddha-recollection for the extinction of all experience (taking no-thought as terminus, negating recollection itself).

This four-diseases framework provides the groundwork for §IV's discussion of Zongmi's dual-layer recollection as a verb-metronome. Zongmi maps these four diseases onto "the four misfires of buddha-recollection," shifting the criterion of pathology from the propositional "says as follows" into the verbal "misplacement of the operation-type of *niàn*." This is the core of §IV's argument.

The Universal-Awakening chapter's final segment also records the final-age vow of aroused mind:[^13]

> "As far as sentient beings fill all space, I shall cause them all to enter final Perfect Enlightenment; yet within Perfect Enlightenment there is no grasper of enlightenment — removing all the marks of self, others, and persons." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Universal-Awakening Chapter*, p.920c12–c15; translation mine)

The structural note on this vow is: the vow's object (all sentient beings filling space) and the vow's essential nature (within Perfect Enlightenment no enlightened subject) stand simultaneously. The former is the maximal outward reach in the Universal-Worthy vow-and-practice sense; the latter is the dissolution of subject-object in the sudden-dharma sense. In final-age buddha-recollection these are not in contradiction but are a bidirectional action: name-invocation outward (saving all beings) + name-invocation inward (no graspable enlightenment). The "single-minded name-invocation" of the Universal Gate chapter in P13 is the *Fǎhuá* version of this structure; the Universal-Awakening chapter's final-age vow is its *Yuánjué* version — the former rising from the mutual reflection of subject and object, the latter rising from the original absence of subject and object.[^14]

### 3.3 The Perfect-Enlightenment Chapter — Binding the Three Periods as Temporal Structuring

After the Universal-Awakening chapter has laid down outward safeguarding, the Perfect-Enlightenment chapter enters temporal structuring — final-age sentient beings need not only severing and leaving but also an explicit rhythm of time-segments. The verb shifts from "leaving" to "binding."

The Perfect-Enlightenment bodhisattva's request cuts directly to the temporal question of final-age practice:[^15]

> "We have now received awakening. If, after the Buddha's extinction, sentient beings of the final age have not yet awakened, how shall they securely dwell and practice this pure realm of Perfect Enlightenment? Among the three pure contemplations within this Perfect Enlightenment, which is to be taken as the starting point?" (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Perfect-Enlightenment Chapter*, p.920c27–921a05; translation mine)

"How shall sentient beings of the final age — those who have not yet awakened after the Buddha's extinction — securely dwell and practice?" This question positions final-age practice as a **structured action within a time-segment**, not the diffuse practice of "cultivating whenever one feels like it." The follow-up question "which is to be taken as the starting point" draws out the action-ordering of the three pure contemplations — action-logic moves from single-action to sequence-of-actions.

The Buddha's reply opens the three-period structure:[^16]

> "Good sons! Among all sentient beings, whether the Buddha is in the world, after the Buddha's extinction, or during the final age of the dharma, if there are beings possessing the Mahāyāna nature who have faith in the Buddha's secret great mind of Perfect Enlightenment and wish to cultivate practice… they should build a ritual site (*dàochǎng* 道場) and set a period: a long period of one hundred and twenty days, a middle period of one hundred days, a short period of eighty days; they should arrange a pure dwelling.
>
> If the Buddha is present, they should rightly contemplate him. If the Buddha has entered extinction, they should set up his likeness, holding it in mind, with the eye imagining, giving rise to right recollection — as though returning to the days when the Tathāgata constantly abided. They should hang banners and flowers; for three seven-day spans (*sān-qī-rì* 三七日), they should bow to the names of the Buddhas of the ten directions and ardently seek repentance. Should good signs come, the mind obtains tranquil ease. Beyond the three seven-days, they should single-mindedly gather in recollection." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Perfect-Enlightenment Chapter*, p.921a06–a24; translation mine)

The temporal structure of the Perfect-Enlightenment chapter has three strata:

**Stratum one: the three times co-listed.** "Whether the Buddha is in the world, after the Buddha's extinction, or in the final age of the dharma." This triple-time co-listing is the sūtra's **structural verdict** on final-age practice: it is not a degenerate set of methods specially crafted for the final age, but the same dharma-gate applied uniformly across three times. The final age is not "the degraded version"; it is the presentation of the one Perfect Enlightenment within a different timing. This position refutes the "final-age-equals-evolutionary-regression" presumption of certain Western secularized Buddhist readings, and equally refutes the "final-age-equals-self-power-failure" presumption of certain Chinese Pure Land formulations — the positioning of the final age is not dharma-failure but a change in time-segment rhythm.

**Stratum two: the three-period time-limits.** 120 / 100 / 80 days. The three-level structure of long, middle, and short periods is an explicit time-boundary of final-age practice. This is not an unbounded drifting lifelong-slow mode, but a diligent segment with a clear deadline. The significance of the three-period ordering is: final-age practice, through time-segment structuring, pushes back against the unstructured drift-tendency of time itself.

**Stratum three: the twenty-one-day beat-pattern.** "Hanging banners and flowers, for three seven-days, bowing to the names of the Buddhas of the ten directions and ardently seeking repentance" is the first beat within each period (the *repentance beat*); "beyond the three seven-days, single-mindedly gather in recollection" is the second beat (the *recollection beat*). The **repentance → recollection** two-beat rhythm is the micro-structure of final-age buddha-recollection action-logic: first, over three seven-days, clear karmic obstacles by the action of repentance; thereafter, secure the pure recollection by the action of recollection-gathering. This two-beat structure will be further unpacked in §IV's Zongmi exegesis as the dual-layer verb of phenomenal and noumenal recollection.

The clause "holding in mind, with the eye imagining, giving rise to right recollection — as though returning to the days when the Tathāgata constantly abided" is the most direct action-definition of "buddha-recollection" in T0842 — not a single oral axis but **three axes running together: holding-in-mind, eye-imagining, and right-recollection**. Compared with P13's Universal Gate chapter single-oral-axis of "single-minded name-invocation," P14's *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* is three-axis co-running. The verb-structure extends from P13's "invoking" to P14's tripartite "holding-in-mind · eye-imagining · recollecting."[^17]

The eight characters "as though returning to the days when the Tathāgata constantly abided" name the psychological-spatial-temporal effect of final-age buddha-recollection — buddha-recollection / contemplation of the likeness causes the final age to be compressed back into the site of the true dharma through action. This is not a metaphysical claim (no assertion of physical time-compression); it is a phenomenological claim (at the level of the practitioner's experience, the very moment of buddha-recollection is the very moment of the true dharma). Final-age buddha-recollection is not a remedial expedient; it is the time-compression mechanism of action.

The Perfect-Enlightenment chapter closes by opening the first-expedient of the three pure contemplations:[^18]

> "If sentient beings cultivate *śamatha*, they first grasp utter stillness, not arousing a thought. … If they cultivate *samāpatti*, they should first recollect the Tathāgatas of the ten directions and all the bodhisattvas of the ten-direction worlds, and by various gates gradually cultivate *samādhi* with diligent labor, vastly issuing great vows that self-perfume to become seeds. … If they cultivate *dhyāna*, they first take the gate of counting, their mind clearly knowing the arising, abiding, and ceasing of thoughts. … This is called the first-expedient of the three contemplations.
>
> If sentient beings comprehensively cultivate the three kinds of diligent practice in earnest, then this is called the Tathāgata appearing in the world. If final-age sentient beings of dull capacities, whose mind wishes to seek the way, fail to realize it by reason of past karmic obstacles, they should diligently repent and constantly arouse hope; first severing hatred, love, jealousy, and flattery-and-crookedness; seeking a surpassing mind; according to the three pure contemplations, take up a single practice — once begun, do not practice in another contemplation; not letting the mind let go, gradually seek verification." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Perfect-Enlightenment Chapter*, p.921b02–b22; translation mine)

The verb-matrix of the three pure contemplations maps onto three final-age buddha-recollection types: **śamatha** (utter stillness · no thought) = still-recollection buddha-recollection (close to *chán*-school silent illumination); **samāpatti** (recollecting the ten-direction Tathāgatas and bodhisattvas · gradual labor · vast vow-arousal) = recollective-visualization buddha-recollection (directly answering to Pure Land remembrance-of-the-Buddha); **dhyāna** (counting-gate · knowing arising-abiding-ceasing thoughts · segmented by numeric units) = counting-breath buddha-recollection (close to *ānāpāna*).

The *samāpatti* sub-branch deserves particular attention — "they should first recollect the Tathāgatas of the ten directions and all the bodhisattvas of the ten-direction worlds" is the *Yuánjué* version of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate. This is sūtric evidence that the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* itself has buddha-recollection built in — buddha-recollection is not an expedient imported into the sūtra from the Pure Land gate and attached externally, but one gate within the internal structure of the *Yuánjué*'s three pure contemplations.

The closing lines, "Final-age sentient beings of dull capacities… according to the three pure contemplations, take up a single practice — once begun, do not practice in another contemplation; not letting the mind let go, gradually seek verification," are the gentlest and most stringent directive that the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* issues to the final-age practitioner. **The gentle side:** acknowledging the fact of "final-age dull capacities" without blame. **The stringent side:** "Once begun, do not practice in another contemplation" forbids gate-hopping; one must select one gate and deepen. This directive refutes the common error of "final-age practice needs many-gate mixed practice" — precisely because final-age beings' capacities are dispersed, they need all the more to choose one and go deep.

The three characters "binding the three periods" (*jié sān qī* 結三期) summarize the progression of "severing" (Maitreya), "leaving" (Universal-Awakening), and "binding" (Perfect-Enlightenment).[^19] These three chapters are not linear stages but three simultaneously running axes: severing is inward (loosening the structure of grasping what-is-mine); leaving is outward (avoiding the meditative diseases); binding is temporal (building the rhythm). In each utterance of the name by the final-age practitioner, these three axes arise together — one utterance of the name is one act of severing (loosening this moment's grasping) + one act of leaving (not falling into the four diseases) + one act of binding (embedded within the rhythm of the time-segment). The three axes co-running is what the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* permits as final-age buddha-recollection.

## IV. Zongmi's Dual-Layer Recollection and the Four-Diseases Metronome

Guifeng Zongmi (780–841), fifth patriarch of Huayan and fifth patriarch of Heze *chán*, unites Huayan doctrinal study and Heze *chán*; his *Subcommentary on the Abridged Commentary to the Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* (*Yuánjué jīng lüèshū zhī chāo* 圓覺經略疏之鈔, X09n0248), in twenty fascicles, is the most systematic Chinese commentarial treatment of final-age practice in this sūtra.[^20] Zongmi's handling of final-age buddha-recollection presents a surface tension — his exegesis of the Maitreya chapter explicitly verdicts "thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind," while his exegesis of the Universal-Awakening chapter permits "bowing to the names of the ten-direction Buddhas" as a final-age entry action. This tension is not a contradiction but a **phenomenal-noumenal dual-layer verbal grammar of buddha-recollection** — and this stratification is the core argument of §IV.

### 4.1 "Thinking of the Buddha is also a Samsaric Mind" — The Verdict-of-Pathology on the Subject-Object Structure

When Zongmi glosses the Maitreya chapter's "first sever the root of beginningless cyclic existence," he issues an extremely stringent verdict on "thinking of the Buddha":[^21]

> "'Thinking and measuring the Buddha's realm' — not only does it grasp onto worldly phenomena, but even thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind. The intent of that whole chapter of the sūtra is precisely this. If one merely takes the text and applies the thinking [thus criticized], the text says: 'Using thought to discriminate the Buddha's realm is like an empty flower further bearing empty fruit.' And as to measuring, the text says: 'Using a measuring mind to measure the Tathāgata's realm of Perfect Enlightenment is like taking a firefly's light to burn Mount Sumeru — one will never make it catch.' All these belong to the cyclic mind." (X09n0248, Fascicle 8; translation mine)

The six characters "thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind" (*sīfó yì lúnhuí xīn*) are the most direct negative verdict on buddha-recollection in the entire subcommentary. The reasoning is: "With a samsaric mind engendering samsaric views, seeking to enter the Tathāgata's ocean of great calm-extinction — one can never arrive." Any fixing upon the subject-object opposition of "the recollecting-self and the Buddha-name-recollected" is the cyclic mind. If buddha-recollection is operated as a subject-object structure of "the self-that-recollects" grasping "the Buddha-name-that-is-recollected," then, no matter how long one invokes or how many recollections one counts, one is moving within the operation of the cyclic mind; this mind can never enter the Tathāgata's ocean of great calm-extinction.

The structural keystone of this verdict is the character *sī* 思 (to think) — *sī* is the activity in which conscious discrimination is the subject, structurally necessarily a subject-object opposition of "thinker-versus-thought." Once the subject-object is posited, the cyclic mind is established. Zongmi verdicts this as the propositional hardening of "says as follows" (echoing the "says as follows" structure of the four diseases in the Universal-Awakening chapter). If buddha-recollection hardens into the proposition "I am able to recollect this Buddha in order to seek buddhahood," it falls into the fabrication-disease; such buddha-recollection is structurally at odds with the nature of Perfect Enlightenment ("because it cannot be attained by fabrication").

If this stringent verdict were read as a negation of buddha-recollection as such, it would be a misreading. In the same subcommentary, Zongmi gives a positive exegesis of the Universal-Awakening chapter's passage "bowing to the names of the ten-direction Buddhas." How do the positive and negative coexist? The next section addresses this tension.

### 4.2 "Bowing to the Names of the Ten-Direction Buddhas" — Regulated-Recollection and Gathered-Recollection as the Verb of Phenomenal Recollection

When Zongmi glosses the Universal-Awakening chapter's final-age ritual-site preparatory-practice segment, he gives a concrete affirmation of the buddha-recollection act:[^22]

> "The root-practice and verification. The text says: 'If after the Buddha's extinction, those who have faith in the mind of Perfect Enlightenment and wish to cultivate practice… should build a ritual site, setting this limit (long one hundred and twenty, middle one hundred, short eighty), arranging a pure dwelling.' The commentary adds that regulated-recollection (*kè-niàn* 尅念) and gathered-recollection (*shè-niàn* 攝念) are the preparatory practice — meaning that they set up an image … hang banners and flowers; for three seven-days, bow to the names of the Buddhas of the ten directions and ardently seek repentance; should good signs come, the mind obtains tranquil ease (all the foregoing is regulated-recollection); beyond three seven-days, single-mindedly gather in recollection, taking nothing of whatever appears that should not be taken (all the foregoing is gathered-recollection)." (X09n0248, Fascicle 1; translation mine)

Here Zongmi divides the action of the three-period ritual-site of the Perfect-Enlightenment chapter into two segments: the segment before the three seven-days is **regulated-recollection** (restraining thoughts), and the segment after the three seven-days is **gathered-recollection** (gathered into pure recollection). The action-difference between the two segments is:

**Regulated-recollection** includes bowing to the names of the ten-direction Buddhas, ardent repentance, and the attaining of tranquil ease upon good signs. The action-structure is **outward action initiating inward loosening**: body-karma bowing (prostration) + speech-karma name-invocation (vocalization) + mind-karma repentance (introspection) rise together; the outward action loosens the inner structures of grasping. Name-invocation here is a **component of the regulated-recollection action**, not an independent object of buddha-recollection.

**Gathered-recollection** — after the three seven-days, a continuous state of not-grasping-any-realm. The action-structure is **inward continuous integration**, corresponding to P12's "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted," the dual-axis structure of the Mahāsthāmaprāpta Perfect-Penetration chapter.

The critical discovery of Zongmi's distinction is this: **name-invocation as a component of the regulated-recollection action is one thing; "thinking of the Buddha" as a subject-object structure is another.** The former is action-initiation; the latter is object-grasping. The former's purpose is the loosening of internal structure through vocalization; the latter's purpose is taking the name as an existent object in order to seek a response. The *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* permits the former and refutes the latter — this is the structural rationale for how "thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind" and "bowing to the names of the ten-direction Buddhas" can coexist without conflict in Zongmi's subcommentary.

This distinction is structurally isomorphic with P12's Jiaoguang's *Commentary on the Straight Vein of the Śūraṅgama* distinction between "faculty-gathering recollection" (*shè-gēn niàn*) and "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness" (*fēnbié shíxīn suǒ niàn*).[^23] Within the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter, Jiaoguang establishes this distinction: faculty-gathering recollection takes the limpid stillness of the six roots as its mechanism; discriminative-consciousness recollection takes discriminating consciousness as subject. Within the *Yuánjué*, Zongmi establishes the parallel distinction: name-invocation-verb as the functioning of regulated-recollection initiation; "thinking of the Buddha" as the misplacement of subject-object grasping. The two commentators are about three hundred years apart, relying on different sūtras; but the structure of their distinctions is isomorphic — both treat "the partition of action-types" as the core diagnostic of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate. This is the structural manifestation of Part V's cross-paper commentarial axis: different Mahāyāna sūtra commentators independently evolve isomorphic verbal distinctions — not by coincidence, but as the historical manifestation of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate's own structure.

### 4.3 The Four Diseases Mapped onto Four Misfires of Buddha-Recollection

In his exegesis of the four diseases of the Universal-Awakening chapter, Zongmi adds, in Fascicle 8, a supplementary passage juxtaposing the Heze (*Hézé* 荷澤) teaching of "at the arising of the delusion, there is awakening":[^24]

> "Now, contemplation-wisdom is of two kinds: first, contemplation-illumination that matches the essence… second, the contemplation of the principles of empty, existent, and so forth. Now, among the four [diseases], only the annihilation-disease speaks of body and mind as utterly empty and lacking anything, which at first glance looks like the meaning of dharmas being empty. Yet this is also not contemplation-wisdom but merely the making of a count ('saying as follows') that one has arrived at the understanding of emptiness… To put it briefly, each of the four [diseases] is spoken of as 'says as follows' — this is precisely a verbal formation of reckoning. A mind that makes such a reckoning is called 'grasping views' — not 'contemplation-wisdom.' … As Master Heze taught his people: 'When the delusion arises, awakening is already there; when the delusion ceases, awakening ceases.' Is this not thoroughly different from the four diseases?" (X09n0248, Fascicle 8; translation mine)

Zongmi's argument is: the shared pathology of the four diseases is not in the specific content of "fabrication / passivity / cessation / annihilation" but in the structure of "**says as follows**" — the **propositional hardening** that replaces the rise-and-awake of action with a propositional self-announcement. "A mind that makes such a reckoning is called 'grasping views' — not 'contemplation-wisdom.'" The moment a practitioner erects the proposition "I am now doing this method in order to attain Perfect Enlightenment," that very proposition is the grasping-view; practice has devolved from a verb into a proposition.

The Heze dictum "when the delusion arises, awakening is already there; when the delusion ceases, awakening ceases" is the correct view Zongmi approves — the true structure of practice is the verbal flow of rise-and-awake, not a fixation upon any one of the four diseases as a proposition. Applied to buddha-recollection, the mapping of the four diseases to four misfires of buddha-recollection is the metronome of §IV:

| Zongmi's Four Diseases | Four Misfires of Buddha-Recollection | Typical Misreading Scenario |
|---|---|---|
| Fabrication | Subject-object buddha-seeking | Treating name-invocation as "doing a thing to invoke the Buddha's response"; name and name-recollected hardened into subject-object opposition |
| Passivity | Dismissal-of-scattered-recollection | Letting the mind scatter without integration, on the excuse that "mind is itself Buddha; no recollection needed" |
| Cessation | Buddha-recollection as *śamatha*-*vipaśyanā* tool | Recollection merely for *samādhi*, for thought-suppression; recollection serves *śamatha*-*vipaśyanā* rather than the reverse |
| Annihilation | Recollecting toward no-thought | Taking no-thought as the ultimate goal; negating recollection itself; all that remains is "empty nothing" |

These four misfires are the verb-metronome of final-age buddha-recollection: within each beat of recollection, all four misfires must be simultaneously avoided for the fluid motion of the verb to be maintained. Avoiding fabrication means not hardening recollection into a subject-object object; avoiding passivity means not diluting recollection into ineffective scatteredness; avoiding cessation means not instrumentalizing recollection as preparation for *śamatha*-*vipaśyanā*; avoiding annihilation means not pushing recollection toward the nihilistic terminus of negating recollection itself. With all four simultaneously avoided, buddha-recollection becomes the verbal flow of "rise-and-awake."

### 4.4 Dual-Layer Phenomenal / Noumenal Repentance and "Buddha-Recollection as Repayment of Grace"

In his exegesis of the Chapter on the Bodhisattva Who Purifies Karmic Obstacles (*Jìng zhūyèzhàng púsà* 淨諸業障菩薩), Zongmi establishes the dual-layer structure of phenomenal-recollection and noumenal-recollection more sharply still:[^25]

> "Whoever wishes to repent must first revere the Three Jewels. Why so? The Three Jewels are the field of merit, good friends to sentient beings. … One should first arouse seven kinds of mind as an expedient: first, shame; second, dread; third, revulsion; fourth, arousal of *bodhicitta*; fifth, evenness between enemy and friend; sixth, recollection of the Buddha's grace; seventh, contemplation of the emptiness of the nature of offenses. … Also, the *Samantabhadra-Dhyāna Sūtra* and the 'Chapter on Following Appearance' of the *Huayan Sūtra* both prescribe two kinds of repentance-contemplation. The sūtra says that ardently prostrating to the Buddha day and night is *phenomenal repentance*; and contemplating mind as no-mind, whatever arises as arising from inversion — if one wishes to repent, sitting upright and recollecting reality — this is *noumenal repentance*. … Phenomenal repentance dispels the branches; noumenal repentance dispels the root." (X09n0248, Fascicle 9; translation mine)

The structural contribution of this section is: repentance is divided into phenomenal repentance (*shì-chàn* 事懺) and noumenal repentance (*lǐ-chàn* 理懺), with the explicit verdict that "phenomenal repentance dispels the branches; noumenal repentance dispels the root." Applied to buddha-recollection:

**Phenomenal recollection** — name-invocation, reverence, the three-seven-day ritual-site, day-and-night diligence, and other outward actions. The function is "dispelling the branches" — clearing the surface effects of karma. Phenomenal recollection is usable for final-age entry and must not be diluted.

**Noumenal recollection** — contemplating mind as no-mind, sitting upright and recollecting reality, and other inward actions. The function is "dispelling the root" — clearing the fundamental ignorance that gives rise to karma. Noumenal recollection is the deep layer of final-age practice, requiring phenomenal recollection as its prior course.

The relation of phenomenal and noumenal recollection is **dual-layer co-running**, not stage-progression. It is not that the practitioner first reaches a certain level of phenomenal recollection before turning to noumenal; phenomenal and noumenal recollection co-operate within the same name-invocation act — the vocal-stratum of name-invocation is phenomenal recollection; the subject-object-loosening stratum of the same invocation is noumenal recollection. In Zongmi's "seven-mind expedient," "recollection of the Buddha's grace" is placed between "arousal of *bodhicitta*" and "contemplation of the emptiness of offenses" — precisely at the positional design-point of this dual-layer structure. Buddha-recollection is not an independent trunk of practice but an intermediary verb passing from "arousing the vow" (inward origin) to "emptiness" (inward deepening).

The four characters *niàn-fó bào-ēn* 念佛報恩 ("buddha-recollection as repayment of grace") are a key locus in Zongmi's subcommentary where the two characters *niàn-fó* are explicitly used, and their usage is profoundly meaningful — the object of recollection is not the sound of the Buddha's name but **the Buddha's grace**; the act is not invocation but **repayment**. This usage thoroughly verbalizes: *niàn-fó* = recollecting the Buddha's grace and returning it through practice. This is not buddha-recollection in the Pure Land "holding-the-name" sense; it is buddha-recollection in the *Yuánjué* structural sense — the content recollected is the Buddha's grace (the object of recollection), the action is repayment (the essence of the verb).

With this §IV is closed. Zongmi's structural verdict on final-age buddha-recollection is: **phenomenal recollection is usable; noumenal recollection must dissolve subject-object; phenomenal dispels branches, noumenal dispels roots; the four diseases set the metronome, the dual layers establish the verb.** This verdict is structurally isomorphic with P12's Jiaoguang distinction between "faculty-gathering recollection" and "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness" in the Mahāsthāmaprāpta Perfect-Penetration chapter, and constitutes the second node of Part V's cross-paper commentarial axis.[^26]

## V. The Twenty-Five Pure Cultivation Wheels — "Excepting Those of Sudden Awakening" as the Sūtric Warrant for the Final-Age Alternate Gate

The Bhadrasvara chapter (Chapter 9) holds the pivotal position at the midpoint of the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*, serving as the transition from the causal-ground chapters (ontology) into the final-age chapters (expedient means). Its structure is the apex expression of *chán* meditation theory — the Twenty-Five Pure Cultivation Wheels, the sudden-and-gradual permutation of the three contemplations *śamatha*, *samāpatti*, and *dhyāna*. This chapter's apex-status is not evidence against final-age buddha-recollection; on the contrary, it is its positive sūtric warrant. The Buddha, immediately after exhorting the Twenty-Five Wheels, actively limits the scope of their capacity-response, leaving a sūtric opening for the final-age alternate gate (including buddha-recollection).

### 5.1 Overview of the Bhadrasvara Chapter — The Exhaustion of the Permutations of the Three Contemplations

Bhadrasvara's request is extremely brief:[^27]

> "World-Honored One! Of all these expedients, how many are the modes of practice by which bodhisattvas enter the gate of Perfect Enlightenment? We pray that for the great assembly and for the sentient beings of the final age, you will by expedient means reveal the marks of reality, so that all may awaken." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Bhadrasvara Chapter*, p.917b24; translation mine)

The structural significance of this request: the preceding chapter (Awe-Inspiring-and-Sovereign, *Wēidé zìzài* 威德自在) has already established the tripartite distinction among *śamatha*, *samāpatti*, and *dhyāna*; Bhadrasvara asks for the **exhaustion of permutations** among these three — how many modes of practice are, in the end, possible? The Buddha answers with twenty-five wheels: three single-practice wheels (wheels 1–3), seven *śamatha*-leading wheels (4–10), seven *samāpatti*-leading wheels (11–17), seven *dhyāna*-leading wheels (18–24), and one perfect-practice wheel (25), totaling twenty-five.[^28]

Each wheel has the predicative structure "If a bodhisattva… this bodhisattva is called one who practices X." The twenty-five wheels exhaust all sudden-and-gradual permutations of the three contemplations. This exhaustiveness is the apex of *chán* meditation theory within the whole sūtra — the Buddha displays to the assembly, in the most exhaustive permutation, the complete spectrum of possible paths of bodhisattva cultivation of Perfect Enlightenment.

### 5.2 The Dice-Wheel Method — The Prototype of the Self-Power / Other-Power Transition

The exhortation at the end of the Bhadrasvara chapter is the application-method of the twenty-five wheels:[^29]

> "Good sons! These are what are called the bodhisattvas' twenty-five wheels; all bodhisattvas practice as such. If bodhisattvas and sentient beings of the final age wish to rely upon these wheels, they should uphold the pure conduct, quiet their contemplation, ardently seek repentance, and pass through three seven-days. Then on each of the twenty-five wheels they should place a marker, and with sincere mind seek compassionately, taking one with the hand by lot; by what they obtain, the sudden or gradual is revealed — but a single thought of doubt or regret and the practice will not be accomplished." (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Bhadrasvara Chapter*, end of p.917c; translation mine)

This exhortation reveals a structural fact: the twenty-five wheels are **not** meant for the practitioner to cultivate comprehensively, but are to be selected for particular beings by a dice-method corresponding to their affinity. The action sequence is: three-seven-day upholding of pure conduct + repentance (self-power preparation) → placing a marker on each of the twenty-five wheels (structural selection) → sincere mind, and taking one by lot (a lot-taking under the Buddha's empowering response) → practicing according to what is taken.

The structural keystone of this dice-wheel method is: the self-power part (three-seven-day upholding of pure conduct, quieting contemplation, ardent repentance) and the other-power part (lot-taking under the Buddha's empowering response) co-operate within the same method. The capacity-difference among final-age sentient beings has become so great that the selection of practice must proceed through this self-power / other-power transition mechanism — self-power selection is no longer sufficient; Buddha-power intervention is required. This is the **prototype structure of the Pure Land other-power gate** in its early appearance inside the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*.

"A single thought of doubt or regret and the practice will not be accomplished" is an extremely stringent admonition: even after the wheel has been determined, the three-seven-day pure conduct undertaken, and the repentance completed — a single thought of doubt or regret during practice nullifies all prior effort. This difficulty is precisely what final-age buddha-recollection, as a direct expedient, answers to. The doubt-and-regret mind of final-age sentient beings is intrinsically hard to control; if the criterion for qualification is "not one thought of doubt within three-seven-days," the vast majority of final-age sentient beings are excluded from the twenty-five wheels. This difficulty-threshold itself is the seed of the Buddha's own opening of the alternate gate in the final line of the summary verse.

### 5.3 The Final Line of the Summary Verse — The Apex of the Buddha's Self-Limitation of the Wheels' Scope

The summary verse of the Bhadrasvara chapter is the passage of greatest structural significance in the whole chapter:[^30]

> "Bhadrasvara, you should know:
> all bodhisattvas'
> unobstructed pure wisdom
> is all born of *dhyāna*.
> These are *śamatha*,
> *samāpatti*, and *dhyāna* —
> three dharmas of sudden or gradual cultivation,
> comprising twenty-five kinds.
> The Tathāgatas of the ten directions,
> the practitioners of the three times —
> there is none who has not,
> by this dharma, attained *bodhi*,
> **excepting those of sudden awakening,
> and those whom the dharma does not accord with**.
> All bodhisattvas
> and sentient beings of the final age
> should constantly uphold these wheels,
> following along and diligently practicing;
> relying on the Buddha's great compassionate power,
> they will before long realize *nirvāṇa*."
> (T0842, Fascicle 1, *Bhadrasvara Chapter*, closing verse at p.918a; translation mine)

The structure of the twelve-line summary verse is a three-segment form of **affirmation — exception — exhortation**:

**Affirmation segment** (first eight lines): the Tathāgatas of the ten directions and practitioners of the three times, all without exception attain *bodhi* through this dharma. This is the highest affirmation of the efficacy of the twenty-five wheels.

**Exception segment** (the central two lines): "**Excepting those of sudden awakening, and those whom the dharma does not accord with.**" These two lines are, for the whole chapter — indeed for the whole sūtra — the most forceful structural warrant for final-age buddha-recollection. The Buddha actively limits the range of capacity-response of the twenty-five wheels: **those of sudden awakening** (capacities extremely sharp, needing no wheel, no gradual practice) and **those whom the dharma does not accord with** (those whose capacities do not accord with any wheel) — these two kinds of beings stand outside the twenty-five wheels.

**Exhortation segment** (the last four lines): they should constantly uphold these wheels, rely on the Buddha's great compassionate power, and before long realize *nirvāṇa*.

The final line "excepting those of sudden awakening, and those whom the dharma does not accord with" is the apex single-line of §V. It carries four structural significances:

**First**, this line is the Buddha's **self-limitation of the wheels' efficacy** — not a critique from an opponent, not a later doubt, but the Buddha himself, in the final line of the summary verse, explicitly stating that the twenty-five wheels do not universally cover all sentient beings. Such a self-limiting act is extremely rare in Chinese-Buddhist canonical literature — most sūtras, on the dharmas they expound, make the broadest possible affirmation, rarely actively leaving an "excepting" zone.

**Second**, the five characters "those whom the dharma does not accord with" (*bìng fǎ bù suíshùn*) are the **sūtric basis for the alternate gate of final-age buddha-recollection**. Final-age sentient beings do not universally belong to "those of sudden awakening"; more commonly, they belong to "those whom the dharma does not accord with" — those whose capacities accord with none of the twenty-five wheels. Such beings require an alternative expedient — not a "degenerate version" external to the twenty-five wheels, but a **separate expedient gate** the Buddha himself actively opens. Pure Land's dharma-gates of the final-age — the ten recollections of name-invocation for the lowest of low grades in the *Contemplation Sūtra*, the ten-recollection rebirth of Amitābha's eighteenth vow in the *Larger Sūtra*, the holding-the-name-single-minded-and-undistracted of the *Amitābha Sūtra* — are all seated within this "alternative expedient."

**Third**, the Chinese commentarial tradition has largely passed over this line lightly. The reason is not hard to surmise: an active self-limitation of wheel-efficacy does not serve sectarian-scholastic elaboration — if a given school wants to establish its own *chán* basis on the twenty-five wheels, the self-limitation "excepting those of sudden awakening and those whom the dharma does not accord with" weakens that school's claim of universal applicability. But a faithful reading must face this line head-on: the dignity of the twenty-five wheels lies not in their universality but in their exhaustiveness along the sudden-and-gradual structure; the legitimacy of final-age buddha-recollection lies not in its superiority over the twenty-five wheels but in its being the alternate gate that the twenty-five wheels themselves **actively leave open**.

**Fourth**, the concluding lines "relying on the Buddha's great compassionate power, they will before long realize *nirvāṇa*" foreshadow the other-power entrance. If the twenty-five wheels were purely a self-power permutation, this closing "relying on the Buddha's great compassionate power" would have no ground. This very line is the **foreshadowing of the Pure Land other-power gate**: the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* itself plants the other-power entrance in the final line of its summary verse.

### 5.4 Closing — The Apex Sūtric Warrant for Final-Age Buddha-Recollection, and the Functional Stance on Authenticity

With this, §V closes its structural argument: the apex-status of the twenty-five wheels is not counter-evidence against final-age buddha-recollection but positive evidence for it. The Buddha, in the final line of the summary verse, actively limits the efficacy of the wheels and opens a sūtric seat for the final-age alternate gate (including buddha-recollection). The line "excepting those of sudden awakening, and those whom the dharma does not accord with" is the warrant the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* itself supplies for the legitimacy of final-age buddha-recollection — final-age buddha-recollection is not a dharma-gate introduced into the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* from elsewhere but the alternate gate actively opened by the final line of the summary verse.[^31]

⚠️ **Functional stance on the textual authenticity question.** Modern philological scholarship largely maintains that the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* was composed in China in the late seventh to early eighth century, with translation attribution to Buddhatrāta as a pseudepigraphic device (Mochizuki Shinkō's *History of the Formation of Buddhist Canonical Texts*, 1946; Lü Cheng's "The Hundred Forgeries of the *Śūraṅgama*," 1943, includes the *Yuánjué* in its tabulation; Peter N. Gregory 1991 and 1993).[^32] The present paper does not enter this controversy, reading the sūtra within the *Chan-lin* tradition of Chinese Buddhism — a stance methodologically parallel to P12's handling of the *Śūraṅgama* authenticity debate: with commentarial internal evidence (Zongmi's "straight-stream" exegesis here; Jiaoguang's "faculty-gathering recollection" distinction there) as the analytic basis, and the sūtra's structural role in the Chinese-Buddhist buddha-recollection dharma-gate as the object of argument, the paper sets aside philological-and-Sanskrit-source debates as a methodological bracket. Readers wishing to pursue the authenticity question may consult the authors cited above.

## VI. Conclusion — From Time-Dependency to Precept-Dependency

### 6.1 Précis

The three final-age chapters of the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* — Maitreya, Universal-Awakening, and Perfect-Enlightenment — through the action-progression of "severing → leaving → binding," constitute the Chinese-Buddhist canonical tradition's complete response to the question of how to practice buddha-recollection in the final age: Maitreya's severing the root of cyclic craving is the inward origin; Universal-Awakening's leaving the four meditative diseases is the outward safeguarding; Perfect-Enlightenment's binding the three periods of days is the temporal structuring. The action-progression among the three chapters is not staged but simultaneous — three axes co-arising in every utterance of the name. Zongmi's *Subcommentary on the Abridged Commentary to the Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*, through the dual-layer structure of "thinking of the Buddha is also a samsaric mind" and "bowing to the names of the ten-direction Buddhas," establishes the phenomenal / noumenal verbal distinction of final-age buddha-recollection — phenomenal recollection is usable at final-age entry; noumenal recollection must dissolve the subject-object structure. This dual-layer distinction is structurally isomorphic with P12's Jiaoguang distinction between "faculty-gathering recollection" and "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness," forming the second node of Part V's cross-paper commentarial axis. The Twenty-Five Pure Cultivation Wheels of the Bhadrasvara chapter are the apex expression of *chán* meditation theory; the final line of their summary verse, "excepting those of sudden awakening, and those whom the dharma does not accord with," is the apex sūtric warrant — the Buddha himself self-limiting the wheels' efficacy and leaving a canonical position open for the final-age alternate gate (including buddha-recollection).

### 6.2 Practice Guidance

Three concrete directions for the final-age practitioner of buddha-recollection follow from this architecture.

**First, time-segment structuring is preferable to unstructured continuity.** The operation of final-age buddha-recollection should not follow the diffuse "lifelong-slow-whenever" mode; it should bring in a variant of the Perfect-Enlightenment chapter's three-period structure — a short diligent segment daily (not necessarily 120 days; it may be 21 days, 7 days, or even a single daily session), and within that segment sustain the two-beat rhythm of the three-seven-day pattern (a repentance beat first, a recollection beat after). This is not added burden but rhythm injected into action — the unstructuring tendency of final-age time-segments most easily dilutes practice, and time-segment structuring is the specific action that corrects this tendency.

**Second, the four-diseases framework is a self-check-list for daily buddha-recollection.** After daily name-invocation, the practitioner can check for the four misfires: has today fallen into fabrication-disease (subject-object buddha-seeking)? passivity-disease (scattered letting-go)? cessation-disease (buddha-recollection as *śamatha*-*vipaśyanā* tool)? annihilation-disease (recollecting toward the negation of recollection itself)? If any of the four is triggered, restore immediately to the verbal flow of "rise-and-awake." The check is not retrospective critique; it is internal micro-adjustment within the action.

**Third, "excepting those of sudden awakening" is the positive identity of the final-age practitioner of buddha-recollection.** The final-age practitioner of buddha-recollection need not self-identify as "a being of low capacity requiring special accommodation" — that very self-identification is already a samsaric view. On the contrary, the legitimate identity of the final-age practitioner of buddha-recollection is **"one whom the dharma does not accord with"** — outside the twenty-five wheels, the object for whom the Buddha has actively opened an alternative expedient. This identity is a positive positioning: not the degenerate version of the twenty-five wheels, but the proprietor of the alternate gate the Buddha himself has left.

### 6.3 Open Question — Leading into P15 on the *Brahma Net Sūtra*

The *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* handles the action-rhythm of final-age buddha-recollection through time-segment structure (the three-period day-limits). But besides time-segment, there is another mode of structuring — **precept** (*jièlǜ* 戒律). The *Brahma Net Sūtra* (T1484) takes the precept-body of the bodhisattva precepts as its axis and unfolds another verbal grammar: not the time-segment structure of "binding the three periods" but the boundary-structure of "upholding the precept-marks"; not structuring action through the cutting of time but structuring action through criteria of conduct. Once the final-age buddha-recollection practitioner has completed, in P14, the establishment of time-segment structure, they will, in P15, meet the establishment of the precept structure — the two together constitute the time-and-space twin-axis of final-age practice. P14 handles "when to do it"; P15 handles "what counts as valid doing." Together, the two axes equip final-age buddha-recollection with both rhythm and boundary.

P14's answer: the action-rhythm of final-age buddha-recollection is jointly defined by the "severing → leaving → binding" progression of the three chapters and the apex "excepting those of sudden awakening." P15's pending question: once time-segment and rhythm are set, from where does boundary come?
