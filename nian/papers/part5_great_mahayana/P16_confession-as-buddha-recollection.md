## Abstract
This paper takes up the fifth member of Part V's Mahāyāna-Eight-Sūtras cluster — the *Golden Light Sūtra* (*Jīn guāngmíng jīng* 金光明經) in its three Chinese recensions (T0663 Dharmakṣema; T0664 Baogui's combined recension with Paramārtha's supplementary translation; T0665 Yijing) — arguing three things. **First**, the Chapter of the Dream-Golden-Drum Repentance (*Mèng jiàn jīngǔ chànhuǐ pǐn* 夢見金鼓懺悔品), progressively extracted from a diffuse multi-thread narrative context in T0663 into a self-contained chapter in T0665, articulates a three-segment dynamic mechanism for confession: **(i) reception-in-dream** — Bodhisattva Ruciraketu (信相 in T0663 / 妙幢 in T0665) sees in a dream a brahmin striking a golden drum, the drum emitting a subtle voice that speaks confession verses; **(ii) waking recitation** — upon awakening, the bodhisattva recites before the Buddha what was heard; **(iii) collective ratification** — the buddhas of the ten directions collectively confirm the verses as their own, and karmic obstruction is thereby extinguished. Under this mechanism, confession is not a voluntarist act initiated by the practitioner's resolve but a **distributed-agency event-chain** whose agency is dispersed across seven nodes — golden drum, dream, brahmin, radiance, ten-direction buddhas, practitioner's recitation, and collective ratification — in which the practitioner occupies only the sixth node (the re-telling), never the first. **Second**, this mechanism was systematized in two successive commentarial phases: Zhiyi (*Zhìyǐ* 智顗, 538–597), in his *Profound Meaning of the Golden Light Sūtra* (T1783) and *Text-Lines of the Golden Light Sūtra* (T1785), reconfigured the dream-drum event-chain into the **Five Repentances** (*wǔ huǐ* 五悔: confession, requesting-the-teaching, sympathetic-joy, dedication, aspiration) — a repeatable liturgical sequence that temporalizes the single revelation into cyclically re-performable units; Jizang (*Jízàng* 吉藏, 549–623) in his *Commentary on the Golden Light Sūtra* (T1787) offered an alternative Sanlun reading (Five Repentances as counter-inversions of the Five Evil Roots; the three obstructions analyzed through cause, time, and place) that represents a **philosophical-deconstructive** branch not absorbed into the later Tiantai liturgy; Zhili (*Zhīlǐ* 知禮, 960–1028) in his *Record of the Text-Lines* (T1786) and *Record of Gleanings on the Profound Meaning* (T1784) carried Zhiyi's liturgy to a new philosophical depth, distinguishing **phenomenal repentance** (*shì chàn* 事懺 — bodily, vocal, time-structured ritual performance) from **noumenal repentance** (*lǐ chàn* 理懺 — the contemplation that confessor, confessed offense, and act of confession are all empty of inherent existence), and specifying that the two must run in parallel — phenomenal alone degenerates into ritual formalism; noumenal alone degenerates into empty talk. **Third**, in the distinctive geometry of Part V, the *Golden Light* occupies the **ritual-mechanism axis** complementary to P15's (the *Brahma Net*'s) **ontological-structure axis** — P15 processed Fazang's ontology of the precept-body as "the container of the primordial buddha"; P16 processes Zhiyi's and Zhili's ritualization of the confession event-chain as the repeating machinery by which that container is restored when damaged. Together P15 and P16 constitute the precept-and-repentance twin-axis at the center of Part V. §V's Middle-Way Corrections distinguishes the dream-drum mechanism from Augustinian confession on three structural grounds (double-subject vs distributed-agency; soteriological guilt vs conditioned obstruction; single God's absolution vs ten-direction buddhas' collective ratification), refuses the reduction of Golden Light to pure ritualism (phenomenal and noumenal must co-operate), and resists the misreading that the three Chinese recensions are three attempts at one and the same text (they correspond to three distinct Indic recensions attested by Nobel's 1937 Sanskrit edition and the three Derge Tibetan witnesses D556 / D557 / D558). §VI concludes with the forecast that the *Golden Light* solves the question **how to confess**, while the next paper (P17) on the *Laṅkāvatāra* will solve the deeper question that the *Golden Light* deliberately brackets — **what, ontologically, is the buddha who is being recollected?**

**Keywords:** *Golden Light Sūtra*, *Jīn guāngmíng jīng* 金光明經, *Suvarṇaprabhāsottamasūtra*, T0663, T0664, T0665, Dharmakṣema, Paramārtha, Yijing, recensions, dream-drum, *Mèng jiàn jīngǔ chànhuǐ pǐn* 夢見金鼓懺悔品, Ruciraketu, 信相菩薩, 妙幢菩薩, confession-as-buddha-recollection, distributed-agency event-chain, ten-direction buddhas' collective ratification, Five Repentances, *wǔ huǐ* 五悔, phenomenal repentance, noumenal repentance, *shì chàn* 事懺, *lǐ chàn* 理懺, Zhiyi, Guanding, Jizang, Zhili, *Xuányì* 玄義, *Wénjù* 文句, *Wénjù jì* 文句記, *Shíyí jì* 拾遺記, *Sanlun* 三論, Tiantai Golden Light Repentance Liturgy, Song-dynasty state repentance, karmic-obstruction extinction, *yè zhàng miè* 業障滅, *Mahāsupina Sutta*, AN 5.196, Augustinian confession, Middle-Way Corrections.

## I. Introduction — Where Does the Act of Confession Begin?

Why, in the Chinese-Buddhist world, does one and the same sūtra leave three Chinese translations behind? Why, from the Song dynasty onward, were Tiantai monastics willing to spend months — or indeed entire lifetimes — repeatedly performing a liturgy called the *Golden Light Repentance* (*Jīn guāngmíng chànfǎ* 金光明懺法)? Taking one further step outward: how does the "repentance" of the *Golden Light* differ from *confessio* in the Christian tradition, from the name-invocation of the Pure Land, from the "seeing of auspicious signs" (*jiàn hǎoxiàng* 見好相) of the *Brahma Net* bodhisattva precepts, and from the twenty-one-day binding of the *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment*?

On their face, these questions belong to the fine grain of Buddhist history, translation studies, and liturgiology. But together they point toward a more fundamental question: **at whose agency does the act of confession actually begin?**

For Western readers familiar with the monotheistic tradition, the initiating end of confession is unambiguous: the agent is the remorseful individual, the recipient is the one God (or His appointed representative), the medium is language (oral avowal of sin, the written *examination of conscience*), and the efficacy derives from divine pardon.[^1] The structure as a whole is double-subject, linear, and forensic.

The confession-architecture of the *Golden Light Sūtra* challenges this model at its root. In the Chapter of the Dream-Golden-Drum Repentance (*Mèng jiàn jīngǔ chànhuǐ pǐn* 夢見金鼓懺悔品), repentance is not initiated by the practitioner's resolve. The initiating end is a golden drum that appears out of thin air, requiring no one to actively seek it before manifesting itself; and this golden drum is then, within a bodhisattva's *dream*, struck by a brahmin; radiance disperses from it; the ten-direction buddhas manifest; and they, in unison, recite repentance verses into empty space. Where in this event-chain does the practitioner stand? He is neither the initiator nor the principal recipient — he is the receiver, the re-teller, the verifier. The verses he saw in his dream must be recited again after waking, before the Buddha, and confirmed by the Buddha; only then does the extinction of karmic obstruction take place.

This is not a double-subject (practitioner + deity) confession-structure. It is the receiving-structure of a **distributed-agency event-chain**. The core thesis of this paper is that the *Golden Light*, through the three-segment dynamic mechanism of "dreaming the golden drum," casts confession as a distinctive form of buddha-recollection — its initiating end is not located in the practitioner's decisional will, but in a chain of events distributed across the golden drum, the dream, the brahmin, the radiance, and the collective ratification of the ten-direction buddhas. Zhiyi and Zhili, having *seen* this mechanism, were the commentators who systematized it into the archetype of the Chinese repentance liturgy.

This paper does not set out to write a translation history of a single sūtra; nor does it take up a sectarian defence of Tiantai. What it sets out to do is threefold: comparison of the three Chinese recensions, structural analysis of the dream-drum mechanism, and the Zhiyi → Zhili → Song-dynasty social-historical liturgization trajectory as three successive strata. Taken together, these allow us to see what specific problem the *Golden Light* is solving within the overall map of buddha-recollection.

## II. Canonical Grounding: The Surface Configuration of the Three Recensions

The transmission of the *Golden Light Sūtra* into Chinese spans three hundred years and three distinct strata.

**T0663, Dharmakṣema's *Golden Light Sūtra* (Northern Liang 414–421 CE, 4 fascicles, 19 chapters).** This is the earliest Chinese layer, produced by Dharmakṣema (*Tánwúchèn* 曇無讖) — a native of Central India who established his translation workshop in Guzang. In T0663, the *Chapter of Repentance* (*Chànhuǐ pǐn* 懺悔品) places the confession-mechanism in an as-yet-undifferentiated unified state: dream, golden drum, verses of praise, repentance verses, the four heavenly kings' protection of the state, and Jalavāhana the Elder's son rescuing the fish are all gathered within a relatively short composite sūtra. The diagnostic feature of this layer is that **the dream-golden-drum repentance scene has not yet been separated out as a self-contained ritual specification**; it stands as one node within the sūtra's multi-thread narrative.[^2]

**T0664, *Combined Golden Light Sūtra* (Sui 597 CE, 8 fascicles, 24 chapters, Baogui's combined recension).** This is the second layer, not a fresh translation but the re-stitching, onto T0663's skeleton, of Paramārtha's (*Zhēndì* 真諦) supplementary translations of several chapters — including the *Chapter on the Extinction of Karmic Obstruction* (*Yèzhàng miè pǐn* 業障滅品), the *Chapter on the Dhāraṇī and the Purest Stages* (*Tuóluóní zuì-jìng dì pǐn* 陀羅尼最淨地品), and the *Chapter on Wishes Fulfilled Through Dependence on Emptiness* (*Yī kōng mǎn yuàn pǐn* 依空滿願品) — into a combined recension prepared by Baogui (*Bǎoguì* 寶貴). The decisive supplementation is the *Chapter on the Extinction of Karmic Obstruction*, which **procedurally formalizes** several passages that stood diffusely in T0663 — treating "karmic obstruction" (*yè zhàng* 業障) as a specialized theological category and establishing a more explicit correspondence between the mechanism of extinguishing karmic obstruction and the act of confession.[^3] The *Chapter on the Distinction of the Three Bodies* (*Sān shēn fēnbié pǐn* 三身分別品) is added in this layer as well, introducing the Dharmakāya / Saṃbhogakāya / Nirmāṇakāya trikāya framework — a structure missing from T0663 but of great importance in the later Chinese reception of the *Golden Light*.

**T0665, Yijing's *Sūtra of the Supreme King of Golden Light* (*Jīn guāngmíng zuì-shèng wáng jīng* 金光明最勝王經; Tang 703 CE, 10 fascicles, 31 chapters).** This is the third layer, a complete re-translation by Yijing (*Yìjìng* 義淨), who had spent more than twenty years at Nālandā, working from a late Sanskrit witness. The most conspicuous editorial act of T0665 is that the "dream-golden-drum repentance" is separated out as a **dedicated chapter** — the *Chapter of the Dream-Golden-Drum Repentance, Fourth* (*Mèng jiàn jīngǔ chànhuǐ pǐn dì sì*) — of roughly eleven hundred characters in length, containing the full event-chain "bodhisattva sees in dream → drum sounds of itself → brahmin strikes it → radiance disperses → buddhas manifest → repentance verses are uttered."[^4] This editorial separation into an independent chapter signals that, by Yijing's hands, the confession-mechanism had already been **extracted from** the sūtra's multi-thread narrative and **elevated** into a self-contained cultivation-text.

The three translation layers correspond to three distinct textual states, not to three different qualities of translation of a single original. Nobel's 1937 critical edition of the Sanskrit *Suvarṇaprabhāsottamasūtra*, together with the three Derge Tibetan recensions (D556 / D557 / D558), has established in Western Sanskrit-Tibetan philological scholarship that the three Chinese recensions correspond to **different source-texts**: Dharmakṣema depended on an earlier Sanskrit recension of his era; Paramārtha's supplementary layer depended on an intermediate recension; Yijing's text depended on a later, expanded recension.[^5] This point serves as the philological anchor of §III's three-layer evolutionary analysis; but this paper does not cite Sanskrit or Tibetan verbatim (the author does not read these languages), and takes only the comparison of the three Chinese layers as its direct object of analysis.

## III. Core Argument: The Dream-Drum Mechanism and the Distributed Subject

### 3.1 The Three-Layer Evolutionary Structure

In the three translations' handling of the dream-golden-drum repentance, a clear evolutionary axis emerges:

| Layer | Translation | Textual State of the Dream-Drum Scene | Degree of Differentiation of the Confession-Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Early | T0663 (Dharmakṣema, 414–421) | Not independent; juxtaposed with praise, four heavenly kings, *jātaka* | Undifferentiated unified mechanism |
| Middle | T0664 (Baogui's recension with Paramārtha, 597) | Follows T0663's skeleton; adds the *Chapter on the Extinction of Karmic Obstruction* | Procedurally formalized: "karmic obstruction" as an explicit category |
| Late | T0665 (Yijing, 703) | Separated into an independent *Chapter of the Dream-Golden-Drum Repentance, Fourth* | Dream becomes a self-contained chapter; the core of a free-standing ritual text |

This evolutionary axis does not reflect differences of translators' taste but **the text's own self-differentiation in history**. In Dharmakṣema's era, the dream-drum scene shared a single narrative field with the rescue-of-the-fish and the protection-of-the-state — the confession-mechanism was one thread among many. By the time of T0664, Paramārtha's source-text had already treated "karmic obstruction" as an independent theological category; the combined recension therefore had to add a dedicated chapter for the extinction of karmic obstruction. By the time of T0665, Yijing's late Sanskrit witness had already separated the dream-drum into a self-contained passage — Yijing's editorial decision merely renders this separation into Chinese faithfully.

For the map of buddha-recollection, the meaning of this evolutionary axis is this: **confession within the Chinese *Golden Light* system has gradually crystallized from a diffuse atmosphere of practice into a self-contained ritual mechanism**; and in the course of this crystallization, its initiating structure — the dream-drum itself — has been released into ever-clearer view.

### 3.2 The Shift of the Initiating End: The Distributed-Agency Event-Chain

This gradually crystallized initiating structure is the central thesis of this paper. Contrast two distinct confession-structures:

**Structure A (typical confession).** Practitioner's will-decision → examination of own faults → directed avowal toward some recipient (Buddha, God, priest) → recipient hears and ratifies / absolves → karmic obstruction extinguished. The initiating end is located in the practitioner; the entire chain is unilaterally driven by the practitioner.

**Structure B (the *Golden Light* dream-drum mechanism).** Golden drum (impersonal, self-luminous) → manifestation in a dream (dream rather than waking state, passive rather than active) → brahmin appears and strikes the drum (a third party intervenes, not the practitioner and not the buddhas) → radiance disperses from the drum (mechanistic energy-transition) → ten-direction buddhas manifest (collective rather than singular) → the buddhas in unison pronounce the repentance verses (actively uttered by the buddhas, not by the practitioner) → the practitioner, on waking, re-recites those verses from memory before the Buddha → the buddhas ratify → karmic obstruction extinguished.

The initiating end is not located in the practitioner. The chain contains at least seven nodes; the practitioner occupies the sixth node (the waking re-recitation), not the first. The first node is the golden drum — an impersonal, volition-less, identity-less ritual object. But this ritual object is simultaneously *contextual*: it appears within **a dream** (not the waking practitioner's chosen field of action), and it is **struck by a brahmin** (neither the practitioner himself nor the buddhas), a striking that summons **the collective manifestation of the buddhas** (not the practitioner's avowal to a single recipient).

This is what this paper calls a **distributed-agency event-chain**. The chain's agency — its active initiating energy — is not concentrated in any single subject but **dispersed** across seven nodes: golden drum, dream, brahmin, radiance, ten-direction buddhas, practitioner's recitation, buddhas' ratification. No single node is the complete initiator; no single node can be removed without breaking the whole chain.

This structure carries three profound implications. *First*, it dissolves the voluntarist presupposition of confession-as-active-avowal. The practitioner does not confess by force of decisional will, but by receiving a dream, a struck drum, and a set of verses actively uttered by the buddhas. Within the *Golden Light* system, confession is, in the first instance, a **given** gift, not a **generated** act. *Second*, it dissolves the double-subject structure of confession. There is no "I"-toward-"Thou" two-party avowal; in its place stands a multi-node event-network, in which each node is a necessary condition and none is the sufficient cause. *Third*, it recasts the relation of repentance to buddha-recollection — from a serial logic ("buddha-recollection after confession" or "confession as a sub-act of buddha-recollection") into something quite different: **confession is itself a distinctive form of buddha-recollection as its initiation event** — because the terminus of this chain is the collective manifestation and ratification of the buddhas, and the practitioner's awareness, remembrance, and re-recitation of the chain *is* his act of recollecting the buddhas.

### 3.3 The Three-Segment Dynamic Mechanism

Operationalizing the distributed-agency event-chain one step further, one can compress it into three segments:

**First segment: reception-in-dream.** The practitioner enters sleep; volition-control is *disengaged*. Golden drum, brahmin, radiance, buddhas, verses — all are received passively in the dream. The theological significance of this segment is that **the material of confession does not arise from self-reflection; it arrives from a heterogeneous revelatory event**.

**Second segment: waking re-telling.** The practitioner wakes, recalls what was seen and heard, and recites the verses before the Buddha. The key feature of this segment is that it is **memory-recitation**, not **invention-avowal**. The confession-content the practitioner speaks is not something he has compiled from his own faults; it is what he received in the dream, already actively uttered by the buddhas. This gives the entire speech-act a distinctive double character: it is simultaneously the practitioner's own action and a re-performance of verses that have already been given to him.

**Third segment: the buddhas' ratification extinguishes the karmic obstruction.** The buddhas, having heard the practitioner's re-telling, ratify. The efficacy of extinguishing karmic obstruction does not derive from the buddhas' *pardon* (the *Golden Light* does not employ the theological model of pardon); it derives from the **completion** of the event-chain — once the chain's final node closes (the ratification), the karmic obstruction as an occluding *conditioned formation* loses its sustaining condition.

This three-segment mechanism is at once structural (the three segments stand in definite causal relation) and dynamic (each segment is a flow of energy-and-information, not a static theological statement). Zhiyi, in the Tiantai Golden Light Repentance Liturgy, further operationalized it into the seven-day cycle of the Five Repentances; Zhili philosophically deepened it into the binary of phenomenal and noumenal repentance. Both are derivations from this three-segment base structure.

### 3.4 Initiating-End Differences from P14 and P15

Within the overall architecture of NIAN Part V, P14 (the *Perfect Enlightenment*'s binding-the-three-periods), P15 (the *Brahma Net*'s seeing-auspicious-signs), and P16 (the *Golden Light*'s dream-drum) all treat the practice-structure of "temporal binding + reception of the buddhas' ratification" — but their initiating ends differ at the root:

- **P14 (*Perfect Enlightenment*).** The initiating end lies in the practitioner's verb-decision ("to bind the three periods" — *I* decide to enter the bounded field of the three periods); the ratifying end lies in the practitioner's own awakened nature discerning the four diseases (the phenomenal–noumenal recollection distinction).[^6]
- **P15 (*Brahma Net*).** The initiating end lies in the receiver's self-vow ("I vow to receive the bodhisattva precepts"); the ratifying end lies in the "seeing of auspicious signs" as the Buddha's manifestation within the practitioner's own field of experience.[^7]
- **P16 (*Golden Light*).** The initiating end lies in the distributed-subject chain of golden-drum-dream-brahmin; the ratifying end lies in the collective manifestation and ratification of the ten-direction buddhas.

The form that buddha-recollection takes in each is accordingly different. P14's recollection is **remembrance-holding within a self-bound field**; P15's recollection is **the buddha's manifest appearance attained within the process of self-vowing**; P16's recollection is **a distributed event that is received, re-told, and collectively ratified**.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Interlocution: Tiantai's Liturgiology of the Golden Light Repentance

The historical process by which the *Golden Light*'s dream-drum mechanism was systematized first by Zhiyi and then by Zhili into the archetype of the Chinese repentance liturgy is the main axis of this section. This axis stands in a **ontology ↔ ritual** polar alignment with P15's §IV on Fazang's ontology — P15 treated the ontological status of the precept-body; P16 treats the ritual structure of repentance.

### 4.1 Zhiyi's Five Repentances: The Skeleton of Liturgization

Zhiyi (538–597), in his *Profound Meaning of the Golden Light Sūtra* (*Jīn guāngmíng jīng xuányì* 金光明經玄義) and *Text-Lines of the Golden Light Sūtra* (*Jīn guāngmíng jīng wénjù* 金光明經文句), abstracts the three-segment structure of the dream-drum mechanism into the **Five Repentances** (*wǔ huǐ* 五悔): confession (*chànhuǐ* 懺悔), requesting-the-teaching (*quànqǐng* 勸請), sympathetic-joy (*suíxǐ* 隨喜), dedication (*huíxiàng* 迴向), and aspiration (*fāyuàn* 發願).[^8] The five do not correspond to the three segments by one-to-one mapping; rather, they *re-configure* the dream-drum event-chain's several nodes into a repeatable ritual sequence:

- **Confession** corresponds to reception of the verses in the dream and their re-recitation before the Buddha after waking — it is the core act of confession.
- **Requesting-the-teaching** corresponds to the buddhas' manifestation — transformed now into the practitioner's active request that the buddhas dwell in the world and turn the dharma-wheel, elevating the passive reception of manifestation into an actively requested object.
- **Sympathetic-joy** corresponds to the dispersal of radiance — the practitioner rejoices in all merits of the buddhas and sentient beings, participating in the energy-field the radiance represents.
- **Dedication** corresponds to the completion of the event-chain — the practitioner does not retain merit for himself, but dedicates all benefit arising from the mechanism to the dharma-realm.
- **Aspiration** corresponds to the future continuation of the chain — ensuring that the mechanism does not come to rest in the present event but persists in re-performance.

As a liturgical skeleton, the Five Repentances in effect **temporalize** and **render repeatable** the *Golden Light*'s single event-chain. What in the sūtra was a one-time revelation, the Five Repentances convert into a cyclically operable ritual unit — able to be performed daily, every seven days, or every twenty-one days. This is the shared technical principle of the Chinese repentance liturgies: the one-time revelation of a canonical text is converted into a temporally recurring practice.

### 4.2 Jizang's Divergent Perspective

Jizang (549–623), in his one-fascicle *Commentary on the Golden Light Sūtra* (*Jīn guāngmíng jīng shū* 金光明經疏, T1787; Sanlun school), offers a different interpretive framework for the confession-structure: the **Five Repentances as counter-inversions of the Five Evil Roots; the three obstructions comprising cause, time, and place**.[^9] In contrast to Zhiyi's path of liturgization, Jizang processes confession through the Sanlun school's dialectic of emptiness — the Five Repentances are the counter-turning remedies of the Five Evil Roots, and the three obstructions (afflictive obstruction / karmic obstruction / retribution obstruction) are analyzed as a conditioned structure along three dimensions: cause, time, and place. Jizang's line of reading was not absorbed into the later Tiantai repentance liturgy, but it supplies an important *cross-school perspective*: the confession of the *Golden Light* need not necessarily move toward liturgization; it can equally move toward the dialectical deconstruction of emptiness. The two lines represent two distinct modes of reception — **ritualization** and **philosophical deconstruction**.

### 4.3 Zhili's Phenomenal and Noumenal Repentance

Zhili (960–1028), in his *Record of the Text-Lines of the Golden Light Sūtra* (*Jīn guāngmíng jīng wénjù jì* 金光明經文句記) and *Record of Gleanings on the Profound Meaning of the Golden Light Sūtra* (*Jīn guāngmíng jīng xuányì shíyí jì* 金光明經玄義拾遺記), carries Zhiyi's liturgy of the Five Repentances to a new philosophical depth. He distinguishes repentance explicitly into two parallel strata: **phenomenal repentance** (*shì chàn* 事懺) and **noumenal repentance** (*lǐ chàn* 理懺).[^10]

**Phenomenal repentance** refers to the prostrations, name-invocations, sūtra-recitations, image-contemplations, and the time-bound seeing of auspicious signs carried out according to definite liturgical procedure. Its core is **temporality** and **bodiliness**: within a fixed time-segment, the practitioner completes a temporally structured ritual event through the body's prostration, the voice's invocation, and the mind's contemplative image. Zhili is highly specific about the rules of phenomenal repentance — when, which liturgy, which chapters, which contemplation — each has a strict protocol.

**Noumenal repentance** points toward a wholly different dimension: the contemplation that the nature of offense is empty, that the one who confesses and what is confessed are both unobtainable. Noumenal repentance is not a *parallel* practice set up *outside* phenomenal repentance; rather, it is a contemplation operating **within** phenomenal repentance, simultaneously — contemplating that the offense one is confessing, the repentance-dharma one relies upon, and the very mind that confesses are all *empty of inherent existence*. The core of noumenal repentance is a **loosening at the ontological level**: when the practitioner becomes aware that every subject, verb, and object in the sentence "I am confessing this offense" is *dependently arisen*, the *solidity* of the offense itself begins to dissolve.

Zhili's critical insight is that phenomenal and noumenal repentance **are not alternatives; they run in parallel**. Phenomenal without noumenal easily degenerates into ritual formalism — the practitioner supposing that "how many times I have confessed" equates to "how much karma I have extinguished," which treats karma as an object tractable by accounting. Noumenal without phenomenal easily degenerates into empty talk — the practitioner declaring "since the nature of offense is originally empty, what need is there to confess?" — and falling into the error of nihilistic emptiness. Only with the two co-operating is the original intent of the *Golden Light*'s dream-drum mechanism observed: the event-chain really does happen (phenomenal), yet each node on that chain is simultaneously *empty of solidity* (noumenal).

This philosophical structure is also why the Tiantai Golden Light Repentance, from the Song dynasty onward, was able to be deployed at the level of the state (for prayers for rain, the aversion of calamity, the cessation of warfare, the protection of the realm) without degenerating into a purely state ritual — for each performance of the liturgy is simultaneously a contemplation of the emptiness of offense, karma, and merit.

### 4.4 The Dual-Track Contrast with the *Mahāsupina Sutta*

Set against the Buddha's own teachings on dreams in the Pāli tradition, the *Golden Light*'s dream-drum mechanism stands out as all the more distinctive. The *Mahāsupina Sutta* (AN 5.196) records the five great dreams the Buddha had before his awakening, each of which prefigures some dimension of his future teaching career.[^11] Within this dual-track contrast, the two traditions share the structure of "dream as revelatory event," but the dream's content, subject, and function are altogether different:

- In AN 5.196, the dream's subject is the Buddha himself; its content is a *typological prefiguration* of the future teaching; its function is to supply a semiotic confirmation of the Buddha's personal path of awakening.
- In T0665, the dream's subject is a bodhisattva (Ruciraketu: 信相 in T0663 / 妙幢 in T0665); its content is repentance verses and the manifestation of the buddhas; its function is to supply a distributed-agency initiation of the extinction of karmic obstruction *for all sentient beings*.

The contrast is not drawn to show which tradition is superior, but to see that **the dream as a Buddhist practice-mechanism admits of two distinct directions of use**. The early tradition uses the dream for the semiotic confirmation of the Buddha's personal awakening; the *Golden Light* uses the dream as a distributed initiation device for the collective confession of sentient beings. The two traditions share the modality of "dream," but differ fundamentally in agency, receiver, and purpose.

### 4.5 The Social-Historical Closure: The Song-Dynasty State Repentance Liturgy

By the Song dynasty, the Golden Light Repentance had developed a rich practice of state liturgy. Across the Tiansheng, Qingli, Xining, and Yuanfeng reigns, there are repeated records of the court requesting Tiantai monastics to perform the Golden Light Repentance in monasteries to pray for rain, avert calamity, halt warfare, and protect the realm.[^12] This social-historical dimension connects directly to the sūtra's *Chapter on the Four Heavenly Kings Protecting the State* (*Sì tiānwáng hù-guó pǐn* 四天王護國品) — the four heavenly kings, as sovereigns of the dharma-protectors, recur throughout the *Golden Light*'s narrative, and the state-level application of the Tiantai repentance liturgy is precisely the operationalization of the canonical narrative of "the four heavenly kings protecting the state" into a repeatable ritual intervention. This historical trajectory demonstrates that the dream-drum mechanism of the *Golden Light* is not merely an individual initiation device; it is equally a ritual infrastructure operable at collective levels — the state, the local community, the saṅgha.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### (a) ❌ *Golden Light* Repentance ≠ Augustinian *Confessio*

Western readers are easily tempted to read the *Golden Light*'s repentance as a Buddhist version of Augustinian confession. This is a structural misreading, untenable on at least three fronts.

**Structural non-isomorphism.** The Augustinian model of confession — taking the *Confessiones* as its archetype — is a double-subject structure, a direct dialogue between the penitent and the one God. The *Golden Light* is a distributed-agency event-chain — golden drum, dream, brahmin, radiance, collective of buddhas, practitioner's re-recitation, buddhas' ratification, seven nodes of which none may be removed. Reducing it to a double-subject structure effaces the mechanism's distinctive character.

**Differences in theological ontology.** The Augustinian system is a *soteriology* — the human being, by virtue of original sin, is in a state of essential guilt, and the one God, through pardon, rescues the human from that sin. The *Golden Light* system is a *karmic-obstruction account* — karma is a conditioned formation; it occludes awareness and obstructs the path, but it is not *metaphysical guilt*, and it does not require *metaphysical redemption*. The extinction of karmic obstruction is not pardon; it is the dissolution of conditions.

**Difference of the ratifying subject.** In Augustinian confession, ratification comes from the one God. In the *Golden Light*, ratification comes from **the collective of the ten-direction buddhas** — not a single metaphysical authority but the co-present consensus of the community of buddhas. This difference is not a matter of counting but of a *fundamental difference of agency*: in the *Golden Light*, **no single buddha can complete the ratification alone**; only the collective manifestation of the ten-direction buddhas closes the event.

### (b) ❌ *Golden Light* ≠ Pure Ritualism

Another common misreading is to take the Golden Light Repentance as *mere ritual* — a set of external observances having little to do with inner mental cultivation. This misreading overlooks the core design of Zhili's parallel operation of phenomenal and noumenal repentance.

Any particular performance of the Golden Light Repentance is simultaneously a phenomenal stratum (bodily prostration, vocal invocation, time-bounded seeing of auspicious signs) and a noumenal stratum (that the one who confesses and what is confessed are both unobtainable, that the nature of offense is originally empty) running in parallel. Phenomenal alone degenerates into formalism; noumenal alone degenerates into empty talk. Only when the two operate together is the original intent of the dream-drum mechanism observed. This design also means that the *Golden Light*'s repentance is not an ontologically independent operation — it already includes a self-reflexive contemplation of its own ontological status. Yet this contemplation remains at the ritual level; it does not cross over into the ontology of mind-consciousness proper (that is P17's *Laṅkāvatāra* territory).

### (c) ⚠️ The Three Chinese Recensions ≠ Three Attempts at One Text

A common misunderstanding is to read T0663, T0664, and T0665 as three attempts at Chinese translation of the *same* Sanskrit original — as if the translators had engaged in a contest of literary quality over a shared text. This framing is incorrect. Nobel's 1937 critical edition of the *Suvarṇaprabhāsottamasūtra*, together with the three Derge Tibetan recensions, jointly demonstrates that the three Chinese layers correspond not to a single source-text but to **three distinct recensions** — an early recension in Dharmakṣema's era, a middle recension in Paramārtha's era, and a later expanded recension in Yijing's era. The relation among the three layers is one of *textual evolution*, not of *translation fidelity*. Grasping this point prevents the misreading of the three-layer evolution as a mere variation in translators' taste.

## VI. Conclusion

### Précis

The *Golden Light*, through the three-segment dynamic mechanism of the dream-golden-drum (reception-in-dream → waking re-telling → buddhas' ratification extinguishing karmic obstruction), casts confession as the initiation device of a distributed-agency event-chain form of buddha-recollection. The chain's agency is dispersed across seven nodes — golden drum, dream, brahmin, radiance, ten-direction buddhas, practitioner's re-recitation, buddhas' ratification — and no single subject is the initiator. Zhiyi's Five Repentances ritualize this mechanism into a repeatable temporal structure; Zhili's phenomenal-and-noumenal binary philosophically deepens it into the ontological self-awareness of phenomenal and noumenal operating in parallel; the Song-dynasty Tiantai Golden Light Repentance finally operationalizes it as a ritual infrastructure at the state level.

### Practice Guidance

For anyone today undertaking the Golden Light Repentance or a similar confession-based practice, it is worth remembering that this tradition does not ask the practitioner to *generate* the content of repentance by force of will. Its design runs in the opposite direction — the practitioner's role is to *receive and re-tell*, not to *invent and avow*. This structural *dis-identification* with "confessing my own offense" is in fact the tradition's deepest therapeutic value. When the practitioner ceases to mount himself within the double-subject architecture of confession, the *solidity* of karmic obstruction as a conditioned formation stands a chance of loosening. Further, the parallel operation of phenomenal and noumenal is the tradition's technical safeguard against liturgy degenerating into either formalism or empty talk — neither may be dispensed with.

### Open Question

The *Golden Light* solves the question of *how to confess* — it supplies a distributed-agency initiation mechanism. But it does not address a more fundamental question: **what, ontologically, is the buddha who is being recollected?** What is the ontological relation between the collective of buddhas that manifests within the dream and the practitioner's own mind-consciousness? Is it a buddha outside the mind, or a reflection of the mind? A two-party recipient, or a unitary projection? This question is provisionally suspended in the *Golden Light* system — the sūtra's concern is with the mechanism's operation, not with the ontology of mind-consciousness.

The next paper, P17 on the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*, will take up this suspended question head-on. The *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "the minds of the buddhas are foremost," "to recollect the buddha is to recollect the mind," and the Bodhidharma "sealing of the mind" tradition together form the systematic response. **The *Golden Light* solves *how to confess*; the *Laṅkāvatāra* solves *what is being recollected*.** Read in concert, the two reveal the complete architecture of Chinese buddha-recollection at the levels of both ritual operation and ontological depth.
