## Abstract
This paper opens Part V, the Mahāyāna-Eight-Sūtras cluster, by taking up its first member: the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (*Dà Fódǐng Shǒuléngyán Jīng* 大佛頂首楞嚴經, T0945), Fascicle Five, the "Chapter on the Perfect Penetration of Buddha-Recollection by the Dharma-Prince Mahāsthāmaprāpta" (*Dàshìzhì niànfó yuántōng zhāng* 大勢至念佛圓通章) — "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted, attaining *samādhi* — this is foremost." It argues three things. **First**, T0945's "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted" (*dōushè liù-gēn, jìngniàn xiāngjì* 都攝六根、淨念相繼) is the dual-axis deepening of T0366's "holding the name, one mind undistracted" (*zhíchí míng-hào, yī-xīn bú-luàn* 執持名號、一心不亂) argued in P11 — the spatial axis extends from "the mind" to "the six sense-roots" (deepening from a single-organ or hierarchical model into a fully integrated model), and the temporal axis extends from "one-mind" non-distraction to "pure-recollection" continuation (deepening from a present-moment criterion into a diachronic continuity). **Second**, Mahāsthāmaprāpta's perfect-penetration chapter holds position 24 among the twenty-five chapters of perfect penetration (under the seven great elements, the "faculty-element" *gēn-dà* 根大), just one step below Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty (position 25, selected by Mañjuśrī as "the true teaching-body of this realm," *cǐfāng zhēn-jiāotǐ* 此方真教體); but late-Ming Jiaoguang Zhenjian's *Commentary on the Straight Vein of the Śūraṅgama* (*Léngyán Zhèngmài Shū* 楞嚴正脈疏, X0275) judges openly that the seven-element sequence in Fascicle Five "ends with the faculty-element and not with the consciousness-element" — that this is the sūtra's own structural affirmation of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate: "that the seven elements conventionally terminate in the consciousness-element, and yet here terminate in the faculty-element — is because Mahāsthāmaprāpta's perfect-penetration of buddha-recollection stands just one step below Avalokiteśvara's great root"; "for those who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind, nothing is better than buddha-recollection and seeking rebirth" (translation mine). Thereby, on the axis of "response-to-capacity in this realm, at this time," buddha-recollection in fact serves as the first alternate — establishing the multi-axis reading in which **sequential position is not qualitative rank** (*wèi-cì fēi děng-dì* 位次非等第). **Third**, the late-Ming trio of *Śūraṅgama* commentators stand in parallel — Lianchi's *Commentary and Notes* (X0424) weights the technique of "holding"; Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* (T1762) weights the provisions of faith, vow, and practice; and Jiaoguang originates a distinctive verbal distinction between "faculty-gathering recollection" (*shè-gēn niàn* 攝根念) and "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness" (*fēnbié shíxīn suǒ niàn* 分別識心所念). Three irreducible verdicts — on the structural partition of the verb *recollection*, on the *Śūraṅgama*'s positional hierarchy, and on the ontology of *recollection* itself — meet in Jiaoguang as the middle figure, whose "straight-vein" classification threads the perfect-penetration of buddha-recollection through the entire twenty-five-chapter structure. §V treats the T0945 authenticity debate under a **functional stance**: it does not enter the philological controversy between Lü Cheng, Ouyang Jingwu, Robert Sharf, James Benn and others, but grounds its reading on Jiaoguang's "straight-vein" internal evidence — the perfect-penetration of buddha-recollection is not externally added to the *Śūraṅgama*, but is the straight-vein manifestation of the faculty-element's perfect penetration from within.

**Keywords:** gathering in the six sense-roots, pure recollection uninterrupted, T0945 *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, Chapter on the Perfect Penetration of Buddha-Recollection by Mahāsthāmaprāpta, twenty-five perfect-penetration chapters, seven great elements, faculty-element, Jiaoguang Zhenjian, X0275 *Commentary on the Straight Vein of the Śūraṅgama*, straight-vein classification, faculty-gathering recollection, recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness, sequential position is not qualitative rank, the true teaching-body of this realm, Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty perfect penetration, Mañjuśrī's verse of selection, fragrance-and-light adornment, mother and child recollecting each other, dual-axis deepening, late-Ming trio of *Śūraṅgama* commentators, functional stance, apocryphal scripture, Lianchi's *Commentary and Notes*, Zhixu's *Essential Explanation*, verbal grammar of "holding."

## I. Introduction: When Mindfulness Shrinks to a Single Organ

The mindfulness (*mindfulness*) practice most familiar to Western English-language readers is structurally a **single-organ action** — anchoring on the "breath" (the body-root), or on "sound" (the ear-root), or on "thought" (the mind-consciousness): one entry is selected, while the other five sense-roots are left to drift freely. This is the standard design of the MBSR (Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction) and Vipassanā-secular traditions: one particular sensory faculty is made the gate, and sustained clarity at that gate is the target.

When this single-organ model is transplanted onto buddha-recollection practice, Western readers naturally read T0366's *Amitābha Sūtra* "holding the name, one mind undistracted" as the vocal-action variant of the single-organ model — anchoring on the tongue-root (or the voice within thought) and taking "one mind" as a single-metric indicator of mental state. P11 has already argued this reading to be mistaken.[^1] Lianchi's *Commentary and Notes on the Amitābha Sūtra* (X0424) seals off the mindless-recitation misreading through the philological gloss "*zhí* 執 (gripping with resolute courage, not to be shaken) + *chí* 持 (preserving in abiding fidelity, without forgetting)"; Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* (T1762), using the six faiths (faith-in-self / other / cause / effect / phenomenon / principle), proves that within a single utterance of the name the density of provisions is already saturated. "Holding the name" has never been the repetition of a single organ; it is a high-density mental action in which six mind-activities are simultaneously in play.

But when one enters Fascicle Five of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (T0945), into Mahāsthāmaprāpta's "Chapter on the Perfect Penetration of Buddha-Recollection," the structural level is raised one step further. The Dharma-Prince Mahāsthāmaprāpta does not use "holding" (*zhíchí*) to expound the ultimate of buddha-recollection *samādhi*; he uses "gathering-in-all" (*dōushè* 都攝):

> "When the Buddha asked about perfect penetration, I, making no special selection, gathered in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted, and attained *samādhi* — this is foremost." (translation mine)

The four characters *dōushè liù-gēn* 都攝六根 ("gathering in the six sense-roots") are the most fundamental dissolution of the single-organ model above. One no longer anchors on a single organ while letting the rest drift — instead the six sense-roots are simultaneously gathered in; none of the six is excepted from integration into the act of buddha-recollection. The next four characters, *jìngniàn xiāngjì* 淨念相繼 ("pure recollection uninterrupted"), further deepen the criterion from "one mind" (the present-moment indicator of mental state) into "pure recollection" (a continuity-quality requirement). A full integration along the spatial axis (expanding from single-root to six-root) and a continuous extension along the temporal axis (expanding from present-moment non-distraction to uninterrupted succession) — this is **dual-axis simultaneous deepening**.

This chapter is the first paper of NIAN Part V: the Mahāyāna-Eight-Sūtras cluster (*Śūraṅgama*, *Lotus*, *Perfect Enlightenment*, *Brahma Net*, *Golden Light*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Dense Adornment*, *Mañjuśrī Prajñā*). Part V is the centre of gravity of this volume — these eight sūtras have never been treated as a whole in the English-language world, and their divergent entry-points into buddha-recollection constitute this book's signature contribution. T0945, taking the stage first, has a specific structural task: to **carry buddha-recollection from its stand-alone channel in the Three Pure Land Sūtras up into the structurally-deepening stratum of the Mahāyāna sūtras.**

The chapter advances through a three-layered argument. **Layer one** (§3.1): the spatial axis — from T0366's single action of "holding the name" to T0945's whole-body-mind integration of "gathering in the six sense-roots"; Jiaoguang Zhenjian's *Commentary on the Straight Vein of the Śūraṅgama* (X0275) gives this deepening its specific operational grammar through the distinction between "faculty-gathering recollection" and "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness." **Layer two** (§3.2): the temporal axis — from the present-moment criterion of "one mind, undistracted" to the continuity-quality of "pure recollection uninterrupted"; Jiaoguang's joint reading of the phenomenal and principial — "pure recollection is, after all, still recollection *with* an object" alongside "succession as perfect illumination of the three bodies and four lands" — proves that this temporal axis is not a simple extension of duration but a deepening of the operational layer. **Layer three** (§3.3): the hierarchical axis — Mahāsthāmaprāpta's chapter holds position 24 of the twenty-five perfect-penetration chapters (under the seven great elements, the faculty-element), just one step below Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty (position 25, selected by Mañjuśrī as "the true teaching-body of this realm"); but Jiaoguang's "sequential position is not qualitative rank" reading — "for those who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind, nothing is better than buddha-recollection and seeking rebirth" — proves that sequential position and qualitative rank are two distinct axes, and that on the axis of "response-to-capacity in this realm, at this time," buddha-recollection is in fact the foremost alternate.

§IV then presents three irreducible readings from the late-Ming triad of *Śūraṅgama* commentators — Lianchi's *Commentary and Notes*, Jiaoguang's *Straight Vein*, and Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* — on the same core phrase "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted." Lianchi weights technique; Zhixu weights provision; Jiaoguang originates the verbal distinction "faculty-gathering recollection." §V offers middle-way corrections of three circulating misreadings, and marks the chapter's functional stance on the T0945 authenticity debate: non-engagement, with Jiaoguang's "straight-vein" internal evidence as the working basis. §VI closes the transition from Part IV's name-layer to Part V's structural-deepening stratum and briefly foreshadows the structural positions of the remaining seven sūtras (P13–P19).

The chapter serves a dual academic-and-practice aim. **Academically**, it gives a precise positional reading of T0945's Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter within the twenty-five-chapter perfect-penetration structure, sealing off the vulgar reading that "buddha-recollection is demoted by the *Śūraṅgama*." **For practice**, it points out that T0945's "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted" is not a higher-order practice harder than T0366's "holding the name," but the same verbal grammar of buddha-recollection unfolded along two axes, spatial and temporal — the practitioner may at any moment enter at a single point, and within the same action extend naturally into six-root integration and uninterrupted pure recollection.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 T0945 and the Mahāsthāmaprāpta Chapter

The *Great-Buddha-Crown Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (*Dà Fódǐng Rúlái Mìyīn Xiūzhèng Liǎoyì Zhū Púsà Wànxíng Shǒuléngyán Jīng* 大佛頂如來密因修證了義諸菩薩萬行首楞嚴經, T0945), in ten fascicles, was translated by Pāramiti and collaborators in the Shenlong era of Tang (705 CE).[^2] Fascicles Five and Six present the twenty-five perfect-penetration chapters, each one a bodhisattva or arhat declaring the dharma-gate by which they entered perfect penetration — "perfect penetration" (*yuántōng* 圓通) being the capacity, at any of the four layers (six sense-objects, six sense-roots, six consciousnesses, seven great elements), to penetrate that layer through to *samādhi*. Mahāsthāmaprāpta's "Perfect Penetration of Buddha-Recollection" stands at position 24 (the seven great elements, faculty-element) — the last of the seven great elements, just before Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty perfect penetration at position 25.[^3]

The chapter is not long, but its structure is complete. It opens with Mahāsthāmaprāpta's autobiographical causal stage:

> "The Dharma-Prince Mahāsthāmaprāpta, together with his fifty-two fellow bodhisattvas of the same order, rose from his seat, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, and addressed the Buddha: 'I recall that in ages past, as many as the sands of the Ganges river, a buddha appeared in the world named Unlimited-Light; for a whole kalpa, twelve Tathāgatas followed one upon another, and the last one of them was named Surpassing-Sun-and-Moon-Light. That buddha taught me the buddha-recollection *samādhi*.'" (translation mine)[^4]

The Buddha Surpassing-Sun-and-Moon-Light thus transmitted the buddha-recollection *samādhi* to Mahāsthāmaprāpta — this is the source-line of the dharma-gate presented in the chapter. Two metaphors then follow — the metaphor of mother and child recollecting each other, and the metaphor of fragrance-and-light adornment:

> "Suppose two persons: one is entirely absorbed in recollecting, the other is entirely in forgetfulness. Such two, whether they meet or do not meet, whether they see each other or do not see — it is as though they did not. But when the two recollect each other, and the two recollections run deep, then from life to life they are as body and shadow, never parting. All Tathāgatas of the ten directions regard sentient beings with pity, as a mother regards her child; but if the child flees, what can the mother's recollection accomplish? If the child recollects the mother as the mother recollects the child, then through life after life mother and child will not be far apart. If the sentient being's mind recollects the Buddha, is mindful of the Buddha, then in the present and in the future, they must see the Buddha — the distance from the Buddha is not far; without need of any other expedient, one's mind naturally opens. Just as a person perfumed with incense takes on the fragrance in his body — this is called the adornment of fragrance-and-light." (translation mine)[^5]

The mother-and-child metaphor unfolds in a three-layer structure: unilateral (one recollects, the other forgets) → bilateral (the two recollect each other deeply) → analogy (sentient beings recollecting the Buddha as a child recollects the mother). The phrase "whether they meet or do not meet" does crucial work: even if a buddha were personally to come to this land, personally to sit on this very mountain, none of it would avail for the sentient beings who do not recollect the Buddha. Only *after* the bilateral condition is satisfied can the declaration "must see the Buddha" stand. Whether one sees the Buddha does not depend on whether the Buddha is present, but on whether the sentient being is recollecting the Buddha. With this structure in place, buddha-recollection is converted from a request for rescue (unilateral seeking) into a relation of mutual recollection (bilateral structure) — the internal tension of the mother-and-child model being precisely that the mother's recollection alone cannot bring about the meeting: only the child's non-flight and the child's recollection of the mother constitute the structural condition of meeting.

The metaphor of fragrance-and-light adornment goes one layer further: "Just as a person perfumed with incense takes on the fragrance in his body" — one near the Buddha becomes the Buddha in body. Jiaoguang's *Straight Vein Commentary* glosses this: "In the figure, by proximity to fragrance the body also becomes fragrance; in the dharma, by proximity to the Buddha the mind also becomes Buddha" (translation mine).[^6] The practice-metaphors most familiar from Western secular mindfulness are "clearing" (*clear the mind*) or "emptying" (*empty out*); structurally these are *subtractive* — miscellaneous thoughts are taken away, and clarity remains. The fragrance-and-light adornment is *additive* — near the Buddha's dharma-body fragrance, one's body shares the Buddha's light; the practitioner is not emptied but perfumed. This structural distinction will be drawn out further in §IV's comparison of Lianchi, Jiaoguang, and Zhixu.

Immediately following, the closing two passages of the chapter form the argumentative fulcrum of the whole:

> "I, in my causal stage, by the mind of buddha-recollection, entered the tolerance of non-arising. And now, in this world, I gather in those who are mindful of the Buddha and return them to the Pure Land. When the Buddha asked about perfect penetration, I, making no special selection, gathered in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted, and attained *samādhi* — this is foremost." (translation mine)[^7]

"I, in my causal stage, by the mind of buddha-recollection, entered the tolerance of non-arising" (*rù wúshēng-rěn* 入無生忍) — this sentence situates the buddha-recollection *samādhi* as a causal-stage practice for entering the Mahāyāna ultimate fruit of the tolerance of non-arising (*anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti*), not a shallow or dispersed good. "And now, in this world, I gather in those who are mindful of the Buddha and return them to the Pure Land" — in this Sahā realm, Mahāsthāmaprāpta uses the power of the Buddha to embrace those who are mindful of the Buddha, returning them to Amitābha's Land of Bliss; this is one of the canonical scriptural bases for Mahāsthāmaprāpta's structural function as Amitābha's left-hand attendant. "Making no special selection, gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted, attaining *samādhi*": "making no special selection" means no single sense-root is picked out, no single gate is preferred; the six sense-roots are gathered in on equal footing. "Gathering in the six sense-roots" is the spatial-axis proposition of the chapter; "pure recollection uninterrupted" is the temporal-axis proposition; "attaining *samādhi*" is the fruit. "This is foremost" — in the buddha-recollection perfect-penetration that Mahāsthāmaprāpta has just expounded, the four items stand together as foremost.

The structure of the chapter — causal-stage source, two metaphors (mother-and-child, fragrance-and-light), fruit declaration, and core operational sentence — is already complete in itself. §2.2 treats the chapter's position within the twenty-five-chapter whole; §2.3 treats Jiaoguang's *Straight Vein Commentary* on the core operational sentence.

### 2.2 The Twenty-Five Perfect Penetrations and Mahāsthāmaprāpta's Position

T0945 Fascicles Five and Six present the twenty-five perfect penetrations in the following sequence: sense-object perfect penetrations (1–6: form, sound, scent, taste, touch, dharma-object), sense-root perfect penetrations (7–11: eye, nose, tongue, body, mind), consciousness perfect penetrations (12–17), seven-element perfect penetrations (18–24), and the ear-faculty perfect penetration (25). The sense-root perfect penetrations include only five, because the ear-faculty is extracted and placed last (position 25) as its own chapter — Mañjuśrī's selection-verse judges the ear-faculty explicitly as "the true teaching-body of this realm." The seven-element perfect penetrations include the full set — earth, water, fire, wind, space, consciousness, and faculty — with Mahāsthāmaprāpta's buddha-recollection perfect penetration at the terminus of the seven (position 24, the faculty-element).[^8]

Two structural features of this ordering merit flagging.

**First, the order of the seven elements differs from the usual sequence in sūtra and treatise literature**. In traditional Buddhist sūtras and treatises the seven elements are commonly ordered "earth / water / fire / wind / space / seeing (or faculty) / consciousness" — with the consciousness-element at the end. But T0945 Fascicle Five orders the seven as "earth / water / fire / wind / space / consciousness (Maitreya) / faculty (Mahāsthāmaprāpta)" — **ending with the faculty-element, not with the consciousness-element**. Jiaoguang's *Straight Vein* gives an unambiguous reading of this ordering:

> "That the seven elements terminate in the consciousness-element, and yet here terminate in the faculty-element — is because Mahāsthāmaprāpta's perfect-penetration of buddha-recollection stands just one step below Avalokiteśvara's great root. The *Amitābha Sūtra* says: the Land of Bliss's purity stands just below *nirvāṇa*. What Avalokiteśvara cultivates is the single pathway to the *nirvāṇa* of all the buddhas, the very ultimate fruit of *nirvāṇa*. For those here who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind, nothing is better than buddha-recollection and seeking rebirth. This is precisely the sūtra's deep intent in ending not with Maitreya, but with Mahāsthāmaprāpta." (translation mine)[^9]

Jiaoguang's reading is direct: the reason that T0945's seven-element sequence "anomalously" terminates with the faculty-element (Mahāsthāmaprāpta's buddha-recollection) rather than with the consciousness-element (Maitreya) is precisely to place buddha-recollection perfect penetration at the position immediately adjacent to Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty (25) — with Mahāsthāmaprāpta at 24, Avalokiteśvara at 25, forming the "just one step below Avalokiteśvara's great root" adjacency. This ordering — "not ending with Maitreya, but ending with Mahāsthāmaprāpta" — is a structural verdict embedded by the sūtra in itself: the perfect-penetration of buddha-recollection is not ornamental to the twenty-five chapters, but is the foremost alternate — immediately adjacent to the true teaching-body of this realm (the ear-faculty's reverse-hearing).

**Second, the two axes of "selection" and "non-selection" in Mañjuśrī's verse**. Fascicle Six: the Buddha asks Mañjuśrī, who, observing the Buddha's compassionate command, speaks in verse his selection:

> "Now I address the World-Honored One: The Buddha has emerged in the Sahā realm; the true teaching-body of this realm abides purely in sound-hearing. To reach *samādhi*, truly one must enter through hearing. To leave suffering and attain liberation — how excellent is Avalokiteśvara!" (translation mine)[^10]

Mañjuśrī selects Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty perfect penetration as "the true teaching-body of this realm" openly. For the other twenty-three perfect penetrations (including Mahāsthāmaprāpta's buddha-recollection), Mañjuśrī uses the formula "if one contemplates by [such-and-such] nature, … how could one attain perfect penetration?" — but **this "non-selection" is not a negation**. It is a selection relative to the sentient-capacities of this Sahā realm. Jiaoguang comments:

> "Speaking of the twenty-five gates generally, the six sense-objects are ordinarily reckoned beginning with form. But here they begin with sound, and the ear-faculty is left for last — precisely because, although all the gates are equally teaching-bodies, not every gate fits the capacity of this realm." (translation mine)[^11]

"All the gates are equally teaching-bodies" — all twenty-five perfect penetrations are teaching-bodies; "not every gate fits the capacity of this realm" — not all fit the sentient-capacities of Sahā. Mañjuśrī's "selection" is a selection according to *capacity*, not according to *essence*. "Selection" and "teaching-body" stand as two axes: on the axis of essence, the twenty-five are equal; on the axis of capacity, the ear-faculty is foremost for this realm. The reading "sequential position is not qualitative rank" is thereby grounded: *sequential position* is serial number (24 / 25); *qualitative rank* is the indexing to capacity (foremost for this realm / alternate / foremost for other realms …). These are distinct axes.

Mahāsthāmaprāpta's perfect-penetration of buddha-recollection can therefore be described along two axes. **Position**: at the end of the seven elements (24), immediately adjacent to Avalokiteśvara (25). **Rank**: on the axis of "sentient beings of this realm and this time who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind," the foremost alternate ("nothing is better than buddha-recollection and seeking rebirth"); on the axis of "other-power reception into the Pure Land," the foremost channel ("gather in those who are mindful of the Buddha and return them to the Pure Land"). Jiaoguang's classification does not demote buddha-recollection; it places buddha-recollection's position within a multi-axis coordinate system — on some axes (like this realm's pure self-power sudden cultivation) it yields place to the ear-faculty; on other axes (like response-to-capacity of Dharma-ending-age sentient beings, like reception into an other-realm Pure Land) it is foremost. This multi-axis reading is the third layer of §III's core argument.

### 2.3 Jiaoguang's *Commentary on the Straight Vein of the Śūraṅgama* — Faculty-Gathering Recollection, Pure Recollection, the Straight-Vein Classification

Jiaoguang Zhenjian (交光真鑒, ca. 1547–1626), a monk from the West Lake in the late Ming capital district, is the representative *Śūraṅgama* commentator of the late Ming, contemporary with Lianchi Zhuhong (1535–1615) and Ouyi Zhixu (1599–1655).[^12] His *Commentary on the Straight Vein of the Great-Buddha-Crown Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (X0275, *Manji zokuzōkyō* vols. 18–19) was completed between Wanli 22 and 24 (1594–1596).[^13] The name "straight vein," Jiaoguang explains in Fascicle One:

> "Truly this sūtra fills tens of thousands of characters. Though its text is long and broad, its sacred words are doubly marvelous in diction and meaning; head and tail answer to each other, the veins are threaded through — nowhere is there a passage that fails to answer, or fails to run through. Older commentaries often took each passage at its face and explained it there, without looking fore and aft; sometimes missing the root intent, they forcibly rationalized and accordingly found before and after failing to echo, with the language-veins running against each other. This present commentary does not dare to pierce between the lines with private inference; it only submissively follows the sacred sūtra's own native language-veins and draws them through, only to ensure that fore and aft do answer each other and the language-veins run through. For this it is named the straight vein." (translation mine)[^14]

"Straight vein" is thus the sūtra's own native threading of veins — not a commentarial framework imposed from without, but a through-reading that restores the text's own internal language-flow. Jiaoguang's own disclaimer — "not daring to pierce between the lines with private inference, only submissively following the sacred sūtra's own native language-veins" — grounds his classification not in any sectarian presupposition, but in the head-and-tail answering of the text itself. The application of this "straight-vein" stance to the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter is precisely the deep intent described in §2.2, "ending not in the consciousness-element but in the faculty-element" — not a foreign insertion by Jiaoguang, but a design self-displayed by the sūtra's own structure.

Jiaoguang gives a three-layered exegesis of the chapter's core operational sentence "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted" — *gathering-in-all*, *pure recollection*, and *faculty-gathering recollection*.

**First, gathering in the six sense-roots**:

> "As for 'gathering in the six sense-roots' — it is to cause the six sense-roots not to stir. … To gather in each sense-root severally is to belong to a six-root perfect penetration; to gather in all six sense-roots is to belong to the faculty-element perfect penetration. The old commentaries take this as the mind-consciousness [root], which conflates it with Subhūti's practice." (translation mine)[^15]

Jiaoguang glosses "gathering" (*shè* 攝) as "causing the six sense-roots not to stir" — not the static closure of the six sense-roots, but a dynamic gathering and integration, a calm stillness of the six sense-roots such that they do not arise in deluded motion. "All" (*dōu* 都), as "comprehensive," is distinguished from "gathering in each sense-root severally" of the six-root perfect penetrations (the preceding six-sense-object, five-sense-root, and the 25th, Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty, all of which enter through one particular sense-root). Gathering in all six sense-roots is the simultaneous integration of all six within a single recollection. Hence "gathering in the six sense-roots" does not belong to the "six-root" category among the twenty-five perfect penetrations; it belongs to the seven-element faculty-element — for "faculty-element" (*gēn-dà* 根大) means *the six sense-roots taken as one whole*.

Jiaoguang specifically rebuts the old reading that would gloss "gathering in all" as "gathering in the mind-root" — for if "gathering in all" simply means gathering in the mind-consciousness-root (with the mind leading the other five), then this practice would be redundant with the mind-root perfect penetration, and the positional ordering would collapse. "Gathering in all" must be the simultaneous integration of the six sense-roots, for only then can it settle in its independent position among the seven elements, under the faculty-element. The gloss is precise: it lifts "gathering in all" from the hierarchical model ("one root commands, the other five assist") into the fully-integrated model ("the six sense-roots integrate on equal footing"). This distinction directly answers the Western secular-mindfulness single-organ model described in §I — the single-organ model is substantively either "the mind leading" or "one particular sense-root anchoring," that is, a hierarchical model, structurally different from the full integration of "gathering in all."

**Second, pure recollection uninterrupted**:

> "'Pure recollection uninterrupted' means the perfect illumination of the three bodies and four lands." (translation mine)[^16]

Jiaoguang glosses "pure recollection" (*jìngniàn* 淨念) not merely as "recollection that is purified" (as opposed to defiled recollection), but as "perfect illumination of the three bodies and four lands" — the three bodies being *dharmakāya*, *saṃbhogakāya*, and *nirmāṇakāya*, and the four lands being the Land of Eternally Quiescent Light, the Land of True Reward Adornment, the Land of Expedient Remainder, and the Land of Common Dwelling of Common and Holy. The content of pure recollection is: within a single recollection, simultaneously illuminating the essence of the Buddha's three bodies, and simultaneously contemplating the four lands' spheres. "Uninterrupted" (*xiāngjì* 相繼) is the temporal-axis continuity — this illumination is not one-time-and-done, but succeeds itself without interruption.

But Jiaoguang further acknowledges, honestly, a specific tension: "'Pure' is spoken in contrast to 'defiled'; thus pure recollection, after all, is still recollection *with* an object" (translation mine).[^17] "Pure recollection" stands in a dualistic framework over against "defiled recollection"; within this framework, pure recollection is still "*with*-an-object-recollection" (relative to object-less recollection), not the ultimate object-less state. Jiaoguang acknowledges this as the phenomenal character of *recollection*-on-the-phenomenal-track — phenomenal recollection of the Buddha cannot entirely shed the phenomenal mark of "recollection." This acknowledgment avoids the confusion "pure recollection = object-less recollection" and preserves the phenomenal integrity of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate. "Pure recollection uninterrupted," in Jiaoguang's reading, is a succession of the perfect illumination of the three bodies and four lands achieved *within* the phenomenal character of with-an-object recollection — not a forcible cancellation of the phenomenal form of "recollection" to achieve some pure object-less state, but a completion of principial illumination *within* phenomenal recollection.

**Third, faculty-gathering recollection vs. recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness**:

When glossing the passage "I, in my causal stage, by the mind of buddha-recollection, entered the tolerance of non-arising," Jiaoguang offers the most pivotal verbal distinction of the entire chapter:

> "The first three lines describe self-benefit. His narrating his own buddha-recollection here is not the use of the discriminative mind-consciousness's recollection; it is the faculty-gathering recollection. The dharma of it: with a single thought unarisen and the six sense-roots calmly still, one may yet perfectly illumine the three bodies and penetrate the four lands, and so reach the fulfillment of faith and the entry into abiding, realizing the tolerance of non-arising. The next three lines describe benefiting others. 'Gathering' — by majestic power and blessing, causing them not to retreat from recollection. And, at the moment of dying, coming to receive them, causing them surely to attain rebirth." (translation mine)[^18]

"Recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness" (*fēnbié shíxīn suǒ niàn* 分別識心所念) — recollection whose subject is the sixth consciousness's discriminative activity; its structure is the discriminating-subject-consciousness (the "mind that recollects") performing a conceptual analysis upon the object ("the name of the Buddha"). "Faculty-gathering recollection" (*shè-gēn niàn* 攝根念) — the six sense-roots calmly still and not stirring in deluded motion, the three bodies and four lands simultaneously perfectly illumined: the operation of six sense-roots integrated within a single buddha-recollection mind. The difference is this: the former is the discriminative action of consciousness, the latter the integrative action of the faculty; the former is a cogitation *on* recollection as an object, the latter is a merger with the Buddha *as* essence.

This distinction is Jiaoguang's most distinctive contribution among late-Ming buddha-recollection commentators. Lianchi's *Commentary and Notes on the Amitābha Sūtra*, with the "*zhí* (gripping decisively) + *chí* (preserving in fidelity)" double-action gloss on "holding," partitions the verb *recollection* structurally within itself (recollection's catching-hold-face + recollection's preserving-face); Zhixu's *Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra*, with the six faiths (faith-in-self / other / cause / effect / phenomenon / principle), fills in the motivational cognitive premises of *recollection* at multiple layers. **Jiaoguang, in contrast, makes a root-level distinction *in the type of operation* of "recollection"** — even though both are buddha-recollection, "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness" and "faculty-gathering recollection" are two different *kinds* of action, not two *aspects* of a single action.

This distinction is the direct skeleton of the spatial-axis argument of §3.1: T0366's "holding the name" can be either "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness" (a single-organ vocal action) or "faculty-gathering recollection" (a six-root integrated body-mind action) — T0945's "gathering in all six sense-roots" points explicitly to the latter. The verbal deepening from T0366 to T0945 is not an increase in difficulty, but a clarification of the type of operation.

## III. Core Argument

The three core arguments of this chapter are developed in three layers: the spatial-axis deepening (§3.1), the temporal-axis deepening (§3.2), and the multi-axis reading of position and rank (§3.3). The first two layers continue the verbal grammar argued for T0366 in P11, showing T0945 to be its dual-axis deepening; the third layer treats the structural problem peculiar to T0945 — the positional reading of Mahāsthāmaprāpta within the twenty-five perfect penetrations.

### 3.1 Spatial Axis: From a Single Action to Six-Root Integration

P11 has already argued that T0366's "holding the name" is not mechanical repetition, but the double mind-action of Lianchi's gloss — "*zhí* (decisively catching-hold) + *chí* (steadfastly preserving)": catching-hold-of the buddha-name decisively, preserving-the-action with steadfast fidelity. But the spatial location of this double mind-action is not specified — on *which* sense-root is "holding" executed?

The practitioner's most direct answer is: the tongue-root (vocal recitation) + the ear-root (hearing one's own voice) + the mind-root (mental thought) — with the vocal action as trunk, hearing and thinking as auxiliaries. This is the most common body-mind configuration of buddha-recollection, and it is also the most natural reading of T0366's "hearing the discourse on Amitābha Buddha, holding the name." But this configuration is still a **single-organ-leading-with-auxiliary-organs model** — the tongue-root leads, the mind-root assists, the ear-root loops back — while the remaining three sense-roots (eye, nose, body) have no structural position and are left to drift or remain neutral.

T0945's "gathering in the six sense-roots" is a root-level rewriting of this model. Jiaoguang's gloss — "*gathering in all the six sense-roots* means causing them not to stir" — means: the six sense-roots on equal footing calmly still, integrated on equal footing, with no leading-assisting grading. In concrete operation: the eye-root's seeing, the ear-root's hearing, the nose-root's smelling, the tongue-root's tasting, the body-root's touching, the mind-root's thinking — all six are simultaneously "gathered in" into the act of buddha-recollection. Not "the other roots are suppressed; only the mind-root functions"; but "all six are present, all calmly still, all integrated within a single recollection."

The significance of this deepening may be seen from two directions.

**Direction one: from within the buddha-recollection dharma-gate**. P11 has argued that the criterion of T0366's "one mind undistracted" has been flattened in Western psychologizing readings into the cognitive indicator of "attention-concentration." But if "holding" operates only on a single sense-root (tongue-root dominant), then "one mind" naturally equates to "attention does not wander" — a state of mind fully describable in psychological language. T0945's "gathering in all the six sense-roots" externalizes the criterion into body-mind integration — not some interior state of the mind-root, but the simultaneous calm stillness of the six sense-roots as a whole. This integration cannot be exhausted by the "selective attention" language of cognitive psychology — it involves a thorough stilling of body-sense (the nose-root, body-root, and eye-root naturally at rest), the simultaneous integration of sensory activity with conceptual activity, and the temporary dissolution of the body-mind boundary of the "I" within the action of buddha-recollection.

**Direction two: from the comparison with Western secular mindfulness**. The MBSR and Vipassanā-secular traditions anchor on some particular sensory faculty; their structure is the "single-anchor-plus-drifting" single-organ model. Mahāsthāmaprāpta's "gathering in the six sense-roots" is structurally "six-anchors-in-synchrony-without-drift," a fully integrated model. The former can be fully described by the cognitive psychology of "selective attention"; the latter requires the language of "embodied cognition," even of a "phenomenological whole," to begin to be approached. The two models are not differences of difficulty, but differences of structure — the single-organ model can be trained to greater skill and stability but will not spontaneously become the full-integration model; the full-integration model must be designed from the outset with a different verb. Jiaoguang's distinction between "faculty-gathering recollection" and "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness" is precisely the classical formulation of this structural difference.

The relation between T0366's "holding" and T0945's "gathering in all" is therefore not one of difficulty, but one of two operational types. A practitioner may enter from T0366 at a single point; but to linger only in the tongue-root-dominant model is to mistake "holding the name" for the totality of buddha-recollection. T0945 externalizes the latent full-integration structure — from single point outward to six-roots, from holding on inward to gathering in all, from some one possible depth of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate up into the full spatial unfolding of the dharma-gate as a whole.

### 3.2 Temporal Axis: From a Present-Moment State to a Continuity of Quality

The parallel to the spatial axis is the temporal axis. The time-structure of T0366's "one mind undistracted" criterion is **present-ness** — the mental state "undistracted" reached at some point in time. The time-structure of T0945's "pure recollection uninterrupted" criterion is **continuity** — the uninterrupted succession of pure recollections one after another.

The difference is conspicuous at the level of cultivational experience. "One mind undistracted" can be achieved within a single sitting and then dissipate immediately upon leaving — one-pointed on the cushion, dispersed off the cushion — and such an achievement still counts as meeting the criterion "one day, up to seven days, one mind undistracted." The philological reading of T0366 allows this intermittent achievement: the time-frame is fixed as "one day to seven days," and within that span there may be intervals, rests, and scattered minds — provided that within the recollection-period itself "undistracted" is achieved, the criterion is met. The advantage of this design is a reachable threshold, suited to beginners; the disadvantage is that "one mind" as a present-moment state offers no cross-period continuity guarantee.

T0945's "pure recollection uninterrupted" rewrites the criterion from "present-moment achievement within a time-span" to "the uninterrupted succession of recollection upon recollection" — the previous recollection is pure, the next recollection is also pure, and between the two there are no defiled recollections interspersed, no scattered-mind gaps. Jiaoguang glosses "uninterrupted" as the continuity of the "perfect illumination of the three bodies and four lands" — this illumination is not a momentary sphere within a single sitting, but the continuity-quality within the practitioner's ordinary daily life. The time-structure of pure recollection uninterrupted is therefore **diachronic** — not an achievement within some single time-span, but the continuity from time-span to time-span.

Yet this deepening carries a danger — if "pure recollection uninterrupted" is read as the absolute demand of "never ever interrupted," then the practitioner, meeting even a single intrusion of defiled recollection, would rule himself a failure. Jiaoguang is specifically honest here: "'Pure' is spoken in contrast to 'defiled'; thus pure recollection, after all, is still recollection *with* an object" — pure recollection is itself still "with-an-object," still belonging to the phenomenal level in comparison with ultimate object-less recollection (the inner realization of the tolerance of non-arising). This acknowledgment means: pure recollection uninterrupted does not demand that the practitioner attain some absolute object-less pure sphere; it demands, *within* the phenomenal level of "recollection," the preservation of succession *among* pure recollections. Specifically: if the present recollection is pure and the next arises defiled, one immediately on the very next recollection turns back to the pure recollection — this turning-back is not a fresh start, but the self-repair of the continuous structure.

The temporal-axis deepening is therefore not an absolute increase in difficulty (demanding an uninterrupted "never-break"), but a structural rewriting of the criterion (from "achievement within a time-span" to "recollection-to-recollection succession"). The concrete implication for the practitioner: not "I must achieve an ever-undistracted sphere before I count as meeting the criterion," but "at every single recollection I may choose to maintain pure recollection, or immediately perform the repair-turn from defiled recollection to pure recollection" — a displacement of the criterion from the ultimate-result position to the micro-operation of every recollection.

### 3.3 The Hierarchical Axis: Sequential Position Is Not Qualitative Rank

The third layer of argument addresses the structural problem peculiar to T0945 — Mahāsthāmaprāpta's position within the twenty-five perfect penetrations. The circulating readings swing between two extremes — "the *Śūraṅgama* selects Avalokiteśvara, so buddha-recollection is demoted" (the demotion reading), or "because the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter is contained in the *Śūraṅgama*, it is already of equal status with Avalokiteśvara" (the parity reading). Both readings take "sequential position" and "qualitative rank" to be one and the same.

**Sequential position** is the serial number — Mahāsthāmaprāpta at 24, Avalokiteśvara at 25. **Qualitative rank** is the ranking of suitability-to-capacity — under what kind of sentient being, at what kind of time, for what kind of purpose, is this the foremost choice? These are not the same axis.

"For those who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind, nothing is better than buddha-recollection and seeking rebirth" — Jiaoguang's verdict here gives a multi-axis ranking structure:

- **Axis One: this realm, this time, self-power sudden cultivation** — Foremost: Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty (reverse-hearing of one's own nature, the single pathway to *nirvāṇa*); alternate: Mahāsthāmaprāpta's buddha-recollection (just one step below Avalokiteśvara's great root).
- **Axis Two: this realm, this time, other-power reception** — Foremost: Mahāsthāmaprāpta's buddha-recollection (gathering in those who are mindful of the Buddha and returning them to the Pure Land); Avalokiteśvara is auxiliary on this axis.
- **Axis Three: response-to-capacity for "those who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind"** — Foremost: Mahāsthāmaprāpta's buddha-recollection ("nothing better than"); the other twenty-three perfect penetrations are not in response to this axis.
- **Axis Four: by essence** — the twenty-five perfect penetrations are equal, all are teaching-bodies, no superior or inferior.

What Mañjuśrī's selection-verse selects is Axis One — "the true teaching-body of this realm abides purely in sound-hearing" — the Sahā sentient beings' ear-roots being sharper and the sound-object more familiar, on the axis of self-power sudden cultivation, the reverse-hearing of the ear-faculty is foremost. This selection does not negate the foremost on other axes — Axes Two, Three, and Four each have their own foremost; Mahāsthāmaprāpta's buddha-recollection is foremost on Axes Two and Three, and on Axis Four it stands in parity with Avalokiteśvara, Maitreya, Dhāraṇīṃdhara, and the remainder of the twenty-five as a teaching-body.

The structural reading that sequential position is not qualitative rank therefore dissolves both extreme readings. "The *Śūraṅgama* selects Avalokiteśvara, so buddha-recollection is demoted" conflates Axis One with the totality of the axes — using the ranking of Axis One to negate the independent ranking on the other axes. "Because the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter is contained in the *Śūraṅgama*, it is of equal status with Avalokiteśvara" conflates Axis Four with the totality of the axes — using the essence-parity of Axis Four to displace the response-to-capacity differentiations of the other axes. Both fail to see the multi-axis structure of the twenty-five perfect penetrations.

This multi-axis structure also explains the anomaly of T0945 Fascicle Five's seven-element sequence "ending with the faculty-element, not with the consciousness-element" — Jiaoguang judges this openly as the sūtra's structural internal affirmation of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate along the "response-to-capacity in this realm, at this time" axis. It is not a post-hoc external elevation; it is the structural foremost of buddha-recollection on a specific axis, within the multi-axis coordinate system that the sūtra itself displays.

The three-layered argument closes: the spatial axis (deepening from single action to six-root integration), the temporal axis (deepening from present-moment achievement to recollection-upon-recollection), and the hierarchical axis (deepening from single ranking to multi-axis ranking). All three are *deepenings* — not increases in difficulty, not difficulty-comparisons. T0945's Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter is the first structural deepening of T0366's name-layer channel within the Mahāyāna sūtra corpus.

## IV. Lianchi ↔ Jiaoguang ↔ Zhixu: Three Readings from the Late-Ming *Śūraṅgama* Commentators

§IV of P11 already presented the two parallel late-Ming Pure Land commentarial perspectives of Lianchi's *Commentary and Notes* (X0424, 1595) and Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* (T1762, 1647) on T0366. Entering T0945, all three late-Ming figures simultaneously bear a complete commentarial tradition on the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter: Lianchi's position is primarily seen in X0424's gloss of "holding" and its extensions, and in the scattered *Śūraṅgama*-related pieces of his *Yunqi Collected Dharma* (*Yúnqī Fǎhuì* 雲棲法彙); Jiaoguang Zhenjian's *Straight Vein Commentary* (X0275, 1594–1596) is the most comprehensive single work of late-Ming *Śūraṅgama* exegesis; Zhixu authored *Lines and Verses of the Śūraṅgama* (*Léngyán Jīng Wénjù* 楞嚴經文句) and his *Profound Meaning* (*Xuányì* 玄義) in 1651.[^19] The three commentators give three irreducible readings of the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter's core operational sentence "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted."

### 4.1 Three Ways of Partitioning the Verb *Recollection*

**Lianchi** (taking X0424's gloss of "holding" as his core position): partitions *within the verb "recollection" itself* as **action-structure partition** — *zhí* (resolute courage, decisively catching-hold) + *chí* (abiding fidelity, preserving without forgetting).[^20] Lianchi reads "gathering in all the six sense-roots" as an extension of *zhí* — from the *zhí* of one root to the *zhí* of six roots; and reads "pure recollection uninterrupted" as an extension of *chí* — from the *chí* of one recollection to the *chí* of recollection upon recollection. The core of his reading is: T0945's core sentence is the spatial extension and temporal prolongation of T0366's "*zhí*-*chí*" double action, and is essentially the deepening of the same technique of holding.

**Jiaoguang** (X0275): partitions *the kind of operation* of recollection as **root-level distinction** — recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness vs. faculty-gathering recollection.[^21] The former is the discriminative action of the sixth consciousness; the latter is the integrative action of the six sense-roots calmly still. Jiaoguang reads "gathering in all the six sense-roots" as the explicit externalization of the operational layer of faculty-gathering recollection — not a new action, but the operational type that buddha-recollection already contains, clearly flagged in the *Śūraṅgama*; and reads "pure recollection uninterrupted" as the continuity-quality of faculty-gathering recollection — the succession of the perfect illumination of the three bodies and four lands, not a present-moment achievement. The core of his reading is: T0945's core sentence is the clarification of the *type* of operation of "recollection" — though both are buddha-recollection, discriminative-consciousness recollection and faculty-gathering recollection are two different kinds of action, and T0945 points explicitly to the latter.

**Zhixu** (T1762 *Essential Explanation* + *Lines and Verses of the Śūraṅgama*): partitions *the cognitive premises* of recollection as **multi-layered analysis** — six faiths (faith-in-self / other / cause / effect / phenomenon / principle) + the three provisions of faith, vow, and practice, "not sequenced and not rigidly three."[^22] Zhixu reads "gathering in all the six sense-roots" as the appearance of perfect fulfillment of the practice-provision — when faith, vow, and practice are together present in a single utterance, the body-mind integration of the buddha-recollection action naturally displays itself as the gathering in of the six sense-roots; and reads "pure recollection uninterrupted" as the token of the succession of the three provisions — each utterance of the name already perfectly contains the three provisions, and pure recollection uninterrupted is the continuous three provisions. The core of his reading is: T0945's core sentence is the *Śūraṅgama*-idiom formulation of T0366's "faith, vow, and practice together present" — not a new dharma-gate, but the same buddha-recollection structure re-stated in the *Śūraṅgama* context.

### 4.2 The Three Irreducible Differences

The three positions are irreducible along three axes.

**First, verbal structure**. Lianchi does action-structure partition (*zhí* + *chí*); Jiaoguang does operation-type distinction (discriminative-consciousness recollection vs. faculty-gathering recollection); Zhixu does cognitive-premise analysis (six faiths + three provisions). All three are structural answers to the question "what is buddha-recollection?" — but the axis of partition differs. Lianchi cuts the action-face of *zhí-chí*; Jiaoguang cuts the operation-type face; Zhixu cuts the cognitive-premise face of faith-vow-practice. The three axes cannot reduce to one another.

**Second, the view of the *Śūraṅgama* hierarchy**. Lianchi holds that buddha-recollection is on parity with the various gates of the twenty-five perfect penetrations (X0424's spirit — "contemplation *is* recollection; recollection *is* contemplation" — extended into the *Śūraṅgama*); Jiaoguang uniquely judges buddha-recollection to be "just one step below Avalokiteśvara's great root" while also "foremost for those who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind," establishing the axis-distinction of position / rank; Zhixu holds buddha-recollection to be sudden-within-sudden, not involved in the twenty-five-perfect-penetration ranking ("not belaboring visualization, not requiring *chan* investigation" directly asserts the superiority of name-holding). The three hierarchical views — parity / multi-axis / transcendent — are not reconcilable.

**Third, the ontological verdict on *recollection*.** Lianchi takes recollection as phenomenal (phenomenal holding, phenomenal one-mindedness as the main line; principial holding, principial one-mindedness as extension); Jiaoguang takes recollection as faculty-integrative (phenomenal-and-principial non-two fuse on the spot within "faculty-gathering recollection" as operation, with no need of a phenomenal-principial bifurcation); Zhixu takes recollection as phenomenal-while-principial (faith-in-phenomenon and faith-in-principle simultaneously complete, phenomenal one-mindedness already containing principial one-mindedness). Three ontological verdicts — phenomenal-principial layered, phenomenal-principial non-two, phenomenal-principial perfectly-fused — reflect three philosophical judgments on the very nature of the verb *recollection*.

### 4.3 Jiaoguang as the Middle Figure of the Three — Structural Significance

§IV of P11 was a Lianchi ↔ Zhixu two-party conversation. P12 adds Jiaoguang for a three-party conversation — and this structural change has a specific significance.

Lianchi dives deep at the *phenomenal* end (action technique); Zhixu dives deep at the *principial* end (faith-vow-provision) — the two constitute the two ends of the phenomenal-principial spectrum. Jiaoguang's operation-type distinction, "faculty-gathering recollection," lies at the middle of this spectrum — neither entirely reducible to Lianchi's action technique (faculty-gathering recollection is not simply an extension of *zhí-chí*, but a distinction of action-type), nor entirely reducible to Zhixu's faith-vow-provision (the operation of faculty-gathering recollection is not merely the token of faith-vow-practice-together-present, but the concrete mechanism of six-root equal integration). Jiaoguang's distinctive contribution lies precisely in this middle position — threading the perfect-penetration of buddha-recollection and the twenty-five-perfect-penetration structure together through "straight-vein" classification, clarifying the type of operation of the verb *buddha-recollection* through the distinction "faculty-gathering recollection," and establishing the multi-axis response-to-capacity of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate through the reading "sequential position is not qualitative rank."

The parallelism of the three is not a conflation — it cannot be combined into a unified thesis "Lianchi speaks of technique, Zhixu speaks of provision, Jiaoguang speaks of operation." The three-party parallelism is a *display* — the display of three irreducible readings by the late-Ming *Śūraṅgama* commentators of the same sūtra passage and the same verb, exhibiting through this parallelism the richness of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate's structure. The core sentence of the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter is as short as eight characters ("gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted"); the three late-Ming figures each unfold it with a complete commentarial system — this very irreducibility is indirect evidence of the saturation-of-design of the sūtra passage itself: within eight characters, at least three structurally different entry-routes are sealed in, each able to support a complete commentarial system.

Three sūtras in parallel (P11 §3.3) × three commentators in parallel (P12 §IV) — this volume's Part IV closes in the isomorphism "textual-layer × exegetical-layer double parallel." Part V opens in the same isomorphism re-presented — the Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter at the end of the seven elements, adjacent to Avalokiteśvara; the late-Ming three *Śūraṅgama* commentators reading this chapter in parallel, each arriving at an independent system. This double isomorphism is not an authorial narrative choice; it is the historical display of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate's structure itself — designed minimalist sūtra-text, saturated commentarial unfolding, parallelized rich display.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading 1: Gathering In the Six Sense-Roots = Shutting Down the Six Sense-Roots

The popular reading — especially prominent in Chan-inflected contexts that read buddha-recollection as a preparation for *dhyāna* — reads "gathering in the six sense-roots" as closing-down-and-suppressing the sense-organs: eyes closed (shutting the eye-root), lips sealed (shutting the tongue-root), breath held (shutting the nose-root), deaf to sound (shutting the ear-root), untouching (shutting the body-root), thoughtless (shutting the mind-root). This reading takes *shè* as "closure" — analogous to Daoist inner-cultivation's "drawing-in sight and reversing hearing," a sensory in-gathering by suppression.

Correction: Jiaoguang's *Straight Vein Commentary* openly establishes "gathering in all the six sense-roots means causing them not to stir" — "not-stir" is not "closed," but "not arising in deluded motion."[^23] The six sense-roots remain in operation — the eye still sees, the ear still hears, the nose still smells, the tongue still tastes, the body still touches, the mind still thinks — but they do not follow sense-objects into deluded conceptual differentiation; under the buddha-recollection mind all six are simultaneously calmly still and not agitated. This is a **six-roots-calmly-still-under-a-single-recollection** integrative model, not a six-roots-closed-down model of suppression. The "gathering" of "gathering in all" means *marshaling, integrating, taking up* — not *closing, stopping, suppressing*. A practitioner who has gathered in all the six sense-roots may still open the eyes, still speak, still walk — the six sense-roots are not closed; they simply no longer scatter-and-chase after external sense-objects. This reading frees buddha-recollection from the scenic constraints of "*dhyāna* inward cultivation, reserved for sitting" — the gathering in of all the six sense-roots may be done equally in walking, standing, sitting, and lying, and need not be confined to closed-door retreats.

### ❌ Misreading 2: Sequential Position Is Qualitative Rank (the *Śūraṅgama* Demotes Buddha-Recollection to Secondary)

The popular reading — common to modern Pure Land apologetic literature and to Western introductory readers on Pure Land studies — reads "Mahāsthāmaprāpta at 24, Avalokiteśvara at 25, Mañjuśrī selecting Avalokiteśvara" as "the *Śūraṅgama* takes Avalokiteśvara as primary and buddha-recollection as secondary," a demotion reading. The inference: T0945's arrangement places buddha-recollection in a secondary position; the *Śūraṅgama* is essentially a Chan sūtra; the inclusion of the buddha-recollection chapter is a Chan-Pure Land conciliatory convenience; the true core of buddha-recollection still lies in the Three Pure Land Sūtras and not in the *Śūraṅgama*.

Correction: §2.2 and §3.3 have already argued "sequential position is not qualitative rank."[^24] Jiaoguang's *Straight Vein Commentary* judges openly that T0945 Fascicle Five's seven-element sequence "ending with the faculty-element, not with the consciousness-element" is the sūtra's own structural affirmation of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate — "the deep intent in ending not with Maitreya but with Mahāsthāmaprāpta." Mañjuśrī's selection of Avalokiteśvara is foremost on "Axis One (this realm, this time, self-power sudden cultivation)," and does not negate buddha-recollection's foremost on "Axis Two (other-power reception), Axis Three (response-to-capacity of those who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind)." To read sequential position as qualitative rank is to flatten a multi-axis coordinate system into a single-axis ordering — a flattening that not only misreads the *Śūraṅgama*, but also misreads the twenty-five perfect penetrations as a basic structure of multi-gate coexistence. T0945's design is to place buddha-recollection within the complete structure of the twenty-five perfect penetrations, displaying, through the "just one step below Avalokiteśvara's great root" adjacency, buddha-recollection's straight-vein position as the foremost alternate for sentient beings of this realm — not a demotion, not an ornament.

### ❌ Misreading 3: The *Śūraṅgama*'s Buddha-Recollection = Chan-Pure Land Syncretic Compromise

The popular reading — common to modern Chinese introductions to Buddhism and to contemporary English-language Buddhist overviews — positions T0945's Mahāsthāmaprāpta chapter as early documentary evidence for "Chan-Pure Land dual cultivation," and thereby reads the *Śūraṅgama*'s treatment of buddha-recollection as the Chan tradition's external incorporation of Pure Land practice — the *Śūraṅgama* is a Chan sūtra; the inclusion of the buddha-recollection chapter accommodates Pure Land sentient beings; it is not the *Śūraṅgama*'s own intent.

Correction: this reading underestimates T0945's structural deepening of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate. The dual-axis deepening from T0366's "holding the name, one mind undistracted" to T0945's "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted" (§III) is not the Chan tradition's external revision of Pure Land; it is the spatial-axis and temporal-axis unfolding of the verbal grammar of buddha-recollection within the *Śūraṅgama*'s idiomatic field. Jiaoguang's distinction between "faculty-gathering recollection" and "recollection by the discriminative mind-consciousness" (§2.3) is further a clarification of the operation-type internal to the buddha-recollection dharma-gate — it shares with *dhyāna* cultivation the mechanism of "faculty-gathering calm stillness," but points to the causal-fruit structure of seeing-the-Buddha-in-the-Pure-Land ("I, in my causal stage, by the mind of buddha-recollection, entered the tolerance of non-arising; and now, in this world, I gather in those who are mindful of the Buddha and return them to the Pure Land"). T0945's treatment of buddha-recollection is the deepening of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate itself, not the Chan tradition's external interpretation of it. To read the *Śūraṅgama*'s buddha-recollection as "Chan-Pure Land compromise" methodologically pre-loads the "Chan / Pure Land" dualism and imposes it onto T0945 — but T0945's own structure does not admit this dualism; it treats the twenty-five perfect penetrations as a system of multi-gate coexistence and places buddha-recollection within it at the faculty-element of the seven elements, as one gate among many.

### ⚠️ Functional Stance: The T0945 Authenticity Debate

The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* T0945 has been the subject of philological authenticity debate in modern Chinese Buddhist scholarship (Lü Cheng's *The Hundred Forgeries of the Śūraṅgama* 楞嚴百偽; the Zhina Inner-Studies Institute under Ouyang Jingwu) and in contemporary Western Buddhist studies (Robert Sharf, James Benn, Charles Muller, and others) — most scholars incline toward identifying it as an apocryphal scripture composed in China in the eighth century, while the counter-position (Jinhua Jia, Ron Epstein, and others) maintains that it is a translation from a Sanskrit original or a culturally-adapted translation of a Sanskrit text.[^25] This chapter adopts a **functional stance** on this debate: **it does not enter the authenticity controversy, but takes as its basis T0945's pedagogical function and historical standing within the Chinese-tradition buddha-recollection dharma-gate**.

This stance has three layers of scholarly support. **First, historical fact**: from Tang and Song onward, T0945 has been an indispensable basis for Chinese-tradition buddha-recollection, *dhyāna*, and mantra practice; as source-text for "the *Śūraṅgama* Mantra," "the twenty-five-perfect-penetration structure," and "the *Tathāgatagarbha* doctrine of mind-consciousness," it occupies an irreplaceable millennium-long standing in the Chinese cultivational tradition. **Second, scholarly consensus crosscuts the authenticity question**: skeptics, defenders, and Western scholars alike acknowledge T0945's generative role in Chinese practice; "apocryphal" in Western Buddhist-studies terminology is a neutral classification and does not carry the pejorative implication of "false sūtra" or "untrustworthy." **Third, Jiaoguang's "straight-vein" internal evidence**: the positional arrangement of buddha-recollection perfect penetration within the twenty-five-chapter structure (§2.2's ending of the seven elements with the faculty-element, §2.3's "deep intent of not ending with the consciousness-element but with the faculty-element") is a design displayed by the sūtra's own structure, not an inference of external commentary.[^26] This internal evidence is independent of the philological authenticity judgment — even if T0945 is indeed an eighth-century Chinese composition, its twenty-five-perfect-penetration structure's structural affirmation of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate remains a fact of the text's internal logic.

The subsequent conclusion of this chapter, as well as the research of the later seven papers of Part V (P13–P19), is developed under this functional stance — taking T0945 as canonical basis for the Chinese-tradition buddha-recollection dharma-gate, and focusing on its doctrinal contributions to the verbal grammar of buddha-recollection, the positional structure, and the phenomenal-principial unity, without entering the philological disputes over its Sanskrit source, translator identity, or dating. This stance does not endorse the skeptics, nor does it endorse the defenders — it is a methodological decision, at this stage of the scholarly work, to focus on doctrinal analysis and set the philological debate aside.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Summary

The core operational sentence of T0945's Fascicle Five Chapter on the Perfect Penetration of Buddha-Recollection by Mahāsthāmaprāpta — "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted, attaining *samādhi*, this is foremost" — is the dual-axis deepening of T0366's "holding the name, one mind undistracted." The spatial axis extends from the mind to the six sense-roots (deepening from a single-organ or hierarchical model into a fully integrated model); the temporal axis extends from present-moment non-distraction to recollection-upon-recollection succession (deepening from time-span achievement into diachronic continuity). Mahāsthāmaprāpta holds position 24 among the twenty-five perfect penetrations (seven great elements, faculty-element), just one step below Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty at position 25 (selected by Mañjuśrī as "the true teaching-body of this realm"). But Jiaoguang's *Commentary on the Straight Vein of the Śūraṅgama* reads this through "sequential position is not qualitative rank" — T0945 Fascicle Five's seven-element sequence's anomalous "ending with the faculty-element, not with the consciousness-element" is the sūtra's own structural affirmation of buddha-recollection on the axis of "this realm, this time, those who cannot cultivate the *nirvāṇa* of their own mind." The late-Ming trio of *Śūraṅgama* commentators stand in parallel (Lianchi's holding-technique, Jiaoguang's faculty-gathering distinction, Zhixu's faith-vow-practice provisions) — three irreducible readings of the structural partition of the verb *recollection*, the *Śūraṅgama*'s positional hierarchy, and the ontological verdict on *recollection* — displaying the historical saturation of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate's structure.

### 6.2 Practical Guidance

This framework carries two concrete implications for the practitioner.

**First, spatial unfolding from holding to gathering-in-all**. The practitioner may enter at a single point from T0366's holding the name, but T0945 intimates that this single point already conceals a full-integration structure. Concretely: in buddha-recollection, one need not forcibly shut down the other five sense-roots in order to highlight the tongue-root or the mind-root; on the contrary, one may allow the six sense-roots to remain in operation (eyes seeing the Buddha-image, ears hearing one's own voice, nose smelling the incense, body maintaining bearing, mind holding the Buddha-name), but under the buddha-recollection mind the six sense-roots are simultaneously calmly still, not arising in deluded conceptual differentiation. This is the plain operation of "gathering in all" — not an addition of extra difficulty, but allowing the latent structure to unfold naturally.

**Second, temporal unfolding from one-mind to uninterrupted**. The practitioner need not demand of some particular time-span the absolute sphere of "never distracted"; the criterion is rewritten as "every recollection pure, and upon defilement arising, immediately repair back into pure" — a micro-operation on the recollection-to-recollection structure. The "attention persistence" indicator of Western psychologizing readings is here re-structured as a micro-operation of recollection-upon-recollection succession — each recollection is an independent unit, and each conversion from defiled to pure is the self-maintenance of the continuous structure. This displaces practice from the "ultimate-result position" to the "micro-operation at every recollection," dissolving the self-blockage of "I must attain some sphere before I count as qualified."

### 6.3 Open Questions: Toward the Later Papers of Part V

The eight Mahāyāna sūtras of Part V — *Śūraṅgama*, *Lotus*, *Perfect Enlightenment*, *Brahma Net*, *Golden Light*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Dense Adornment*, *Mañjuśrī Prajñā* — each present a distinctive entry-point into the buddha-recollection dharma-gate. T0945, as the opening text, has carried buddha-recollection from the stand-alone channel of the Three Pure Land Sūtras (Part IV) up into the structurally-deepening stratum of the Mahāyāna sūtras. The structural position of each of the remaining seven may be briefly foreshadowed. The *Lotus* (P13), through the Avalokiteśvara-Universal-Gate chapter's name-invocation, the Expedient-Gate chapter's opening of expedient devices, and Sadāparibhūta's body-posture of universal reverence, extends buddha-recollection into the universal gate of Mahāyāna bodhisattva practice. The *Sūtra of Perfect Enlightenment* (P14), with Dharma-ending-age sentient beings as its capacity-target and the twenty-five pure meditation-wheels as its framework, situates buddha-recollection within the dialectical structure of sudden awakening and gradual cultivation. The *Brahma Net Sūtra* (P15), with the bodhisattva precepts as precept-body and continuous buddha-recollection as precept-aspect preservation. The *Sūtra of Golden Light* (P16), with confession as entry-route and buddha-recollection as the structural support of confession. The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* (P17), on the axis of Bodhidharma's mind-seal "the Buddhas' mind is foremost," deepens buddha-recollection into the mind-and-recollection one-body cultivation. The *Dense Adornment Sūtra* (P18), with the transformation of the *ālaya-vijñāna* as skeleton, constructs buddha-recollection as the concrete mechanism of consciousness-transformation. The *Mañjuśrī Prajñā Sūtra* (P19), with the one-practice *samādhi* (*yī-xíng sānmèi* 一行三昧) as pivot, closes buddha-recollection into the cross-sectarian scriptural witness.

The eight sūtras each enter distinctively — neither a single-line derivation nor a flat parallel listing, but the multi-axis structural unfolding of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate within the Mahāyāna sūtra corpus. T0945, as the first sūtra, sets the tone for Part V with its dual-axis deepening "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted" — the multi-axis structural reading along the spatial, temporal, and hierarchical axes will be re-unfolded in the later seven papers in the scripture-specific idioms of each. Part V's structural task: to restore buddha-recollection from its Pure-Land-sectarian designated dharma-gate back to its universal-deepening structure within the Mahāyāna canonical system. This restoration is neither the demotion of the Pure Land school's standing nor the Mahāyāna sūtras' incorporation of buddha-recollection; it is the display of the historical saturation of the buddha-recollection dharma-gate within the Mahāyāna scriptural space.
