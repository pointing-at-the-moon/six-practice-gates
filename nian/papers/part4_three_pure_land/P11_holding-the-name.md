## Abstract

This paper closes Part IV, the Three Pure Land Sūtras cluster, by taking up the earliest-translated and shortest member of the three: the *Amitābha Sūtra* (*Fóshuō Āmítuó Jīng* 佛說阿彌陀經, T0366, translated by Kumārajīva in 402 CE, also known as the *Shorter Sukhāvatīvyūha*). It argues three things. **First**, T0366's minimalism — "holding the name, one to seven days, one mind, undistracted" (*zhíchí míng-hào, yī rì zhì qī rì, yī-xīn bú-luàn* 執持名號、一日至七日、一心不亂) — is *not* a retrospective compression executed after T0360's vow-architecture and T0365's contemplation-architecture were already in place. It is Kumārajīva's *literary distillation* of an early six-direction recension of the *Sukhāvatīvyūha* Sanskrit text. T0366's Chinese translation precedes T0365's (Kālayaśas, 424–442 CE) by twenty to forty years, and precedes T0360's actual textual completion (fourth–fifth century) by more than a generation. This chronological hard evidence seals the compression hypothesis: T0366 is the *third parallel access channel* of the Three Pure Land Sūtras — the **name-layer** — set alongside the vow-layer and the contemplation-layer, not derivative of them. **Second**, beneath T0366's minimalist surface, provision-density is already saturated. Yunqi Zhuhong's *Commentary and Notes on the Amitābha Sūtra* (*Āmítuójīng Shūchāo* 阿彌陀經疏鈔, X0424) breaks the mindless-recitation misreading open through philological exegesis — "*zhí* 執 is to grip with resolute, unshakeable courage; *chí* 持 is to preserve in abiding, unwavering fidelity, without forgetting" (translation mine) — and then opens the practice along a phenomenal-holding / principial-holding (*shì-chí / lǐ-chí* 事持／理持) dual track that runs from the power of faith that suppresses delusion to the power of wisdom that breaks it. Ouyi Zhixu's *Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra* (*Āmítuójīng Yàojiě* 阿彌陀經要解, T1762) analyzes "good roots, merit, and causal conditions" into **six faiths** — faith-in-self / faith-in-other / faith-in-cause / faith-in-effect / faith-in-phenomenon / faith-in-principle — and, with the doctrine that the three provisions of **faith, vow, and practice** "are not sequenced and are not rigidly three" (*fēi yǒu xiān-hòu yì fēi dìng sān* 非有先後亦非定三), demonstrates that a single utterance of the name already possesses the three provisions in full. **Third**, Zhuhong and Zhixu stand in no teacher-disciple relation and became authorities independently; their three irreducible differences — parity of name-holding with visualization versus name-holding *above* visualization / two-layered versus three-layered phenomenal-principial architecture / technique-oriented exegesis versus provision-oriented exegesis — constitute two *parallel* late-Ming commentarial perspectives. The parallelism of the three sūtras (at the textual layer) and the parallelism of the two commentators (at the exegetical layer) together form a *structurally isomorphic double parallel*. Part IV thus closes, pointing toward P12, which takes up T0945's *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* and Mahāsthāmaprāpta's "Chapter on the Perfect Penetration of Buddha-Recollection" (*Dàshìzhì niànfó yuántōng zhāng* 大勢至念佛圓通章), where "holding the name" is further distilled into "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted."

**Keywords:** holding the name, T0366 *Amitābha Sūtra*, T0367 *Sūtra on the Praise of the Pure Land and the Protection of the Buddha*, Kumārajīva, Xuanzang, one mind undistracted, seven days, six-direction buddhas' witness, name-layer, three-sūtra parallel, Yunqi Zhuhong, X0424 *Commentary and Notes on the Amitābha Sūtra*, phenomenal holding, principial holding, phenomenal one-mindedness, principial one-mindedness, "contemplation *is* recollection, recollection *is* contemplation," Ouyi Zhixu, T1762 *Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra*, six faiths, faith-vow-practice, "not sequenced and not rigidly three," name-holding as the sudden-and-perfect teaching, "hard among the hard," designed minimalist architecture, provision-density saturation, literary distillation, parallel name-layer, floor-not-pinnacle.

## I. Introduction: When Minimalism Is Misread as Simplification

When Western English-language Buddhist readers (together with Chinese-language readers trained in English-language scholarship) first approach the Three Pure Land Sūtras, the *Amitābha Sūtra* (T0366) is typically received along two flat pathways. Either it is read as "the shortest and easiest of the three, suitable as a self-study introduction," or it is read as "a retrospective compression issued after T0360's expansive vow-system and T0365's sixteen contemplations were already in place." Both readings share a single hidden premise — **less means shallower**. Because the body of the sūtra is under two thousand Chinese characters, because the entry-action is reduced to a single phrase ("holding the name," *zhíchí míng-hào* 執持名號), because the time-frame is locked to "one to seven days," and because the criterion is compressed into the single short phrase "one mind, undistracted" (*yī-xīn bú-luàn* 一心不亂) — for these reasons, the sūtra is read either as an abbreviation of its two canonical companions or as a lightweight entry ramp designed by Pure Land circles for duller faculties.

In the contemporary Western context a third flattening is layered on top. After the industrialization of mindfulness (*mindfulness*), the formula "try reciting a buddha-name for a few minutes" has been popularized as an interchangeable "Eastern version of self-affirmation practice" — and on the surface, this appears isomorphic to T0366's "holding the name." T0366 thereby suffers a twofold misreading: once as "an internal compression within the Three Pure Land Sūtras," and once as "an Eastern simplification absorbed into the mindfulness industry."

This twofold flattening calls for correction. P09 has already argued that T0360's forty-eight vows (*Sūtra of Immeasurable Life*) are a designed architecture, with the container-world / sentient-world dual axis as its skeleton, and with Vow Eighteen's "ten recollections at least" as its *floor-not-pinnacle* node. P10 has extended the thesis to T0365 (*Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus*), showing that its sixteen contemplations form a designed cognitive-visual architecture: a three-axis geometry "outer → inner → grade-of-rebirth," with the lower-grade lower-birth's ten-recollection name-invocation as its own floor-not-pinnacle node.[^1] Both papers have established the architecture-reading of the first two texts. But what of T0366? Does T0360's vow-layer plus T0365's contemplation-layer regress, in T0366, into a flat single line of "one phrase, the name"? Or — and this is the thesis of the present paper — is T0366 itself already the third *parallel* access channel of the Three Pure Land Sūtras: the **name-layer**, in which the minimalist surface conceals provision-density saturation rather than thin content?

The present paper argues T0366 as **a designed minimalist architecture** through the three-layered §III.

**First** (§3.1, *surface minimalism*): T0366's "holding the name, one to seven days, one mind, undistracted" operates as the minimalism of the entry-action and is a recognizable *design signature*; but minimalism of *design* is not reduction of *content*.

**Second** (§3.2, *depth saturation*): Late-Ming Ouyi Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* (T1762) uses the six faiths (faith-in-self / faith-in-other / faith-in-cause / faith-in-effect / faith-in-phenomenon / faith-in-principle), together with the doctrine that the three provisions of faith, vow, and practice are "not sequenced and not rigidly three," to prove that a single utterance of "*Namo Amitābha Buddha*" (*Námó Āmítuófó* 南無阿彌陀佛) already contains the three provisions and the six faiths in their completeness. Late-Ming Yunqi Zhuhong's *Commentary and Notes* (X0424) offers the philological gloss "*zhí* 執 = gripping with resolute, unshakeable courage; *chí* 持 = preserving in abiding, unwavering fidelity, without forgetting," sealing off the mindless-repetition misreading; and he opens phenomenal-holding / principial-holding as the dual track along which name-holding unfolds from suppression-by-faith-power to breakthrough-by-wisdom-power.

**Third** (§3.3, *three sūtras in parallel*): Kumārajīva's T0366 (402 CE) precedes Kālayaśas's T0365 (424–442 CE) by twenty to forty years, and precedes T0360's actual textual completion (fourth–fifth century) by more than a generation — this philological chronology is hard evidence that **seals the "T0366-as-compression-of-the-first-two" hypothesis**. The three sūtras stand as three independent access channels (vow-layer / contemplation-layer / name-layer) jointly constituting the salvific design of the Three Pure Land Sūtras; they are not a single-line derivation chain, but three concurrent distillations within the pre-fifth-century Indian Pure Land thought-space.

§IV takes Zhuhong and Zhixu as *parallel* late-Ming commentarial perspectives, displaying how the two read T0366 from equal footing but along different routes — continuing P10's turn from Part III's Western interlocutors back to intra-Buddhist commentarial perspectives. §V treats four circulating misreadings: "holding the name = mindless recitation," "T0366 = compression of the first two sūtras," "T0366 is shallow and suitable for unsupervised beginners," and "Yinguang's 'even if an ancient Buddha returned he could not surpass this' praise is from T1762's main text." §VI turns toward P12 — the "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted" (*dōushè liù-gēn, jìngniàn xiāngjì* 都攝六根、淨念相繼) of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s "Chapter on the Perfect Penetration of Buddha-Recollection": when "holding the name" is once again distilled into the body-mind integration of "gathering in the six sense-roots," how does the name-layer channel carry into the internal deepening of Part V's eight Mahāyāna sūtras?

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 T0366: Textual Skeleton and the Holding-the-Name Passage

The *Amitābha Sūtra* (T0366) was translated by Kumārajīva in Later Qin, Hongshi 4 (402 CE) in one fascicle of approximately 1,800 Chinese characters.[^2] The full sūtra divides into three sections: *preface* (audience and setting) → *main exposition* (the dependent and direct retributions of the Land of Bliss + holding the name + witness by buddhas of the six directions) → *circulation* (the three-time-tense aspiration + the five defilements). Unlike T0360's vow-making-and-fulfillment dual-axis narrative, and unlike T0365's sixteen-layered contemplation-ladder opened from Vaidehī's distress-petition, T0366 has no vow-number, no contemplation-number, and no stage-ranking — the entire sūtra requires only a single address to "Śāriputra" for the Buddha to proceed without being asked, not waiting for an assembly-petition.

The audience roster at the opening of the preface itself carries a structural signal: the four bodhisattvas who head the assembly are **Mañjuśrī Dharma-Prince, Ajita (Maitreya), Gandhahastin (*qiántuó hētí* 乾陀訶提, "fragrant elephant"), and Nityodyukta (*chángjīngjìn* 常精進, "constantly energetic")**.[^3] The first two are the canonical Mahāyāna wisdom-and-succession pair; the latter two are comparatively rare, and their semantic field points unambiguously toward *energetic practice* — Nityodyukta most directly ("constantly energetic"), and Gandhahastin through the idiomatic image of "fragrant elephant advancing with swift resolve," symbolizing resolute courage.[^4] The bodhisattva audience is not a random selection: T0366's holding-the-name is, at the semantic-field level, already oriented toward *disciplined practice*, not toward a cheat-code for the idle — a structural signal that will return in §3.1 when read together with Zhuhong's philological gloss on "*zhí-chí* 執持."

The core passage of the main exposition is the base on which this entire paper rests:

> "Śāriputra! One cannot be reborn in that land with but small good roots, merits, and causal conditions. Śāriputra! If there are good men or good women who, hearing the discourse on Amitābha Buddha, *hold the name* for one day, or two days, or three days, or four days, or five days, or six days, or seven days — one mind, undistracted — then at the moment of their life's end, Amitābha Buddha together with the host of holy ones will appear before them. At the moment of their passing, their minds will not be upended; they will immediately attain rebirth in Amitābha Buddha's Land of Bliss." (translation mine)[^5]

Five key terms: (a) **"hearing the discourse on Amitābha Buddha"** — the entry-event is *hearing* (*wén* 聞), not *seeing* or *reading*; (b) **"hold the name"** (*zhíchí míng-hào* 執持名號) — Zhuhong's philological exegesis to be unpacked in §2.4; (c) **"one day … up to seven days"** — seven is a perfection-number, not a fixed time-limit; (d) **"one mind, undistracted"** (*yī-xīn bú-luàn* 一心不亂) — Kumārajīva's distinctive phrasing, corresponding to Sanskrit *avikṣipta-citta*; (e) **"mind not upended"** (*xīn bú diāndǎo* 心不顛倒) — the internal state after the Buddha's appearance at the death-moment, corresponding to the "mind not upended" description in T0365's nine-grade rebirth passages.

The **witness by buddhas of the six directions** (east, south, west, north, below, above) in the witness-portion follows a formulaic pattern in which each direction's buddhas "extend their broad-and-long tongues to cover the entire trichiliocosm" and each speak words of truth: "You sentient beings should believe this *Sūtra of Inconceivable Merit Praised and Protected-and-Recollected by All Buddhas*."[^6] The sūtra's own title is **embedded inside the witness formula**: *Sūtra of Inconceivable Merit Praised and Protected-and-Recollected by All Buddhas* (*Chēngzàn bùkěsīyì gōngdé yīqiè zhū-fó suǒ hùniàn jīng* 稱讚不可思議功德一切諸佛所護念經) *is* T0366's native title. This is the sūtra's internal signature: the sūtra's name is *generated by* the collective witness of the six-direction buddhas, not *affixed* by an author or translator retrospectively.

The circulation-portion closes with a three-time-tense aspirational structure — "already-made-vow / presently-making-vow / will-make-vow → already-reborn / presently-reborn / will-be-reborn" — and concludes with the tension of the five defilements, explaining Śākyamuni Buddha's achievement of buddhahood amid "the defilement of the eon, the defilement of views, the defilement of afflictions, the defilement of sentient beings, the defilement of life-span" and his expounding "a dharma difficult for all worlds to believe."[^7] The ease of the Land of Bliss and the difficulty of the Sahā world form mirror images of each other — the structural confrontation at the very end of T0366.

### 2.2 Chronological Hard Evidence: T0366 (402 CE) Precedes T0360 and T0365

T0366 was translated by Kumārajīva in Later Qin, Hongshi 4 (402 CE); T0365 was translated by Kālayaśas in the Liu-Song Yuanjia period (424–442 CE), twenty to forty years later; T0360 is traditionally ascribed to Saṃghavarman in Cao-Wei, Jiaping 4 (252 CE), but modern scholarship has shown that its actual textual completion must fall somewhere between the Eastern Jin and the Liu-Song (fourth–fifth century), and the translator's identity is itself disputed.[^8] **T0366 is the earliest-translated of the Three Pure Land Sūtras in Chinese**.

The significance of this chronological fact lies not at the level of bibliographic detail but at the level of architecture. If "holding the name" minimalism were a reactive compression of T0360's forty-eight vows and T0365's sixteen contemplations, there would be no way to explain why T0366 *precedes the Chinese translation* of both its supposed sources. The compression hypothesis requires "the compressed" to follow "the expansive"; the actual chronology is that **the brief precedes the expansive** — and so the compression hypothesis is **chronologically sealed**.

The correct framing is: T0366, T0360, and T0365 each distill one type of entry-action from the pre-fifth-century Indian Pure Land thought-space. T0360 distills the **vow-layer** (the vow-power structure that produces the adornments of both dependent and direct retributions); T0365 distills the **contemplation-layer** (seeing the Buddha and the land through a cognitive-visual ladder); T0366 distills the **name-layer** (taking the holding-to of the Buddha's name as the single entry-action). The three are parallel, complementary, non-subordinate — together forming the three parallel channels by which the Indian Pure Land tradition entered the Chinese cultural region.

### 2.3 Xuanzang's T0367: Philological Contrast Along Six Divergences

Xuanzang's *Sūtra on the Praise of the Pure Land and the Protection of the Buddha* (*Chēngzàn jìngtǔ fó shèshòu jīng* 稱讚淨土佛攝受經, T0367, 650 CE) is a retranslation based on a different Sanskrit recension, and diverges from T0366 at six philological points:[^9]

| # | T0366 Kumārajīva (402) | T0367 Xuanzang (650) | Sanskrit reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | *zhíchí míng-hào* (holding the name) | *wén-míng, sīwéi, xì-niàn* (hearing-the-name, contemplating, fixing-mindfulness) | *śrutvā … nāma-dhārayati* / *manasikaroti* |
| 2 | *yī-xīn bú-luàn* (one mind, undistracted) | *xì-niàn bú-luàn* (fixed-mindfulness, undistracted) | *avikṣipta-citta* |
| 3 | six-direction buddhas | ten-direction buddhas | Sanskrit recension differs |
| 4 | *hùniàn* (protected-and-recollected; in title) | *shèshòu* (embraced-and-received; in title) | *rakṣā* vs *parigraha* |
| 5 | Buddha's appearance at the death-moment | Buddha's blessing to steady the mind against distraction (split into two events) | Xuanzang's amplification |
| 6 | Gandhahastin | *Bù-xiūxī* (Aniksiptadhura, "the one who does not rest") | Sanskrit bodhisattva-name differs |

The three most significant items are 1, 2, and 3. Item 1, "holding the name" versus "hearing-the-name, contemplating, fixing-mindfulness": Xuanzang stays closer to the Sanskrit *nāma-dhārayati* / *manasikaroti*, which presents a three-phase action (hear + contemplate + fix-mindfulness), while Kumārajīva compresses the three phases into the single compound verb "*zhíchí*" (holding). Item 2, "one mind" versus "fixed-mindfulness": Kumārajīva's rendering carries a higher degree of literary heightening than Xuanzang's. Item 3, "six directions" versus "ten directions": this reflects the internal development within the Indian Sanskrit text of the *Smaller Sukhāvatīvyūha* between the third and seventh centuries, when the witness-scheme expanded from six to ten directions; Kumārajīva's base text was an earlier six-direction recension.

These six divergences carry a twofold implication for the main thesis of this paper: (a) T0366's minimalism is a **translator's word-choice within the Kumārajīva translation workshop**, not a late compression act — "*zhíchí* / *yī-xīn*" is a *translation event*, not a *doctrinal compression event*; (b) T0367, despite being closer to the Sanskrit and structurally more elaborate, did *not* supplant T0366 in Chinese reception — because Kumārajīva's literary distillation fit Chinese expressive habits more naturally and thereby locked in the practice-vocabulary of East Asia for the next sixteen hundred years.

### 2.4 Zhuhong's *Commentary and Notes on the Amitābha Sūtra* (X0424): Philology of "Holding" and the Phenomenal-and-Principial Dual Exegesis

Yunqi Zhuhong (雲棲祩宏, 1535–1615), also known by the soubriquet Lianchi (蓮池, "Lotus Pond"), completed his *Commentary and Notes on the Amitābha Sūtra* (X0424) in four fascicles in 1595; it is the most comprehensive systematic commentary on T0366 in the late Ming. Four points are drawn here.[^10]

**First, the philological exegesis of "holding."** (Fascicle 3):

> "*Zhí* (執): having heard, one receives it — with resolute courage, not to be shaken; *chí* (持): having received, one preserves it — in abiding, unwavering fidelity, without forgetting." (translation mine)[^11]

"Holding-to" breaks into the double volitional act of **grasping** (*zhí*, decisive reception) + **maintaining** (*chí*, sustained guarding): the catching-hold must be resolute and unshakeable; the keeping-hold must be steadfast and unforgotten. Zhuhong further unfolds "holding" into "the turning-toward direction (*guītóu* 歸投) and the turning-back-to-source direction (*guīyuán* 歸元)" — the outward turning-toward of Amitābha (phenomenal holding, *shì-chí* 事持) and the inward turning-back-to-source of one's own mind (principial holding, *lǐ-chí* 理持) are simultaneously constituted. This philological gloss directly seals off the popular reduction that reads "holding the name" as "mechanical repetition."

**Second, the distinction of phenomenal one-mindedness and principial one-mindedness** (Fascicle 3):

> "*Phenomenal one-mindedness*: as in the previous recollection — thought succeeds thought without a second thought, faith-power is accomplished; this is called phenomenal one-mindedness, belonging to the gate of *samādhi*, in which wisdom has not yet emerged. … *Principial one-mindedness*: … within it there are further two: first, knowing that the recollector and the recollected are not two separate things, only one mind; second, neither existent, nor non-existent, nor both existent and non-existent, nor neither existent nor non-existent — freed from the four propositions, only one mind. … It belongs to the gate of wisdom, while also subsuming *samādhi*." (translation mine)[^12]

Phenomenal one-mindedness = the gate of *samādhi* (faith-power suppressing delusion); principial one-mindedness = the gate of wisdom (investigative-insight breaking delusion). Zhuhong states explicitly that the two are capable of gradual ascent: duller faculties first practice phenomenal holding diligently, then gradually investigate toward principial holding; sharper faculties enter principial holding directly. "Their operations differ slightly, but their successful fruition is one." This phenomenal-principial dual track decisively rescues T0366 from the "dispersed-good, lower-grade" categorization and opens for name-holding a complete trajectory from faith-power to wisdom-power.

**Third, "contemplation *is* recollection; recollection *is* contemplation."** (Fascicle 1, "Sideways Penetrating the Contemplation Sūtra"):

> "That sūtra (T0365) takes mental contemplation as its tenet; this sūtra (T0366) takes mental recollection as its tenet. Contemplation *is* recollection; recollection *is* contemplation. … Since the two are a single mind, why is this sūtra alone demoted as 'dispersed'?" (translation mine)[^13]

This is a late-Ming commentarial declaration of authority: T0365's contemplation and T0366's name-holding are **parallel**, not subordinate. The verdict coincides with the chronological hard evidence of §2.2 and reinforces it from the opposite direction: at the fifth-century textual layer T0366 and T0365 are already parallel recensions, and at the sixteenth-century exegetical layer Zhuhong re-confirms them as parallel through the authority of "contemplation *is* recollection."

**Fourth, ten-gate frame ↔ five-fold profound meaning.** (Fascicle 1): Zhuhong reads T0366 using the *Huayan Commentary*'s ten-gate frame (condensed into Tiantai's five-fold profound meaning, *wǔchóng xuányì* 五重玄義), classifying the sūtra as "the sudden teaching partly subsumed by the perfect teaching" — lifting T0366 out of the "minor sūtra of dispersed good" category and placing it on par with the *Avataṃsaka* and *Lotus* as a sudden-and-perfect scripture.[^14]

### 2.5 Zhixu's *Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra* (T1762): The Six Faiths and Faith-Vow-Practice

Ouyi Zhixu (蕅益智旭, 1599–1655) completed his *Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra* in nine days in 1647 at the age of forty-nine; the work is one fascicle and applies Tiantai's five-fold profound meaning to T0366. Three points are drawn here.[^15]

**First, the six faiths** (T37.0364b24–0364c18):

> "*Faith in self*: believing that the present single thought of my mind is neither a lump of flesh nor a shadow of sense-objects; vertically it has no beginning or end, horizontally it has no edge or limit. … If I can, with a single thought, turn back my mind, I shall certainly be reborn in the Land of Bliss already present within my own mind — no further doubt or anxiety. This is called faith in self.
>
> *Faith in other*: believing that Śākyamuni Tathāgata certainly does not speak falsely, that Amitābha World-Honored One certainly does not make empty vows, that the broad-and-long tongues of the six-direction buddhas certainly do not speak with a divided voice. …
>
> *Faith in cause*: deeply believing that even dispersed invocation of the name is still a seed of buddhahood — how much more so one mind undistracted, which cannot fail to be a cause of birth in the Pure Land. …
>
> *Faith in effect*: deeply believing that the assembly of the superior-good in the Pure Land is born entirely from *samādhi* of buddha-recollection. …
>
> *Faith in phenomenon*: … the Land of Bliss truly exists, beyond ten billion lands, supremely pure and adorned, not like the parables of Zhuangzi. …
>
> *Faith in principle*: deeply believing that the Land of Bliss, though distant by ten billion lands, in truth does not lie outside the single fine-particle thought of my present mind. … All phenomenon *is* principle, all delusion *is* truth, all cultivation *is* nature, all other *is* self. …" (translation mine)[^16]

The architecture of the six faiths is precisely engineered. *Faith in self* + *faith in other* constitute the subject-object mutual illumination; *faith in cause* + *faith in effect* constitute the seed-and-fruit relation; *faith in phenomenon* + *faith in principle* constitute the mutual identity of phenomenal and principial. With the six faiths unfolded, T0366's "good roots, merits, and causal conditions" is no longer a vague accumulation of blessings but a subtle requirement for six specific mind-actions simultaneously in place. **Faith in self** dissolves the "outward projection" misreading; **faith in phenomenon** dissolves the "psychological reduction" misreading; **faith in principle** dissolves the "dualistic opposition" misreading — the three central philosophical objections raised by Western readers to the Pure Land tradition are resolved in the same stroke.

**Second, "faith, vow, and practice are not sequenced and are not rigidly three"** (T37.0371c27–0372a04):

> "On the basis of one-mindedness, one speaks of faith, vow, and practice: they are not sequenced, nor are they rigidly three. For without vow and without practice, 'faith' does not deserve the name of true faith; without practice and without faith, 'vow' does not deserve the name of true vow; without faith and without vow, 'practice' does not deserve the name of true practice. Now, since name-holding rests entirely upon faith and vow, every single utterance already possesses faith, vow, and practice in their fullness — and this is why the name is called 'abundant good roots, merit, and causal conditions.'" (translation mine)[^17]

This declaration is the direct foundation of §3.2: **within a single utterance of the name, the three provisions of faith, vow, and practice are all simultaneously in full presence** — beneath the minimalist surface, structural saturation has already been accomplished. The three provisions are not three sequential stages of the path; they are three aspects of a single utterance of the name.

**Third, name-holding as the sudden-within-the-sudden** (T37.0365a04–11; T37.0371c02–08):

> "Only this dharma of name-holding — it gathers in faculties most broadly, and is easiest to apply. … It may be called: the first of expedients among expedients, the supreme of explicit meanings among explicit meanings, the most extreme of perfect-and-sudden among perfect-and-sudden teachings. … A single thought in correspondence is a single thought of buddha; thought after thought in correspondence is thought after thought of buddha. No labor of visualization is needed, no need to apply inquiring investigation — at the present moment, perfect illumination without remainder or deficiency. The highest-root person cannot go beyond its threshold; the lowest-root person can likewise reach its doorway. It may be said to *horizontally span the eight teachings and vertically penetrate the five periods*." (translation mine)[^18]

"Horizontally spans the eight teachings and vertically penetrates the five periods" places T0366 at the summit of Tiantai's doctrinal-classification system; "no labor of visualization is needed, no need to apply inquiring investigation" declares explicitly that name-holding is *not* a preparatory stage for visualization practice but a complete, independent dharma-gate. This positioning will form an irreducible contrast in §IV with Zhuhong's "contemplation *is* recollection" parity verdict.

## III. Central Argument: A Designed Minimalist Architecture

The central thesis of this paper is presented at three layers: the surface minimalism is a design signature, not thin content (§3.1); at depth, provision-density is already saturated, and minimalism is not mindlessness (§3.2); the three sūtras are not a compression chain but stand in parallel, with the name-layer as the third parallel channel of the Three Pure Land Sūtras (§3.3).

### 3.1 Surface Minimalism: The Design Signature

T0366's entry-action is compressed to the minimum — four contours: **a single object** (one Buddha, Amitābha, without mixing in other buddhas or other scenes); **a single action** (holding the name, without pairing with visualization or image-contemplation); **a definite time-frame** (a bounded span of one to seven days, not an indefinite long-term practice); **a single criterion** ("one mind, undistracted," a single indicator of mind-state).

These four contours are not an accidental set of omissions but a recognizable **design signature**. The design signature is verifiable at three levels.

**First, the literary distillation characteristic of Kumārajīva's translation style.** Among the six divergences in §2.3, items 1 and 2 are the most direct: Xuanzang's three-phase action "hearing-the-name, contemplating, fixing-mindfulness" (staying close to the Sanskrit *śrutvā manasikaroti*) is compressed in Kumārajīva's rendering into the single compound verb "holding the name"; Xuanzang's "fixed-mindfulness, undistracted" is compressed in Kumārajīva's rendering into the single short phrase "one mind, undistracted." This is a deliberate literary distillation by Kumārajīva's translation workshop of an early six-direction Sanskrit recension — not a reduction of content, but a concentration of the verb's practical-imperative character. The result is that what the reader encounters is not a set of ritual steps but a highly focused action-command.

**Second, the design signature is consonant with the choice of bodhisattva audience.** §2.1 has already noted that the last two of the four leading bodhisattvas in T0366's opening roster — Gandhahastin and Nityodyukta — unambiguously point toward *energetic practice*.[^19] This is not incidental casting: the internal structure of the text and the semantic field of its entry-action mutually confirm each other. A sūtra that demands "holding" (resolute courage + abiding fidelity) has built into its very bodhisattva audience the signal that this dharma-gate requires discipline — this is not a dharma-gate for the idle. The design signature extends from the bodhisattva audience to the core verb, and the two ends are consistent.

**Third, the three-time-tense aspirational structure in the circulation-portion.** The tripartite "already-made-vow / presently-making-vow / will-make-vow → already-reborn / presently-reborn / will-be-reborn" places the minimalist action inside a complete temporal span — minimalism does not mean present-use-and-immediate-discard; minimalism as a sustainable, cross-generational dharma-gate requires its depth to carry the salvific weight of three-time-tense aspirations precisely because its surface is compressed.[^20] Short text, long time-tense — this tension is at the core of the design.

Minimalism as *design signature* ≠ minimalism as *thin content*. Surface contraction does not entail depth poverty — and that is exactly what §3.2 now proves.

### 3.2 Depth Saturation: Minimalism Is Not Mindlessness

If T0366's "holding the name" were merely one repeated Buddha-name, from where would come the six mind-actions of "resolute courage, not to be shaken" and "abiding fidelity, not to be forgotten" that Zhuhong's philological gloss demands? Where would Zhixu's metaphysical formulation — "the present single thought of my mind is neither a lump of flesh nor a shadow of sense-objects; vertically without beginning or end, horizontally without edge or limit" — find its seating? If name-holding were truly *mindless*, then the two complete late-Ming commentarial traditions would both be misreadings.

The reverse is the case. T0366's minimalist surface conceals three layers of provision-saturation.

**First layer: Zhuhong's philology of "holding."** "*Zhí* = gripping with resolute courage, not to be shaken; *chí* = preserving in abiding fidelity, not to be forgotten": these six mind-actions (*resolute* + *courage* + *not-to-be-shaken* + *abiding* + *fidelity* + *not-to-be-forgotten*) are the psychological state required by a single utterance of the name. The catching-hold must be decisive; the keeping-hold must be steadfast. "Not to be shaken" means refusing mid-course displacement; "not to be forgotten" means the sustained guarding of the very action itself.[^21] Holding the name is not the summoning of an external object; it is the simultaneous execution of a highly disciplined internal action. Zhuhong further unfolds it into the bidirectional turning-toward and turning-back-to-source — the outward turning-toward of Amitābha (phenomenal holding) and the inward turning-back-to-source of one's own mind (principial holding) — within a single utterance of the name, outward action and inward action are accomplished simultaneously.

**Second layer: Zhixu's six faiths.** The six faiths are not six sequential stages but six cognitive preconditions simultaneously required by a single utterance of the name.[^22] **Faith in self** (the present single thought is equipped with the cause of the Land of Bliss) + **faith in other** (Amitābha's vow-power and the words-of-truth of all buddhas) + **faith in cause** (even dispersed name-invocation is a seed of buddhahood) + **faith in effect** (buddha-recollection-*samādhi* necessarily evokes the assembly of the superior-good) + **faith in phenomenon** (the Land of Bliss truly exists, unlike the parables of Zhuangzi) + **faith in principle** (the Land of Bliss does not lie outside the single fine-particle thought of one's present mind) — when all six faiths are in place, the specific content of "good roots, merits, and causal conditions" actually descends into the concrete. Zhixu's move thereby restores the sūtra's phrase "one cannot be reborn in that land with but small good roots, merits, and causal conditions" from a vague clause about blessing-accumulation back into six layers of concrete cognitive requirement.

**Third layer: faith, vow, and practice are "not sequenced and are not rigidly three."** Zhixu's declaration is the structural pivot of this entire layer — "now, since name-holding rests entirely upon faith and vow, every single utterance already possesses faith, vow, and practice in their fullness."[^23] This is the most crucial structural claim beneath the minimalist surface: a single utterance of "*Namo Amitābha Buddha*" is temporally an extremely brief action, but structurally it is the *simultaneous fullness* of faith + vow + practice. The three provisions are not three stages of the path (first faith, then vow, then practice), but three aspects of a single utterance of the name existing simultaneously — faith without vow and practice is not true faith; vow without faith and practice is not true vow; practice without faith and vow is not true practice. Beneath the minimalist surface of "one utterance of the name," provision-density has therefore saturated to its maximum — no item can any longer be added or removed.

The three layers combined: Zhuhong's philology of holding gives the mind-action structure required within a single utterance of the name (the double action of grasping + holding); Zhixu's six faiths give the cognitive preconditions (six layers simultaneously in place); Zhixu's faith-vow-practice gives the topology of the provisions (three provisions simultaneously in fullness). **Minimalism is the surface; saturation is the depth** — the surface contracts to the extreme; the depth saturates to the maximum. This is not "less = shallower"; it is "less = denser."

### 3.3 Three Sūtras in Parallel: The Name-Layer as the Third Access Channel

The relation of T0366 to T0360 and T0365 is the decisive verdict that closes Part IV.

**Sealing the compression hypothesis.** The chronological hard evidence of §2.2 is conclusive: T0366 (402 CE) precedes T0365 (424–442 CE) by twenty to forty years, and precedes T0360 (fourth–fifth century actual completion) by more than a generation. If T0366 were a compression of the first two sūtras, then the compressed object could not precede — in Chinese translation — the objects it is said to compress. The chronological break severs the compression hypothesis at the root.

**The correct framing: three parallel channels.** Each of the three sūtras distills one type of entry-action from the Indian Pure Land thought-space:

- **T0360 vow-layer**: taking the forty-eight vows of Dharmākara Bodhisattva in the causal stage of Amitābha Buddha as architecture, distilling the causal-and-fruitional structure by which vow-power generates the Pure Land; P09 has argued for its container-world / sentient-world dual-axis design, with Vow Eighteen's "ten recollections at least" as its floor.[^24]
- **T0365 contemplation-layer**: taking Vaidehī's petition as narrative hook, distilling the cognitive-visual ladder of the sixteen contemplations; P10 has argued for its designed cognitive-visual architecture, with the "outer → inner → grade-of-rebirth" three-axis geometry as its skeleton and the lower-grade lower-birth's ten-recollection name-invocation as its floor.[^25]
- **T0366 name-layer**: taking Śākyamuni's *unsolicited* self-teaching as its form, distilling the single-action channel of name-holding; the present paper has argued for its designed minimalist architecture, with the literary distillation of "holding the name" as its signature and the saturation of provisions within a single utterance of the name as its content.

The three channels are parallel, complementary, non-subordinate. Each channel provides, independently and completely, an entry from ordinary-being into the Land of Bliss without requiring the other two to be completed — this is the strict meaning of "parallel." Zhuhong's sixteenth-century late-Ming declaration "contemplation *is* recollection, recollection *is* contemplation" and the fifth-century chronological fact that T0366 precedes T0365 together form evidence at two layers: T0365 contemplation and T0366 name-holding are already parallel traditions at the Indian source-flow, and are again formally declared parallel by Zhuhong's exegetical authority in the late Ming.[^26]

**The externalization of the floor-structure.** P09 argued that Vow Eighteen is the floor-node within the forty-eight vows; P10 argued that the lower-grade lower-birth's ten-recollection name-invocation is the floor-node within the sixteen contemplations — **in both cases, the floor is located inside the architecture**. T0366 then pushes the floor-design logic up to the level of *structure itself*: the entire T0366, as the minimalist channel of the Three Pure Land Sūtras, takes holding the name as its single entry-point, a single utterance of the name as its smallest action, and seven days as its definite time-frame — **the floor is externalized from "a node within a structure" to "a sūtra that is itself an entire structure."** This externalization is not compression (already sealed) but another architectural act: embodying the floor-design independently as the overall form of a whole sūtra.

The complete architectural picture of the Three Pure Land Sūtras is therefore as follows:

```
The Three Pure Land Sūtras Architecture
    │
    ├── T0360 Vow-Layer: architecture of the causal stage / internal floor = Vow 18
    ├── T0365 Contemplation-Layer: architecture of cognitive-visual access / internal floor = ten-recollection name-invocation in the lower-grade lower-birth
    └── T0366 Name-Layer: architecture of externalized minimalism / the sūtra *is* the floor
```

The three sūtras in parallel are not a linear chain, not a hierarchical tree, but a *network* jointly constituted by three parallel channels — this is the real architecture of the Three Pure Land Sūtras. Part IV closes here.

## IV. Zhuhong ↔ Zhixu: The Late-Ming Commentators in Parallel

§IV of P10 has already turned from Part III's Western interlocutors (Thompson / Kripke-Lewis / Austin-Searle / Alexander-Simon) back to an intra-Buddhist commentator (Zhiyi). P11 continues along that route, taking Yunqi Zhuhong's *Commentary and Notes on the Amitābha Sūtra* (X0424, 1595) and Ouyi Zhixu's *Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra* (T1762, 1647) as two parallel late-Ming commentarial perspectives. This choice is not merely a strategy for avoiding English-drift — more importantly, it is a *structural echo*: the parallelism of the three sūtras (at the textual layer) + the parallelism of the two commentators (at the exegetical layer) = a structurally isomorphic double parallel, which directly corroborates the main argument of §3.3.

### 4.1 The Historical Independence of the Two Commentators

Zhuhong's *Commentary and Notes* and Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* stand fifty-two years apart (1595 / 1647) and do *not* constitute a teacher-to-disciple transmission. In the preface to the *Essential Explanation*, Zhixu states explicitly:

> "I dare not contend in difference with the two elders (Yunqi and Youxi), nor dare I force myself into agreement with the two elders." (translation mine)[^27]

"The two elders" are Yunqi Zhuhong and Youxi Chuandeng (1554–1628) — Zhixu neither deliberately takes a contrary position nor forces himself into conformity. A CBETA-level verification shows that the main text of T1762 **does not cite verbatim** any passage from Zhuhong's *Commentary and Notes*; Zhixu's conceptual system (six faiths + faith-vow-practice + phenomenal-holding / principial-holding) and Zhuhong's (philological exegesis of "holding" + phenomenal-one-mindedness / principial-one-mindedness) display evident differences.[^28] The two are the twin peaks of parallel late-Ming Pure Land authorship on T0366, not a single-line transmission.

### 4.2 Three Irreducible Differences

Zhuhong's and Zhixu's readings of T0366 are irreducible along three specific axes.

**First, the relative rank of name-holding and visualization.**

Zhuhong: "Contemplation *is* recollection; recollection *is* contemplation."[^29] — T0365 contemplation and T0366 name-holding are **parallel**, with no superior-inferior verdict.

Zhixu: "Name-holding alone — without borrowing the expedients of *dhyāna*-contemplation" (from the third item of the "five-fold inconceivability") — and in the "hard-among-the-hard five-layer graduation," he states explicitly that name-holding is "singularly marvelous, surpassing what can be conceived, the first expedient — and therefore the hardest among the hard." Thus: **name-holding > visualization**.[^30]

The two verdicts cannot be reconciled: Zhuhong places name-holding and visualization on horizontal parity; Zhixu elevates name-holding *above* visualization. This is not two formulations of a single position — it is two irreducible verdicts on the structural relation between T0365 and T0366.

**Second, the number of layers in the phenomenal-principial architecture.**

Zhuhong: phenomenal-holding / principial-holding + phenomenal-one-mindedness / principial-one-mindedness — **two layers in a double division**. Name-holding admits the distinction phenomenal / principial; what is obtained admits the parallel distinction phenomenal-one-mindedness / principial-one-mindedness; the two pairs correspond but do not cross, forming a clean 2 × 2 structure.[^31]

Zhixu: six faiths + phenomenal-holding / principial-holding + phenomenal-one-mindedness / principial-one-mindedness + faith-vow-practice — **three nested layers**. The six faiths' *faith-in-phenomenon* and *faith-in-principle* precede name-holding's phenomenal-holding / principial-holding, and both are brought to fruition in the one-mind-undistracted's phenomenal-one-mindedness / principial-one-mindedness; all three layers operate within the simultaneous completeness of the three provisions of faith, vow, and practice.[^32]

Zhuhong's architecture is clean and compact; Zhixu's is rich and multilayered. This is not a difference of detail versus summary — it is a different verdict on the structural granularity of T0366's interior: Zhuhong holds that two layers suffice; Zhixu holds that no fewer than three layers can exhaust it.

**Third, the substance of the commentarial axis.** Zhuhong's axis is a *technique-oriented* exegesis — applying philological analysis to the verb "holding" and partitioning name-holding's two methodologies through phenomenal-holding / principial-holding; this is the reading of a Chan practitioner grounding his own meditative experience in a written commentary. Zhixu's axis is a *provision-oriented* exegesis — filling in "good roots, merits, and causal conditions" with the six faiths, and arguing for "a single utterance of the name already containing the three provisions in full" via faith-vow-practice; this is the reading of an intra-Buddhist scholar framed by Tiantai's doctrinal classification and centered on the architecture of the faith-mind. Technique versus provision are not opposed — they are two different entry-points into the same text: one concerned with "how to do it," the other with "what has already been done by doing it."

### 4.3 Structural Echo: A Double Parallel

Two reflections close §IV.

**First, the irreducible differences between the two commentators are not chaotic interpretive pluralism.** Zhuhong and Zhixu both share the integrated late-Ming context of Chan-Pure Land dual cultivation; both use Tiantai's five-fold profound meaning as their formal tool (Zhuhong's ten gates condensed into five; Zhixu directly using the five); and both acknowledge T0366 as perfect-and-sudden (Zhuhong's verdict: "the sudden teaching partly subsumed by the perfect teaching"; Zhixu's verdict: "the most extreme of perfect-and-sudden among perfect-and-sudden teachings"). The difference lies at the finer layer of structural verdict, not at the level of broad orientation.

**Second, the double isomorphism of three-sūtra parallelism + two-commentator parallelism.** §3.3 has argued that the three sūtras (T0360 vow-layer / T0365 contemplation-layer / T0366 name-layer) are parallel independent transmissions, not a linear derivation chain; §4.1–4.2 have argued that Zhuhong and Zhixu are parallel independent authorities, not a teacher-to-disciple transmission. **The textual-layer parallel × the exegetical-layer parallel = a structurally isomorphic double parallel.** This double isomorphism is not a coincidence; it is the structural consequence of T0366's own minimalist design inviting multi-channel exegesis — a sūtra whose surface does *not* pre-lock an exegetical pathway can therefore house the two so-divergent yet equally complete late-Ming readings. The minimalism of the surface is precisely what permits the saturation of the depth to unfold simultaneously along multiple routes.

## V. Middle-Way Correction

### ❌ Misreading 1: Holding the Name = Mindless Recitation

The popular reading — especially prominent in the contemporary Western mindfulness industry and self-affirmation practice contexts — identifies T0366's "holding the name" with simple repetitive chanting and equates it with "mantra recitation" or "self-affirmation." This reading presupposes that a more repeated action is a simpler action, with the consequence that holding the name becomes "the verbal action of lowest cognitive load."

Correction: Zhuhong's *Commentary and Notes*, Fascicle 3, establishes the philological gloss explicitly — "*zhí*: with resolute courage, not to be shaken; *chí*: in abiding fidelity, not to be forgotten."[^33] Holding the name is an action whose execution *simultaneously* requires **six mind-actions** (resolute + courage + not-to-be-shaken + abiding + fidelity + not-to-be-forgotten), not mechanical repetition. Catching-hold must be decisive and resolute; keeping-hold must be steadfast and unforgotten — at any moment, if any one of these six loosens, "holding" has already been broken. To read "holding" as mindless repetition is to misread a highly disciplined mental action as a mere vocal action.

### ❌ Misreading 2: T0366 = Retrospective Compression of T0360 + T0365

The popular reading — including parts of the vernacular homiletic tradition within Chinese Pure Land circles and the introductory literature of Western Pure Land studies — presupposes that T0366's minimalism is a reactive compression issued after T0360's vow-architecture and T0365's contemplation-architecture were already in place: because the first two sūtras are too elaborate, too difficult, and too long, a third brief-and-easy T0366 was produced as "the easy-version."

Correction: §2.2 has established the philological chronology — T0366 (Kumārajīva, 402 CE) precedes T0365 (Kālayaśas, 424–442 CE) by twenty to forty years, and precedes T0360 (actual textual completion in the fourth–fifth century) by more than a generation. The compressed object cannot precede — in Chinese translation — the objects it is said to compress. The compression hypothesis is chronologically sealed. T0366's minimalism is its intrinsic character, originating in Kumārajīva's translation-workshop distillation of an early six-direction Sanskrit recension (§3.1), not a late compression. The three sūtras are three parallel independent transmissions constituting three access channels (§3.3), not a single-line derivation chain.

### ❌ Misreading 3: T0366 Is Shallow and Suitable for Unsupervised Self-Study by Beginners

The popular reading — often found in contemporary Chinese introductory literature and English-language popularizations — positions T0366 as "the easiest of the three to enter, suitable as a self-study introduction for beginners," and recommends that beginners first read this sūtra before gradually moving on to T0360 / T0365.

Correction: Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* establishes explicitly the "hard-among-the-hard five-layer graduation":[^34] difficulty of being born as a human → difficulty of hearing the Buddha-Dharma → difficulty of hearing the Pure Land dharma-gate → difficulty of hearing the name-holding dharma-gate → **difficulty of believing in this dharma-gate**. T0366's "difficulty" lies not in the complexity of its content but in the cognitive requirements of **faith** — if any one of the six faiths is not in place, the structural precondition of "holding the name" does not obtain. Beneath dispersed thinking, the action can continue even while the six faiths may all be missing; "apparently simple" name-holding in fact demands the subtlest faith-roots. T0366 is not "shallow" — it is a sūtra whose surface is simple but whose depth is subtle and intricate. Beginners without exegetical companionship and without a complete faith-root architecture will find it extremely difficult to penetrate the core design of this sūtra on their own.

### ❌ Misreading 4: Yinguang's "Even if an Ancient Buddha Returned He Could Not Surpass This" Praise Is from T1762's Main Text

The popular practice — including parts of contemporary academic citation of Zhixu's *Essential Explanation* — attributes Master Yinguang's famous praise "even if an ancient Buddha were to return, he could not surpass this" to the main text of T1762, using it to reinforce the authority of Zhixu's work.

Correction: Direct verification of the CBETA electronic edition of T1762 XML[^35] shows that the main text contains no preface or colophon by Yinguang — the only auxiliary texts included in the XML are Zhixu's own colophon (T37.0374b24–0374c11) + the disciple Zhengzhi's "Postface to the Printing of the Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra" (T37.0374c12–0375a02) + Shen Chunshe's printing-donation merit-transfer text. Yinguang's "ancient Buddha returning" praise is in fact from *Master Yinguang's Collected Writings*, Third Compilation, Fascicle 3, "Preface to the Reprinting of the Essential Explanation of the Amitābha Sūtra" (early Republican era), and **is not part of T1762's main text**. Any citation of this praise must carry the attribution *Master Yinguang's Collected Writings*; it cannot be phrased as "Yinguang's praise in T1762 says…." This philological clarification is structurally isomorphic with P10's §V alert "'Zhiyi's four kinds of buddha-recollection' is not T1750's original terminology" — the maintenance of philological rigor is a basic discipline that Part IV carries forward into Part V.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Summary

The *Amitābha Sūtra* (T0366)'s minimalism is a design signature, not thin content. Kumārajīva's 402 CE translation distilled the three-phase Sanskrit action *śrutvā manasikaroti* of the early six-direction recension of the *Smaller Sukhāvatīvyūha* into the single compound verb "holding the name," and distilled *avikṣipta-citta* into the short phrase "one mind, undistracted" — this is a literary distillation, not a doctrinal compression. The chronology — T0366 precedes T0365 by twenty to forty years and precedes T0360's actual textual completion by more than a generation — seals the retrospective-compression hypothesis; the three sūtras stand as three parallel independent channels (vow-layer / contemplation-layer / name-layer), jointly constituting the complete architecture of the Three Pure Land Sūtras. The provision-density beneath T0366's minimalist surface — Zhuhong's six mind-actions in the philology of holding, Zhixu's six faiths, and Zhixu's "faith, vow, and practice are not sequenced and are not rigidly three" — proves that minimalism is not mindlessness: the surface contracts to the extreme, the depth saturates to the maximum.

### 6.2 Practical Guidance

The architecture has two concrete implications for practitioners.

**First, holding is not emptying out.** A single utterance of "*Namo Amitābha Buddha*," if chanted according to the complete structure of Zhuhong's philology and Zhixu's six faiths, requires a density of mind-action no less than the visualization-detail of the sixteen contemplations — only, this density is internalized into the in-body tension of a single action, rather than externalized as sequential steps. The reading of "holding" popular in Western mindfulness-industry contexts — "empty the mind, let thoughts come and go" — is the *reverse* of T0366's original design: holding *demands* resolute decisiveness and steadfast fidelity; it *demands* non-displacement and non-forgetting; it *demands* the simultaneous presence of the six faiths. A single utterance of the name is *not* an expedient for temporarily pausing thought — it is the simultaneous ignition of six cognitive commitments.

**Second, provisions saturate with the utterance.** "Faith, vow, and practice are not sequenced and are not rigidly three" implies that the practitioner does not need "first to establish faith, then to arouse the vow, and only then to begin practice" — within a single utterance of the name, the three provisions are simultaneously in full presence. This releases the self-blockage of "I'm not ready yet; I'll start chanting once faith and vow are firm." The threshold of name-holding is low, but the internal structural requirement of name-holding is precise — low threshold and high density are simultaneously constituted within T0366.

### 6.3 Open Question: Toward P12

T0366's name-layer channel closes Part IV with "holding the name, one mind, undistracted." Entering Part V's eight Mahāyāna sūtras, the first is T0945, the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, with its Fascicle Five "Chapter on the Perfect Penetration of Buddha-Recollection" by Mahāsthāmaprāpta Bodhisattva:

> "Gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted — attaining *samādhi* — this is foremost."

From T0366's "holding the name, one mind, undistracted" to T0945's "gathering in the six sense-roots, with pure recollection uninterrupted," the verb shifts from "holding" to "gathering in," the object expands from "the name" to "the six sense-roots," and the criterion deepens from "one mind" to "pure recollection." Is this another literary distillation, or a shift at the architectural level? When the name-layer channel enters the internal deepening of the eight Mahāyāna sūtras, does it alter the three-sūtra parallel configuration established in Part IV, or instead confirm its structural elasticity? P12 awaits.
