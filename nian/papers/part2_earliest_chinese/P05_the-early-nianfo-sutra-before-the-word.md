# The Early *Niànfó* Sūtra Before the Word

**The *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* and the Origins of Chinese Recollection Vocabulary**

## Abstract

The first Buddhist scripture to enter China—the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* (四十二章經, T0784), traditionally ascribed to Kāśyapa Mātaṅga (迦葉摩騰) and Dharmaratna (竺法蘭) and translated at the White Horse Monastery in Luoyang in 67 CE—does not contain the compound term *niànfó* (念佛, "buddhānussati") even once across its forty-two chapters. Yet in the same scripture we find *niàndào* (念道, recollection of the path) three times, *niàn-fēicháng* (念非常, recollection of impermanence) three times, recollection of the four great elements within the body once (a *kāyagatāsati* echo), mental recollection of the precepts once (a *sīlānussati* echo), and "between breaths" once (a double echo of both *ānāpāna-sati* and *maraṇa-sati*). The vocabulary distribution thus covers five of the six standard recollection objects—missing only *buddhānussati* itself.

This paper argues that the critical absence is not evidence of early Chinese disinterest in recollection of the Buddha, but a phase of vocabulary history: the compound term had not yet been coined in the Chinese translation workshops, while its functional vocabulary was already being carried by the *niàndào* cluster. The paper further argues that T0784, lacking even a single occurrence of *chándìng* (禪定, meditative absorption), *sānmèi* (三昧, samādhi), *zhèngdìng* (正定, right concentration), *chánnà* (禪那, dhyāna), or any element of the *jhāna* pentad, sits entirely within the *upacāra*-platform register established in NIAN P04 §3.2—specifically, on what we there call its third track: ethical reminder, long-term continuity, middle-way adjustment, gradual deepening.

Read against the contemporaneous translation T0602 *Fó shuō Dà Ān-bān shǒu-yì Jīng* (佛說大安般守意經) by An Shigao (安世高, ~148–170 CE)—a technical *samādhi* text built around the six modes of breath-work (counting, following, fixing, observing, returning, purifying)—we see two registers of recollection coexisting in second-century Luoyang: ethical (T0784) and technical (T0602). This dual-track origin is the historical possibility-condition for the later three-way split of Chinese recollection traditions—name-recitation (*chímíng* 持名), image-contemplation (*guānxiàng* 觀像), and reality-recollection (*shíxiàng* 實相).

The paper is simultaneously the historical confirmation of P03's "one engine, multiple objects" framework: the engine is stable, while the distribution of objects is culturally and historically determined.

**Keywords:** *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*, T0784, An Shigao, *Foshuo Da Anban Shouyi Jing*, T0602, *buddhānussati*, *niàndào*, Chinese reception, vocabulary history, dual-track origins

## I. Introduction

When a contemporary Western Buddhist reader encounters the word *niànfó* (念佛), what usually comes to mind is the rhythmic chanting of *Nāmó Ēmítuófó* (南無阿彌陀佛, "Homage to Amitābha Buddha") that rises and falls in the dining halls of Chinese monasteries. For the more curious reader, the historical thread behind this practice is usually traced back to three sūtras—the *Longer Sukhāvatīvyūha*, the *Contemplation Sūtra*, and the *Shorter Sukhāvatīvyūha* (the so-called "Three Pure Land Sūtras")—and the origins of Chinese *niànfó* are, as a matter of course, located in the Chinese reception history of these three texts after their translation.

This genealogical direction is not wrong, but it skips over an important earlier chapter: before the Pure Land sūtras were fully translated, how did the earliest Buddhist scriptures to enter China treat the practice of *niàn* (念, recollection / mindful holding-in-mind)? Did they contain the word *niànfó*? If yes, how was it used? If no, what was the object of *niàn*?

This paper addresses the earliest single datapoint of that earlier chapter: the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* (T0784), translated by Kāśyapa Mātaṅga and Dharmaratna at the White Horse Monastery in Luoyang, in the tenth year of the Yongping era of the Later Han (67 CE).

Two facts about this sūtra are worth fixing in mind. First, by traditional account, it is the first Buddhist scripture ever translated into Chinese—the first set of the Buddha's teachings that a Chinese reader ever encountered in Chinese. Second, and this is what this paper will demonstrate: **in this earliest of Chinese Buddhist scriptures, the two characters *niànfó* do not appear**.

Not "are unimportant," not "appear only briefly"—but rather: **across the entire forty-two chapters plus the prefatory narrative, the compound term *niànfó* occurs zero times**.

This paper argues: T0784 is indeed one of the sources of Chinese *niànfó* tradition, but in a particular way—**the word has not yet been coined, while its function is already in place**. Vocabulary for several *anussati* objects is already formed in this sūtra (*niàndào*, recollection of the body, recollection of the precepts, recollection of the breath, recollection of death, recollection of impermanence); only *niànfó* itself is absent. This is not a reflection of doctrinal priorities, but a phase phenomenon in vocabulary history: in first-century Luoyang, the translation workshops had not yet settled on how to combine *niàn* (a native Chinese verb) with *fó* (佛, an imported transliteration of "Buddha").

NIAN P04 has already established a key technical coordinate for this series—the boundary between the *upacāra* (近行定, access-concentration) platform and *appanā* (安止定, absorption-concentration), and the judgment that *anussati*-type practices terminate at *upacāra* not as a failure to reach *appanā* but by design. The present paper extends this coordinate back to the earliest Chinese reception: T0784 sits not merely within the *upacāra* platform, but—to put it precisely—its register is **one discursive layer earlier than even *upacāra***. The sūtra simply does not operate inside the discourse of "concentration" at all. It inhabits what P04 §3.2 called the **third track** on the *upacāra* platform: grounded in ethical reminder, long-term continuity, middle-way adjustment, and gradual deepening. The mainstream register of later Chinese *niànfó* extends along this very track, unbroken.

The second thesis is equally critical: T0784 does not stand alone. Within roughly the same period (148–170 CE), another important translator in Luoyang—An Shigao (安世高)— produced the *Fó shuō Dà Ān-bān shǒu-yì Jīng* (佛說大安般守意經, T0602), a text whose register is entirely different: T0602 is built end-to-end around the technical details of breath, setting out a complete operational system of the six modes of *ānāpāna-sati*—counting, following, fixing, observing, returning, purifying—and constitutes the earliest technical document on meditative cultivation in China. Chinese recollection-practice thus began, from the very first generation of translators, with a **dual-track coexistence**: the ethical register of the T0784 lineage and the technical register of the T0602 lineage. This dual structure is the historical possibility-condition for the later three-way branching of Chinese *niànfó* into name-recitation, image-contemplation, and reality-recollection.[^1]

## II. Canonical Sources

### 2.1 Primary Source · *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*, One Fascicle (T0784)

The primary source for this paper is T0784, traditionally attributed to the joint translation of Kāśyapa Mātaṅga and Dharmaratna in the tenth year of the Yongping era of the Later Han (67 CE), catalogued in the *Taishō Shinshū Daizōkyō* (大正新脩大藏經) vol. 17, no. 784. The text runs to 3,177 CJK characters and is traditionally divided into forty-two chapters plus a prefatory narrative (Emperor Ming's dream, and the sending of envoys to the Western Regions to obtain the scripture).[^1]

The structure of T0784 is not that of a continuous treatise but of forty-two independent short "Buddha said" (*Fó yán* 佛言) utterances—one instruction per chapter, covering monastic discipline, the ten virtuous acts, giving, patience, recollection-practice, mental defilements, the countering of craving, and gradual cultivation of the path. This format is close to the aphoristic style of the Pāli *Dhammapada* and *Udāna*, and starkly unlike the long-scroll discursive treatises of later Mahāyāna.

For the three vocabulary-identification tasks of this paper:

**First, the two characters *niànfó* 念佛**: full-text grep confirms zero occurrences.[^2]

**Second, *niàn* + object compound expressions:**

- *Niàndào* 念道, three occurrences: Chapter 14, "What do I recollect? I recollect the path. What do I practice? I practice the path. What do I speak? I speak the path. I recollect the true path, not neglecting it for even a *xūyú* (須臾, kṣaṇa / moment)"; Chapter 16, "Practicing for a single day, constantly recollecting the path and practicing the path, one thereby attains the faculty of faith (*xìngēn* 信根, *saddhindriya*), and one's merit is immeasurable"; Chapter 40, "An upright mind recollecting the path can be freed from all sufferings."[^3]

- *Niàn-fēicháng* 念非常, three occurrences (in a single parallelistic structure): Chapter 15, "Gazing upon heaven and earth, recollect impermanence; gazing upon mountains and rivers, recollect impermanence; gazing upon the abundant and flourishing forms of the ten thousand things, recollect impermanence. Holding the mind in this way, one attains the path swiftly."[^4]

- Recollection of the four great elements within the body, one occurrence: Chapter 17, "Carefully recollect the four great elements within your own body: names are merely names, all [ultimately] nothing; the 'I' and 'self' are lodged as borrowed life, and even that life does not last long—the whole matter is but an illusion."[^5]

- Mental recollection of the precepts (*yì-niàn wú jiè* 意念吾戒), one occurrence: Chapter 37, "Should a disciple depart and be thousands of *lǐ* away from me, yet mentally recollects my precepts, he will surely attain the path. Were he at my left side but with his mind turned toward the crooked, he would never attain the path."[^6]

- "Between breaths" (*hūxī zhī jiān* 呼吸之間), one occurrence: Chapter 36, "'In what span does human life reside?' — 'In the space of a single breath.' — 'Excellent! You may indeed be called one who cultivates the path.'"[^7]

**Third, vocabulary related to meditative absorption**: *chándìng* 禪定, *sānmèi* 三昧, *zhèngdìng* 正定, *chánnà* 禪那, *dìng* 定 (in the technical meditative sense), *xún* 尋 (*vitakka*), *sì* 伺 (*vicāra*), *xǐ* 喜 (*pīti*), *lè* 樂 (*sukha*), *yīxīn* 一心 (*ekaggatā*)— full-text grep confirms **zero occurrences for all of these**.

The two critical absences—*niànfó* and the entire *jhāna* vocabulary—constitute the hard factual basis of this paper's argument.

### 2.2 Comparative Source · An Shigao, *Fó shuō Dà Ān-bān shǒu-yì Jīng* (T0602)

The comparative source for this paper is T0602, translated by An Shigao between the second year of the Jianhe era and the third year of the Jianning era of the Later Han (148–170 CE), catalogued in *Taishō* vol. 15, no. 602, divided into upper and lower fascicles and running to approximately 12,000 characters.[^8]

T0602 belongs to the same earliest layer of Chinese translation as T0784, but the register diverges sharply:

| Dimension | T0784 *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* | T0602 *Ānāpānasati Sūtra* |
|---|---|---|
| Primary object | path, four elements, precepts, impermanence (plural) | breath (singular) |
| Technical density | very low (no meditative-absorption vocabulary) | high (six modes: count, follow, fix, observe, return, purify) |
| Register | ethical-reminder | technical-meditative |
| Character count | 3,177 CJK | ~12,000 CJK |
| Form of recollection-object | implicit plural recollection (no compound *niànfó*) | explicit *ānāpāna-sati* |

T0602 is the earliest Chinese text to present, in nascent form, the full operational architecture of the Pāli sixteen-step *ānāpāna-sati*—its "six modes" (count, follow, fix, observe, return, purify) correspond to the early reorganization and Chinese reception of the sixteen Pāli stages. This paper does not cite T0602 directly; it only paraphrases the overall contrast in register. The technical details are deferred to NIAN P06 and to the two fascicles DĀNA / DHYĀNA.[^9]

### 2.3 Inheritance of Pāli Baselines

The Pāli baselines for this paper are inherited from the three sets already established in P02–P04: AN 6.10 *Mahānāma Sutta* (the framework of the six recollections)[^10]; AN 11.11–12 (recollection in the four postures—walking, standing, sitting, lying)[^11]; and *Visuddhimagga* VII.1–7 (the six-recollection treatise-commentary, maintaining the paraphrase discipline)[^12]. This paper does not re-expound these baselines; it invokes P02–P04's already-established judgments directly when argumentation requires.

### 2.4 Dual-Track Citation Discipline

The NIAN series' established principle of "Chinese translation + Pāli dual-track citation" continues to apply. But the primary source of this paper, T0784, has no direct Pāli original correspondence—it is a selected anthology compiled by the earliest Chinese translation workshops from Central Asian intermediate sources (possibly Gāndhārī, Kuṣāṇa-era hybrid Sanskrit, or some Central Asian oral transmission), and Pāli correspondences can only be sought passage by passage in the nearest *Nikāya* parallels (some chapters correspond to single *suttas* in *Aṅguttara-* or *Saṃyutta Nikāya*, others are integration passages drawing on multiple doctrinal sources). This paper does not force a Pāli parallel for every chapter; it points out Pāli echoes only where argumentation requires (for example, Chapter 14's "recollecting the true path" corresponds to a partial function of *dhammānussati*).[^13]

## III. Core Argument

The core argument is organized into four sections under four pillars: §3.1 treats the critical absence of *niànfó* and close-reads the three occurrences of *niàndào*; §3.2 proposes the register-placement of T0784 on the **third track** of P04's *upacāra* platform; §3.3 develops the dual-track divergence of T0784 and T0602, establishing the thesis of two source-streams for the Chinese *niànfó* tradition; §3.4 uses the distribution of six-recollection-object vocabulary in T0784 to provide historical confirmation of P03's "one engine, multiple objects" frame.

### 3.1 The Critical Absence of *Niànfó* and a Close Reading of the Three *Niàndào* Passages

**The first fact**: Across all forty-two chapters plus prefatory narrative of T0784, the compound term *niànfó* occurs zero times.

A modern analogy may help the reader grasp what this absence weighs. The word "internet" entered common usage only in the mid-to-late 1990s, yet what it refers to—multiple independent computer networks interconnected to exchange data—was already operating in the ARPANET project of the 1970s. Similarly, the phrase "viral marketing" was not widely adopted until after the Hotmail case of 1996, though the phenomenon itself—word-of-mouth exponential diffusion—appears in commercial history considerably earlier. A concept can be spoken of repeatedly, through adjacent vocabulary, circumlocution, or functional echo, long before its standard name is coined. The word *niànfó* had not yet been fashioned in first-century Luoyang, but this does not mean the function of *anussati* was absent there.

**The second fact**: The three occurrences of *niàndào* in T0784 constitute the proto-vocabulary of *anussati* in China.

Chapter 14 is the most direct passage: "What do I recollect? I recollect the path. What do I practice? I practice the path. What do I speak? I speak the path. I recollect the true path, not neglecting it for even a *kṣaṇa*." The syntactic structure here repays attention: three parallel verb-object pairs, *niàn-dào* / *xíng-dào* / *yán-dào* (念道／行道／言道)— "recollecting / practicing / speaking the path"—are closed by *niàn dì-dào* 念諦道 "recollecting the true path," and finally sealed by the functional marker *bù hū xūyú* 不忽須臾, "not neglecting it for a moment"—that is, continuity, unbrokenness. This is not casual rhetorical repetition. It states one thing: *anussati* is not an isolated act of "recollection," but a continuous orientation integrating three modes of action—thought, conduct, speech—whose internal core is "recollecting the true path."

*Bù hū xūyú* is especially critical. One of the core functional dimensions of *anussarati* in the Pāli literature is precisely continuity—not a single concentrated session of visualization, but ongoing remembrance that can be carried through walking, standing, sitting, and lying. AN 11.11's core instruction to Mahānāma makes exactly this point: the disciple can cultivate recollection in any activity—walking, staying, sitting, lying, working, at home with children. The four characters *bù hū xūyú* in T0784 Chapter 14 are the earliest Chinese record capturing this continuity dimension.

Chapter 16 links this structure to a more technical element: "Practicing for a single day, constantly recollecting the path and practicing the path, one attains the faculty of faith, and one's merit is immeasurable." *Cháng niàn dào* 常念道 ("constantly recollecting the path") extends the continuity theme of Chapter 14; *xìngēn* 信根 (*saddhindriya*) is the first of the five Pāli faculties (*saddhā*, *viriya*, *sati*, *samādhi*, *paññā*)—this is the earliest Chinese linkage of *anussati* to the five-faculty doctrinal framework.

Chapter 40, "with an upright mind recollecting the path, one can be freed from all sufferings," appears at the close of the ox-and-mud-burden simile, as the functional summation of the release-from-suffering argument. The two characters *zhíxīn* 直心 ("upright mind") were a nativizing coinage by the translation workshop to express "a mind that is neither crooked nor scattered." Later schools—Chan, Pure Land, and various Mahāyāna lineages—each appropriated the term to carry different ontological weights, but its origin lies in exactly this kind of early translation attempt to fashion a native vocabulary for "the quality of mind in recollection-practice."

**The third fact** (derived from the first two): **The earliest *anussati* object received in China was *dào* 道, not *fó* 佛.**

This is the single most important claim about T0784. Facing the foreign practice of *anussati*, the Chinese translation workshop had to select a native, intelligible object-word. The Pāli tradition offered at least six options (Buddha, Dharma, Saṅgha, precepts, giving, deities, supplemented later by body, breath, death, peace); but T0784's translated vocabulary is concentrated on *dào*.

The plausible inference is this: *dào* was a term already familiar to the Chinese literati class (used in both Daoist and Confucian discourse); *fó* was a newly imported transliteration. The compound expression *niàn-fó* requires a longer interval before it can settle naturally into the rhythms of classical Chinese. The translation workshop chose *niàndào* as the path of least resistance: readers could understand a new practice within an existing vocabulary frame. This choice had two long-range consequences. First, it allowed the ethical-philosophical register of early Chinese recollection-practice to form naturally. Second, it reserved a structural slot for the eventual coinage of *niànfó*: once *fó* had become sufficiently familiar, *niàn-fó* could take over or supplement the position held by *niàn-dào*, without requiring the reinvention of the whole *anussati* architecture.

### 3.2 T0784 on the Third Track of P04's *Upacāra* Platform: The Register Judgment

P04 §3.2 judged the meditative depth of *anussati*-type practices to be "the *upacāra* platform of objects-with-specific-character"—a technical boundary that Buddhaghosa draws at the end of *Visuddhimagga* VII on two grounds: "the depth of the Buddha's qualities" and "the mind's dispersal among the variety of recollected virtues." This section extends that technical coordinate back to the earliest Chinese reception: T0784 sits not merely within the *upacāra* platform but, to be precise, at a register **one discursive layer earlier than even *upacāra***—it simply does not inhabit the discourse of "concentration."

**The first piece of register evidence**: In the full text of T0784, the terms *chándìng* 禪定, *sānmèi* 三昧, *zhèngdìng* 正定, *chánnà* 禪那, and *dìng* 定 (in the technical meditative sense) occur zero times.

**The second piece of evidence**: No element of the *jhāna* pentad occurs—*xún* 尋 (*vitakka*), *sì* 伺 (*vicāra*), *xǐ* 喜 (*pīti*), *lè* 樂 (*sukha*), *yīxīn* 一心 (*ekaggatā*)—all zero.

These two zeroes are not accidental omissions. T0784 is a sūtra **entirely outside the discursive field of meditative absorption**. It speaks of *niàndào*, of *niàn-fēicháng*, of recollecting the four great elements within the body, of *zhíxīn* 執心 and *dìngyì* 定意 (the latter used only in the sense of "resolute will" and "setting one's intent," not as a technical term of meditative doctrine), of *zhíxīn* 直心—but none of these describe themselves through the technical vocabulary of meditative absorption.

And yet T0784 does not sit at the level of "shallow concentration" or "not-yet-entered-concentration." Anyone who reads the whole sūtra will sense clearly that it has its own internal rigor, its own detailed guidance for practice, its own articulated levels of "practitioner-state." That rigor is not expressed through the vocabulary of meditative absorption—so how is it expressed?

This paper argues: T0784 occupies the **third track** on the *upacāra* platform identified in P04 §3.2—neither pressing forward into *appanā* (the sūtra does not raise the topic), nor stagnating in shallow, scattered attention, but operating in a third mode of cultivation: **grounded in ethical reminder, long-term continuity, middle-way adjustment, gradual deepening**.

Four markers of this track are clearly visible in T0784:

**Marker One · Ethical Reminder**: Chapter 14's "recollecting the true path, not neglecting it for a moment"; Chapter 16's "constantly recollecting the path"; Chapter 40's "with an upright mind recollecting the path"—the functional meaning of *niàn* here is ethical reminder and orientation, not a calibration of attention-technique. The practitioner does not need, via *vitakka* and *vicāra*, to lock the mind onto a single special characteristic of an object; it suffices to keep the ethical-metaphysical direction-sense called "the path" present in the background of ordinary consciousness.

**Marker Two · Long-Term Continuity**: Chapter 2, "one meal at midday, one night beneath a tree—be careful not to repeat"; Chapter 33, "cultivating the path is like forging iron—the work deepens by gradual stages." The temporal scale of T0784 is not a single session of meditation, but the long-term discipline of an entire renunciant life.

**Marker Three · Middle-Way Adjustment**: Chapter 32, the lute simile: "'If the strings are loose, what happens?' — 'They will not sound.' — 'If tight?' — 'The sound breaks.' — 'If tension and slackness are balanced?' — 'All the notes resound together.' — 'Cultivating the path is just so: when the mind is held in balanced adjustment, the path can be attained.'" This simile has a Pāli correspondence (the Buddha's lute simile for the balance of effort, AN 6.55 *Soṇa Sutta*). T0784 brings this simile into China already in the first century, laying the basic tenor of Chinese Buddhist cultivation: "neither excessive effort nor slackness."

**Marker Four · Gradual Deepening**: Chapter 33's iron-forging simile; Chapter 39's pearl-picking-from-the-string simile. T0784's description of the path of cultivation is one of incremental accumulation, not sudden attainment. This aligns perfectly, in register, with P04 §3.4's argument that "the designed endpoint is not the failed endpoint."

These four markers together define T0784's **third track** on the *upacāra* platform: neither advancing toward *appanā* (nor expecting to), nor stagnating at shallow attention, but proceeding in the register of **ethics-continuity-middle-way-gradualism**. This track is the historical source of the mainstream tenor of Chinese *niànfó*—the register descriptions frequently heard in Chinese *niànfó* practice, such as *lǎoshí niànfó* 老實念佛 ("honest *niànfó*"), *píngshí xiūxíng* 平實修行 ("plain-and-honest practice"), and *miánmián mì-mì* 綿綿密密 ("unbroken and dense")—all have their root on this track of T0784.

P04's claim that "the designed endpoint is not the failed endpoint" receives here its historical confirmation: the earliest Chinese reception of recollection-practice already received it in this register, and never mistook *upacāra* for a failed state of "not yet *appanā*." This register begins with T0784, runs through a millennium of development, and reaches its mature form in Pure Land name-recitation—but the fundamental tenor of the register was there from the beginning.

### 3.3 Dual-Track Divergence: The Coexistence of T0784 (Ethical) and T0602 (Technical)

§2.2 has already tabulated the register contrast between T0784 and T0602. This section addresses the further question of **what this dual-track structure means for the historical possibility of later Chinese *niànfó*'s three-way branching**.

First- and second-century Luoyang was a translation-workshop ecosystem where several Central Asian lineages coexisted in one geographical space. Kāśyapa Mātaṅga and Dharmaratna came from the Indian route; An Shigao came from Parthia (Ān-xī 安息); Lokakṣema (支婁迦讖) came from the Kuṣāṇa realm (月支). The register divergence between T0784 and T0602 is therefore not "an internal division of labor within a single lineage," but rather **two independent transmissions received simultaneously in China**:

**Downstream of the T0784 lineage (ethical register)**: This register emphasizes ethical reminder, long-term continuity, and portability across walking-standing-sitting-lying. Its downstream trajectory can be traced to the mature Pure Land name-recitation—Tanluan (曇鸞), Daochuo (道綽), and Shandao's (善導) argument that "even ordinary beings, by reciting the Name, enter the Reward-Land" (covered in NIAN P20, P22); Yunqi Zhuhong (雲棲祩宏) and Ouyi Zhixu's (蕅益智旭) late-Ming *niànfó* discourse (NIAN P24); and, in Japan, Hōnen's and Shinran's Other-Power schools. The core tenor of this register—that a layperson, a laboring person with no leisure for meditation, an elder on their deathbed, can all participate in the heart of the Dharma by the simple act of *niàn*—has its source precisely in early vocabulary such as *cháng niàn dào* and *zhíxīn niàn dào* in T0784.

**Downstream of the T0602 lineage (technical register)**: This register emphasizes breath-technique, step-by-step operations, and detailed calibration of meditative states. Its downstream trajectory flows into meditation-manuals such as Kumārajīva's *Zuo-chán Sānmèi Jīng* (坐禪三昧經, T0613, one of NIAN P06's comparative sources) and Buddhabhadra's *Dámóduōluó chán jīng* (達摩多羅禪經, T0618), ultimately merging into the meditative lineages that informed Tiantai *zhǐguān* and the pre-Fifth-Patriarch Chan meditative stream. Image-contemplation *niànfó* (from T0418 *Pratyutpanna-samādhi Sūtra* to the sixteen contemplations of the *Contemplation Sūtra*) also lies on the extension of this technical register—the object shifts from breath to Buddha-image, but the technical posture of "detailed calibration of a specific meditative object" is continuous.

**The register location of the third stream—reality-recollection *niànfó* (*shíxiàng niànfó* 實相念佛)**: Reality-recollection *niànfó* (a concept that developed out of the synthesis among Tiantai, Chan, and the three late-Ming masters; covered in NIAN P21, P23) is neither a simple extension of T0784's ethical track nor of T0602's technical track, but a **synthesis** of the two: it takes the long-term ethical continuity as its base, adds the metaphysical dimension of Mahāyāna emptiness-discourse, and shifts the object from a concrete Buddha-body to the reality-itself of all dharmas. The historical possibility of this synthesis was precisely that China already had, from the earliest period, both registers coexisting.

Chinese *niànfó* was never a single-line development; it was the long-term coexistence and cross-fertilization of two independent registers, produced in the first- and second-century Luoyang translation workshops. The sustained dialogue treated in NIAN PLAN Part VI ("two forces in sustained dialogue"—Pure Land patriarchs ↔ Chan's counter-thesis ↔ Tiantai bridging ↔ late-Ming synthesis) has its root in this dual-track origin. The technical content of T0602 will be treated more specifically in NIAN P06 and in the two fascicles DĀNA / DHYĀNA; this paper only establishes the thesis of **dual-track origins**.

### 3.4 The Distribution of Six-Recollection Vocabulary: Historical Confirmation of the P03 Frame

P03 §3.1 established a methodological frame: "one engine, multiple objects"—*anussati* as a functional engine can be applied across multiple objects (Buddha, Dharma, Saṅgha, precepts, giving, deities, plus the supplementary body, breath, death, peace); the engine's internal structure (recollection + continuity + ethical orientation) is stable, while the distribution of objects is shaped by culture, period, and the stage of doctrinal development.

T0784 provides the earliest historical datapoint for this frame. Having cited the passages individually above, this section presents them as a single distribution table:

| Pāli object | Chinese vocabulary | T0784 occurrence | Object nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| *buddhānussati* | (none) | 0 | absent |
| *dhammānussati* | *niàndào* (partial) | §14, §16, §40 | object = path (*dharma* broadly) |
| *saṅghānussati* | (none) | 0 | absent |
| *sīlānussati* | *yì-niàn wú jiè* 意念吾戒 | §37 | object = precepts |
| *cāgānussati* | (none) | 0 | absent |
| *devatānussati* | (none) | 0 | absent |
| *kāyagatāsati* | *niàn shēn-zhōng sì-dà* 念身中四大 | §17 | object = body (four elements) |
| *ānāpāna-sati* | *hūxī zhī jiān* 呼吸之間 | §36 | object = breath (paired with recollection of death) |
| *maraṇa-sati* | *hūxī zhī jiān* / *rén mìng zài jǐ jiān* 人命在幾間 | §36 | object = death |
| *upasamānussati* | (none) | 0 | absent |
| not in the standard six but still *anussati* (*aniccānussati*) | *niàn fēicháng* 念非常 | §15 (three times) | object = impermanence |

The table reveals a clear pattern: the recollection-object vocabulary already formed in T0784 covers **path, precepts, body, breath, death, impermanence**—six items; absent are **Buddha, Dharma (as the Dharma of the Triple Gem), Saṅgha, giving, deities, peace**—six items.

**Worth noting is the object *niàn fēicháng* (*aniccānussati*)**: it is not on Vism VII's standard list of six recollections, but appears in Vism VIII's supplementary list (the remaining four of the ten recollections) in the forms of *maraṇasati* and *aniccā-saññā*. The triple parallelism of "recollect impermanence" in T0784 Chapter 15 is the earliest Chinese record of combining contemplation of impermanence with the recollection-practice format. This has a source-significance for later Chinese "impermanence recollection"—the *Anityatā Sūtra* (無常經) family T0801, and the visualization-content of the Pure Land "chanting-companionship at the deathbed" (*lìnzhōng zhùniàn* 臨終助念) tradition.

**Three things can be read off this table:**

**First, the engine is stable.** All "*niàn*-X" functional structures in T0784 conform to P03 §3.1's description of the *anussati* engine (recollection + continuity + ethical orientation). Chapter 14's "recollecting the true path, not neglecting it for a moment" is a pure-form description of the engine; Chapter 17's "carefully recollect the four great elements within your body" is the engine applied to the body-object; Chapter 36's "between breaths" is the engine applied to the double object of breath and death. The engine-structure remains consistent across all applications.

**Second, the distribution of objects is culturally and historically determined.** T0784 emphasizes the ethical-philosophical dimension (path, precepts, impermanence), retains the core Pāli practice elements (body, breath, death), skips the triple-gem structure (that Buddha, Dharma, and Saṅgha are all absent is one structural fact), and defers the introduction of giving and deities (two objects less familiar to early Chinese readers). This choice is not random but the translation workshops' judgment about readers' existing vocabulary and psychological readiness.

**Third, the absences themselves are historical information.** The simultaneous absence of *niànfó*, *niànfǎ*, and *niànsēng* 念僧 is especially meaningful: the early Chinese reception of recollection-practice **deferred the entire structure of "the Triple Gem as object of recollection."** This structure had to wait for later translations (T0418 *Pratyutpanna-samādhi Sūtra*, which P06 will treat, is the key node at which *niànfó* becomes an explicit topic in its own right) before completion. The historical order in which the objects were filled in reflects the Chinese translation workshops' incremental absorption of *anussati* theory as a whole.

Taken together, these three points upgrade P03's "one engine, multiple objects" frame from a theoretical claim to a **historical thesis**: the reception-history of *anussati* in China can be read as a long-term process of "stable engine, gradually filled-in objects"; T0784 is the earliest snapshot of that process.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Integration: Skinner's Historical Contextualism and "Concepts Before Their Standard Vocabulary"

The phenomenon that T0784 lacks the term *niànfó* while its functional vocabulary is already in place lends itself to a ready-made methodological tool from intellectual history—the principle of historical contextualism put forward by the Cambridge School, particularly Quentin Skinner, in his 1969 essay *"Meaning and Understanding in the History of Ideas"*: the historical existence of a concept must be traced **not only through the appearance of its standard name**, but through **how its function is borne by surrounding vocabulary and discursive strategies**.[^14]

For the absence of *niànfó* in T0784, the Skinnerian reading is this: the concept of *niànfó* **already existed** in first-century Luoyang; the vocabulary carrying it was *niàndào*; the discursive strategy was "wrapping recollection-of-virtue in ethical reminder"; the register was "the path of least resistance within the existing vocabulary of the Chinese literati." When the compound *niànfó* was finally coined in later translations (the precise moment still requires philological study, but certainly no later than Lokakṣema's translation of the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi Sūtra* T0418 in 179 CE), it was **not the introduction of a new concept** but the fitting of a formal name onto a function already in operation.

This methodological borrowing should retain its ⚠️ caveat: Skinner worked on political intellectual history (e.g., the shifting vocabulary carrying the concept of "the state" in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century England), which differs in many details of source-target cultural dynamics from religious translation history. This paper borrows only the structural insight—"a concept can precede its standard name"—without importing the whole methodology.

The borrowing is particularly useful for Western readers: contemporary English-language textbook answers to "when did Buddhism in China begin to ___" often judge a period as "*niànfó* not yet developed" or "Pure Land thought not yet formed" because a key term cannot be found. Skinner's framework suggests reversing the question: **on what vocabulary was the function borne during this period?** T0784's answer—the *niàndào* cluster—is a good deal richer than "whether *niànfó* had appeared."

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

**❌ Correction One**: The *dào* in T0784's *niàndào* must not be misread as "Tao" (the Daoist *dào*). Here *dào* is *dharma* (the Dharma, the path of cultivation, the path of awakening)—not the Daoist ontological *dào*. Although the translation workshop's use of the character *dào* did involve strategic borrowing from native vocabulary, and although the later Wei-Jin phenomenon of "matching-meanings Buddhism" (*géyì Fójiào* 格義佛教) did interpret the Dharma through Daoist vocabulary, within T0784's own context *dào* consistently carries its Buddhist meaning. Reading it back into Daoist ontology is a historically inverted misreading.[^15]

**❌ Correction Two**: The absence of *niànfó* in T0784 must not be read as "early China did not value *niànfó*." §3.1 has shown that this is a phase phenomenon in vocabulary history (the compound was not yet coined), not a reflection of doctrinal priorities. The same function was already being borne by the *niàndào* cluster. Equating vocabulary-absence with doctrinal-absence is one of the most common methodological errors of modern readers facing historical texts.

**❌ Correction Three**: T0784's ethical register must not be assessed as "meditative absorption not deep enough." This is precisely the historical antecedent of the misreading P04 §3.4 warned against—"taking a designed endpoint to be a failed endpoint." T0784 is not outside the discursive field of meditative absorption because it "failed to reach" absorption; it is outside because it **chose a different discursive track**—ethics-continuity-middle-way-gradualism. That choice has its own criteria of accomplishment; it does not need to be evaluated by the standards of meditative-absorption theory.

**⚠️ Caveat One**: This paper's use of Skinnerian historical contextualism to analyze the vocabulary-absence in T0784 is a heuristic analogy, not a methodological transplant. Skinner treats Western political intellectual history; T0784 is religious translation history. The cultural dynamics differ in detail.

**⚠️ Caveat Two**: The analogies of "internet" and "viral marketing" draw only on the structural insight "concept before vocabulary"; they do not draw on the detailed history of technological development. Readers should not over-extend the analogy.

## VI. Conclusion

China's earliest Buddhist scripture, the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*, contains no occurrence of the compound term *niànfó* across its forty-two chapters; yet the same text already covers six objects of *anussati* vocabulary—path, body, precepts, breath, death, impermanence. This paper argues that this critical absence is not evidence of doctrinal absence, but a phase phenomenon in vocabulary history: the compound *niànfó* had not yet been coined in first-century Luoyang, but its functional vocabulary was already being carried by the *niàndào* cluster. The earliest *anussati* object received in China was **the path (*dào*), not the Buddha (*fó*)**.

T0784 contains no meditative-absorption vocabulary and no element of the *jhāna* pentad; its register sits entirely on the **third track** of the *upacāra* platform established in P04 §3.2: ethical reminder, long-term continuity, middle-way adjustment, gradual deepening. The source of the mainstream tenor of later Chinese *niànfó* lies on this track.

T0784 (ethical register) and An Shigao's T0602 (technical register) coexisted in second-century Luoyang, establishing the **dual-track origin** of Chinese *niànfó*. The historical possibility of the later three-way branching into name-recitation, image-contemplation, and reality-recollection was born out of this dual structure.

T0784's object distribution (path, body, precepts, breath, death, impermanence) provides historical confirmation of P03's "one engine, multiple objects" frame: the engine is stable; the distribution of objects is culturally and historically determined. The reception-history of *anussati* in China can be read as a long-term process of "stable engine, gradually filled-in objects"; T0784 is its earliest snapshot.

**Preview of P06**: From T0784 (67 CE, no compound *niànfó*, no meditative-absorption vocabulary, ethical register) to Lokakṣema's translation of the *Pratyutpanna-samādhi Sūtra* (T0418, 179 CE, the direct introduction of *pratyutpanna-samādhi* 般舟三昧 as visionary *samādhi* in which one sees the Buddhas of the ten directions)—this is the **first register-jump** in Chinese Buddhist reception. This jump corresponds historically to the shift, within P04 §3.5's "three senses of *niànfó-sānmèi*," from Sense A (*upacāra* platform) to Sense C (Mahāyāna visionary). P06 will address how T0418, for the first time in China, **explicitly establishes the Buddha as the object of *niàn*** and introduces the Mahāyāna visionary mode.
