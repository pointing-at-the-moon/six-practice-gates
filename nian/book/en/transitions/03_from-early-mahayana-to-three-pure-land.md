## Transition ③ · From Early Mahāyāna to the Three Pure Land Sūtras

Part Three moved fast, and it moved hard.

In the space of a few chapters, the whole country of buddha-recollection burst open. The Buddha is no longer only a teacher gone far ahead, but measureless Buddhas of the ten directions, teaching at this very moment. The place where a Buddha dwells is no longer a vague direction, but a land that can be vowed into being, a land one can seek birth in. Even the act of *niàn* itself split into two faces — recollection in the mind and calling with the mouth — clasped each into the other.

It was a magnificent expansion. But expansion brings a kind of vertigo.

What Part Three left us is a floor covered with parts — here a Buddha standing in presence, there a land selected and assembled vow by vow, on this side a vow about "hearing the name," on that side a deathbed "calling of the name." Every one of them is powerful; but they lie scattered. A person who actually means to practice, standing in the middle of all these parts, can hardly help asking: which Buddha, then? Which land? Which set of vows? Where do I begin?

The work of gathering the scattered parts into one finished building — the language of Part Three had not yet done it.

What did that work was three scriptures.

They drew the scattered possibilities of early Mahāyāna to a single focus: one Buddha — Amitābha; one land — Bliss; one clearly spoken set of vows, contemplations, and the name. From then on, the person who recollects the Buddha no longer faces the vastness of "ten directions, measureless, take your pick," but a road drawn plain and clear: this Buddha, this land, vow like this, contemplate like this, hold the name like this — and you can get there.

These three scriptures, later generations call together the Three Pure Land Sūtras. They are the liveliest stretch of this whole book, and the one that sank deepest into people's hearts — from here, buddha-recollection in China truly entered every household.

We turn the page, and walk into that land — drawn, at last, clearly.
