## Transition ① · From the Pāli Foundations to the Earliest Chinese Translations

Part One has walked up to the door of a small scripture, and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath.

With Part One done, you hold a complete set of things in your hands: a track (recollection), a format (the nine epithets), a system (the six recollections), and a boundary (the door of concentration, with that threshold later generations would cross). This set was taught by the Buddha's own mouth, recorded by the earliest scriptures, and kept word by word in the Pāli of the southern transmission down to this day. It is complete. It is precise. It has roots.

Is it enough?

By the language of Part One—yes. An Indian householder, or a monk in the forest, could take this set and set out on the road.

But something the language of Part One cannot handle is about to happen.

That something is this: this buddha-recollection is about to leave the land where it was born.

Everything Part One described stood on a single soil—one language, one audience, one set of background truths that went without saying. Karma, rebirth, liberation, the Four Noble Truths: these were common sense shared between the one speaking and the ones listening. On that soil, the weight, the direction, the use of the word "buddha-recollection" were all steady.

But when this buddha-recollection traveled east—along the trade roads, with generation after generation of translator monks, over the Pamirs—it arrived in a place that had never heard who "Buddha" was, a place whose language had no words for "rebirth" or "liberation": China. There it would have to land again, and take root again, in wholly new soil.

And new soil changes a tree.

China had its own common sense: here people spoke of filial piety, of ancestors, of self-cultivation, of long life; here people read the classics of the Confucians and the books of the Daoists. When "buddha-recollection" was first turned into Chinese characters and first read by Chinese eyes, this was the field of mind it fell into. It would not grow unchanged. It would be received, rewritten, selected by this soil—growing into a shape connected to the Indian tree, yet not entirely the same.

This is what Part Two, Part Three, and most of the rest of this book will be tracking: how the same buddha-recollection, in China, step by step grew into the shape we know today.

And the first step on that long road—the earliest, smallest, plainest step—is a little scripture of barely two thousand characters: the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*. By tradition it is the first scripture translated when the Dharma first entered China. In it there is none of the later Pure Land splendor—no visualization, no name-recitation, no Western Land of Bliss. It simply, plainly, sets down some things the Buddha said—and in those sayings, the matter of *niàn*, of recollection, quietly shows its face for the first time, in a way China could understand.

We turn the page, and go to see it.
