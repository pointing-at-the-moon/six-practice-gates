## 7.4 Requesting the Dharma-Wheel and the Buddhas to Abide: Keeping the Dharma in the World

The sixth and seventh of the ten great vows — requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel, and requesting the Buddhas to abide in the world — are again a pair. One asks the Buddha to teach; the other asks the Buddha, and every good teacher, not to depart. Together they are a single act: keeping the Dharma in the world.

These two vows have a wellspring far back in the Āgamas. Go back first to that scene.

### Beneath the Tree of Awakening, an Entreaty

The *Ekottarika Āgama* has a chapter named for it: the "Chapter on Entreaty." Its first sūtra records what happened just after the awakening:

> At one time the Buddha was in Magadha, beneath the tree of the site of awakening. The World-Honored One, not long after attaining the Way, had this thought: "This profound Dharma I have now realized is hard to understand, hard to fathom, hard to come to know. … If I teach this wondrous Dharma and people neither believe nor receive it, nor practice it, the labor will be vain and the loss real. Better that I now keep silence — why should I teach at all?"

The World-Honored One, newly awakened, sitting beneath the tree, and a thought arises: the Dharma I have realized is too deep; taught to those who will not believe or practice, the effort is wasted. Better silence — why teach?

Feel the peril of that moment: the Buddha has awakened, the Dharma is in the world — and the Dharma comes within a breath of stopping inside that one mind, never to emerge.

At that moment Brahmā, knowing from afar the Tathāgata's thought, "as swiftly as a strong man bends and stretches his arm," vanished from the Brahmā heaven and stood before the Buddha, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, and pleaded: "This Jambudvīpa will surely fall to ruin; the three realms will lose their eye" — if the Buddha does not teach, the world goes blind. And he offered that most beautiful of figures: beings are like lotuses in a pond, "risen from the soil but not yet above the water" — some still beneath the surface, some about to break it, some already standing clear, unwetted; if those whose roots are ripe "perish without hearing the Dharma, is it not a grief?" "Now is the very time — may the World-Honored One teach the Dharma!"

The World-Honored One, knowing Brahmā's thought and out of tender care for all beings, spoke this verse: "Brahmā has come to entreat Me: the Tathāgata opens the Dharma's gate; those who hear shall gain firm faith, discerning the Dharma's profound essentials." From this, the wheel of Dharma turned.

So it was: that this world ever heard the Buddha's Dharma hung, at the first, upon one entreaty. To request the turning of the dharma-wheel is no polite formula of liturgy; it is to take Brahmā's ancient plea onto one's own shoulders.

### The Sixth Vow: Earnestly Entreating the Wondrous Wheel

Samantabhadra spreads that one plea across the dharma-realm. Throughout the realms of dharma and space, he says, within every dust-mote, in every moment, Buddhas numerous beyond speech are just now attaining perfect awakening, encircled by oceans of bodhisattvas — "and I, with body, speech, and mind, by every skillful means, earnestly entreat them all to turn the wondrous dharma-wheel." Before every newly awakened Buddha, "I" am present, pleading: teach. The verse runs:

> All lamps of the world in the ten directions, who have newly accomplished awakening — I now entreat and invite them all: turn the unsurpassed, wondrous dharma-wheel.

Every newly awakened Buddha is a lamp just lit in the world; to entreat is to go and carry every such lamp before the eyes of beings.

### The Seventh Vow: Entreating Them Not to Enter Nirvāṇa

The seventh vow goes a step further: when the Dharma has been taught, its teachers must be asked to stay. All Buddhas "about to manifest parinirvāṇa," says Samantabhadra — "and the bodhisattvas, śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, learners and adepts, down to all good teachers whatsoever — I entreat them all not to enter nirvāṇa," but to remain for kalpas numerous as the dust of all buddha-fields, for the benefit and joy of all beings. The verse:

> When Buddhas wish to show nirvāṇa, I entreat them with utmost sincerity: abide for kalpas many as the dust of all lands, to benefit and gladden every being.

Mark the reach of this vow: not the Buddhas only. Bodhisattvas, śrāvakas, learners and adepts, "down to all good teachers whatsoever" — everyone who can show another the Way stands within the entreaty. For ordinary people like us, this vow lands very near: the teacher willing to instruct you, the friend who leads you in reciting the Buddha's name — these are where the "abiding" of the Dharma is lodged. To request the Buddhas to abide, brought down into daily life, is to honor, protect, and never slight anyone who speaks the Dharma beside you — and not to learn that the room was lit only after the lamp goes out.

### Why the Dharma Deserves Such Asking: Even the Buddha Reveres It

Beneath these two vows lies one more stratum: what is it about the Dharma that deserves such entreaty? The *Saṃyukta Āgama* records the Buddha's own answer.

When the Buddha had newly attained perfect awakening, He reflected: among devas and men, ascetics and brahmins, there is none who surpasses Me in virtue, concentration, wisdom, and liberation, whom I might honor and serve and dwell in dependence upon — "only the true Dharma, by which I awakened of myself and became a fully perfect Buddha: that I shall honor and revere, serve and venerate, and dwell in dependence upon." Brahmā, knowing the Buddha's thought, appeared before Him in praise, and spoke this verse:

> The perfect Buddhas of the past, and all the Buddhas yet to come, and the present Buddha, World-Honored One, who can dispel the sorrows of beings — all of them revere the Dharma, dwelling in reliance on the true Dharma. Such reverence as this is the Dharma of all Buddhas.

Past, future, and present — no Buddha but reveres the Dharma and abides by it; such reverence is itself the way of all Buddhas.

Even the Buddha takes the Dharma as what He honors and depends upon. Then the sixth and seventh vows are, at bottom, the guarding — on behalf of all beings — of the one thing the Buddhas of the three times themselves rely upon. To one who recites the Buddha's name this should feel close to home: the Name you recite is the whole Dharma condensed into a single line; to ask that the Dharma remain in the world, and to hold the Name thought after thought, is to tend the same lamp.

The Buddhas, oneself, others, and the Dharma have each been faced. Three vows remain — following the Buddha constantly in study, accommodating sentient beings, dedicating all universally — the vow-king's close, and its deepest breath. The next chapter walks it to the end.
