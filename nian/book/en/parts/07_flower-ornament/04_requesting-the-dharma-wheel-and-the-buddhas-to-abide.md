## 7.4 Requesting the Turning of the Dharma-Wheel, Requesting the Buddhas to Abide: Asking the Buddha to Teach, Asking the Buddha to Stay

After the first five vows — reverence turned toward the Buddhas, then the facing of oneself and of others — Samantabhadra's vows now pivot to a new action: "requesting."

Vow six, requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel; vow seven, requesting the Buddhas to abide in the world — these two are the only pair among the ten that share a single verb, "to entreat and invite." One asks the Buddhas to teach (to set the dharma-wheel turning); one asks the Buddhas not to leave (do not enter *parinirvāṇa* — stay long in the world). A turning asked of the Dharma; an abiding asked of the Buddhas. Together, the two vows do one thing: they keep the Buddha-Dharma here, in this world, long, long into time.

### Requesting the Turning of the Dharma-Wheel: The Dharma Was Asked Into the World

Vow six: requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel.

It asks, in particular, "those who have just attained enlightenment" — the newly awakened Buddhas — to turn the dharma-wheel, to open their mouths and teach.

The root of this vow lies in a deeply moving story.

Beneath the bodhi tree, the Buddha had just attained the Way — realized the deepest, hardest-to-utter of truths. And He hesitated: this Dharma was too deep, too subtle, and beings were wound so tightly in their desires. If He taught it, who would understand? Better, perhaps, not to teach at all.

At just that moment, the great Brahmā king came down from his heaven, knelt before the Buddha, and pleaded: World-Honoured One, may you teach the Dharma! May you teach the Dharma! He asked once, again, a third time. The Buddha, in silence, accepted. And so He rose, walked to the Deer Park, and turned the dharma-wheel for the first time.

You see — that there is any Dharma in this world to hear was never a matter of course. It is because, long ago, someone asked — earnestly. The Dharma was asked into the world.

Samantabhadra's "requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel" stretches that one scene at the Deer Park across the whole dharma-realm: in measureless buddha-fields, moment by moment, Buddhas are just now attaining the Way; and moment by moment Samantabhadra is there, entreating each of them: turn the dharma-wheel. Wherever a Buddha awakens, there sounds this one cry — "may you teach the Dharma."

For us, too, this carries a meaning: the Dharma does not deliver itself to your door. You must go and seek it, go and ask for it — and your own earnestness to hear is itself one of the hands that sets the Dharma turning.

### Requesting the Buddhas to Abide: Do Not Be Like Ānanda, Silent When It Was Time to Speak

Vow seven: requesting the Buddhas to abide in the world.

It asks the Buddhas "about to manifest *parinirvāṇa*" — on the point of leaving — begging them: "do not enter *parinirvāṇa*"; stay longer, stay for measureless kalpas, "to bring weal and benefit to every sentient being."

The root of this vow also lies in a story — but this one makes you sigh: the story of a request that was never made.

In the Buddha's old age, beside a shrine called Cāpāla, He hinted to His attendant Ānanda three times over: one who has cultivated and made much of the four bases of psychic power can abide for a kalpa or longer… Three times He was opening for Ānanda the gate of requesting the Buddha to abide — had Ānanda only asked, "World-Honoured One, may you remain in the world!", the Buddha could have stayed.

But at that hour Ānanda's mind was covered by Māra, and three times he fell silent — no reply, no request. Seeing that he would not ask, the Buddha said: "Ānanda! You may now also make a request to me." Ānanda was silent still. Then Māra slipped in through the opening, and the Buddha declared: in three months, He would enter *parinirvāṇa*.

Listen — the Buddha left not because He had to; it is because the one who should have asked Him to stay, at the moment that mattered most, did not open his mouth.

Samantabhadra's "requesting the Buddhas to abide" takes that failed request and mends it: in every buddha-field, at the moment any Buddha is about to enter extinction, Samantabhadra is there ahead of all, earnestly asking: please abide. To the end of all future time, that one cry — "may you remain in the world" — will never once be absent. What was missed beneath the Cāpāla shrine will never be missed again.

And this vow asks more than the Buddhas alone. The sūtra extends it to bodhisattvas, *śrāvakas*, *pratyekabuddhas*, "and indeed all good spiritual friends" — all who can teach the Dharma and lead you in practice: Samantabhadra asks them all to remain. For us this is a reminder: the people near you who can truly teach you the Buddha-Dharma — your teachers, your good friends — do not take their staying for granted. When it is time to treasure them, to hold them back, to open your mouth — do not be Ānanda, silent until the moment is gone.

### The Buddha Has Always Been Here — Then What Is There to Request?

Here a sharp question rises: the Buddha's dharma-body (*dharma-kāya*) is ever-abiding from the start, unborn and unceasing — what use, then, is "requesting" Him to teach, "requesting" Him to stay? Does your one request change His state of being?

Master Zongmi untied this knot with a single line: "The request to the Buddhas is the purification of one's own mind." When you "request" the Buddha, what you are truly doing is not changing the Buddha — it is purifying your own mind.

He gave it a simile, the wisdom-sun and the vessel of water: the Buddha is like the sun of wisdom in the sky, shining everywhere across the ten directions, never having come, never having gone. Whether you see it depends entirely on the basin of "water" in your own mind — clear, or not. When the water is clear, the sun's reflection appears; when it is muddy, the reflection hides — not that the sun comes and goes; the water is clear or muddy. And so Zongmi said: "When the mind is pure and wisdom is clear, the Buddha is constantly seen."

"Requesting the Buddhas to abide," it turns out, is the clearing of your own basin of water. The Buddha, that sun, has been shining all along; your one earnest "please" is the settling of the mud in your own mind — so you may see the Buddha who was always here, who never left.

The *Lotus Sūtra* says this layer right through: "The time since I attained buddhahood is exceedingly long, immeasurably long; the lifespan is unbounded *asaṃkhyeya* kalpas, ever-abiding and not extinguishing." The Buddha's true lifespan is measureless, boundless, never once extinguished. The visible "entering of nirvāṇa" is only a manifestation — the coming and going of the manifestation-body (*nirmāṇa-kāya*); the dharma-body has been here the whole time.

So the deepest layer of "requesting the Buddhas to abide" is this: the Buddha never truly left. It is our minds that keep turning muddy, until we can no longer see. That one cry, "may you abide in the world," is the clearing of the mind, time after time — until we recognize: He has been here all along.

### One Line That Makes Both Requests

In China, Master Shandao gathered these two vows into a single line: "inviting them to abide and turn the dharma-wheel." "To abide" is requesting the Buddhas to abide — vow seven; "to turn the dharma-wheel" is requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel — vow six. In one line, both vows are asked in full.

Walking to this point, Samantabhadra's vows have carried us: toward the Buddhas in reverence (one through three), facing oneself and others (four and five), asking for the Dharma's long abiding (six and seven).

Three vows remain — the close of the whole ten great vows, and the close of this book: upward, following close behind the Buddha's own learning (constantly following the Buddha in study); downward, according with all sentient beings (accommodating sentient beings); and at the last, giving everything away (universal dedication).
