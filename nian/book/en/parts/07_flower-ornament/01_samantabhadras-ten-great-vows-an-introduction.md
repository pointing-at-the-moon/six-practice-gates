## 7.1 Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows: Stretching Buddha-Recollection into a Whole Way of Living

The last station on the road of buddha-recollection is the *Flower Ornament Sūtra* — the *Avataṃsaka*. Leading the way is the great bodhisattva Samantabhadra, with those "ten great vow-kings" of his.

The ten great vows are these: reverencing all Buddhas, praising the Tathāgatas, extensively making offerings, repenting karmic obstructions, rejoicing in merit, requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel, requesting the Buddhas to abide in the world, constantly following the Buddha in study, accommodating sentient beings, and universal dedication.

Do not rush to take them one at a time. First look at what they are when taken together.

### Not Ten Things, but One Vow-King

People see "ten great vows" and naturally read them as ten good deeds set side by side, like a to-do list: bow today, offer a word of praise tomorrow, make an offering the day after…

Samantabhadra does not see them that way. He calls these ten the "vow-king" — the king among vows. When the sūtra tells you to "hear this vow-king," it says *one vow-king*, not *these ten vows*. Ten is the number when they are listed out; but received and held, they are one.

What glues the ten into one? A passage that repeats after every single vow. Take the first vow, reverence, as the example:

> When the realm of empty space is exhausted, my reverencing shall be exhausted; but because the realm of empty space cannot be exhausted, my reverencing has no end … moment by moment continuous, without interruption, my actions of body, speech, and mind without weariness.

What this says is: only when empty space itself has been exhausted will my reverencing stop; but empty space can never be exhausted, and so this reverencing of mine will never see its ending day — one moment joining the next, never interrupted; body, speech, and mind never wearying.

Look at what this passage has done. It has taken "reverencing all Buddhas" — a countable thing, done a few times and finished — and turned it into an unbroken river of time. Bowing to the Buddha is no longer "how many prostrations I made today"; it is a single continuity running to the very end of future time, moment joining moment, never stopping.

And this passage comes once after every vow. So the ten vows are strung into one by the same cord — "moment by moment continuous, to the end of all future time" — into a single whole that exhausts space, pervades the dharma-realm, and never rests.

### Sudhana Called at Fifty-Three Doors, and Came Last to Samantabhadra

Where did these ten great vows come from? The story begins with a youth in the *Flower Ornament Sūtra* named Sudhana.

Sudhana gave rise to the awakening-mind and set out to call on good friends — asking the way from door to door, fifty-three teachers in all. The first he visited was Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva; the last was Samantabhadra Bodhisattva.

That order is deliberate. Mañjuśrī stands for wisdom; Samantabhadra stands for practice. Sudhana set out from Mañjuśrī — wisdom — and came to completion at Samantabhadra — practice. The whole journey of fifty-three visits walks a single road: wisdom opened first, practice perfected after.

And Samantabhadra's ten great vows are precisely the end of that long road, the gathering-up of everything on it. In other words: you have heard and received wisdom upon wisdom (Mañjuśrī); in the end, it all has to land on these ten vows of Samantabhadra and be lived out. Wisdom is the point of departure; practice is the homecoming.

### Here, Buddha-Recollection Swells into the Whole Dharma-Realm

So what has buddha-recollection become, by the time it reaches the Flower Ornament?

On his long round of visits, Sudhana came to the fifth — a good friend called the lay-elder Vimuktika. What the elder taught him was buddha-recollection — but a buddha-recollection now very different from anything in the six parts before this one. The elder set out twenty-one gates of buddha-recollection, and every one of them closes upon "the dharma-realm," upon "all buddha-fields." For instance: "each Buddha's body filling the dharma-realm" — the body of every single Buddha pervades the entire dharma-realm. Or again: "in a single hair-pore," seeing innumerable Buddhas — Buddhas beyond all telling.

Do you see? This buddha-recollection is no longer recollecting one Buddha or contemplating one mark. It is this: within a single moment of mind, seeing the measureless, countless Buddhas who fill the whole dharma-realm.

The Flower Ornament stretches this "moment by moment" to its furthest point. When the Pure Land and Chan parts spoke of "moment by moment continuous," they meant time — one moment joining the next. But the Flower Ornament's "moment by moment" gathers in space as well — "in every dust-mote there are Buddhas as numerous as dust-motes": inside one grain of dust, as many Buddhas as there are dust-motes; "all kalpas in their entirety as a single moment": all the measureless aeons gathered into this one present moment. Within one moment: all Buddhas, all times, all places.

This is the Flower Ornament's buddha-recollection — layer upon layer without end; one is all. To recollect one Buddha is to recollect all Buddhas; and this present moment of yours opens onto the whole boundless dharma-realm.

### The Long Way Around, and Back to Amitābha

And yet the most moving thing of all is how the ten great vows end.

A Flower Ornament this vast, a dharma-realm buddha-recollection layered this endlessly — where does Samantabhadra lead it? The sūtra says: when a person who practices these ten great vows comes to the hour of death, everything scatters — the body fails, family and kin are left behind, power and wealth, not one of them can be carried along; "only this vow-king will not depart," but goes before him, leading the way. And then, "in a single instant, he attains birth in the Land of Bliss," and he sees Amitābha Buddha.

In the verses, Samantabhadra vows it in his own voice: "May I, when about to die … behold face-to-face the Buddha Amitābha, and attain birth in the Land of Bliss."

Look — after this whole great circuit through the Flower Ornament, after buddha-recollection has been stretched to fill the entire dharma-realm, in the end it settles back on the one Buddha we know best — Amitābha; back on the one land we have been walking toward all this way — the Land of Bliss.

That dharma-realm buddha-recollection, vast beyond every edge, and that plain single phrase, "*Namo Amitābha Buddha*," here become one. The Ming-dynasty Master Yunqi said it well: "Samantabhadra's vow is the king — never apart from the present moment." These ten vows of Samantabhadra, exhausting space and pervading the dharma-realm, are not somewhere else; they are right inside this present moment of your buddha-recollection. In the very moment you honestly recite this one buddha-name, the whole dharma-realm, endless layer upon layer, is in it.

### Living Buddha-Recollection as a Whole Way

In the end, Samantabhadra's ten great vows do one thing: they take buddha-recollection — a practice you had been doing alone — and stretch it open into a whole way of living.

At the Flower Ornament, buddha-recollection is no longer only "I recollect my Buddha." It is: bowing in reverence toward all Buddhas (reverencing all Buddhas), extolling them (praising the Tathāgatas), making offerings to them (extensively making offerings); it is facing your own faults (repenting karmic obstructions) and facing the good in others (rejoicing in merit); it is asking the Buddhas to teach and asking them to remain (requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel, requesting the Buddhas to abide in the world); it is following close behind the Buddhas in their learning and according with all beings (constantly following the Buddha in study, accommodating sentient beings); and at the last, it is turning all the merit of all of this over to the beings of the dharma-realm (universal dedication).

Between you and all Buddhas, between you and all sentient beings — woven layer into layer, dedicated without end. These ten vows are buddha-recollection lived out as that one whole way: to the end of future time, moment after moment, unbroken.

In the four chapters ahead, we will walk these ten great vows through, group by group — beginning with the first three: reverence, praise, and offering, the three ways of paying respect to the Buddhas.
