## 1.1 Recollection: Another Track

Twenty-five centuries ago, Kapilavatthu. South of the city lay the Nigrodha Park, where the Buddha often stayed when He returned to His home country. Among the Sakyans was a layman named Mahānāma, head of a great household, a man who went about with a retinue and was tangled in worldly affairs. He came to see the Buddha, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, sat down to one side, and asked a question: a practitioner still on the learner's ground, who has not yet walked to the end — how should he train, so as to reach, within this Dharma and discipline, the exhaustion of the taints?

The Buddha's answer was not to send him into some recondite meditative method. The Buddha said: such a person should cultivate the six recollections. And He gave a most homely simile:

> It is like a starving man, his body wasted and thin: obtaining fine food, his body grows full and lustrous.

A man starved to thinness, given good food, naturally fills out again — and the six recollections are just such food for the mind. And the first of the six is recollection of the Buddha.

This scene is recorded in the *Saṃyukta Āgama*; the Pāli *Aṅguttara Nikāya* likewise preserves the Buddha's teaching of the recollections to Mahānāma. It is the earliest and cleanest scene we have for understanding what "buddha-recollection" actually is. To read the whole of this book rightly, one must first stand firm at this scene.

### Mindfulness and Recollection Are Two Different Things

First, two words need to be told apart, because they look alike and are often taken for the same thing.

One is "mindfulness" (*sati*). Its meaning has two layers. One layer is "to remember"—not letting what one already knows fall off the mind. The other is "to hold"—keeping the mind in place, not letting it slip. What the Buddha teaches in the foundations of mindfulness is exactly this: toward body, feeling, mind, and mental objects, a continuous watching and holding, knowing as it is whatever is happening now. Mindfulness is a method. It governs *how* to attend; it does not prescribe what you attend to.

The other is "recollection" (*anussati*). It carries one prefix more than *sati*—the prefix *anu*-, which means "along, following, repeatedly." So recollection is not "remembering something once." It is returning, again and again, along one object.

The difference of this one prefix props open two different tracks.

Mindfulness has a method and specifies no object—it is like a vehicle that can carry the mind along, yet never tells the mind where to go. Recollection has an object and sets up no separate method—the method is hidden inside "repeatedly adhering to this object." It is like a route already marked out, handed to you destination and all: the mind walks along this object, and every step lands on something with nourishment in it.

Why does this difference matter? It matters because of this: every form of buddha-recollection in the rest of this book—the remembrance during the interval of a single meal in the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*, the seeing of the Buddha in the *pratyutpanna-samādhi*, the sixteen contemplations of the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus*, all the way to the homage to all Buddhas in Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows—grows more solemn and more elaborate on the surface, but underneath, all of it runs on this one same track of recollection: toward an object possessing liberative qualities, adhering repeatedly, letting the mind be turned by that adherence.

See this track clearly, and the whole tradition joins into one line. Miss it, and it will look like a pile of rituals growing ever more mystical.

### How This Track Operates

Back to the Nigrodha Park. Of the six recollections the Buddha taught Mahānāma, the first is recollection of the Tathāgata. The sūtra reads:

> What are the six recollections? The noble disciple recollects the Tathāgata thus: "the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Rightly and Fully Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Tamer of Persons, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One."

Notice: the Buddha is not telling Mahānāma to "visualize" the Buddha's appearance, nor to "stay aware of" the Buddha as if He were some neutral object like the breath. What the Buddha tells him to do is recollect this string of epithets—each one pointing to a quality of the Buddha as an awakened one. To recollect these epithets is to let the mind adhere along these qualities, one after another, again and again.

And once it adheres, what happens? The sūtra goes on in fine detail:

> When the noble disciple recollects thus, the entanglement of craving does not arise, nor do thoughts of hatred or delusion arise; his mind is straight. He gains the meaning of the Tathāgata and gains the Tathāgata's true Dharma; in the Tathāgata's true Dharma, and toward the Tathāgata, a mind of gladness arises in him. Gladdened, joy springs up; joyful, his body grows tranquil; his body tranquil, he feels ease; feeling ease, his mind becomes concentrated. His mind concentrated, that noble disciple — even amid violent and perilous beings — is without obstruction or barrier; he enters the stream of the Dharma-water, even unto nirvāṇa.

This is not a passage of lyrical praise. It is a precise diagram of operation. The moment the mind adheres to the Buddha's qualities, the three things that ordinarily entangle it—craving, hatred, delusion—cannot at that moment wrap around it. The mind is therefore straight, and then, link by link—gladness, joy, tranquility of body, ease, concentration—it is carried upward. And what triggers this whole chain of change is one single action: "recollecting the Tathāgata."

The things that ordinarily cover the mind and sap its strength—sensual desire, ill-will, sloth-and-torpor, restlessness-and-remorse, doubt—are together called "the five hindrances." While recollection is operating, the five hindrances draw back for a time, and the mind grows soft and bright. Spread the process out and it has four phases: first establish the object (call up one epithet); then adhere along it; then confidence—the mind's brightening, its non-hesitation—and joy arise; and last, the hindrances temporarily absent, body and mind turn pliant and workable, fit to receive deeper practice.

Establishing the object, adhering, confidence and joy arising, pliancy and workability—these four phases are the complete rhythm of the track of recollection.

And here is a key easily overlooked: these four phases work not because the object "Buddha" carries some mystical power, but because the act of repeated adherence is itself what transforms the mind. The sūtra puts it very plainly. It does not say "recollect the Buddha and you will be saved." It says: when the noble disciple recollects thus, the entanglement of craving does not arise. This is a structurally necessary result, not a bestowed grace. The object supplies the direction; the act of adherence completes the transformation.

This principle is the spine of the entire book.

### Why This Track Is So Good for Householders

The close of that passage is the most practical part of all. Once the mind is concentrated, the sūtra says:

> That noble disciple, even amid violent and perilous beings, is without obstruction or barrier; he enters the stream of the Dharma-water, even unto nirvāṇa.

Amid violent and perilous beings — in the jostle of a crowd, among rash and reckless people — this person who recollects the Buddha carries a mind without obstruction, without barrier. From the very beginning, the setting for recollection was never "first find a quiet place and sit cross-legged." It can operate in the market, at home, in the thick of handling worldly business—so long as the mind still remembers to return to those epithets, the whole rhythm will engage.

This is exactly why the prescription the Buddha wrote for Mahānāma was the six recollections, and not the four *jhānas*. Mahānāma was a householder running a family, his days full, his affairs tangled — he himself later complained to the Buddha that whenever he went in and out of the city, retinue before and behind, runaway elephants, madmen, and careering carriages would press in upon him in the streets. The Buddha did not ask him to become a different kind of person first. What the Buddha gave him was a practice that could be fitted into the life he already had.

### Recollection and Mindfulness: One Prepares, One Employs

To say recollection is another track is not to say it stands above mindfulness, nor that the foundations of mindfulness fall short of it. They belong to one path of training, each with its own place, and they support each other.

Recollection's work is to *prepare* the mind: to give rise to confidence, press down the five hindrances, knead the mind soft—soft enough to be usable. Mindfulness's work is to *employ* the mind: to guard awareness, sustain observation, and turn this now-workable mind onto body, feeling, mind, and mental objects, knowing them as they are, driving toward deeper insight.

One readies the mind; the other takes the readied mind and uses it. For householders, recollection is very often the entrance—because it is choosy about neither place nor posture. Once the mind has softened and steadied, the observation of the foundations of mindfulness has something to grip. Two tracks, neither higher nor lower: a sequence, and a division of labor.

### Recollecting the Buddha Means His Qualities, Not His Form

Last, one thing must be seen clearly, lest the first step go crooked.

Buddha-recollection in the earliest scriptures, from beginning to end, never asks you to "see" the Buddha's appearance. What it asks you to recollect is the Buddha's qualities—the qualities that string of epithets points to. Visualizing the Buddha's marks and splendor is another technique, developed by later practice (such as the sixteen contemplations of the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus*); it has its own place, but that is a later story, not the original face of this track.

At this root, buddha-recollection is simply this: recollect the Buddha's qualities, let the mind adhere along them again and again, and so confidence arises, the hindrances recede, the mind turns pliant. That is all—and that is already enough.

Then these "qualities of the Buddha" to be recollected—which ones, exactly, are they?

The Buddha did not leave His disciples to imagine them. He left a ready-made format—a string of epithets. The Pāli tradition calls the string the *itipi so*, after its first two words; in the Chinese translations it is the very list quoted above — the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Rightly and Fully Enlightened, on down to the Buddha, the World-Honored One. The first concrete practice on the track of buddha-recollection has never started from the word "Buddha." It starts from this string of epithets.
