## 1.3 The Six Recollections: One Engine, Six Objects

The last chapter ended by saying: what the Buddha left behind is not buddha-recollection alone. Recollection of the Buddha is only the first of the six recollections. In this chapter, we look at all six doorways together.

The six recollections are: recollection of the Buddha, of the Dharma, of the Saṅgha, of virtue, of generosity, and of the deities.

Many people take these six as six separate practices—finish recollecting the Buddha, switch methods and recollect the Dharma; finish that, switch again and recollect the Saṅgha—as if they were six divided roads.

But what the Buddha taught is not six roads. It is one engine, fitted with six objects.

### The Same Engine

Open the discourse from the Nigrodha Park—the *Saṃyukta Āgama* where the Buddha teaches Mahānāma the six recollections—and something remarkable shows itself: the sentences of the six recollections are nearly identical.

The Buddha first teaches recollection of the Tathāgata—the object is that string of epithets—then walks the chain of the mind's changes: the entanglement of craving does not arise; the mind goes straight; gladness, joy, tranquility, ease, concentration. Having taught recollection of the Buddha, He teaches recollection of the Dharma:

> Again, the noble disciple recollects the Dharma: the Blessed One's Dharma and discipline, which in the present can part one from the burning of birth-and-death, not bound to a season, penetrating the present, realized by oneself. When the noble disciple recollects the Dharma thus, he gives rise to no craving, hatred, or delusion … and so, perfumed by recollection of the Dharma, he ascends toward nirvāṇa.

The phrase "and so" is the scripture's own shorthand—meaning: the chain of changes in between is exactly the same as for recollection of the Buddha, and is not copied out again. Then recollection of the Saṅgha, of virtue, of generosity, of the deities: each segment closes with "gives rise to no craving, hatred, or delusion … and so, perfumed by [the recollection], ascends toward nirvāṇa," reproduced to the same pattern.

This is not scriptural laziness. It is deliberate. It is telling you: the six recollections are not six different practices but one practice with six focal points. The engine—the mind adheres to an object and is carried through transformation, link by link—is the same engine throughout. The object changes; the engine does not.

### Six Objects, Two Directions

Then why these six? Look closely at their arrangement and a very clear structure emerges: the first three face outward, the last three face inward.

The first three recollections—Buddha, Dharma, Saṅgha—all have their objects outside oneself. The Buddha is another who has already accomplished; the Dharma is the teaching that has been proclaimed; the Saṅgha is the company of those who have already walked the road through. Recollecting these three is a work of external borrowing: taking qualities others have already accomplished and laying them against one's own mind.

Recollecting the Buddha borrows "He has severed." The Buddha has severed craving, hatred, and delusion—you adhere to that fact, and what it counters is the panic of "I have not yet severed."

Recollecting the Dharma borrows "this Dharma is real, and verifiable." That sentence just quoted is densely packed: *apparent in the present life, it can part one from the burning of birth-and-death*—this Dharma, within the very life now before you, can quench the fire of birth-and-death; *not bound to a season*—no need to wait for a future life, no need to pick an auspicious time; *penetrating the present, realized by oneself*—it goes the whole way through, and is known by one's own direct realization. Recollecting the Dharma, what one adheres to is: this road can be verified.

Recollecting the Saṅgha borrows "someone has walked it through—and not only the Buddha." The sūtra says:

> The Blessed One's disciples have entered well, entered rightly, entered straight, entered truly, practicing in accord with the Dharma: there are those bound for stream-entry and those who have gained it, bound for once-returning and gained it, bound for non-returning and gained it, bound for arahantship and gained it. These are the four pairs and eight individuals, the noble ones; this is called the Blessed One's disciple-Saṅgha—accomplished in pure virtue, in concentration, in wisdom, in liberation, in the knowledge and vision of liberation—worthy to be welcomed, attended, and served, a good field of merit.

The "four pairs and eight individuals" are the noble ones who have realized the fruits, stage by stage, along the path. Recollecting the Saṅgha, what one adheres to is: others have walked this road through too—you are not walking alone.

The three together: severing is possible (Buddha), the Dharma is real (Dharma), the road is not solitary (Saṅgha). These are the three outward-facing anchor points.

The last three recollections—virtue, generosity, the deities—all have their objects within oneself. Their work is internal verification: confirming qualities already present in oneself.

Recollecting virtue confirms "my virtue already stands." What the Buddha teaches is that "the noble disciple recollects his own pure virtue"—recollecting virtue unbroken, unflawed, unstained, unmixed. Here one thing must be seen clearly: recollecting virtue is not combing through one's transgressions and sinking into remorse—that is recollecting backwards. Recollecting virtue recollects the share of purity one has already kept, already made firm; what rises is a grounded gladness, not guilt.

Recollecting generosity confirms "I am already able to give." Generosity means giving, the willingness to let go. What the Buddha teaches reads almost like a glad confession:

> The noble disciple recollects his own giving: I have gained good benefit—amid beings stained by stinginess, I have come to dwell apart from the stain of stinginess; not at home, I practice the giving of liberation, ever giving with my own hand, delighting in the practice of relinquishment, accomplished in impartial giving.

*I have gained good benefit*—amid a sea of stingy people, I, of all things, have parted from stinginess; *ever giving with my own hand*—again and again handing things over with my own hands. To recollect generosity is not to fault oneself for giving too little; it is to confirm that the freedom from stinginess, the readiness to hand over, has already arisen.

Recollection of the deities is the most easily misread. On its surface it looks like "recollecting the gods." But look at what the Buddha actually teaches:

> The noble disciple recollects the deities: there are the Four Great Kings, the Thirty-Three, the Yāma, the Tuṣita, the Nirmāṇarati, the Paranirmita-vaśavartin gods. If there be one of right faith, who at death is born among those deities—I too shall practice this right faith; they gained their pure virtue, giving, learning, relinquishment, and wisdom, and at death were born among those deities—I too shall now practice this virtue, giving, learning, and wisdom.

Those deities were born into the heavens because they had right faith, and virtue, giving, learning, wisdom; and "I too shall practice this right faith," "I too shall now practice this virtue, giving, learning, and wisdom." So recollecting the deities does not recollect gods at all. It takes the deities as a mirror, and in that mirror one sees the same several qualities in oneself.

The three together: virtue established, stinginess departed, the five qualities present. These are the three inward-facing anchor points.

The six recollections together form a complete loop: from "He has severed" all the way down to "I have established." From the Awakened One outside, to the Dharma outside, to the Saṅgha outside, then turning back to one's own virtue, one's own generosity, one's own qualities. Outer evidence comes to rest as inner evidence.

This is why it is these six, and why they stand in this order.

### Recollection of the Deities Is Prepared for the Moment of Death

Within the six, recollection of the deities holds one special use. It is the quiet ending the whole system leaves in place—anchoring the practice to the moment of death.

To see it, set two passages side by side.

The first is the recollection of the deities just read: what is recollected is right faith, and virtue, giving, learning, wisdom.

The second comes right before it—again in the Nigrodha Park, Mahānāma once more before the Buddha. This time he brings a very concrete worry:

> World-Honored One! This land of Kapilavatthu is secure and prosperous, its people thriving. Whenever I go in and out, with my many attendants, mad elephants, mad people, and runaway carriages are forever with me. I fear that I may live and die together with these mad things, forgetting recollection of the Buddha, the Dharma, the bhikṣu-Saṅgha. I wonder to myself: at the time of death, where shall I be reborn?

The city is crowded, the alleys narrow; mad elephants, mad people, careering carriages press in upon him daily. What he fears is not death but its suddenness—that his mind will panic and forget the Buddha, the Dharma, the Saṅgha; then where will he go?

The Buddha first sets his heart at rest: "Do not fear, do not dread; after death you will not be born into an evil destiny." Then He poses a simile in return:

> It is like a great tree that has long leaned, long inclined, long tended toward one side: if its root be cut, which way will it fall?

A great tree that has leaned one way, grown one way, for years—cut its root, and which way does it topple? Mahānāma answers: naturally, the way it has always leaned. The Buddha says: so it is with you—

> You have through the long night cultivated recollection of the Buddha, the Dharma, the Saṅgha. When life ends, though this body be burned by fire, or cast in a charnel-ground, blown by wind and parched by sun until it long becomes dust, yet the mind, the consciousness, long perfumed through the long night by right faith, perfumed by virtue, giving, learning, and wisdom, rises upward, headed for a place of peace, to be born hereafter among the gods.

The body will burn, be cast in the charnel-ground, weather to dust—but this mind, perfumed through the long night by right faith and by virtue, giving, learning, wisdom, will of itself go upward, toward peace. In the same exchange as the Pāli tradition transmits it, the Buddha gives a further simile: like a pot of ghee sunk in deep water and broken—the shards and chips of pottery sink down, but the ghee rises. The tree falls the way it has long leaned; the ghee rises the way its nature inclines—two similes, one meaning.

Note this—what the Buddha lists here, right faith plus virtue, giving, learning, wisdom, are exactly the five recollected in recollection of the deities. Two passages, one set of provisions.

Put the two together, and the true sense of recollecting the deities comes out. It is not longing for heavenly reward. It takes the deities as a measure and steeps right faith, and virtue, giving, learning, wisdom, into the mind, pass after pass, until they become something carried on one's person. Carried to where? To the moment of death. Because the streets are too chaotic and the elephants too fast, at the end there may be no time to recollect the Buddha at all; but a mind steeped through rises by itself—like the ghee in the broken pot, like the tree whose direction was settled long ago.

Here is where the Buddha fastened the tail-end of this practice: in the moment when Buddha, Dharma, and Saṅgha are all out of reach, there is still something in your mind that floats upward.

And "the Buddha out of reach" is something Mahānāma actually met. Still in the Nigrodha Park, he once heard that many monks had gathered in the dining hall sewing robes for the World-Honored One—the robes finished, the rains-retreat over, the World-Honored One would take up bowl and robe and wander among the people again. He hurried to the Buddha, nearly in panic:

> World-Honored One! My four limbs go slack, I lose my bearings in every direction, and the Dharma I have heard, I forget it all.

At the mere thought of "when shall I see the World-Honored One again," his limbs went weak, he could not tell the directions apart, and all the Dharma he had heard fell away. The Buddha's answer handed him the floor under the whole practice: whether you can see the World-Honored One or not, whether you can see the monks or not—"take right faith as foremost," take virtue, learning, giving, and wisdom as the root, and on these cultivate the recollections. And He gave a most tender simile: like a hen brooding her eggs, who needs only to have warmed and watched and nourished them in season—then even if at times she is careless, the chicks will peck their way out of the shell themselves. The work is perfumed in ordinary days; it is not pieced together on the spot.

### A Seed Called "Limitless"

One deeper thread lies hidden in the six recollections. Another discourse in the *Saṃyukta Āgama* records that the Venerable Mahākātyāyana—the disciple foremost in exposition—taught these six recollections to the monks in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika's Park, opening with the words that they are

> six dharmas that lead out of the place of suffering and up to the place of distinction, the one-vehicle path that purifies all beings, parts them from anguish and pain, and extinguishes all sorrow and grief, attaining the true suchness of things.

Six dharmas leading out of suffering and up to distinction—and they are "the one-vehicle path," one same road. In the same teaching as the Pāli tradition transmits it, Mahākātyāyana says further that recollection cultivated to its depth makes this mind become like space—abundant, expansive, without measure or limit, without enmity, without ill will.

"Without measure or limit"—the later Mahāyāna line "Infinite Light, Infinite Life" answers faintly to these words. And those three words, "the one-vehicle path," would later grow into a towering tree in the great sūtras. All that belongs to later chapters. Here, simply mark it: both seeds were already planted in this earliest teaching of the six recollections.

### Stepping Up to the Threshold

One engine, six objects, anchored outward and inward into a loop, with a death-insurance hidden inside and a seed called "limitless." This is the map the Buddha left.

Then—how deep can this practice carry the mind?

It can make the mind settled, pliant, glad. It can carry the mind to the door of concentration—yet, as the previous chapter said, it stops at the door and does not enter the deepest kind of concentration. Why? This boundary between "at the door" and "through the door"—what exactly is it? And how did it determine the road that buddha-recollection in China would later take?

In the next chapter, we step up to that threshold and look at it clearly.
