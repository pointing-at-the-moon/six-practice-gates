## 1.3 The Six Recollections: One Engine, Six Objects

The last chapter ended by saying: what the Buddha left behind is not buddha-recollection alone. Recollection of the Buddha is only the first of the six recollections. In this chapter, we look at all six doorways together.

The six recollections are: recollection of the Buddha, of the Dhamma, of the Saṅgha, of virtue, of generosity, and of the deities.

Many people take these six as six separate practices—finish recollecting the Buddha, switch methods and recollect the Dhamma; finish that, switch again and recollect the Saṅgha—as if they were six divided roads.

But what the Buddha taught is not six roads. It is one engine, fitted with six objects.

### The Same Engine

Open the discourse where the Buddha teaches Mahānāma the six recollections, and something remarkable shows itself: the sentences of the six recollections are nearly identical.

The Buddha first teaches recollection of the Buddha—the object is the nine epithets—then walks the chain of the mind's changes: craving, hatred, and delusion cannot wrap around; the mind goes straight; gladness, joy, tranquility, ease, concentration. Having taught recollection of the Buddha, He teaches recollection of the Dhamma—the object changes to a handful of qualities of the Dhamma—and the chain He walks is exactly the same, down to the identical closing sentence. Then recollection of the Saṅgha, of virtue, of generosity, of the deities: each one copied to the same pattern.

This is not scriptural laziness. It is deliberate. It is telling you: the six recollections are not six different practices but one practice with six focal points. The engine—the mind adheres to an object and is carried through transformation, link by link—is the same engine throughout. The object changes; the engine does not.

### Six Objects, Two Directions

Then why these six? Look closely at their arrangement and a very clear structure emerges: the first three face outward, the last three face inward.

The first three recollections—Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha—all have their objects outside oneself. The Buddha is another who has already accomplished; the Dhamma is the teaching that has been proclaimed; the Saṅgha is the company of those who have already walked the road through. Recollecting these three is a work of external borrowing: taking qualities others have already accomplished and laying them against one's own mind.

Recollecting the Buddha borrows "He has severed." The Buddha has severed craving, hatred, and delusion—you adhere to that fact, and what it counters is the panic of "I have not yet severed."

Recollecting the Dhamma borrows "this Dhamma is real, and verifiable." The Dhamma the Buddha taught is apparent in the present life, immediately effective, inviting inspection, leading toward the goal, individually known by the wise. Recollecting the Dhamma, what one adheres to is: this road can be verified.

Recollecting the Saṅgha borrows "someone has walked it through—and not only the Buddha." The "four pairs and eight individuals" are the noble ones who have realized the fruits, stage by stage, along the path. Recollecting the Saṅgha, what one adheres to is: others have walked this road through too—you are not walking alone.

The three together: severing is possible (Buddha), the Dhamma is real (Dhamma), the road is not solitary (Saṅgha). These are the three outward-facing anchor points.

The last three recollections—virtue, generosity, the deities—all have their objects within oneself. Their work is internal verification: confirming qualities already present in oneself.

Recollecting virtue confirms "my virtue already stands." Here one thing must be seen clearly: recollecting virtue is not combing through one's transgressions and sinking into remorse—that is recollecting backwards. Recollecting virtue recollects the share of purity one has already kept, already made firm; what rises is a grounded gladness, not guilt.

Recollecting generosity confirms "I am already able to give." Generosity means giving, the willingness to let go. To recollect it is not to fault oneself for giving too little; it is to confirm that the freedom from stinginess, the readiness to hand over, has already arisen in one's own mind.

Recollection of the deities is the most easily misread. On its surface it looks like "recollecting the gods," as if one were dwelling on beings in the heavens. But look at what the Buddha actually teaches: those deities were born into the heavens because they had faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom—"and I, too, possess the same faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom." So recollecting the deities does not recollect gods at all. It takes the deities as a mirror, and in that mirror one sees the same five qualities in oneself.

The three together: virtue established, stinginess departed, the five qualities present. These are the three inward-facing anchor points.

The six recollections together form a complete loop: from "He has severed" all the way down to "I have established." From the Awakened One outside, to the Dhamma outside, to the Saṅgha outside, then turning back to one's own virtue, one's own generosity, one's own qualities. Outer evidence comes to rest as inner evidence.

This is why it is these six, and why they stand in this order.

### Recollection of the Deities Is Prepared for the Moment of Death

Within the six, recollection of the deities holds one special use. It is the quiet ending the whole system leaves in place—anchoring the practice to the moment of death.

To see it, set two passages side by side.

The first is recollection of the deities, just described: the five things recollected are faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom.

The second is another of Mahānāma's visits to the Buddha. Mahānāma lived in Kapilavatthu, a city densely peopled and tight in its alleys, where stray elephants, horses, carts, and people came charging through the streets. He worried: if one day I die suddenly out there, flustered and scattered, with no time to recollect the Buddha, the Dhamma, the Saṅgha—where will I go?

The Buddha set his heart at rest, saying in effect: do not fear. If a person has steeped this mind through long years in faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom, then even when this body breaks, rots, and crumbles to dust, the mind so steeped will rise upward, headed for a higher place. And He gave a simile: like a pot of ghee sunk in a deep lake and broken open—the shards and chips sink down, but the ghee rises upward.

Note this—the five things the Buddha lists here, faith, virtue, learning, generosity, wisdom, match the five recollected in recollection of the deities word for word.

Put the two passages together, and the true sense of recollecting the deities comes out. It is not longing for heavenly reward. It takes the deities as a measure, and steeps faith, virtue, learning, generosity, and wisdom into the mind, pass after pass, until they become something carried on one's person. Carried to where? To the moment of death. Because the streets are too chaotic and the elephants too fast, at the end there may be no time to recollect the Buddha at all. But a mind steeped through with the five qualities rises by itself—like the ghee in the broken pot.

Here is where the Buddha fastened the tail-end of this practice: in the moment when Buddha, Dhamma, and Saṅgha are all out of reach, there is still something in your mind that floats upward.

### A Seed Called "Limitless"

One deeper thread lies hidden in the six recollections. Teaching them on another occasion, the Buddha says that practiced to its depth, this mind becomes "everywhere like space—abundant, expansive, limitless, free from enmity, free from ill will."

"Limitless"—this word later grew into that line at the heart of Mahāyāna buddha-recollection: "Infinite Light, Infinite Life." That belongs to later chapters. Here, simply mark it: the seed of "limitless" was already planted in the six recollections the Buddha taught at the very beginning.

### Stepping Up to the Threshold

One engine, six objects, anchored outward and inward into a loop, with a death-insurance hidden inside and a seed called "limitless." This is the map the Buddha left.

Then—how deep can this practice carry the mind?

It can make the mind settled, pliant, glad. It can carry the mind to the door of concentration—yet, as the previous chapter said, it stops at the door and does not enter the deepest kind of concentration. Why? This boundary between "at the door" and "through the door"—what exactly is it? And how did it determine the road that buddha-recollection in China would later take?

In the next chapter, we step up to that threshold and look at it clearly.
