## 1.4 Recollection and the Jhānas: To the Door of Concentration

The last chapter stepped up to the threshold. The question: how deep, in the end, can this practice of buddha-recollection carry the mind?

First divide "concentration" into two stages.

One stage is called access concentration (*upacāra-samādhi*)—call it "at the door of concentration." When the mind arrives here, the five hindrances—sensual desire, ill-will, sloth-and-torpor, restlessness-and-remorse, doubt—have been temporarily pressed down; joy, tranquility, and settledness have all arisen; the mind is very steady. But it has not yet shrunk to a single point and locked fast. It is steady, yet still moving.

The other stage is called absorption (*appanā-samādhi*)—"through the door." The mind draws in, locks onto one point, and moves no more. This is the true depth of meditative concentration.

Buddha-recollection reaches the door; it does not pass through. Why?

### Why Buddha-Recollection Stops at the Door

The key lies in the shape of the object.

Some objects are single-faceted. Like a candle flame: you gaze at it, the gaze narrows, and the flame slowly condenses into a single interior point of light. The mind can keep gathering, gathering, until it locks on this one point and holds still. Mindfulness of breath (*ānāpānasati*) is of this kind: one breath in, one breath out, a single plain strand on which the mind can gather to a point. Objects like this can pass through the door.

The object of buddha-recollection is multi-faceted. Nine epithets are nine doorways; six recollections are six directions. As the mind recollects, it finishes looking through one doorway and must move to the next—by its very nature this object asks to be walked among. It is like standing before a wall hung with nine paintings: each painting must be viewed on its own, and when one is done, the gaze has to move on to the next. There is no way to shrink nine paintings into one point and stare.

So in buddha-recollection the mind is always moving within "the whole wall": settled, but never locked. It reaches the settledness at the door; it does not reach the locked stillness inside.

### This Is Not Weakness. This Is Design.

Here is the sentence that matters most in this chapter: reaching the door is not a half-finished failure to get through the door. It is the built-in terminus of buddha-recollection as the Buddha designed it.

Think back. The Buddha taught buddha-recollection to householders like Mahānāma—telling him to recollect while walking, while sitting, while working, while the children clamored around him. The absorption inside the door requires sitting still and entering deep; the settledness at the door holds up in walking, working, in the thick of the noise. And that is exactly what buddha-recollection is for: a steadiness that can be carried anywhere. It was never designed for the sealed retreat and the deep absorption.

And the "moving among many facets"? That is not your skill falling short; that is what buddha-recollection is. If the mind truly locked dead on one epithet and stopped moving, it would no longer be recollection of the Buddha—it would have become the breath-gazing kind of practice. The movement is recollection's proper work, not its defect.

Moreover, the door is already enough—enough to launch insight, enough to see the path.

Among the old masters there is such a story. An elder was practicing buddha-recollection. Once, Māra conjured the form of a Buddha to disturb him. Gazing at that splendid counterfeit, the elder turned one thought: this is a mere phantom, and it is already this majestic—how much more splendid the real Buddha, who is free of craving, hatred, and delusion altogether. At that one thought, joy welled up; from that joy he raised insight, observed onward, and attained arahantship.

Look at the shape of it—his whole course set out from the "door" of buddha-recollection, and from beginning to end he never entered the deepest absorption.

For anyone who recollects the Buddha, this is a weighty reminder: if your recollection has produced no deep meditative experiences, no floating descent into absorption, do not conclude that your recollection "lacks power" or has gone wrong. The settledness at the door is exactly where buddha-recollection is meant to arrive; and standing at the door, one can already raise insight, already see the path.

### How That Door Was Later Crossed

That said, the practitioners of China did, later, find a way through that door.

The way is simple to state: if "many-faceted" cannot enter, then gather the object into "one facet."

Gather the nine epithets, the whole sky of the Buddha's qualities, into one visualized form of the Buddha; into one ray of light (the *ūrṇā* between His brows, the radiance at His crown); and at last, into one name—"Homage to the Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha." Once the object is gathered from many facets into one, the mind can lock to a point as if gazing at the candle flame—and the absorption inside the door is finally reachable.

Gathering the whole sky of the Buddha's qualities into a single recited name is the most consequential creative step Chinese buddha-recollection ever took. The contemplation of the Buddha, the seeing of the Buddha, the holding of the name that fill the rest of this book—even the Chan question "Who is recollecting the Buddha?"—are all, at the bone, doing this same thing: gathering the object down to the point where the mind can lock.

Here, Part One is complete.

From the root, we have seen clearly the recollection the Buddha taught with His own hands. It is another track, distinct from mindfulness (chapter one). It has a ready-made format, the nine epithets (chapter two). It is a whole system—six objects, facing outward and inward, with a deathbed insurance hidden inside (chapter three). And it can carry the mind to the door of concentration, leaving there a threshold that later generations would cross (this chapter).

All of this is the foundation that the Buddha and the earliest scriptures laid down on the soil of India.

Now the story changes lands. This buddha-recollection, crossing the sea to China for the first time—what did that look like? When Chinese readers first read the word *niànfó*, what was it they actually read?
