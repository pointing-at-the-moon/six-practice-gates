## 3.3 From Recollection to Calling

By this point, the word *niànfó*—buddha-recollection—has found firm footing in China. But beneath the word *niàn*, two different actions lie hidden.

One is *niàn*—the mind's affair. To recollect, to hold dear, to keep the Buddha on the mind and not let go. It can be voiced or unvoiced; its home territory is the mind.

The other is *chēng*—the mouth's affair. To call, to invoke, to cry the Buddha's name aloud. It is almost always aimed at a name, and almost always voiced; its home territory is the mouth.

Chinese buddha-recollection, from beginning to end, runs on these two actions. Later generations often suppose that buddha-recollection turned, step by step, from mental recollection into vocal calling—as if *chēng* had replaced *niàn*. It did not. Neither verb ever displaced the other; the two have been bound together all along, like the lining and the outer face of a single garment.

Let us watch how these two actions wove themselves together, step by step.

### The Name Begins to Circulate

First comes the vow we met in the last chapter—the one that sends the name abroad: let my name spread through the eight directions, above and below, let the Buddhas praise it, and let all beings who "hear my name" leap in joy to be born in my land.

Mark that word—*hear*. On the beings' side, the action is hearing; but for this side to hear, someone on the other side must speak the name out. A name must first be spoken, passed on, called, before anyone can hear it. Right here, the seat for the action *chēng* is quietly left empty, waiting for someone to fill it.

### A Number: Ten Recollections

Next comes the most famous vow of all—the eighteenth: if beings, sincerely trustful and desiring to be born in my land, should "even ten recollections" not be born there, I shall not become a Buddha.

Inside this vow, for the first time, a number appears: ten.

Before this, the Buddha had said only "always recollect me"—no count. Now there is a count: ten. And the moment there is a count, this *niàn* changes from one unbroken stretch of mental continuity into discrete beads, things that can be counted one by one. And what is the handiest way to count them off, bead by bead? Aloud—one voice, one recollection; one recollection, one voice.

Yet the word this vow uses is still *niàn*; it does not say whether the ten are held in the mind or called by the mouth. It leaves the door open a crack. And through that crack, the patriarchs who came after gradually read "ten recollections" as "ten utterances of the name."

### The Hour of Death: When Recollection Fails, Call

The place where the two actions are truly pressed into one spot, and seen most clearly, is the passage on lower-grade lower-birth in the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus*—the deathbed scene of a man who has committed grave evil.

The sūtra says: at the hour of death this man meets a good friend, who first "instructs him to recollect the Buddha"—to recollect Him in the mind. But the man, "pressed by suffering, cannot complete the recollection"—the pain squeezes so hard that he can no longer even settle his mind to recollect.

So the good friend changes his instruction and says something of utmost weight: "If you cannot recollect that Buddha, call [the name] with self-surrender: Namo Amitāyus Buddha." If you can no longer recollect in the mind, then cry it aloud—cry "Namo Amitābha Buddha."

And so this man, "with sincere mind, letting the voice not be broken, completing ten recollections," calls "Namo Amitābha Buddha"—with one earnest heart, letting voice follow voice without break, until the full ten are called. The sūtra says: "Because of calling the Buddha's name, in recollection-upon-recollection, eighty kotis of kalpas of birth-and-death karma are destroyed."

Look closely at how the two actions run a relay in this passage.

It begins with *niàn*. Only when recollection can no longer move does the instruction change to *chēng*—*chēng* is *niàn*'s backup: when pain has crowded the mind until it can no longer quiet itself to recollect, the buddha-name cried out of the mouth is the last rope still within grasp.

But look again: where does the karma-destroying effect land? It lands "in recollection-upon-recollection"—the reckoning returns to *niàn*. *Chēng* presses that one recollection out of the mind into voice; the effect is still entered in *niàn*'s ledger.

See it now—*chēng* and *niàn* were never two things. They are two faces of one thing. When the mind cannot recollect, the mouth's calling holds the line; while the mouth is calling, the effect still belongs to the mind's recollection. At the hour of death, neither face can be spared.

### Even in Shandao, the Lining Is Still *Niàn*

Later, in the Tang dynasty, Master Shandao established calling the name as the principal act of Pure Land practice—he named it the primary settled act. From him onward, holding the name—calling the buddha-name phrase after phrase—became the most widespread practice in households beyond counting.

Many people conclude from this that Shandao turned buddha-recollection into calling with the mouth.

But open Shandao's own definition of the primary settled act, and the word he uses is, of all things, not *chēng*:

> Single-mindedly holding-in-mind Amitābha's name, in walking, standing, sitting, lying, not asking about time—long or short—holding-holding unceasingly—this is named the primary settled act.

"Holding-in-mind," not "calling." "Holding-holding unceasingly," not "voice-after-voice unbroken." The load-bearing word he chose is *niàn*.

Why? Because Shandao knew exactly what he was measuring. Whether this practice is on the mark is measured by whether the mind's holding stays connected, not by whether the voice rings out. A person can keep the buddha-name going on his lips while his mind has long since flown to the ends of the sky—that does not count as the primary settled act. A person can pass a stretch without a sound while his mind never leaves the name for an instant—that still counts.

So even in Shandao, the very master who installed vocal calling as the principal act, *chēng* is the form displayed on the surface, and *niàn* remains the measuring-rule hidden within. A buddha-name called loud on the lips must still be held true in the mind. Calling with the mouth and no mind behind it is an empty shell; Shandao's name for it is "not from a sincere mind"—not true enough.

One thing in passing: this interlocking of *niàn* and *chēng* is not the property of the Pure Land school alone. The One-Practice Samādhi taught by the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī has the same shape: first "tether the mind to one buddha"—fasten the mind on a single Buddha; then "devotedly call the name"—call that name with undivided focus; and thereby "recollection-upon-recollection unbroken"—one recollection following another without break. Tethering the mind, calling the name, recollection-upon-recollection: the same pieces, clasped together. The interlocking of these two actions is the common grammar of all Chinese buddha-recollection, not the private possession of any one school.

### What That Land Is Really Like

Buddha-recollection has now gathered its three great pieces: the Buddha has stepped to the front of the stage (the seeing of the Buddha), the buddha-land has been built (the construction), and *niàn* and *chēng* have been clasped into one.

But one thing has so far only been pointed at from a distance, never examined up close: that buddha-land called the Land of Bliss—what is it actually like? What is inside it? The vows Amitābha made—how is the whole set arranged? And how, exactly, must a person practice in order to be born there?

Three sūtras answer these three questions, and later generations call them together the Three Pure Land Sūtras: the *Sūtra of Immeasurable Life* tells of the forty-eight great vows; the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus* teaches the method of the sixteen contemplations; the *Amitābha Sūtra* tells of the adornments of the Land of Bliss and of "upholding the name."

That is Part Four—the liveliest stretch of this whole book.
