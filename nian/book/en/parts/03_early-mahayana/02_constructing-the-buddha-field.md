## 3.2 Constructing the Buddha-Field: How a Buddha's Land Is Built

The last chapter ended on a question. The Amitābha you recollect lives in a place called Sumati—the place everyone would later call the Land of Bliss. So what, exactly, is this "buddha-field"? And how was it built up, stroke by stroke?

In truth, the *Pratyutpanna-Samādhi Sūtra* had already pushed the door open. In that sūtra, Amitābha says it in his own words: "Those who wish to be born in my land—let them always recollect me." If you wish to come and be born in my land, then recollect me, constantly. And a disciple asks, face to face: "What Dharma must I hold in order to be born in Amitābha's buddha-field?"—what practice must one take up, to be born into Amitābha's land?

You see—in this sūtra, "being born into a buddha-field" is already a goal one can ask about, can aim for. Only, this sūtra never goes on to paint what that buddha-field looks like, or how it came to be.

The painting was taken up by two other sūtras. And here is the interesting part: these two sūtras were translated by the same translator—still that same Lokakṣema—within a few years of each other. And in them he brought across two very different ways of building a buddha-field.

### The First: An "Unshakable" Buddha, and His Land

The first is called the *Sūtra of Akṣobhya's Buddha-Field*.

Long, long ago, in the east, there was a world where a Buddha was teaching the Dharma. In that assembly sat a *bhikṣu*, and he made a vow. From its very first clause, the vow was hard: he swore that until he attained buddhahood, he would never give rise to a single thought of anger toward any living being—not even a flying insect, not even a crawling ant.

Because of this vow of non-anger, the *bhikṣu* received a name: Akṣobhya—"Unshakable," he who cannot be moved.

What follows is a long chain of vows, nearly every one of them an "I will not": no anger, no lust, no stealing, no false speech… clause by clause, he set himself up as a man whom no storm, however great, could move. In time he became a Buddha, and his land was called Abhirati—"Marvelous Joy."

Look at the axis of this path: the buddha-field is the result of this bodhisattva's own standing firm. He first made himself into an unshakable man, and the land he gained on becoming a Buddha is simply what that unshakableness looks like, grown naturally outward. The center of gravity of the vows is "what I will not do."

### The Second: A Buddha Who "Chose and Chose Again," and His Land

The second is called the *Sūtra of Immeasurable Purity and Equal Awakening*—the earliest Chinese translation on the "Amitābha" line.

The protagonist here is a bodhisattva named Dharma-Treasury. He stands before a Buddha of long ago, the buddha Lokeśvararāja. But his first move is nothing like Akṣobhya's. He does not vow straightaway. He opens by asking the Buddha: recount to me, one by one, the excellences of all the buddha-fields; I will follow what I hear, choose what I want, and build it into a land of my own.

Lokeśvararāja consented. The sūtra says this Buddha displayed to Dharma-Treasury "two hundred and ten billion" buddha-fields, one after another, together with all in them that was good and bad, beautiful and ugly.

Two hundred and ten billion—the number is not exaggeration. It is saying: the buddha-fields are countless, each with its own face, laid out there for the choosing.

So the bodhisattva Dharma-Treasury, out of this vast catalogue, picked the excellences he wanted, one by one, fitted them together, and bound them into twenty-four vows—the blueprint of the land he meant to build.

This is an entirely new thought. With Akṣobhya, the buddha-field is the byproduct of cultivation; here, the buddha-field has become something that can be chosen, matched, and designed.

And what did he choose—what did he vow? The first two vows make it plain. First: when I have become a buddha, let there be in my land no hells, no hungry ghosts, no beasts. Second: let those who are born into my land never again fall into the hells, the hungry-ghost realms, or animal birth.

Have you noticed?—the center of gravity of the vows has turned clean over. Akṣobhya's vows are "what I will not do"; Dharma-Treasury's vows are "what I will give to those who come to be born in my land." One faces inward and establishes the self; one faces outward and receives others. One is self-steadying; the other is the saving of others.

Even the names carry this difference. "Akṣobhya"—Unshakable—speaks of the bodhisattva's own hardness. "Amitābha"—Immeasurable—speaks of the land's light and the land's life, which are there to be given. And "Sumati," the "Land of Bliss"—bliss, quite directly, prepared in advance for the ones who will come to live there.

### Two Paths, Arriving Together

Hold on to one fact: these two paths—Akṣobhya's self-steadying, Amitābha's saving of others—were carried into the Chinese world together, by the same translator, at very nearly the same time.

So from the very beginning, the "buddha-field" in the Chinese mind was never a single road. It was two roads running side by side. That the Land of Bliss later stood out alone, that everyone came to recollect Amitābha—that is the outcome of a thousand years and more of slow development; it does not mean Akṣobhya's road was a grade lower. At the starting line, they were two answers standing on equal footing.

A word, in passing, about format. Each of Dharma-Treasury's twenty-four vows has the same shape: "When I have become a buddha… (such-and-such shall be so); and if not, I shall not become a buddha"—"if this is not fulfilled, I shall not become a buddha." Buddhahood itself, the greatest matter there is, staked on the keeping of every single vow. This formula is the skeleton of the famous "forty-eight vows" to come. The forty-eight do not start from scratch; they take these twenty-four and refine them, multiply them—and among the additions is one supremely weighty clause: "even ten recollections."

That clause gave the whole direction of buddha-recollection another push.

### From "Recollecting" to "Calling the Name"

Come back, now, to this newly built buddha-field. Among Dharma-Treasury's vows there is one—later numbered the seventeenth—which says: let my name be heard throughout the numberless buddha-fields of the ten directions; and let the beings of the ten directions, all who "hear my name," leap in joy and come to be born in my land.

Notice the verb here—no longer simply "recollect," but "hear the name."

The ticket of entry into the buddha-field has quietly shifted: from holding the Buddha's qualities and the Buddha's form in the mind, recollecting them over and over—toward hearing, and crying out, that one name.

From recollecting, to calling the name; from remembrance held in the mind, to a name called with the mouth. A small step, easy to overlook—yet it is the key step by which buddha-recollection in China would later walk into every home and household.
