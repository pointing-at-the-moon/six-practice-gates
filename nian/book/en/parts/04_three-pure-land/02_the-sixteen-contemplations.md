## 4.2 The Sixteen Contemplations: Seeing That Land into View

### A Mother's Request

This sūtra, the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Amitāyus*, opens out of a human tragedy.

In the city of Rājagṛha, the prince Ajātaśatru threw his father the king into prison and cut off his food, meaning to starve him to death. His mother, Queen Vaidehī, secretly smeared honey and parched flour on her body and poured grape juice into her ornaments, and went in to visit and feed the old king. When this was discovered, Ajātaśatru would have killed his own mother too; only the pleading of his ministers held him back, and he locked her away as well.

In her cell, Vaidehī was broken with grief. She gazed from afar toward Vulture-Peak and pleaded with the Buddha: World-Honored One, expound for me a place free of sorrow and affliction, that I may be reborn there — I want nothing more of this defiled world. I beg you: teach me to see that pure destination.

So the Buddha emitted light from between His brows, revealing the measureless pure buddha-lands of the ten directions, each appearing before Vaidehī's eyes. She looked through them all and chose one — the Western Land of Bliss. And then she spoke the line: "Teach me to think upon it; teach me to receive it rightly." Teach me how to think this land out — how to see it into view.

Hold on to this opening. The sixteen contemplations did not grow out of some eminent monk's meditation. They were called forth by a mother at the end of her road, crying out from a prison cell. From the very first, they were prepared for the people who suffer most.

### Not Sixteen Techniques, but One Ladder

What the Buddha taught Vaidehī was sixteen contemplations.

Many people read these as sixteen visualization techniques set side by side — first contemplate the sun, second the water, third the ground — like a checklist. But it is not a checklist. It is a **designed ladder**: rung after rung, with a beginning and an end, every rung set in place so that you can step onto the next.

The ladder runs in three segments: first look at the "outside" (the environment of that land), then at the "inside" (the Buddha's body), and finally come down to the "person" (the nine grades of rebirth).

### Beginning from a Setting Sun

The first contemplation is the sun-contemplation — face the west, fix your gaze on the sun about to set, and watch it until it is shaped like a suspended drum.

Why begin from the setting sun? Because the setting sun is something you can see simply by opening your eyes. Anyone not blind can see it. The Buddha set the ladder's first rung where every person can step onto it. On this most ordinary of objects, you first train one thing until it is familiar: fixing the gaze, holding the mind, keeping it from scattering.

The second, the water-contemplation, shows even finer craftsmanship. The ground of the Land of Bliss is beryl, transparent and reflecting through and through — but we have never seen a beryl ground, and we cannot imagine one. What to do? The Buddha has you contemplate water first: clear water you have seen. Then think the water into ice: ice you have also seen. Then through that crystalline ice, imagine the translucent beryl ground. Take something you have seen, something similar, as a stepping-stone, and step by step walk toward the real scene you have never seen.

That is how this ladder is built: every rung borrows what you already have, to reach what you do not yet have.

### First the Image, Then the True Buddha

When the outer scene is complete — the ground, the jewel-trees, the jewel-ponds, the whole land at once (the sixth, the comprehensive contemplation) — the ladder turns inward: the Buddha's body.

But here the Buddha does not tell you to contemplate His true body straightaway. He first has you contemplate a lotus-throne (the seventh), then a Buddha-"image" (the eighth) — a Buddha as in a painting or a statue, not the true Buddha.

Why contemplate a "false" image first? Master Zhiyi put the reason most plainly: the Buddha's Dharma-body is subtle to the utmost; it cannot be contemplated in a single leap. So first contemplate the image, and when that has grown fluent and practiced, the contemplation of the Dharma-body becomes easy.

This is the ladder's most essential mechanism — "the false for entering the true": first borrow a false image to settle the mind and train it, then cross over from that image to the true Buddha. The image is not a second-rate substitute. It is a necessary rung on the way to the true Buddha.

And right inside this eighth contemplation, the image-contemplation, the Buddha speaks the deepest line of the entire sūtra:

> This mind makes the Buddha; this mind is the Buddha.

When you contemplate the Buddha, this mind of yours is, at that very moment, the thirty-two marks and the eighty minor characteristics. This mind makes the Buddha — and this mind that can make the Buddha is itself the Buddha. The Buddha you see has never been outside the mind. This line and the line from Part Three on the *pratyutpanna-samādhi* — the mind makes the Buddha; the mind is the Buddha — are one bloodline.

When the image-contemplation has grown fluent, the ninth contemplates the true body — the true body of Amitāyus Buddha: eighty-four thousand marks, each mark holding eighty-four thousand minor characteristics, each characteristic emitting eighty-four thousand rays of light, the light illumining the worlds of the ten directions. And then the sūtra speaks eight characters — the heart of the whole Pure Land teaching of reception:

> Gathering in and never abandoning the sentient beings who are mindful of the Buddha.

So long as you are mindful of the Buddha, this light gathers you in and holds you, and it never lets go.

### The Nine Grades: Not Three Ranks in Three Levels, but Nine Kinds of Conditions

The ladder's final three contemplations (the fourteenth through the sixteenth) teach the nine grades of rebirth — upper, middle, and lower classes, each class divided again into upper, middle, and lower, making nine grades in all.

Many people assume the nine grades sort human beings into three ranks with three levels apiece, like marks on an examination. Not so. What differs across the nine grades is four things, each varying on its own: what provisions of practice you bring, what ceremony the Buddha comes to receive you with, how long the lotus takes to open, and what fruit you finally attain. The nine kinds of person are nine different combinations of these four, each with its own shape — not nine notches on a single ruler.

Master Shandao saw deeper still. He said: these nine grades are all ordinary beings of the five defilements, living after the Buddha's passing — not one of them is a born sage. The difference lies not in their "rank" but in what conditions they met: the upper three met Mahāyāna conditions; the middle three, the conditions of the smaller vehicle; the lower three met evil conditions — yet at the last moment met a good friend. The nine grades are not nine steps of a staircase. They are nine paths along which ordinary beings fan out, each according to the conditions they happened to meet.

### The Lowest Grade of All: The One Who Cannot Even Be Mindful

The lowest of the nine — lower-grade lower-birth — lays bare the design floor of that land.

The sūtra says: there is a kind of person who has committed the five heinous deeds and the ten evil deeds, every manner of unwholesomeness. At the end of such a person's life, the fires of hell already appearing before him, he meets a good friend who teaches him to be mindful of the Buddha — but "pressed by suffering, he has no leisure to be mindful of the Buddha." The pain is such that he can no longer even settle his mind to recollect.

So the good friend changes the instruction: "If you cannot be mindful of that Buddha, then make the sound — call out in refuge to Amitāyus Buddha."

And so, "with sincere mind and sound unbroken, accomplishing ten recollections, reciting *Namo Amitābha Buddha*" — with an earnest heart, cry upon cry, until the ten recollections are complete. And the sūtra says that just so, "in thought after thought the sins of eighty *koṭi* eons of birth-and-death are removed," and he is born in the Land of Bliss — only born inside a lotus, which takes twelve great *kalpas* to open.

Do you see it? The gate of that land stands open all the way down to here — open to a person who has done every evil and who, at death, cannot even be mindful of the Buddha. Even he can enter.

This is the same "lowest threshold" we met in the last chapter, in the eighteenth vow — appearing once more in this sūtra. A land lays its rescue all the way down to the feet of the most unbearable person. It is the one signature the Three Pure Land Sūtras sign over and over: the threshold set at its lowest; the hand reached out to its farthest.

### Ten Thousand Words, Gathered into One Name

The sixteen contemplations run from a single setting sun, to the true Buddha of eighty-four thousand rays of light, to the nine grades of rebirth — an immense edifice of contemplation.

Yet at the very end of this sūtra, the Buddha gathers all those ten thousand words, gently, into one sentence. He says to Ānanda:

> Hold well to these words. To hold these words is to hold the name of Amitāyus Buddha.

After the whole long circuit of the sixteen contemplations, the Buddha says at the last: what holds all of this is, in truth, holding that one name.

Then, if in the end one name can gather all of it in, could it be that one need not even climb the sixteen rungs — that this one name alone is enough?

The third sūtra, the *Amitābha Sūtra*, does exactly that.
