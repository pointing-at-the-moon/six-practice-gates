## 2.1 The *Niànfó* Sūtra Before the Word: The *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*

Tradition tells that Emperor Ming of the Later Han dreamed one night of a golden man flying before his palace hall. The next day he asked his ministers, and one said: in the West there is a god whose name is "Buddha." So Emperor Ming sent envoys westward to seek the teaching. They returned with scriptures and images, the White Horse Monastery was built, and one scripture was translated—the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*.

However much of this origin story is history and however much is legend, one thing is broadly credible: the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections* is among the earliest Buddhist scriptures translated on Chinese soil—the first time the Chinese read the Buddha's words in their own script.

And inside this first scripture lies a fact easily overlooked, yet very weighty.

### A Sūtra Without the Word *Niànfó*

Search all forty-two chapters of the sūtra, and the two characters *niànfó*—"recollect the Buddha"—do not appear even once.

It is not that the text was too short to fit them. Across the full forty-two chapters, the word *niànfó* occurs zero times.

Yet the act of *niàn*—recollection—is everywhere. The sūtra speaks of "recollecting the path," of "recollecting impermanence," of "recollecting the four great elements within the body," of "mentally recollecting my precepts"—and there is that famous phrase, "the space of a single breath." Of the six recollection objects Part One walked through—Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, deities—several already cast their shadows here. One item alone is absent: recollection of the Buddha.

What is going on?

It is not that the Chinese of that age set no store by buddha-recollection. It is that the word *niànfó* had not yet been coined in the Luoyang of that day. But the work it would one day do—recollection—was already running in this sūtra, only under a different name. That name was *niàndào*: recollecting the path.

### What *Niàn* Recollected First Was the Path

In Chapter Fourteen, the Buddha speaks a line that cuts straight to the bone:

> What do I recollect? I recollect the path. What do I practice? I practice the path. What do I speak? I speak the path. I recollect the true path, not neglecting it for even a *kṣaṇa*.

"I recollect the true path"—settling the mind on the path that is real. "Not neglecting it for even a *kṣaṇa*"—not letting it slip from the mind for so much as a moment. Look: this is exactly the track Part One described. The lifeline of recollection lies not in recollecting hard, but in recollecting without break. When the Buddha taught Mahānāma to recollect while walking, while sitting, while working, what He wanted was precisely this "not neglecting it for a moment." Now that same unbroken continuity was written down in Chinese characters for the first time—only, the object written was "the path."

Chapter Sixteen carries it forward: "Practicing for a single day, constantly recollecting the path and practicing the path, one thereby attains the faculty of faith, and one's merit is immeasurable." That one word, "constantly," is still the same unbrokenness. And Chapter Forty says: "An upright mind recollecting the path can be freed from all sufferings." An "upright mind"—a mind neither crooked nor leaning, neither scattered nor disordered.

Three passages of *niàndào* raise up the earliest skeleton of buddha-recollection on Chinese soil.

Then why *niàndào*, and not *niànfó*?

The reason is plain. The character *dào*—the path—the Chinese had long known: the Confucians spoke of the *dào*, the Daoists spoke of the *dào*, it was on the lips of every street and lane. But *fó*—"Buddha"—was a raw new word, freshly carried over the sea, and no one's mouth was yet used to it. The translators took the path of least resistance: take a word you already understand, and let it catch a practice you do not yet understand. So when recollection first set foot on Chinese soil, what it recollected was the path.

The step looks like a detour, but it left a door open. Once the word *fó* had settled in and grown familiar in China, *niàn-fó* could simply step into the place *niàn-dào* had held—the whole architecture of recollection had already been laid out for it by *niàndào*.

### As Long as a Single Breath

There is one more passage in the sūtra—very short, yet it has been handed down for nearly two thousand years.

> The Buddha asked a śramaṇa: "In what span does human life reside?" The reply: "In a span of several days." The Buddha said: "You are not yet capable of the path."
> He asked another: "In what span does human life reside?" The reply: "In the span of a single meal." The Buddha said: "You are not yet capable of the path."
> He asked another: "In what span does human life reside?" The reply: "In the space of a single breath." The Buddha said: "Excellent! You may indeed be called one who cultivates the path."

How long does a person live? That last śramaṇa answered best: as long as a single breath. One breath goes out and does not come back in, and this life is over.

This brief exchange lights up, at one stroke, two things Part One spoke of: mindfulness of breath, and recollection of death. To watch this breath going out and coming in is to watch the nakedest truth of a life—that it is thin, no thicker than one breath. Three times, too, the sūtra tells its reader to "recollect impermanence": gazing upon heaven and earth, upon mountains and rivers, upon the abundant flourishing of the ten thousand things, recollect through all of it that it is impermanent.

Part One said that the tail of the six recollections is recollection of the deities, and that recollection of the deities is in truth an insurance laid up for the moment of death. This earliest scripture of China never uses the words "recollect the deities," yet with these few strokes—"the space of a single breath," "recollect impermanence"—it planted the very same thing: at all times remember death, remember impermanence. The urgency in later Chinese buddha-recollection—every thought aware of impermanence, honestly working the path—has its root right here.

### It Is Not in Concentration. It Is in Honesty.

This sūtra holds one more silent fact: from beginning to end, not a single word of meditative absorption appears in it. No "meditative absorption," no "*samādhi*," no *vitakka* or *vicāra*, no "joy," no "ease," no "one-pointedness." It simply does not live inside the topic of concentration at all.

Yet it is not shallow for that. It has its own close weave and its own measure—only it does not speak through the technical terms of meditative absorption. It speaks in another tone of voice: an honest one.

In Chapter Thirty-Two the Buddha gives the simile of the lute. A renunciant was practicing, and his mind had grown restless. The Buddha asked him: when you lived at home, did you play the lute? If the strings are loose, will they sound? They will not. If they are too tight? The sound breaks. And if tension and slackness are tuned exactly right? Then all the notes resound together. The Buddha said: learning the path is just the same—too tight, and the mind snaps; too loose, and it cannot be lifted; tune this mind onto the middle way, neither loose nor tight, and the path can be attained.

This is the sūtra's tone of voice: it does not press you toward deep concentration; it asks only that you tune the mind even and steady, and recollect on, honestly, day after day. It says practice is like forging iron, refined pass after pass; like fruit high on the tree, reached one bough at a time. Slow, but unbroken.

The phrases later Chinese buddha-recollection would say most often—"honest *niànfó*," "plain and honest," "unbroken and dense"—have their roots in this earliest little scripture.

One thing in passing: at about the same time, another translator, An Shigao, was also translating scriptures in Luoyang. What he brought was a different kind of text altogether—about the breath from beginning to end: one in, one out, how to count it, how to follow it, how to fix it. So from the very start, *niàn* in China walked in two tones of voice side by side: one like the *Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections*—honest, close-woven, about how to live and how to act; one like An Shigao—fine-grained, technical, about the work of one sitting after another. These two tones will tangle with each other all the way to the end of this book—but that is a later story.

### The Buddha Has Not Yet Stepped to the Front

Come back to this sūtra. *Niàn* had arrived in China; but what it recollected was the path. The Buddha Himself had not yet been set up as the object to be recollected.

So when did the Buddha step from backstage to the front—to become the one whom people would recollect with their own lips, and even long to see with their own eyes?

When did people stop being content to recollect the path, and want instead to meet the Buddha face to face—to stand in His presence?

That step had to wait for a new wind to enter China—the early Mahāyāna, and the strange sūtra it carried: stand for seven days, until the Buddhas of the ten directions appear before your eyes.

That is the next sūtra's story.
