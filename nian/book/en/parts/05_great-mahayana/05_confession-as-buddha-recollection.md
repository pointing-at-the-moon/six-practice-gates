## 5.5 Confession as Buddha-Recollection: The Golden Drum in a Dream

In the last chapter, whether it was receiving the precepts or extinguishing offenses, everything leaned on confession. So what, exactly, is confession? And what does it have to do with buddha-recollection?

In the *Golden Light Sūtra* there is a chapter called the Chapter of the Dream-Golden-Drum Repentance. It tells the whole matter of confession as a dream.

### The Golden Drum in the Dream

The sūtra says: there was a bodhisattva who, one night, had a dream.

In the dream he saw an immense golden drum, blazing with golden light. A brahmin walked up, raised the drumstick, and struck it. The moment the drum sounded, golden light came pouring out of it, ray upon ray; and within that light, the Buddhas of the ten directions all appeared at once. From the air, the Buddhas, with one voice, spoke a set of confession verses.

The bodhisattva, inside the dream, heard those verses.

When he woke at dawn, he held in memory, word for word, the confession verses he had heard in the dream; then he went before the Buddha and recited them through, just as he had heard them. The Buddhas ratified it — and his karmic obstruction, just like that, was extinguished.

### Confession Is Received, Not Squeezed Out

Look carefully at the structure of this dream. A very deep principle is hidden in it.

The confession we ordinarily imagine goes like this: I make up my mind, dig out the wrong things I have done one by one, lay them before the Buddha or before someone, own them, admit them, and then ask to be forgiven. In that picture, the one who sets it all in motion is "I"; I summon my strength and squeeze the confession out of my own heart.

But the dream in the *Golden Light Sūtra* is nothing like this.

From beginning to end, the one who initiates the confession is never "I." It is the golden drum that sounds — of itself. It appears in a dream — a place where I am not in command. It is the brahmin who strikes it, not I. The confession verses are spoken by the Buddhas themselves; I did not compose them. Where, in this long chain of links, does the practitioner stand? I am only the one who — wakes, remembers, and recites it once again.

So the confession here is not something I squeeze out; it is something I receive. It comes first as a gift — handed to me from the dream, from the golden drum, from the Buddhas; what I do on waking is only to say that gift over again.

This is a different thing from the pattern we know: "I admit my sins to someone and ask his absolution." That is one person facing a judge enthroned high above, back and forth between the two. Here it is one whole chain — drum, dream, brahmin, light, Buddhas, my recitation, the Buddhas' ratification — link locked into link, with no single link the lone initiator.

### Why Does This Extinguish the Karmic Obstruction?

This "not squeezed out by me" structure — where does its importance lie?

Here: it loosens the dead knot of "I, confessing my own offense."

In ordinary confession, the more one confesses, the more solid that "I" and that "offense of mine" become — because the whole stance is the protagonist "I" straining against the object "my offense." But the *Golden Light Sūtra* moves "I" off the initiator's seat: the confession is not launched by me; it is received by me. And when that clenched "I," deadlocked against its own offense, loosens — the karmic obstruction loosens with it.

Understand this: "karmic obstruction" in the Buddha-Dharma is not the West's "original sin" — not something you are born carrying, that cannot be washed away, that must wait upon a god's pardon. Karmic obstruction is dependently arisen — conditions gathered together, covering you for a time. So "extinguishing karmic obstruction" is not someone signing a pardon with a grand stroke of the pen. It is this: when that string of conditions — above all the condition "I, deadlocked against my offense" — loosens and comes apart, the obstruction loses the very ground it stood on.

### Confession Is Itself Buddha-Recollection

Look once more at how the dream ends: that long chain of links — where does it finally lead? It leads to this — the Buddhas of the ten directions, all appearing at once.

And what has this practitioner been doing the whole way through — aware of the Buddhas within the dream, holding in memory on waking the verses the Buddhas spoke, reciting them before the Buddha? Is this not, precisely, recollecting the Buddhas?

So the *Golden Light Sūtra* makes one thing plain: confession and buddha-recollection are not two things in sequence — not "first confess, then recollect the Buddha," and not "confession tucked in along the way." Confession, in itself, is a form of buddha-recollection. For at the far end of true confession stand the Buddhas, manifest and present; the whole course of your confessing is the course of your connecting with the Buddhas, recollecting the Buddhas.

The last chapter said that holding the precepts is buddha-recollection; this chapter says that confession too is buddha-recollection. See how this one practice keeps widening as it walks — it is not merely a buddha-name on the lips; it is the holding of precepts, it is confession, it is every act that turns your mind toward the Buddha and lays it close against Him.

### It Really Happens, and Yet It Is All Empty

In the Song dynasty, Master Zhili divided this confession into two strata, and said the two must be practiced together.

One stratum is called phenomenal repentance — really and solidly doing it, according to the liturgy: prostrating, calling the name, reciting the sūtra, contemplating the image, bowing prostration after prostration through the appointed days. This stratum really happens: you really are bowing, and the karmic obstruction really is loosening.

The other stratum is called noumenal repentance — while bowing, the mind keeps watch: this offense I am confessing, this repentance-method I am relying on, and this very "I" who is doing the confessing — which of the three has an independent, unchanging substance of its own? None. All are dependently arisen; all are empty.

Zhili said these two strata must walk together; neither can be dropped.

Practice the phenomenal alone, without the noumenal, and it turns into going through the forms — supposing "I have confessed a hundred times, so a hundred portions of karma are erased," treating karma as an account to be tallied entry by entry. Practice the noumenal alone, without the phenomenal, and it turns into empty talk — "the nature of offense is originally empty anyway, so why confess at all?" — until one never bows a single bow, and lands in nihilistic emptiness.

It must be both at once: that confession really happens (the phenomenal); and yet every link within it is empty through and through (the noumenal). This, and only this, accords with the original intent of that golden drum in the dream.

### The Gift You Receive, and the Hand That Holds It Out

By this point, the *Golden Light Sūtra* has spoken "how to confess" all the way through: it is a gift received, not an admission squeezed out.

But it leaves a deeper question unanswered.

Those Buddhas of the ten directions who appeared in the dream — what, finally, is their relation to this mind of yours? The "Buddhas" who hand you the gift: are they Buddhas outside your mind, standing apart? Or are they, in truth, reflections cast by this very mind of yours? The Buddha you recollect — is He outside you, or is He your mind?

This question the *Golden Light Sūtra* sets down and leaves.

The next great sūtra, the *Laṅkāvatāra*, takes it up face on. The answer it gives is a single sentence, and it lands like a thunderclap — "The hearts of all Buddhas stand first." To recollect the Buddha, it turns out, is to recollect the mind.

In the next chapter, we go to look at that mind.
