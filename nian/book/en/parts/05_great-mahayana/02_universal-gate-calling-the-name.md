## 5.2 Calling the Name at the Universal Gate: One Name, Answering in a Thousand Places

In the last chapter, Mahāsthāmaprāpta gathered the six sense-roots — gathered everything — inward, into this one recollection of the Buddha. In this chapter we change bodhisattvas, and the direction reverses: he does not gather inward; he opens outward.

He is Avalokiteśvara, in the Universal Gate Chapter of the *Lotus Sūtra*.

### The Moment You Call, the Bodhisattva Turns His Head

At the opening of the Universal Gate Chapter, Bodhisattva Akṣayamati asks the Buddha: why is this bodhisattva named *Guānshìyīn* — "the one who contemplates the sounds of the world"?

The Buddha answers: if immeasurable beings, undergoing all kinds of suffering and distress, hear of this Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara and call his name with one mind, Avalokiteśvara at once "contemplates their voices" — he comes, following that sound, and all of them attain liberation.

Look at what is hidden inside this one answer: two actions. The being *calls*; the bodhisattva *contemplates*. What you call is his name; what he contemplates is your voice.

This is very different from the asking-for-help that people know best. To petition is to send a cry up to someone seated far above, then wait for the answer — one going out, one coming back; first this, then that. But the Universal Gate Chapter is not like that. The moment you call, the bodhisattva "instantly" contemplates the sound — your calling and his contemplating meet inside one and the same instant. It is not that you cried out, and only then did he hear, only then set out. The moment you open your mouth, he has already turned his head.

To be mindful of this bodhisattva is never to stand alone, shouting into empty space. It is a meeting, here and now.

### Seven Perils, One Name

The sūtra then lists the "seven perils" — seven situations that can cost you your life: great fire, great flood, *rākṣasas* and evil demons, sword and staff upon the body, spirits pressing in to torment, cangue and shackles binding the limbs, enemy bandits closing in on every side.

Seven perils — and the Buddha gives one and the same remedy: call the name of Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara. The fire cannot burn, the water cannot drown, swords and staves break piece by piece, cangue and shackles fall away of themselves, and the bandits cannot raise a single evil thought.

One name, set against seven utterly different perils — and it answers every one. This is "Universal Gate" — the gate that is universal. Not a narrow door answering one kind of suffering only, but a door standing open toward all suffering, open in every place.

### What Is Called Is the Name; What Is Recollected Is the Power

But right here stands the juncture where buddha-recollection most easily goes crooked — taking the calling of the name to be the mouth repeating a few syllables, like chanting a spell, as if the sound itself carried some supernatural force.

The Universal Gate Chapter blocks that crookedness by itself.

The chapter falls into two halves: the first half is prose; the second half is verse. The prose runs on the word *chēng*, "to call" — "calling the name with one mind," "call his name." But the verses change the word. They run on *niàn*, "to recollect" — and what is recollected is not the name but the *power*: "recollect the power of that Avalokita, and the fire-pit turns into a pool"; "recollect the power of that Avalokita, and the waves cannot submerge" — twelve perils in a row, and every line is "recollect the power of that Avalokita."

Do you see it? *Chēng*, calling, is the name shouted out of the mouth — the outward, voiced face of the practice. *Niàn*, recollecting, is the mind laid close against this bodhisattva's power — the power of his vows, his power to rescue — the inward face, the face of the heart. Calling is the start; recollecting is where it lands. What actually lifts you out of the fire and the water is not the sound you shouted; it is the power your mind is holding.

So the verses end with a charge: "Therefore let one constantly recollect; recollection upon recollection, let no doubt arise." What is recollected is the power, not the sound. A buddha-name, a bodhisattva's name — let the mouth call it loud and clear, yes; but what matters more is that the heart stay pressed against that vow-power.

### A Bodhisattva Who Only Knew How to Bow

In the *Lotus Sūtra* there is one more bodhisattva, who turned buddha-recollection into yet another shape — he hardly recited sūtras, he did not sit in meditation; his whole life, he did only one thing: he bowed.

His name is "Never Disparaging."

Whomever he met — monastic or lay, man or woman — he would bow to that person with full reverence and say one sentence: "I deeply revere you all, and dare not treat you with contempt. Why? Because you all practice the bodhisattva path, and shall all become buddhas."

Look at this one bow: three things are moving in it at once. The body is bowing (body); the mouth is speaking (speech); and in the heart there is that measure of deep respect (mind). Body, speech, and mind — the three karmas, complete in a single bow. This too is buddha-recollection — only his way of recollecting is to bow.

And what matters more: the ones he bowed to were not buddha-images, not bodhisattvas, but ordinary people, one after another, none of them buddhas yet. He bowed to every person because of what he saw: this person is a future buddha.

This opens another door for buddha-recollection. The Mahāsthāmaprāpta of the last chapter gathered the six sense-roots inward, into one recollection of the Buddha; Never Disparaging pushed this reverence outward, toward every being. One faces in, one faces out — yet both, in the end, arrive at purity. At the end of his life, Never Disparaging's six sense-roots, too, were all purified. So gathering inward is buddha-recollection; and revering outward is buddha-recollection as well.

### One Scattered Call, and You Are Already on the Path to Buddhahood

There is one more line in the *Lotus Sūtra*, light as a feather, yet heavy enough to hold down a mountain. It stands in the Expedient Means Chapter:

> If a person with a scattered mind enters a stūpa or shrine, and once invokes "Homage to the Buddha" — such persons, one and all, have already completed the Buddha-path.

A person whose mind is scattered — not collected, scattered — happens to wander into a buddha-shrine and calls out once, in passing, "Homage to the Buddha." The sūtra says: such a person has "already completed the Buddha-path."

Weigh every word of it. "A scattered mind" — one mind is not required. "Once" — no long practice is required. "Homage to the Buddha" — not even a particular buddha need be named. The threshold could not be lower. And the fruit it bears? "Already completed the Buddha-path" — not some small blessing, but already walking the road to Buddhahood.

The sūtra goes further: even children at play, piling sand into a little buddha-stūpa — such ones, too, have "already completed the Buddha-path."

These lines bring the deepest layer of buddha-recollection out into the light.

Look: in the Universal Gate, "calling the name with one mind" sets a high threshold, and what it wins is liberation, here and now, from the fire and the water — a concrete, present-life effect. In Expedient Means, one scattered call sets the threshold lower than low — and what it wins is the highest thing of all: the path to Buddhahood.

This reversal holds the Lotus's true intent. Buddha-recollection, at its deepest, was never "I recollect, so as to trade for some benefit." Buddha-recollection is your bowing your head, once, to a fact — and the fact is this: all beings are already on the path to Buddhahood. Even if you call only once, with a scattered mind, in passing, that one call is your nod to this fact. Nod once, and the Buddha-path has "already" been settled.

That is why Expedient Means uses the word "already" — all have *already* completed the Buddha-path. Not "will someday." Already. That one scattered call of the buddha-name, light as it floats, has already set you back onto the road you were standing on all along.

### Gathering In, Circling Off

The calling of the name in the Universal Gate can be practiced in any place, at any time, scattered mind and all — it is a door standing open toward every moment and every place.

But buddha-recollection has one more posture, and it is the exact opposite: not calling here and there as you go, but taking a stretch of days and circling the whole of it off — closing the door, doing nothing else, recollecting the Buddha with a single, undivided aim.
