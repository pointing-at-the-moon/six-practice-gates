## 5.4 Seeing the Sign, Obtaining the Precept: The Buddha in Person Confirms It for You

In the last chapter, the first three seven-days of "binding the three periods" were spent in "ardently seeking repentance"; when the repentance landed, "good signs" would come — good states, seen. Recollecting the Buddha, repenting, seeing signs: these have always been joined. This chapter pushes that "seeing of signs" to a position of utmost weight.

It comes from the *Brahma Net Sūtra* — the root sūtra of the bodhisattva precepts in Chinese Buddhism.

### The Precept Is Not a Sheet of Rules — It Is a Seed

Say "precept," and most people picture a list of rules: do not kill, do not steal, do not lie… a sheet of regulations — collected on the day you receive it, followed ever after.

The *Brahma Net Sūtra* does not see the precept that way. It says the substance of the precept — the precept-body — is "the luminous, adamantine-treasury precept, the origin of all buddhas, the origin of all bodhisattvas, the seed of buddha-nature."

Listen to that. The precept is not a dead rule; it is a living seed — the very source by which all Buddhas become Buddhas. To receive the precepts is not to be handed a certificate or to sign a register; it is to plant this seed of buddhahood into your life. And once planted, it must be activated — again and again.

And the way to activate it, however the sūtra turns the matter, never leaves one thing: connecting with the Buddha.

### When There Is No Master, How Do You Receive the Precepts?

Here the sūtra asks a profoundly moving question.

To receive the precepts, by rule, you need a qualified ordination-master to transmit them face to face. But the *Brahma Net Sūtra* imagines a situation — exactly our situation in the degenerate age: the Buddha long passed into extinction; masters of real practice and real attainment hard to seek and hard to meet; within a thousand *li*, perhaps no one qualified to transmit the precepts at all.

Can such a person still receive the bodhisattva precepts?

The sūtra says: yes. It opens a path called self-vowed reception —

When within a thousand *li* there is no master who can transmit the precepts, you go before the images of the Buddhas and bodhisattvas and vow to the Buddha yourself, asking to receive this precept. Then, "for seven days before the Buddha one should repent" — earnest repentance, seven days, before the Buddha. And when the repentance lands: "obtaining an auspicious sign, one obtains the precepts." Once you see the auspicious sign, the precept is yours.

What is an auspicious sign? The sūtra is concrete: "the Buddha coming to rub the crown, seeing light, seeing flowers." The Buddha comes in person and lays a hand on the crown of your head; or you see light; or you see flowers.

And if seven days pass and nothing is seen? Then repent on — two seven-days, three seven-days, up to a whole year, until the auspicious sign is seen.

Look at what this path means. When no one is left in this world who can transmit the precept to you on the Buddha's behalf, then — **the Buddha comes in person.** By buddha-recollection and repentance you wash the karmic obstructions clean, layer by layer, until the Buddha is willing to show the sign, willing to come and rub your crown — and in that moment it is the Buddha, in person, who plants this seed of buddhahood into your life. No intermediary.

The "seeing the Buddha" of Part Three's *pratyutpanna-samādhi* here lands on solid ground: seeing the Buddha has become the warrant by which you obtain the precept. Recollecting the Buddha, repenting, seeing the Buddha, obtaining the precept — these were always one line.

### The Precept Is Alive: Broken, It Can Be Mended

More important still: the precept of the *Brahma Net Sūtra* is not a dead asset, received once and secure forever. It is a living artery — one that can be damaged, and can be mended.

Another clause asks: what if someone violates a grave precept? The answer is the same method again: before the images of the Buddhas and bodhisattvas, day and night through the six periods, recite the precepts, "bowing to the exhaustion of one's strength to the three-period thousand buddhas" — prostrating with utmost earnestness to the thousand Buddhas of the three times — until "obtaining the auspicious sign" — still the Buddha coming to rub the crown, the light, the flowers — and "then the offense is extinguished."

You see: receiving the precept and extinguishing the offense use one and the same procedure — before the Buddha-image, recollection and prostration accumulated, until the auspicious sign is seen. One plants the seed (reception); the other mends what was damaged (extinguishing the offense). The precept-body is a live line, always connected to the Buddha: planting takes that connection; repairing takes it too.

So keeping the precept was never the static business of "receive it, then just don't break it." It is this: for a whole lifetime, holding this line to the Buddha open — continuously, without break.

### To Keep the Precept Is to Recollect the Buddha Without Stopping

String these scattered clauses of the *Brahma Net Sūtra* together and a complete net comes into view — a net of "keeping the precept is recollecting the Buddha." At five points in time, it connects you to the Buddha:

Every day — "thrice daily," morning, noon, and evening, reverence. (The root of the morning-and-evening services in Chinese monasteries lies here.)

Every half-month — the assembly gathers before the Buddha-image, one person recites the precepts and everyone listens and receives; once each half-month, the precept is reactivated.

At the moment of reception — self-vow, repentance, the auspicious sign seen: connection made with the Buddha, the seed planted.

After a violation — precepts recited, the thousand Buddhas prostrated to, the auspicious sign seen: connection remade with the Buddha, the seed mended.

At the end of life — the last point, and the most moving. The sūtra says that one who keeps the precepts "shall see the thousand buddhas, each buddha hand-conferring" — at death the thousand Buddhas appear, and one by one, Buddha after Buddha reaches out a hand to receive you.

Every day, every half-month, at reception, after violation, at the end of life — five points in time, joining the precept-keeper's whole life, beginning to end, to the Buddha. To keep the precept is to connect with the Buddha at these five points, over and over again. And what is that, if not recollecting the Buddha without stopping?

### You Recollect the Buddha, and the Buddha Is Recollecting You

At the close of the sūtra, five benefits of keeping the precepts are named. The first two are the warmest:

"First, the buddhas of the ten directions, with sympathetic recollection, constantly guard him." So long as you hold this precept, the Buddhas of the ten directions are recollecting you with tenderness, guarding you, at every moment.

"Second, at the moment of death, right view and joyful mind." When your life reaches its last moment, the mind is not turned upside down; right mindfulness stands clear; you go in gladness.

Look closely at the first. So you were never reciting alone — keeping the precept, recollecting the Buddha, was never a one-sided ardor flung at empty space. At the other end, the Buddhas of the ten directions are holding you "with sympathetic recollection" — they are recollecting you. The truth of the *Śūraṅgama*, earlier in this part — mother and child recollecting each other — has appeared once more: you recollect the Buddha, and the Buddha recollects you; to keep the precept is to place yourself inside the embrace where the Buddha is recollecting you, watching over you, all the time.

Buddha-recollection, by this point, has become a two-way line: you recollecting the Buddha at this end, the Buddha recollecting you at that end — connected along this line of the precept-body.

And this precept joins you into a great commonwealth besides. The sūtra says of these ten major and forty-eight minor precepts: "the buddhas of the three times have recited, shall recite, and now recite" — the Buddhas of the past recited it, the Buddhas of the future will recite it, the Buddhas of the present are reciting it now. The precept you recite today is the very precept the Buddhas of the three times recite. The moment you hold this precept, you are no longer one solitary person facing a pile of clauses; you are a member of the precept-keeping commonwealth of the Buddhas of the three times.

### Repentance, It Turns Out, Is Also Buddha-Recollection

Look back over this chapter. Whether it was the auspicious sign that confers the precept or the auspicious sign that extinguishes the offense, both rested on one and the same thing — repentance.

Repenting until the karmic obstructions loosen; repenting until the Buddha is willing to show the sign.

Then what exactly is repentance to buddha-recollection? Could repentance itself be a way of recollecting the Buddha?

The next great sūtra — the *Golden Light Sūtra* — answers that question head-on. It tells of repentance as a golden drum, struck and sounding in a dream.

In the next chapter, we go to listen to that drum.
