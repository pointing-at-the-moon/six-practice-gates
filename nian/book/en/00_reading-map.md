# Reading Map · How to Read This Book

This book is one road, walked in eight stages. Let me point the eight out to you first — with a map in your mind, the walking will not feel uncertain.

### The Eight Stages

**Part One · Pāli Foundations.** Back to the very source. What the "recollection" the Buddha first taught in the Āgamas actually is — calling to mind the Buddha's nine qualities (the nine epithets), the six recollections, and the discovery that recollection and seated meditative absorption are two different tracks.

**Part Two · The Earliest Chinese Translations.** Recollection speaks Chinese for the first time. In the earliest scriptures brought to China, the word *niànfó* has not yet appeared; what appears first is *niàndào* — recollecting the path.

**Part Three · Early Mahāyāna.** The Buddha steps to the front of the stage. From recollecting the Buddha's qualities, to seeing the Buddha face to face (the *pratyutpanna-samādhi*), to calling the Buddha's name — buddha-recollection begins to grow.

**Part Four · The Three Pure Land Sūtras.** The land called "Bliss," seen clearly. The forty-eight vows, the sixteen contemplations, holding the name — here, buddha-recollection settles into a home.

**Part Five · The Great Mahāyāna Sūtras.** A thousand faces of one buddha-name. The *Śūraṅgama*, the *Lotus*, the *Perfect Enlightenment*, the *Brahma Net*, the *Golden Light*, the *Laṅkāvatāra*, the *Secret Adornment*, the *Mañjuśrī Prajñā* — great sūtra after great sūtra, each lighting up a face of buddha-recollection never seen before. This part goes deepest.

**Part Six · Two Streams.** One buddha-name divides the household, then flows back together. The two great rivers of Pure Land (outward, seeking birth) and Chan (inward, seeing the nature) — how they parted, how they faced each other across the Tiantai bridge, and how at last they rejoined.

**Part Seven · The Flower Ornament.** Buddha-recollection opens into the whole dharma-realm. Samantabhadra's ten great vows — reverence, praise, offerings, repentance, rejoicing, requesting the Dharma, requesting the Buddhas to abide, following in study, accommodating beings, dedication — stretch buddha-recollection into a whole way of living.

**Part Eight · Coda.** Placing this road back in the palm of your hand. In an age when even the machines can talk, does buddha-recollection still count as recollection? And — most important of all — what, exactly, are the steps inside buddha-recollection?

### The Bridges Between the Stages

Between the eight parts lie seven short "transitions." They are bridges between one stretch of road and the next — a place to catch your breath at the end of a stage, look back at the way come, look ahead at the way to go, and then set out again. Do not skip them; those breaths are the breathing of this road.

### Three Ways to Read — As You Please

One: **walk it from beginning to end.** This is best. A thousand years of road, stage upon stage; you will watch with your own eyes how buddha-recollection grew, step by step, into what it is today.

Two: **read the final chapter first.** If you are impatient and want to begin practicing at once, turn first to the final chapter in Part Eight — the plainest four steps are there. Begin practicing, then come back and walk the whole road slowly.

Three: **read whichever stage speaks to you.** If one part's title stirs something, start there. Each chapter largely stands on its own and can be read alone.

### A Few Small Conventions

A few small conventions, stated plainly so nothing trips you as you read:

- When the Buddha is meant, this book writes "He" with a capital, as a mark of reverence; for patriarchs, bodhisattvas, and practitioners, ordinary "he."
- The Original Teacher's name is written in this book as "Homage to the Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha."
- The sūtras' own words are set off as indented quotation blocks, so that you can tell at a glance which words are the scriptures' and which are my explanations to you.
- Catalog numbers and scholarly footnotes are kept out of the body text entirely, so as not to trouble the mind that wants to recollect the Buddha. Pāli and Sanskrit terms appear in the body only in passing, in italics, each explained in plain words at its first appearance. Everything you might wish to check is gathered in the two appendices at the back: the scriptural sources behind each chapter (Appendix A · Academic Notes Index), and a table of names and terms with their full romanizations and diacritical marks (Appendix C · Glossary). The body text does one thing only: it tells you this road, cleanly.

### Setting Out

The map points this far.

A road is for walking, not for framing and hanging on a wall. May you carry this map, and walk steadily, and walk gladly.

We set out from Part One — the Pāli foundations.
