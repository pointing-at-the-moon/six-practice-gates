## Transition ③ · From the Three Vinaya Lineages to the Three Clusters of Pure Precepts

Part Three has walked up to the seven hundred elders casting the last tally, and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath.

With Part Three done, you hold a complete set of things in your hands. A principle of legislation: no laws made in advance—laid down case by case, as offenses arose; behind every rule stands a person and an event, and the reasons for laying down the precepts are gathered into the ten purposes, the last and weightiest of them "the long-enduring presence of the true Dharma." A map of the transmissions: one Vinaya passed down into five houses, the items branching each its own way, while the eight-category skeleton, the fortnightly breath, the four root rules never moved a thread. And the witness of two storms: in the storm of schism, the door of return was left open; in the storm of deliberation, a hundred years after the Buddha's passing, the procedure He had established settled the dispute by itself.

This set of things is rigorous, precise, hammered and tempered a thousand times over. A Saṅgha holding it can dwell together in purity for a thousand years—and in fact it has dwelt so for twenty-five hundred.

Is it enough?

By the Vinaya's own language, yes. The Vinaya governs body and speech; it governs the bounds within the Saṅgha. What it wants is the purity of the Saṅgha and the long endurance of the true Dharma—and these it has accomplished.

But there are questions the language of the Vinaya cannot ask.

When the Vinaya judges an act, it asks: was it done? Is the mark of the offense established? It does not ask: what mind were you keeping? A bhikkhu can be spotless in conduct and cold as iron within—the Vinaya can do nothing with him, for he has broken not a single rule. Nor does the Vinaya ask about anything beyond the wall: the rules guard the purity within the Saṅgha's boundary; all the beings outside the wall—their suffering, their confusion—fall within the offense-marks of no rule at all.

In stopping evil, the Vinaya went as far as anything can go. And yet—is stopping evil alone enough? Is not killing already compassion? Is not stealing already the benefit of others? If a man keeps all two hundred fifty rules without one drop leaking through, what, in the end, has he given to beings?

When the Mahāyāna arose, it raised these two questions head-on. Its answer was to stretch the word "precept" wide open—stretch it into three clusters: the restraint cluster, taking in the whole of the Vinaya's precepts-by-restraint exactly as they stood, not one rule dropped; the accumulation-of-virtue cluster, making the good that ought to be cultivated itself a precept—not to cultivate it is to transgress; the sattva-welfare cluster, making the beings who ought to be carried across themselves a precept—not to carry them across is to transgress. From then on, the precept is no longer only a list of don'ts; it is also a list of must-dos. And the boundary of precept-keeping spreads from inside the Saṅgha's wall out across the whole world of beings.

This is not the overthrow of the Vinaya—the first cluster, the restraint cluster, is precisely the Vinaya's place, kept intact. This is the raising of a building upon the foundation.

And the first blueprint for that building is to be read in the scriptures and treatises of the bodhisattva precepts: how the three clusters are established, how the bodhisattva precepts are received, how precept-by-restraint and precept-by-action close their arch in a single person.

We turn the page.
