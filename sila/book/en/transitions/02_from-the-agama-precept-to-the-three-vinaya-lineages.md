## Transition ② · From the Precept in the Āgamas to the Three Vinaya Lineages

Part Two has walked up to a wisp of fragrance traveling against the wind, and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath.

With Part Two done, you hold a field of daily use in your hands. The precept is three stretches of the great road out of suffering—not a threshold set before the road, but the road's own body. The five precepts each mark out a clear piece of ground: the killing-precept guards a heart that cannot bear to harm a living thing; the stealing-precept guards a line—what is not mine, my hand does not reach for; the sexual-misconduct precept is drawn at different places for the monastic and the householder; the false-speech precept nurtures a mouth that can be trusted; the intoxicant precept keeps the mind that means to cultivate concentration from being muddled. The eight-precept *uposatha* borrows one day and one night for a householder to learn leaving home once, returned at daybreak. And last, the cleanness all this keeping brings forth turns into a fragrance that carries even against the wind.

This field was spoken by the Buddha to living people, one by one, place by place. To Ānanda He spoke of the fragrance of virtue; to the youth Sigāla, the six losses of drinking; to a laywoman, the eight-precept *uposatha*; to those who came asking the way, the noble eightfold path. Wherever there was a person, an affair, there the precept answered to the occasion.

And exactly this answering-to-the-occasion is its strength—and its limit.

The precept of the Āgamas is scattered. One line in this scripture, one line in that, each answering its own person, each meeting its own affair. It tells you what the precept is, how far it is to be guarded, what the keeping looks like—but it never compiled these items into a precept code that runs whole from head to tail, rule joined to rule; nor did it furnish each precept with its file of cases—what occasioned its laying down, how a breach is judged, how light and heavy are told apart, how the Saṅgha handles it, how one returns to purity.

For a householder living his days alone, the scattered precept is enough. But for a Saṅgha that must live together, practice together, and run together for hundreds of years, for thousands, a scattered precept alone falls far short.

For a Saṅgha truly to stand, there must be a compiled precept code—recited fortnightly, kept by all in common: this is the *Pāṭimokkha*. And there must be a living casebook: behind every rule, the origin of its laying down, the cases already judged, the line between light and heavy—nothing missing. Only at this step does the precept grow from reminders scattered across the scriptures into a code that can actually run.

And there is one more thing the language of the Āgamas cannot handle: after the Buddha's passing, this precept code flowed east and spread west along different transmissions, and flowed into several lineages. The items are not all the same; the counts are not all the same—ninety *Pācittiya* rules in this lineage, ninety-two in that; a hundred training observances here, seventy-five there. The counts fail to align not because some house miscopied, but because a living casebook, in different places and at different times, accumulates its own natural shape. To see this sameness-with-difference clearly, the Vinayas of the three lineages must be spread out side by side and compared.

The Āgamas can say "what the precept is"; they cannot fully say "how a Saṅgha casts the precept into a living code, and how, amid divergence, it renegotiates the precept's boundaries, time after time."

To hear these things, you must change where you sit—moving from before the Buddha's teaching seat to the hall where the Saṅgha deliberates; from the sūtras into the Vinaya.

This gate is exactly where Part Three will walk. First, see how that fortnightly-recited code was built up, category by category; then spread the three lineages' Vinayas out side by side, and watch how they converse, how difference shows within sameness; and last, see how two storms in the Saṅgha forced the assembly to renegotiate the precept's boundaries once again.

From scriptures spoken scattered, into a Vinaya compiled. We turn the page, and go to watch the precept grow into a living code.
