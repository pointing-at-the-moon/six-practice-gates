## Transition ⑤ · From the Precept-Substance to Bhaiṣajyaguru's Twelve Vows

Part Five has walked up to a single wave on the sea of the mind, and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath.

With Part Five done, you hold a complete set of things in your hands. An answer: what is gained at the reception of the precepts is a wholesome seed, perfumed deep into the store-consciousness—the debate of the three houses, Daoxuan's verdict, Zhiyi's opening and closing. A book: the *Xingshichao*—receiving is the perimeter wall; following is the house; receiving and following must sustain each other—a classic settled for a thousand years. A cross-section: the seed dwells in the storehouse of the eighth consciousness; the seventh consciousness's grasping at a self makes every wholesome dharma tainted; and the transformation of the basis is the homecoming, with conscience-and-shame as the bridge.

This set of things has spoken the precept down to the depth where nothing more can be spoken. The deepest holdings of Chinese Vinaya learning are all here.

Is it enough?

In the language of Vinaya learning, yes. The substance is established, the framework is established, the cross-section is drawn complete—for a person who has resolved to keep the precepts, from receiving to following, from seed to transformation, the route map is fully furnished.

But there is one kind of person this language cannot catch.

The person who has fallen.

Part Five said it itself: keeping strengthens the perfuming; transgressing damages the seed; grave transgression can even destroy it. This is diagnosis, and the diagnosis is precise beyond compare. But a person who has truly broken a precept—deep in the night, when he remembers the precept he has failed—the theory tells him: your seed is damaged. What he wants to ask is: is there still hope for me?

The doctrine of the precept-substance can diagnose the wound; it cannot write the prescription. The cross-section can mark the lesion; it cannot offer a hand. "Re-purification after a breach" stands there on the blueprint—but the road back to purity, for one who knows his debt is deep and whose own strength can no longer carry him—who will receive him on it?

For medicine, one must look in another direction. Toward the East.

There is a Buddha who, while He walked the bodhisattva path in His causal ground, made twelve great vows. Among the twelve stands one vow set up expressly for those who have transgressed against śīla: those who have received the precepts within His Dharma and broken them, upon hearing His name—"they shall regain purity and not fall into the evil destinies." To write the transgressor's way home into one's own root vows—among the original vows of all the Buddhas of the ten directions, this is a compassion all its own. And this Buddha carries medicine in His very name: the Tathāgata Bhaiṣajyaguru, the Medicine Buddha of Lapis-Lazuli Radiance.

The study of the precept, spoken to this point, must walk from the cross-section drawn by self-power toward the welcoming guidance of a Buddha's vows. This is not a change of subject—it is the same wounded seed, meeting a Medicine King whose art is precisely the healing of wounds.

We turn the page.
