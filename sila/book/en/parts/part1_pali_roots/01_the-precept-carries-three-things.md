## 1.1 The Precept Carries Three Things

Two thousand five hundred years ago: Sāvatthī, Jeta's Grove, the park of Anāthapiṇḍika. One day, near evening, the Venerable Ānanda rose from his sitting meditation, came before the Buddha, bowed at His feet, and asked a question as plain as a question can be:

> World-Honored One, what is the meaning of keeping the precepts?

Keeping the precepts—what, in the end, is it for?

The question is utterly ordinary—so ordinary that today we hardly think to ask it anymore: the precepts are rules; you keep them, and that is that. Yet Ānanda asked it all the same. And the Buddha's answer was not "so as not to fall into the evil destinies," nor "so as to store up merit." He followed the grain of a person's mind and spoke inward, link locking into link:

> Ānanda, keeping the precepts has non-regret as its meaning. Ānanda, if one keeps the precepts, one gains non-regret.

Keeping the precepts is so that regret does not rise in the mind. And further down? Without regret, the mind is joyful; joyful, rapture is born; with rapture, body and mind grow tranquil; tranquil, one feels happiness; with happiness, the mind concentrates; concentrated, it sees things as they really are; seeing, disenchantment is born; disenchanted, one grows dispassionate; dispassionate, one is liberated. Link pushing link, from one precept all the way to liberation. At the end He gathered it into one line:

> This is how dharma benefits dharma and dharma causes dharma; this *sīla* thus approaches the highest—that is, crossing this further shore and reaching the further bank.

Dharma and dharma nourish each other, cause each other—the precept is not a stone held fast in one place; it is the first step of a long road, "approaching and arriving at" the far shore all the way. This discourse is called the *Heyi-jing*—the Sūtra of Purposes—and is gathered in the *Madhyama Āgama*, in its chapter on conditions. It is the starting point of this whole book: to read all the precepts that come later—the Vinaya's, the bodhisattva's, the mind-ground's—one must first stand firm at this scene.

But this "precept" that can push all the way to the far shore—what exactly is it?

Spread the one word "precept" open and you will find it is like a carrying-pole, with three things of different work actually slung beneath it. In the Buddhist texts each of the three has its own name. The patriarchs who carried the canon into Chinese told them apart long ago, setting three translation-words side by side; people today, reading them in one smooth breath, have kneaded the three back into a single lump. This chapter lays the three things out, one by one.

### The First Thing: An Ability Grown into the Body

The first is the one simply called the precept—in Pāli, *sīla*.

The root sense of the word is habit, disposition, the lean of one's conduct. So the root of the precept lies not in how many lines the outer list carries, but in a character grown in a person: a disposition that tends toward the wholesome of itself and keeps from the unwholesome of itself. It is an orderly composing of body, speech, and mind—conduct combed straight, the mind set upright.

A precept like this is an ability grown from within, not a yoke fastened on from without. A person who truly keeps the precepts is not gritting his teeth day after day to "hold back from offending"; it is that those things, in him, gradually stop happening. He is like a man who has written characters for decades—the strokes come out upright of themselves: not that he reminds himself at every stroke, but that the uprightness has grown into his hand.

And it is just because the precept is an ability grown into the body that it can drive the chain of the *Heyi-jing*: when the ability is pure, the heart owes nothing, and non-regret, joy, rapture, happiness come forth of themselves, joint by joint. A list fastened on from outside cannot move the mind.

### The Second Thing: A Pair of Hands at the Six Sense-Doors

The second is called restraint—*saṃvara*.

Many hear "restraint" as suppression, repression—as if one were forcibly pinning oneself down. That is hearing it askew. The word's root sense is to enclose and protect—an active keeping of watch, not a held-in stillness. What it guards are the doors of the six senses: eye seeing forms, ear hearing sounds, nose smelling scents, tongue tasting flavors, body touching objects, mind taking up dharmas—and in the very instant when faculty and object touch, it cuts off, then and there, the line along which craving and distress would arise. Not remorse after the fact, but an active sealing at the sense-doors—a watch kept on the spot.

For this "active," the Buddha once made a superb comparison. He said: a royal elephant truly fit for the battlefield does not shrink and hide behind the lines; he stands amid the very blades and arrows—advancing, charging, enduring, and all the while protecting his trunk, protecting his two tusks, protecting head and neck and four feet, watching over every vital part of his body at once. The practitioner guarding the six senses is just so: not withdrawing to some uneventful corner for a bit of quiet, but standing in the very middle of conditions, with the whole of his vigilance, keeping all six doors well watched at once.

So the essence of restraint is a watch kept in place, not an avoidance that leaves the field. The second thing on the precept's carrying-pole is a pair of hands forever keeping watch at the door.

### The Third Thing: A Living Casebook

The third is called the Vinaya—the *Vinaya-piṭaka*, the basket of the discipline.

The first two both speak of the mind, of the present instant; this third one is different: it is a body of text, a set of institutions. The word's root sense is to lead away—to lead unwholesome conduct, little by little, away from the Saṅgha.

The Vinaya is not a law code decreed out of thin air. Behind every one of its rules hangs something that really happened: first the origin story—what person, what affair occasioned the rule; then the rule itself; then the word-by-word explanation; last, the cases already judged. It grew from cases; it was not promulgated downward. This layer will come into plain view in Part Three, when we walk into the Vinaya itself.

These rules are gathered into a precept-text; once each half-month the whole Saṅgha assembles in one place and recites it word by word. The precept-text most current in China, that of the *Sifenlü*—the Dharmaguptaka Vinaya—closes its recitation with this summing-up:

> Venerables, I have recited the introduction, the four *pārājika* dharmas, the thirteen *saṅghādisesa* dharmas, the two *aniyata* dharmas, the thirty *nissaggiya-pācittiya* dharmas, the ninety *pācittiya* dharmas, the four *pāṭidesanīya* dharmas, the *sekhiya* dharmas, and the seven *adhikaraṇasamatha* dharmas. This is the precept-text spoken by the Buddha, to be recited fortnightly, coming into the recitation.

"To be recited fortnightly, coming into the recitation"—there is the rhythm of breathing inside that line. The Vinaya is not a dead code laid on a shelf; recited every half-month, it is a thing that has stayed alive inside the Saṅgha all along.

The counts of rules are not the same across the transmissions: the *Sifenlü* current in China has two hundred fifty; the southern Theravāda has two hundred twenty-seven. That the numbers do not line up means no school misremembered; it is precisely the natural look of a living casebook—different places, different times, communities meeting different affairs, the accumulated precedents naturally growing into different counts of rules.

### Three Things, One Position

The three things are laid out. The precept is the ability of character grown into the body: its charge is the mind. Restraint is the watch at the six sense-doors: its charge is the present instant. The Vinaya is the Saṅgha's institution of cases: its charge is how rules are laid down and how they are carried out. Mind, instant, institution—the three are not three unrelated kinds of "precept"; they are three faces of one same road. Knead them into one vague lump of "rules," and the division of labor among the three faces disappears from view.

Last, there is one more measure of proportion to make plain at the very start.

The Buddha could expound the precepts exhaustively: from not killing and not stealing, to departing from every kind of wrong livelihood, to keeping far from all the things a renunciant of that day ought not do, layer counted upon layer. Yet in the *Brahmajāla* discourse of the *Dīrgha Āgama*, when all this counting is done, He adds one more line:

> These are but small and minor matters of keeping the precepts—it is by these that the unlearned worldling praises the Buddha… There are further dharmas, deep, profound, and subtly luminous, the radiance of the great Dharma.

When the people of the world praise the Buddha, what they mostly praise is the look of His precept-keeping—and the Buddha says: these are only "small and minor matters"; above them stand dharmas deep, subtle, and great. This one line sets the precept back in its proper place: the precept is not a dispensable secondary rule, and not the highest ultimate truth either; it is the lowest layer of the road—and the layer that must be driven firm first. A foundation will never be the tall building; but there is not one inch of the tall building that does not stand on the foundation.

And that is exactly the road this volume walks: first see this foundation clearly and drive it firm, then follow it upward.

The first thing to ask, then, is this: in a person who has truly stepped onto the noble path—who has "entered the stream"—does this foundation actually count? Is it an item one could do without, or a thing one cannot lack?

Over this very question, two men once quarreled past all reconciling, and carried the quarrel all the way to the Buddha's seat. In the next chapter, we go and watch how the Buddha ruled.
