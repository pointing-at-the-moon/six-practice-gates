## 4.4 A Thousand Li Without a Preceptor: Self-Vow Before the Buddha-Image

Begin with a picture. After the Buddha's passing—some place, some time. A person has aroused the mind and wants to receive the bodhisattva precepts—but looking out a thousand *li*, there is not one preceptor. What is he to do?

The *Pusa yingluo benye jing* (the *Yingluo Sūtra*) gives him a road:

> "Third: when the buddhas and bodhisattvas have passed away and within a thousand *li* there is no preceptor, one should, before the images of the buddhas and bodhisattvas, kneel with palms joined and receive the precepts by self-vow. One should speak thus: 'I, so-and-so, declare to the buddhas of the ten directions and the bodhisattvas of the great earth: I will train in all the bodhisattva precepts.' … The second and third are likewise so declared."

One person, kneeling before the Buddha-image, making his own vow to the buddhas of the ten directions, receiving by the threefold declaration. No ordination platform, no teacher, no assembly—and the precept, even so, is attained.

This road is the third of the "three grades of reception." The same sūtra sets the three grades in order: the higher grade—receiving in person in the present presence of the buddhas and bodhisattvas, attaining the true higher-grade precept; the middle grade—after the Buddha's passing, when within a thousand *li* there is a bodhisattva who has already received the precepts, requesting him as preceptor and receiving from him, attaining the middle-grade precept; the lower grade—a thousand *li* without a preceptor, self-vow before the image, attaining the lower-grade precept. The grades are higher and lower; the attaining of the precept is one. And this sūtra opens the gate of conferral wide indeed—even "spouses and the six kinds of kin may serve as mutual preceptors": between husband and wife, between kin, the precepts may be conferred on one another.

What this chapter looks at is this building called "receiving the precepts"—it has a single seed from India, a building-body of three stories raised in China, and one strange gate at the door.

### A Single Seed, a Building

Self-reception before the image is not a Chinese invention out of thin air. The *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Chapter on Śīla of the last chapter had itself left a line of fallback:

> "If one does not meet a person endowed with the [requisite] qualities, then one should, before an image of the Tathāgata, receive the bodhisattva's pure precept-restraint by oneself … The second and third are likewise so declared."

Meet no person endowed with the qualities, and one self-receives before an image of the Tathāgata, with the threefold declaration. Dharmakṣema's *Dichi jing* carries a parallel line. This is the seed that came from India—a single line: no teacher, then self-receive before the image.

China built that one line into a building. The *Yingluo Sūtra* raises the graded order of the three grades, sets self-ordination as the third grade, and fits it with a precept-substance that is "not lost even by transgression"; the *Brahmā's Net Sūtra* then installs a gate on self-ordination—the auspicious sign:

> "One receives them by self-vow before the images of the buddhas and bodhisattvas; one should repent before the Buddha for seven days; on obtaining the sight of an auspicious sign, one then attains the precepts. If one does not obtain the sign, one should continue for two weeks, three weeks, even up to a year, until it is obtained. … But if one does not obtain the auspicious sign, then though one receive the precepts before the image, one does not attain them."

Self-ordination must first pass through repentance—seven days, fourteen, twenty-one, up to a year—repenting until the auspicious sign is seen; only then is the precept attained. No auspicious sign, and however long one kneels, the precept is not attained. And the very next line:

> "If one receives in the present presence of a preceptor who has already received the bodhisattva precepts, then at the time of receiving before that preceptor one need not see an auspicious sign. Why? Because the preceptor stands in a master-to-master transmission; therefore no auspicious sign is required."

Receiving before a preceptor, one need not see the auspicious sign—because behind the preceptor stands the master-to-master transmission. The two safeguards now stand plain: with a teacher, the lineage bears witness; without one, the Buddha Himself must be asked for a token. As for what the "auspicious sign" concretely looks like—the Buddha coming to rub one's crown, the sight of light, the sight of flowers—that is the later commentators explaining on the text's behalf; the sūtra itself gives only the two words: auspicious sign.

This gate can be understood thus. The last chapter said that the first of the four qualities of intrinsic-nature śīla is "reception from another"—the precept is not a nod given to oneself by oneself; there must always be an "other" to witness and to hand it over. Self-ordination is precisely the case with no "other." The auspicious sign is just that absent "other," supplied again by inviting the buddhas of the ten directions: the teacher absent, the Buddha comes in person. The gate is no hazing; it guards "from another"—the precept's vital point—into the deepest reach of the teacherless land.

### Once Received, Never Lost

The most astonishing thing about this building is not how one receives, but what comes after. The *Yingluo Sūtra* says:

> "Thus know that the bodhisattva precepts have a law of reception but no law of relinquishment; once received, not lost even by transgression, unto the very end of future time."

The precepts of the śrāvaka Vinaya canon, Part Three has seen: conferred by others—the formal saṅgha act of motion-and-three-proclamations, the quorum complete; losable—grave transgression means no longer dwelling in communion, and one may relinquish the precepts and return home; bounded—full twenty years of age, free of the thirteen disqualifications, only then may one receive. The bodhisattva precepts reverse all three axes: self-vowable, unlosable, universal. A law of reception there is; a law of relinquishment there is none; transgress, and the precept is still not lost—it follows you unto the very end of future time. The sūtra goes so far as to say this heavy thing:

> "To have [the precept] and transgress is superior to lacking it and never transgressing; one who has the precept and transgresses is [still] a bodhisattva, while one who has never [received] it is an outsider."

To have received and transgressed is superior to never receiving and never transgressing—received and transgressing, one is still numbered among the bodhisattvas; untransgressing because unreceived, one stands outside the door. This is no indulgence of transgression; it is a weighing of what that one thought of "receiving" weighs: a seed fallen to the ground, beaten by wind and rain, is still a seed; a seed that never falls to the ground leaves, even in fair weather, only empty ground.

On what strength does it dare reverse all three axes? The sūtra gives the root:

> "The precepts of all bodhisattvas, ordinary or noble, have mind as their substance throughout; therefore [only] when the mind is exhausted is the precept exhausted, and because the mind is inexhaustible the precept too is inexhaustible."

The bodhisattva precepts have mind as their substance. The Vinaya canon's precepts are tied to status, tied to the *saṅgha*-boundary, tied to this one span of life—so they can be lost; the bodhisattva precepts are tied to the mind—the mind has no end, so the precept has no end. And precisely because mind is the substance, the gate of reception opens to its widest: "whoever can understand the preceptor's words receives and attains the precepts in full"—whoever understands what the preceptor says, anyone may receive; even the many statuses the *prātimokṣa* restraint would bar, the sūtra says, "because all have the mind that turns toward [the path]," all attain the precepts. One threshold remains, and one only: a mind turned toward it.

So the *Brahmā's Net Sūtra*'s ordination verse dares to sing:

> "When beings receive the Buddha's precepts, they at once enter the rank of the buddhas; their station being now the same as the Great Awakened One's, they are truly sons of the buddhas."

At the moment of reception, one enters the lineage of the buddhas. The *Yingluo Sūtra* says in one voice: the one who receives "enters the number of bodhisattvas in the realm of the buddhas and transcends the suffering of birth-and-death through three kalpas; therefore one should receive them"—therefore: receive.

### A Building Grown on Chinese Soil

Last, let the building's origin be told plainly. The *Yingluo* and the *Brahmā's Net*, as scriptures, have had question marks over their translators' attribution since the ancient catalogues, and later scholars largely hold them to have been compiled and composed on Chinese soil. This need not be hushed up, nor need it cause alarm—what must be seen is precisely its meaning: India sent a single seed (the one line of self-reception before the image), and Chinese Buddhism built it into a great building with the graded order of three grades, the gate of the auspicious sign, and the precept-substance that is not lost. By the Sui, Master Zhiyi composed the *Commentary on the Meaning of the Bodhisattva Precepts*, setting side by side and ordering the reception procedures then in circulation—"first the *Brahmā's Net* base; second the *Dichi* base; third the Gaochang base; fourth the *Yingluo* base; fifth the newly-composed base; sixth the Zhizhi base"—six base-texts laid out together: the Chinese learning of precept-reception had grown into a school of its own. This building grew up on Chinese soil itself—grew with a root, with grounds, with a house-rule of its own.

Here Part Four has been walked to its end.

The four steps by which the Mahāyāna stretched the precept open have all been seen: Upāli asked his new question and the three clusters stood up—beyond the ceasing of evil, cultivating good and liberating beings also became precept, with the Vinaya canon intact in the first cluster (chapter one); prajñā kept the precept all the way to "transgression unobtainable," and the precept proved the more unbreakable—the poison-dragon's one-day precepts, the gift of flesh grown into the gift of the Dharma (chapter two); the *Yogācārabhūmi* drew the bodhisattva precepts as a completed map of nine aspects, re-purification after a breach drawn into the definition itself, the ring closing end to beginning (chapter three); China raised the great building of reception—a thousand *li* without a preceptor, self-vow before the image; once received, not lost unto the very end of future time (chapter four).

One sentence gathers up Part Four: the Mahāyāna laid the precept out from inside the Saṅgha's wall to the whole world of beings—and laid it onto the mind.

But the sūtra-line at the end of chapter four has raised a new word: "mind as its substance." Substance—at the moment of receiving, what exactly was taken into body and mind? What kind of thing is it, that can make "once received, not lost even by transgression, unto the very end of future time" hold true? A form-dharma? A mind-dharma? A seed?

Chinese Vinaya masters spent several hundred years answering that word. Part Five walks into the precept-substance.
