# Part Three · Three Vinaya Lineages · A Vinaya That Grew

The close of Part Two left us with an observation: the precept in the Āgamas lies scattered, sutta by sutta—one dialogue hands over five precepts; one observance day confers the eight factors. Between the precepts scattered through the suttas and a tightly ordered Vinaya, recited once a fortnight, there stands a gate.

Part Three goes in through that gate.

Entering, first set down one assumption that comes too easily: the Vinaya is not a set of celestial statutes the Buddha mounted a throne to promulgate. For twelve years after the Buddha attained the Way, the Saṅgha had not a single formulated precept. The first precept was laid down because of one person and one deed—and so was every precept after it. The Vinaya grew, like case law; it was not handed down, like a statute-book.

Three chapters, three steps. Chapter one returns to the scene where the first precept was born—Vesālī, a bhikkhu named Sudinna, and the "ten purposes" the Buddha laid down with His own mouth. Chapter two watches this living law travel the trade roads and the translation halls, growing into five houses of transmission—the articles differing here and there, the shared core not moving by a thread. Chapter three watches the two great storms the Saṅgha passed through: one of breaking, one of deliberation—the one that broke left behind it a compassionate door of return; the one that deliberated set down a rule that has stood a thousand years.

Read these three chapters through, and one thing comes clear: behind every article of the Vinaya stands a person and an event; and what holds up all the articles is the last of the ten purposes—the long-enduring presence of the true Dharma.

We step through the gate.
