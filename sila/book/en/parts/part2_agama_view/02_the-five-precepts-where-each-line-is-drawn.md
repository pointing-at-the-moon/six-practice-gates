## 2.2 The Five Precepts: Where Each Line Is Drawn

Kapilavatthu again, the Nigrodha Park again—and again, Mahānāma.

That Śākyan head of house from Part One—the man who contended over the four factors and asked after the six recollections—this time asks about the householder's own portion. Fascicle thirty-three of the *Saṃyukta Āgama* records him asking five "accomplishments" in a row: what is a lay disciple's accomplishment in faith? Accomplishment in the precepts? In learning? In letting-go? In wisdom?—faith, precepts, learning, letting-go, wisdom: the five belongings of a complete householder. At the second, the Buddha answered:

> The lay disciple abstains from killing, from taking what is not given, from sexual misconduct, from false speech, and from intoxicants, taking no pleasure in these. Mahānāma! This is called the lay disciple's accomplishment in the precepts.

Five lines, each one an "abstaining." And at the tail, three more words: "taking no pleasure"—not gritting the teeth and holding back, but the thought of doing it never arising at all, no delight taken in it. This is the householder's five precepts.

Recited side by side, the five look like five things of one kind. In truth, each of these five "abstainings" governs a different stretch of ground. One same gate of reception—and inside it, five different plots of field. This chapter draws the boundary of each of the five plots, line by line.

Before drawing the lines, one curious place to visit. Part One quoted that line of the *Brahmajāla-sūtra*—"precept-keeping is a small and minor matter; it is by this that the unlearned worldling praises the Buddha." When the world praises the Buddha, it mostly praises the look of His precept-keeping, and the Buddha said this is only a small and minor matter. But here is the fine part: the discourse then proceeds to record, item by item, exactly *how* the world praises Him. And so this discourse, without intending it, has left behind the best of materials—a portrait of the Buddha Himself keeping the five precepts, one precept to a stroke. The boundary-drawing below borrows this portrait to draw with.

### The Killing-Precept: A Line Drawn at the Heart That Cannot Bear to Harm

The world's first line of praise:

> The recluse Gotama gives up killing, refrains from killing, takes up no rod or weapon, dwells with conscience, and sympathizes with all living beings.

Look at the four layers in this one line: not killing (giving up killing, refraining from killing) is the conduct; taking up no rod or weapon—even the tools have been put down from the hand; dwelling with conscience—a gate of self-examination stands in the heart; sympathizing with all living beings—toward living beings he has grown a tenderness that cannot bear to harm.

The killing-precept's line is drawn at that innermost layer. Not "I have killed no one, so the precept is kept"—but the knife put down, the heart set straight, that tenderness grown. The last chapter said that keeping away from killing is the gift of fearlessness—and for fearlessness to be given out, there must first be this heart that cannot bear to harm a living thing.

### The Stealing-Precept: A Line Between Given and Not Given

The second line:

> He gives up stealing, refrains from stealing; takes only what has been given, takes nothing ungiven; and dwells constantly inclined to giving.

"Takes only what has been given, takes nothing ungiven"—take when it has been given; when it is not given, the hand does not reach out. The stealing-precept's line is drawn on the word "given": what is not mine, however near to hand, is not taken; what has been given me, only that is received in peace.

But the close of the line repays a second look: "dwells constantly inclined to giving." Beyond not-taking, he constantly gives. This plot of the stealing-precept runs from not-seizing at one end to delighting-in-giving at the other—guarding one's own cleanness, then turning around to give one's own away. A precept that looks negative, grown to its depth, has grown into giving.

### The Sexual-Misconduct Precept: Two Different Lines

For this precept, the renunciant and the householder have their lines drawn in different places.

The renunciant keeps the complete chaste-life—keeping away from sexual conduct entirely. The householder keeps abstention from sexual misconduct: guarding his own household, violating no one else's. The one is a whole field left behind; the other, a forbidden ground marked out.

On the householder's line, the Āgamas speak briefly. It is the *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra*, in Chinese translation, that draws the detail: no transgressing beyond one's own spouse; and not at the improper time, not in the improper place. And the same sūtra leaves one heavy sentence that belongs to this precept alone: this precept "cannot be self-recovered in a future life." Other habits—the conditions for them may never assemble again in the next life; but sexual desire is a deep root of affliction, and if it is not countered by one's own hand in this life, in the next it sprouts just the same. Of the five precepts, this one alone is given such a sentence—the weed with the deepest root must be pulled in this life.

### The False-Speech Precept: A Line at a Mouth That Can Be Trusted

The fourth line:

> He gives up false speech; utterly sincere and undeceiving, he does not delude others.

The false-speech precept guards a mouth, but its line is not done at "told no lies." The portrait's words are positive ones: utterly sincere, undeceiving, not deluding—he speaks what is true, his words hold together, he does not coax people with talk. Keep the false-speech precept all the way home, and what is grown is a mouth that others can trust. A man who will not tell a single lie—in time, when he opens his mouth, his words carry their own weight. That weight is not worked up by rhetoric; it is kept into being by the precept.

### The Intoxicant Precept: A Precept That Guards the Path

The fifth precept differs from the first four at the root.

Killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, false speech—these four are evil in themselves; whether or not any king's law happens to forbid them, they are to be kept away from. Drink is otherwise: the act of drinking is not, in itself, necessarily evil. The Buddha set a precept against it because what it loosens is the very pair of hands that keeps watch over the other four lines.

This precept the Buddha explained most thoroughly to a young man. The *Sigāla-sūtra* of the *Dīrgha Āgama* records: the Buddha was then on Vulture Peak outside Rājagaha. Early one morning, He put on His robe, took up His bowl, and entered the city for alms; and at the edge of the city He saw a young man, freshly bathed, hair and skin still wet, bowing to the six directions—east, south, west, north, above, below—one direction at a time. The Buddha walked over and asked: why are you here so early, bowing to the six directions? The young man's name was Sigāla. He answered: my father, on his deathbed, charged me to honor the six directions—I dare not fail him; only I do not know what the six directions mean.

The Buddha did not laugh at him for bowing in the dark. Taking up this theme of "the six directions," He taught over again, whole, the principles by which a householder stands and lives in the world. When He came to the roads by which a household is ruined, drinking stood at the head of the list, and He counted out six losses:

> Drinking has six failings: first, loss of wealth; second, the rise of illness; third, the rise of quarrels; fourth, the spread of ill repute; fifth, the outbreak of rage; sixth, the daily diminishment of wisdom.

The first five are still on the surface of events—wealth broken, illness, brawling, a ruined name, flaring anger. The real crux is the sixth: the daily diminishment of wisdom. Drink fogs the mind, and wisdom dulls a little more each day; and once wisdom dulls, the four lines—killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying—can none of them be watched.

The masters of later generations gave this distinction a name: killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying are called nature-precepts—evil by their very nature; the intoxicant precept is called a protective-precept—not necessarily evil in itself, established to shut out negligence. A precept that guards the path: what it guards is not the matter of "drink"; it guards the mind that must watch the lines and must cultivate concentration, keeping it from being fogged.

### Five Plots of Field, One Road

With the five lines drawn, step back and look: the killing-precept guards the body, the stealing-precept guards the hands, the sexual-misconduct precept guards the household, the false-speech precept guards the mouth, the intoxicant precept guards the mind's clarity. The five plots together enclose a householder's complete daily round—body, hands, household, mouth, mind: no spot left outside the lines, and no spot governed twice over. The accomplishment in the precepts that the Buddha named for Mahānāma holds exactly this: not more, not less, just enough for a householder to stand in the world.

This is the base held for the whole of a life—received once, received for a lifetime. Yet the Buddha's house keeps one further, subtler gate: a way for the householder, without leaving home, to borrow a brief day and night, lift this base a notch higher, and taste with his own mouth, once, the purity of the life of one gone forth.

In the next chapter, we go to the Eastern Park, to the Hall of Migāra's Mother, and watch the Buddha teach this one day and one night.
