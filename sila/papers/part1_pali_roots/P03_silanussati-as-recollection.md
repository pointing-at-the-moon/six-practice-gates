# *Sīlānussati*
**Sīla as an Object of Recollection**

*中文主題：戒隨念——戒作為憶念之對境*

## Abstract

The preceding paper (SILA-P02) argued that the eight Pāli virtues led by *akhaṇḍa* function in the four-limb stream-entry formula as accomplishment-attributes of the stream-enterer — the canonical content of the fourth *sotāpattiyaṅga*. The present paper takes the same eight virtues and reads them in a second doctrinal use: as the content the practitioner reflexively brings into recollection. Anchored in the *Mahānāmasutta* (AN 6.10), the *Paṭhamamahānāma* (AN 11.11), and *Saṃyukta-āgama* sūtra 931 (T0099, fascicle 33, Guṇabhadra trans.), the paper shows that *sīlānussati* is not the rote rehearsal of external rules but the practitioner's own already-cultivated ethical conduct, named in eight items, brought reflexively into the recollection-object position of the present moment. The same canonical formula — same eight items, same translator, same recension — bears two doctrinal uses: in P02, *subject-side* (attributes the noble disciple possesses); in this paper, *object-side* (content the noble disciple recollects). The two readings are complementary, not exclusive nor substitutive. The closing argument turns to the *Visuddhimagga*'s Chapter VII §4 *Sīlānussati-niddesa*, where Buddhaghosa names two reasons for the recollection's psychological ceiling at access concentration (*upacāra-samādhi*) rather than absorption (*appanā-samādhi*): the depth (*gambhīratā*) of the virtues themselves, and the inclination to recollect virtues of many kinds (*nānappakāra-guṇānussaraṇa-adhimuttatā*). Both reasons point to the structure of the object, not to a deficiency in the practitioner. The ceiling is therefore constitutive of what *sīlānussati* is.

**Keywords:** *sīlānussati*, *anussati*, *sati*, eight virtues, *sotāpattiyaṅga*, access concentration, *upacāra-samādhi*, absorption, *appanā-samādhi*, Guṇabhadra, AN 6.10, *Visuddhimagga* chapter VII, reflexive structure, six recollections, pre-sectarian Āgama positioning.

## I. Problem Setting

### 1.1 The lexical distinction between *sati* and *anussati*

In contemporary English-language Buddhist writing, the term *mindfulness* — disseminated since Jon Kabat-Zinn's 1979 inauguration of the Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) curriculum — has become the default lexical container for an entire family of psychological states with which the West engages Buddhist meditation. The reach of the term has come at the cost of a distinction that the Pāli canon itself carefully preserves: between *sati* and *anussati*. *Sati* is the practitioner's non-distracted holding of whatever object the present moment furnishes; it is a channel-like, receptive psychological state whose content is determined by what is immediately at hand. *Anussati* is the practitioner's directed remembering of a content already settled in the doctrinal tradition; it is an active, object-fronted psychological operation whose content is determined by the theme the practitioner has chosen to take up. The first holds the present. The second turns toward what has already been laid down.

Chinese translators of the early canon registered the same distinction at the lexical surface. *Sati* became 念 (*niàn*) — the holding of an undispersed presence — while *anussati* became 隨念 (*suí-niàn*), or more often a verb-prefixed compound such as 念戒 (recollecting ethical conduct), 念施 (recollecting generosity), 念佛 (recollecting the Buddha). The graph 念 carries the work of both, but the contexts mark which is which. *Sīlānussati* — the recollection of ethical conduct — belongs to the latter family. The practitioner reflexively brings the ethical conduct already cultivated in their own life into the recollection-object position of the present moment. When *mindfulness of precepts* is offered as a back-translation, and when the *mindfulness* of that phrase is read as the receptive channel of present-holding, the object-directed and reflexive character of *sīlānussati* is quietly erased. The entire argument of the present paper rests on the prior recovery of that distinction.

### 1.2 The Chinese reflexive structure

Guṇabhadra's fifth-century Chinese rendering of the *Saṃyukta-āgama* (T0099) registers *sīlānussati* in three sūtras of fascicle 30, and in each case the verb takes a reflexive structure. Sūtra 848 has 自念所有戒事 ("recollects their own ethical conduct in its various items"); sūtra 857 has 自持戒事 ("the ethical conduct they themselves uphold"); sūtra 858 has 自所持戒 ("the ethical conduct they themselves uphold"). The graph 自 ("self / their own") in each instance does the same grammatical work: the subject of the recollection and the holder of the recollected ethical conduct are the same person. The construction tracks the Pāli phrase *attano sīlāni anussarati* — "recollects their own ethical conduct" — where *attano* is the genitive of the reflexive pronoun. What the practitioner recollects is the ethical conduct they themselves have already cultivated; not the abstract idea of ethical conduct, not the ethical conduct of someone else, not the precepts as an external rule-set.

This reflexive structure produces a sharp contrast with the third axis of *sīla* laid out in P01 — *vinaya*, the monastic-disciplinary system as an external normative institution. *Vinaya* is what the Order codifies; *sīlānussati* is the in-the-moment psychological re-presentation of the ethical conduct the practitioner has already internalised. The grammatical work done in Chinese by a single graph 自, and in Pāli by a single genitive *attano*, is identical in both directions: it places the recollection's object inside the practitioner's own already-lived life.

### 1.3 The architectural acknowledgment of three recollection tracks

The six *anussati* enter the present four-volume project at three places, each volume taking up a different limb. Volume I (NIAN P03) treats the six recollections as a unified psychological architecture; Volume II (DĀNA P03) takes *cāgānussati*, the practitioner's reflexive recollection of generosity already given, as its specific theme; and the present paper takes *sīlānussati* as its specific theme. The three readings are complementary rather than competitive — each engages the same six-recollection formula from the angle proper to its volume's primary practice. This paper acknowledges the architecture in the body of §I but does not deploy a cross-volume polyphony footnote: the three-track structure is a feature of the project's design rather than a textual entry-point that needs adjudication.

### 1.4 Continuation from P02

P02 established that the eight Pāli virtues led by *akhaṇḍa* — *akhaṇḍa, acchidda, asabala, akammāsa, bhujissa, viññuppasattha, aparāmaṭṭha, samādhisaṃvattanika* — function in the four-limb stream-entry formula as the noble disciple's accomplishment-attributes; the eight items name a state the practitioner possesses. The present paper asks the next question: what occurs psychologically when the practitioner brings that same already-accomplished eight-fold ethical conduct into the recollection-object position of the present moment? The question is not a correction of P02 but a continuation. P02 treated the eight as predicates of the noble disciple — the subject-side reading. The present paper treats the eight as objects of recollection — the object-side reading. A single textual corpus — same translator, same recension — supports two doctrinal uses; the uses are neither exclusive nor substitutive, but stand as two psychological aspects of one canonical formula. The core argument of the paper turns on the transposition this question makes possible: once the eight virtues are read as recollection-objects, why is the resulting concentration capped at access-level rather than reaching full absorption? Where does the cap come from? §V will return to that question.

## II. Canonical Sources

### 2.1 The dual anchor: AN 6.10 and SĀ 931

The Pāli anchor for *sīlānussati* is the *Mahānāmasutta*, AN 6.10 (PTS AN iii 285ff). The Buddha, addressing the Sakyan layman Mahānāma, opens the six recollections, and the *sīlānussati* paragraph reads:

> *Puna caparaṁ, mahānāma, ariyasāvako attano sīlāni anussarati akhaṇḍāni acchiddāni asabalāni akammāsāni bhujissāni viññuppasatthāni aparāmaṭṭhāni samādhisaṁvattanikāni.*
>
> *Yasmiṁ, mahānāma, samaye ariyasāvako sīlaṁ anussarati nevassa tasmiṁ samaye rāgapariyuṭṭhitaṁ cittaṁ hoti, na dosapariyuṭṭhitaṁ cittaṁ hoti, na mohapariyuṭṭhitaṁ cittaṁ hoti; ujugatamevassa tasmiṁ samaye cittaṁ hoti sīlaṁ ārabbha.*
>
> *Ujugatacitto kho pana, mahānāma, ariyasāvako labhati atthavedaṁ, labhati dhammavedaṁ, labhati dhammūpasaṁhitaṁ pāmojjaṁ. Pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṁ vediyati, sukhino cittaṁ samādhiyati.*[^1]

Bhante Sujato's translation, released into the public domain, renders the passage:

> "Furthermore, a noble disciple recollects their own ethical conduct, which is intact, impeccable, spotless, and unmarred, liberating, praised by sensible people, not mistaken, and leading to immersion. … When they're joyful, rapture springs up. When the mind is full of rapture, the body becomes tranquil. When the body is tranquil, they feel bliss. And when they're blissful, the mind becomes immersed in samādhi."[^2]

The Chinese anchor is *Saṃyukta-āgama* sūtra 931, fascicle 33 of T0099 (Liu-Song dynasty, Guṇabhadra trans.):

> 「復次，聖弟子自念淨戒，不壞戒、不缺戒、不污戒、不雜戒、不他取戒、善護戒、明者稱譽戒、智者不厭戒。聖弟子如是念戒時，不起貪欲、瞋恚、愚癡，乃至念戒所熏，昇進涅槃。」[^3]

Both anchors carry the same eight-item formula, and both place that formula inside the same psychological pericope: in the very moment of recollection, the mind is not seized by greed, by hatred, or by delusion, and from that an ascending sequence — upright mind (*ujugata-citta*), joy (*pāmojja*), rapture (*pīti*), bodily tranquility (*passaddhi*), bliss (*sukha*), concentration (*samādhi*) — unfolds. The parallel itself is a philological fact worth pausing on: two languages, two translator-traditions (a fifth-century Chinese translator working from a Sarvāstivāda-affiliated transmission, and a Pāli recension fixed over a millennium of oral transmission within the Theravāda), preserve the same eight items in the same order embedded in the same ascending sequence. The stability across that interval secures the textual basis for the present paper's central reading. P02 has shown that this same eight-item formula can be read in its subject-side use, as the accomplishment-attributes of the noble disciple; the present paper reads the same eight items in their object-side use, as the content the noble disciple recollects. One corpus, one translator, one recension, two doctrinal uses — neither exclusive nor substitutive, but two psychological aspects of one canonical formula.

### 2.2 The optative voice and the five-faculty base

The *Paṭhamamahānāma*, AN 11.11 (PTS AN v 328), restates the same formula in the optative. The Buddha first lays out five qualities as preconditions — *saddho* (confident), *āraddhavīriyo* (energetic in striving), *upaṭṭhitassati* (with mindfulness present), *samāhito* (composed), *paññavā* (wise) — and connects them to the six recollections with a transitional clause: "Mahānāma, established in these five qualities, you should further develop six qualities." The *sīlānussati* clause's verb shifts from the indicative *anussarati* ("recollects") of AN 6.10 to the optative-imperative *anussareyyāsi* ("you should recollect") of AN 11.11. The grammatical shift carries a doctrinal weight: AN 6.10 describes the noble disciple's actual psychological state, while AN 11.11 turns the same content into an instruction for practice. The later Theravāda commentarial tradition's classification of the six recollections as *kammaṭṭhāna* — meditation-subjects — finds its most direct canonical anchor at this point. A single optative-imperative form moves the recollection from a description of an existing state to a programme that can be taught and undertaken.[^4]

### 2.3 The bedside pericope: AN 11.12 and SN 55.21–22

AN 11.12, *Dutiyamahānāma*, repeats the AN 11.11 formula, but with a different framing context: Mahānāma has just recovered from a serious illness, and the Buddha steadies his mind with the six recollections. The SN 55 *Sotāpatti-saṃyutta* preserves a closely related pair, SN 55.21–22 (also titled *Mahānāma-suttas*), set against the same illness register. The repetition of the six-recollection formula at the bedside, across multiple sūttas of two collections, shows that the formula was not held in the early tradition as a technical training-only operation. In the moment when a practitioner faces sickness, ageing, and the approach of death, the recollection of one's own already-cultivated ethical conduct is offered as a psychological anchor. Bhikkhu Anālayo's *Mindfully Facing Disease and Death* (2016) devotes a chapter to this register; §IV will respond to his reading.

### 2.4 The Chinese layout: SĀ 848 and SĀ 858

On the Chinese side, in addition to the eight-virtue formula of fascicle 33 sūtra 931, fascicle 30 holds two further passages on *sīlānussati*. Sūtra 848 — "The path to the heavens" — gives a five-virtue short form and preserves the full five-step ascending psychological chain:

> 「復次，比丘！謂聖弟子自念所有戒事，隨憶念言：『我於此不缺戒、不污戒、不雜戒、明智所歎戒、智者不厭戒。』於如是等戒事正憶念已，心生隨喜；隨喜已，歡悅；歡悅已，身猗息；身猗息已，覺受樂；覺受樂已，三昧定。」[^5]

This is the first place on the Chinese side where the ascending chain — joy, gladness, bodily ease, the experience of bliss, and *samādhi* — is laid down in full. It corresponds directly to the last five steps of the AN 6.10 nine-step sequence: *pāmojja → pīti → passaddhi → sukha → samādhi*. The list of ethical-conduct items has been shortened to five, but the ascending sequence is fully present. §V will return to the doctrinal weight of this fact.

Sūtra 858 furnishes the first Chinese statement that explicitly groups the six recollections under a single rubric, 六念 (the six recollections):

> 「佛告釋氏難提：『若見如來、若不見，若見知識比丘、若不見，汝當隨時修於六念。何等為六？當念如來、法、僧事、自所持戒、自所行施，及念諸天。』」[^6]

The reflexive constructions of sūtra 858 — 自所持戒 ("the ethical conduct they themselves uphold") and 自所行施 ("the generosity they themselves have given") — together with the 自念所有戒事 of sūtra 848 and the 自持戒事 of sūtra 857, repeat the same grammatical fact at three further sites. Across Guṇabhadra's translation, the object of *sīlānussati* is consistently set as the practitioner's own already-cultivated ethical conduct, not as the idea of ethical conduct in the abstract, nor as the ethical conduct belonging to anyone else. §1.2 has already laid out the grammatical analysis; the present paragraph notes only that the textual evidence is converging from four sites.

### 2.5 The pre-sectarian positioning: DA 9 and DA 10

The *Dīrgha-āgama* (T0001), translated into Chinese around 412–413 CE by Buddhayaśas and Zhu Fonian, descends from a Dharmaguptaka exemplar. DA 9, the *Saṅgīti-sūtra* (fascicle 8), lists "six recollections (六思念): recollection of the Buddha, of the Dharma, of the Saṅgha, of ethical conduct, of generosity, of the heavens." DA 10, the *Daśottara-sūtra* (fascicle 9), groups the six recollections under the heading "six things to be cultivated" (六修法), which corresponds to the Pāli *bhāvetabbā dhammā* — qualities to be developed. The same six-item formula, in the same order, is therefore present in two translated transmissions descended from two different schools — Dharmaguptaka on the Chinese side, Theravāda on the Pāli side. The implication is that the six-recollection formula does not originate in either school. It predates the school-period division and represents a piece of early shared material. The DA 10 classification of the six recollections under "things to be cultivated" can further be read as a pre-Visuddhimagga precursor to the *kammaṭṭhāna* classification of Chapter VII: in the early shared material, the recollections were already held as practices to be developed, not merely as devotional acts.[^7]

### 2.6 The reflexive structure: a textual closing

The Pāli *attano sīlāni anussarati* and the three Chinese reflexive forms — 自念所有戒事 (sūtra 848), 自持戒事 (sūtra 857), 自所持戒 (sūtra 858) — together provide four sites of the same grammatical fact, across two languages and two translator backgrounds. The object of *sīlānussati* is the practitioner's own already-cultivated ethical conduct. §IV will set this stable grammatical fact alongside the analyses of three modern scholars.

## III. Philological Notes

### 3.1 Three Chinese variants within one translation

Within the single Chinese translation of the *Saṃyukta-āgama* by Guṇabhadra, three distinct renderings of the *sīlānussati* eight-item formula stand alongside one another. The co-existence is itself a philological fact worth foregrounding:

| Location | Form | Items |
|----------|------|-------|
| Fascicle 30, sūtra 848 | Five-item short form | 不缺戒 · 不污戒 · 不雜戒 · 明智所歎戒 · 智者不厭戒 |
| Fascicle 33, sūtra 931 | Eight-item full form, variant α | 不壞戒 · 不缺戒 · 不污戒 · 不雜戒 · 不他取戒 · 善護戒 · 明者稱譽戒 · 智者不厭戒 |
| Fascicle 20, sūtra 550 | Eight-item full form, variant β | 不缺戒 · 不斷戒 · 純厚戒 · 不離戒 · 非盜取戒 · 善究竟戒 · 可讚歎戒 · 梵行不憎惡戒 |

Variant β is spoken by Mahākātyāyana (in the fascicle 20 sūtra), and its surface vocabulary diverges noticeably from variant α: 不壞戒 / 不缺戒 in the leading position, 不他取戒 / 非盜取戒 in the middle, 明者稱譽戒 / 可讚歎戒 and 智者不厭戒 / 梵行不憎惡戒 at the end. That a single translator's translation of a single canonical collection preserves three variants of the same formula side by side suggests that the Chinese-side rendering of the eight virtues of *sīla* was never locked into one canonical surface form. The list is, on the Chinese side, a multi-variant reading-frame: different variants are activated in different conversational contexts, and they do not need to compete for the title of "correct version." This multiplicity is itself the documentary base of the present section.

### 3.2 The two readings of *bhujissa* and *aparāmaṭṭha*

Two of the eight items have generated philological branching in the Theravāda commentarial tradition.

*Bhujissa*, the fifth item, literally signifies "[that which makes one] a free person" — the antonym of *taṇhā-dāsya*, "enslavement to craving." Bhante Sujato's English glosses it functionally as *liberating*; Guṇabhadra's 不他取戒 ("ethical conduct not appropriated by another") preserves more of the literal sociological texture, the ancient Indian slave-and-freedman context in which the term originally took shape.

*Aparāmaṭṭha*, the seventh item — formed from the verb *parāmasati*, "to grasp at, to take hold of" — has two parallel commentarial readings. The *Visuddhimagga*'s reading takes "not grasped by craving and views" (*taṇhā-diṭṭhi-aparāmaṭṭha*) as the substance; the *sotāpattiyaṅga* reading takes "not mistakenly grasped as the sufficient condition of liberation" — i.e. not converted into *sīlabbata-parāmāsa*, the second of the three fetters whose abandonment is constitutive of stream-entry. Guṇabhadra's 善護戒 ("well-guarded ethical conduct") aligns more closely with the first reading. P02 §IV has already noted this philological branching; the present section does not develop the issue further. The deep treatment of *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* belongs to later papers.

### 3.3 The *Ekottara-āgama* structural difference

The Chinese tradition also preserves an *Ekottara-āgama* (T0125), translated by Gautama Saṅghadeva in the Eastern Jin period and traditionally associated with Mahāsāṅghika-affiliated transmission. Its handling of *sīlānussati* in fascicle 2 does not deploy the eight-item list; it uses metaphor-clusters — "ethical conduct as a body-adorning garland," "ethical conduct as the auspicious vase," and other similes — instead of an enumerated formula. The divergence is not a translator's stylistic preference but reflects a real textual variation in the underlying transmission. The Mahāsāṅghika-adjacent base text and the Sarvāstivāda-system *Saṃyukta-āgama* base text handled *sīlānussati* with different structures — an enumerated formula and a metaphor-cluster — that stood side by side rather than overriding one another. The present paper does not develop the comparison further; it belongs to subsequent work on inter-school differences in the Chinese Āgama transmissions.

### 3.4 The non-transparency of one-to-one mapping

As P02 §IV already laid out, the surface-level one-to-one correspondence between the Pāli eight items and any single Chinese eight-item rendering is not fully transparent. The present paper continues to honour that reservation and adds one further note: given that three distinct variants of the eight-item formula co-exist within the single Guṇabhadra translation, the proper unit of comparison on the Chinese side is the set of three variants, not any single rendering taken in isolation. The selection of sūtra 931 as the Chinese anchor in §2.1 was motivated by its full eight-item form most directly paralleling the Pāli AN 6.10 eight items, but the choice is not a claim that sūtra 931 is the sole Chinese standard.

## IV. Dialogue with Modern Scholarship

### 4.1 Anālayo: two layers of cross-textual philology

Bhante Anālayo's work supplies two contributions that the present paper takes up in full. The first is the strict lexical distinction between *sati* and *anussati*: *sīlānussati* is the directed remembering of a settled content, not the receptive holding of present-moment objects. The second is the cross-traditional philological finding that the six-recollection list appears in mutual agreement across the Theravāda Pāli corpus and the Chinese Āgama corpus, placing the formula's origin earlier than the school-period division. §2.5 of the present paper, with its evidence from DA 9 and DA 10, takes that pre-sectarian positioning as its own ground. The paper also accepts Anālayo's reading of the bedside cluster: AN 11.12 and SN 55.21–22 should not be read as a late therapeutic gloss but as a context-bearing function carried within the canon itself — the recollection of one's own already-cultivated ethical conduct providing psychological steadiness in the face of illness, ageing, and death. Anālayo's *Saṃyukta-āgama Studies* (2015) furnishes the comparative philology on which the dual-anchor mode of §II rests.

Yet Anālayo's analysis operates primarily at the level of the list's structure and at the level of its affective deployment. The present paper's question — what is the inner psychological structure of Guṇabhadra's eight-item list when it functions as a recollection-object — sits a layer finer than that. This is not a correction but an extension: taking the cross-textual stability documented in Anālayo's work as the textual base, the present paper reads the inner enactive structure of the eight items.

### 4.2 Gethin: the survey of faculties and the *bodhi-pakkhiya* architecture

Rupert Gethin's *Buddhist Path to Awakening* (Brill 1992 / Oneworld 2001) provides two structural orientations the present paper takes up. The first is the placement of *sati*, among the five faculties (*saddhā, viriya, sati, samādhi, paññā*), as the channel of non-distracted holding for an object the practitioner has selected. The second is the placement of the six recollections within the *bodhi-pakkhiya-dhammā* — the architecture of the qualities conducive to awakening — such that *sīlānussati* is not a peripheral devotional act but a structural component of the integral path. The paper also takes up his character-ethics reading of *sīla* (already adopted in P01 §4.2): *sīla* is internalised character, not rule-following. Gethin's chapter 4 on the five faculties and his chapter 7 on the six recollections within the *bodhi-pakkhiya* survey furnish the structural ground for placing *sīlānussati* as an internal component of the path rather than as an external devotional gesture.

Gethin's analysis operates primarily at the level of "which component belongs to which system." The inner mechanism by which each of the eight items is reflexively re-enacted by the practitioner in the moment of recollection — the fine-grained psychological layer — is not his level of focus. The present paper supplies that layer; it does not displace his structural survey.

### 4.3 Bodhi: translation register and commentarial footnote

Bhikkhu Bodhi's translations and anthologies supply three foundations. The first is the rendering of *anussati* into English as *recollection*, strictly distinguished from the *mindfulness* that renders *sati*. This convention is the contemporary anglophone scholarly base, and §1.1 of the present paper rests directly on it. The second is the editor's introductions to *The Numerical Discourses of the Buddha* (Wisdom 2012) chapters 6 and 11, and to the *Sotāpatti-saṃyutta* in *The Connected Discourses of the Buddha* (Wisdom 2000), which mark the canonical positioning of the six recollections clearly. The third is the fine-grained literal glossing in Bodhi's footnotes — for example, *bhujissa* glossed as "making one a freeman," preserving the sociological texture of the slave-and-freedman context. The work is precise where translation precision is most needed.

Bodhi's labour reaches the level of translation register and footnote-gloss. The enactive value of Guṇabhadra's eight Chinese phrases as recollection-content within the inner life of a Chinese-tradition practitioner — independent of any back-mapping to the Pāli register — is not the level his apparatus is designed to reach. The present paper extends in that direction: reading Guṇabhadra's eight phrases as performative recollection-content rather than as a descriptive list waiting for verification against another tradition.

### 4.4 A three-scholar synthesis

Across the three readings — Anālayo's cross-traditional philology, Gethin's structural survey, Bodhi's translation register — the present paper takes a further step. It reads Guṇabhadra's eight phrases as recollection-content; it reads *sīlānussati* as the in-the-moment reflexive re-enactment of the psychological character to which those eight phrases point. Character, on this reading, is not described from outside; it is restarted from inside as a present occurrence. The recollection *is* the operation of character in the moment. The three modern scholars' labour reaches the external survey of text and structure; the present paper takes that as ground and enters the inner enactive layer. The canonical authority for that inner layer is the closing of *Visuddhimagga* Chapter VII §4, to which §V now turns.

## V. The Constitutive Ceiling

### 5.1 Buddhaghosa's two reasons

In the closing of Chapter VII §4 (*Sīlānussati-niddesa*) of the *Visuddhimagga*, Buddhaghosa names a specific ceiling for *sīlānussati* and gives explicit reasons. The paraphrase follows Bhikkhu Ñāṇamoli's English (PTS p. 222): when the practitioner takes the eight virtues as the object of recollection of their own ethical conduct, the mind in that moment is not seized by greed, hatred, or delusion; it is upright; the five hindrances are temporarily quieted; the *jhāna*-factors arise together. And yet —

> because of the depth (*gambhīratā*) of the virtues themselves, and because of the practitioner's inclination toward recollecting virtues of many kinds (*nānappakāra-guṇānussaraṇa-adhimuttatā*), this concentration does not arrive at full absorption; it stops at access. (Paraphrase, after Ñāṇamoli.)[^8]

Both reasons point to the structure of the object. The depth-quality of the virtues themselves is not a feature of the practitioner's mental capacity but of what is being recollected. The inclination to recollect virtues of many kinds is not a deficiency of focus but the structural shape of the recollection: the practitioner attends to eight distinct items, each of which carries its own enactive character. The contrast with the *kasiṇa* meditation-subjects (the disk-like uniform colour-and-form objects of Chapter IV) is sharp. A *kasiṇa* presents a single, homogeneous perceptual object; concentration on a *kasiṇa* can pass into absorption. *Sīlānussati* presents a structurally plural object — eight virtues, each distinct in content — and the recollection accordingly halts at access. The boundary between access and absorption, in this register, is not a question of how hard the practitioner is working. It is a question of what shape the object has.

### 5.2 An independent witness: the Chinese five-step chain

§2.4 has already quoted the *Saṃyukta-āgama* sūtra 848 passage, in which the practitioner's *sīlānussati* unfolds in a fully articulated five-step psychological chain: gladness, joy, bodily ease, the experience of bliss, and *samādhi*. The chain preserves the last five of the nine steps of AN 6.10 (*pāmojja → pīti → passaddhi → sukha → samādhi*), and the Chinese line independently of the Pāli commentarial tradition reaches the point of *samādhi*. The presence of *samādhi* in the Chinese pericope is significant because it furnishes an independent witness: the psychological altitude of *sīlānussati* does in fact reach what the Chinese rendering names "*samādhi*" — but the semantic latitude of the Chinese term, like the latitude of the Pāli *samādhi*, covers both the access and the absorption registers without itself adjudicating the distinction. The discrimination between access and absorption requires the systematic framework of the *Visuddhimagga*, and the Chinese Āgama does not deploy that framework precisely because the Āgama is canonical scripture rather than commentarial exposition. The independent reach of "*samādhi*" on the Chinese side is therefore consistent with the *Visuddhimagga*'s positioning of *sīlānussati* at access-level concentration: both speak of *samādhi*; the more granular question of which *samādhi* register is involved is settled only at the commentarial layer.

### 5.3 The ceiling as constitutive feature

P02 §V already noted, in its third correction-paragraph, that the recollection of ethical conduct does not reach absorption. P02 left that observation as a marker but did not argue the positive ground for the ceiling. The present paper supplies that ground. The ceiling is not a deficiency in the practitioner's training; it is structurally entailed by the shape of the object. Eight virtues, distinct from one another in content, must be held simultaneously or in sequence within the practitioner's attentional field, and a multiplicity of distinct items cannot be condensed into a single homogeneous perceptual object. *Sīlānussati* therefore reaches access and not absorption. The ceiling is constitutive of what the practice is: it is an internal feature of "a recollection that takes multiple distinct virtues as its object," not an external limit waiting to be overcome. If a practitioner wishes for absorption, they must take up a meditation-subject of single, homogeneous content — a *kasiṇa*, or the in-and-out breath (*ānāpānasati*) under its absorption-aimed protocols. The difference is not a hierarchy among practices but a division of labour among objects of different structural shapes. The specific function *sīlānussati* performs — the reflexive re-presentation of the eight virtues at access-level concentration — is precisely what absorption cannot deliver, and that gives the practice its own irreducible position among the *kammaṭṭhāna*.

---

## VI. Conclusion

In the moment of *sīlānussati*, the eight items are neither a cognitive list of nouns nor a self-audit against eight rules. They are the practitioner's already-cultivated ethical conduct reflexively brought into the recollection-object position of the present moment. The recollection of the eight — unbroken, unbroken-in-detail, unstained, unmottled, not-grasped-by-others, well-guarded, praised-by-the-bright, wise-not-weary — is not a descriptive statement about an absent state of character. It is the present-moment re-firing of the character-state itself. Recollection *is* the operation of character in the moment.

The closing line of Guṇabhadra's sūtra 931 — 念戒所熏，昇進涅槃 ("perfumed by the recollection of ethical conduct, advancing toward *nibbāna*") — sets the structure in a motion-verb register: the recollection of ethical conduct is not a static description but a directional momentum, a step-by-step ascent in the direction of *nibbāna*. This is the irreducible enactive structure of the *sīla* limb among the six recollections. The next paper (P04) will take up *ekaggatā*, single-pointedness — the structure that takes a single homogeneous object — and read it against the present paper's multi-object structure. The two structures stand in necessary contrast; the access-versus-absorption boundary, read from the side of the object, is constituted precisely by their difference. *Sīlānussati* reaches access and halts there; the function *sīlānussati* serves is completed within access. That is the standing position of the *sīla* limb of the six recollections.
