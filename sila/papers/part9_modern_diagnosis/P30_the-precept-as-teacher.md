# The Precept as Teacher
**Sīla as Daily Art**
*中文主題：以戒為師——戒之日用*

**Date:** June 2026

## Abstract

Across twenty-nine papers this volume has carried the question "what is sīla?" from the structural anatomy of the Pāli Vinaya to the sign-less precept of the Chan tradition. The closing question, however, is not *what* but *where*: now that the doctrine is in hand, where does the keeping of precepts finally come to rest? The answer offered here is that it rests not in *understanding* (view, wisdom) but in *daily doing*—the literal sense of "practice" in the *Six Practice Gates*. Everything the prior twenty-nine papers established—the three axes of intention, reception, and restraint; the twin precepts of reception and conduct; the seed-nature of precept-substance; the sign-less keeping; the four faces of the perfected precept—is here gathered into a single repeatable daily art. Its scriptural pivot is drawn from the Buddha's last instruction before parinirvāṇa: once the teacher is gone, the precept itself stands in the teacher's place as the practitioner's daily master—the precept as teacher. The daily precept opens of itself toward concentration—"grounded in this precept, the concentrations arise"—but the load-bearing mechanism by which precept founds concentration was settled earlier in this volume; the closer only lays this final interface-brick and points the direction toward the volume on *Samādhi*, re-arguing nothing. This is a sealing paper: carrying forward the prior two papers' discipline of "diagnosing without prescribing," it turns here to "sealing without legislating anew." Save for one freshly cited scriptural anchor, all references point back to earlier papers.

> **A note on terms.** The expressions this paper leans on to gather the volume—**daily art**, **sīla as daily life**, **daily doing rather than understanding**, **sealing without legislating anew**, and *the precept as teacher* taken as a closing thesis-pivot—are analytic framings this paper coins, borrowed by analogy from the Western discussion of "philosophy as a way of life" and its *askesis* (the daily exercise that turns a teaching into a transformed life). They are *not* scriptural terms, and the reader should not take them for the plain word of the canon. What scripture supplies is only this: *taking the precept as one's teacher* (以戒為師), *grounded in this precept the concentrations arise*, and *restrain it to one point and nothing is left undone*—all from the *Sūtra of the Buddha's Bequeathed Teaching*. Throughout, where this paper reads a further layer into these passages, it says so in its own voice; it does not let its readings pass as the canon's own statement.

## I. The Question: The Doctrine Is in Hand—Where Does It Land?

This volume has now run through twenty-nine papers. From sīla in the Pāli canon read as a *faculty*, a structure of capacity rather than a checklist of prohibitions, to the undertaking and observance laid side by side across the three sectarian recensions of the Vinaya; from the bodhisattva precept received as a gate, to the perfected-teaching precept-substance probed down into the wholesome seed within the ālaya; and on to the sign-less keeping established by the Prajñā line—*keep in full, grasp no sign*—the question "what is sīla?" has been ground bright from many sides. A reader who has come this far holds in hand a fairly complete doctrine of the precept.

For just this reason, the sealing paper cannot make its task "to answer one more question." A book that is still adding bricks at its final paper has tacitly conceded that the destination of precept-study is the accumulation of knowledge—and that is exactly the wrong turning this volume's earlier papers named and meant to guard against. The preceding paper closed on an unfinished spot: when the present age draws off the *intention* (思) of keeping precepts and leaves only the husk of "speech" and "conduct"—whether by reducing the precept to a psychological technique or by handing the norm over to a machine that has no karma of intention—then, with the precept's root in volition absent, what remains is no longer the precept.[^1] That paper diagnosed the gap and deliberately wrote no prescription. The sealing question takes up from there: the gap having been marked, the doctrine being complete, where, in the end, does the keeping of precepts come to rest?

This question cannot be answered by erecting one more piece of doctrine, for this volume's doctrine is already abundant—abundant to the point of breeding the very two failure modes the earlier papers named: mistaking the precept for a system of rules that is settled once one has "understood it through," or elevating it into an ethical theory that runs only on the plane of speculation.[^2] The two look opposite and share one disease: both keep the precept circling on the plane of *understanding*, slow to come down to *doing*. One who has read thirty papers and still merely "understands a great deal" is not necessarily nearer to the precept than one who cannot read a word and keeps it day by day—a hard saying, but the one the sealing paper cannot avoid.

So the answer this paper gives is plain: the whole doctrine of the precept comes to rest at last not in *understanding* (view, wisdom) but in *daily doing* (practice). That the *Six Practice Gates* is named a gate of *practice* and not a gate of *view* lies just here—what the six gates gather are things one can *do* day by day, not principles one can *think* day by day. View and wisdom have their own gates (this series treats the face of *view* in a separate volume); and the precept, standing fourth among the practice gates, can have for its doctrinal terminus only a thing to be done day by day and which must be done day by day. The doctrine is not discarded here but *sealed*: the whole meaning of its existence is to let the "daily doing" be done in accordance with the dharma, done without deviation.

This paper follows that single thread, gathering the prior twenty-nine papers into one *daily art*. The phrase "daily art" is an analytic term this paper coins, set up by analogy with the Western line on "philosophy as a way of life" and its discussion of *askesis* (the daily discipline of self-cultivation); it is not a phrase the canon itself holds, and wherever it appears below it carries only the layer of sense this paper reads out—so noted here once and for all.[^3] And the sealing paper, being a gathering, keeps a discipline of one kind with the prior two papers: where they "diagnosed without prescribing," this one "seals without legislating anew." Save for one freshly cited scriptural anchor, all the doctrine below points back to earlier papers; it re-argues nothing, kindles no new fire, and least of all writes itself into a manual of "how to keep precepts day by day"—this volume has been descriptive and scholarly from first to last, and so is its close.

## II. The Scriptural Ground: The Precept as Teacher

The scriptural pivot of the sealing paper is drawn from a sūtra this volume has not yet touched—the *Sūtra of the Teaching Briefly Spoken as the Buddha Was About to Enter Parinirvāṇa*, commonly the *Sūtra of the Buddha's Bequeathed Teaching*.[^4] To choose this sūtra as the closing anchor is no accident: it is the Buddha's last instruction before parinirvāṇa, and its setting is precisely the setting of the sealing question—the teacher is about to depart the world; how are the disciples to practice, day by day, thereafter? A book that has discoursed on the precept all the way to its final paper faces, it turns out, the very question a teacher faces at his death. The sūtra's answer is this:

> Bhikṣus, after my passing, you should honor and revere the Prātimokṣa, as one who meets a light in the dark, as a poor man who finds a treasure. Know that this is your great teacher, no different from my remaining in the world.[^5]

The Prātimokṣa (the precept code, the precepts of individual liberation) is here set up as "your great teacher"—your teacher. Its context is locked tight: "after my passing." While the Buddha is in the world, the disciples take the Buddha for teacher; once the Buddha has passed, once the Buddha no longer abides in the world, the precept itself stands in the teacher's place and serves as the practitioner's daily master. The sūtra pairs two similes to give the measure of it: as one in the dark suddenly meets a lamp, as a poor man suddenly finds a treasure—what these two figure is not a recondite doctrine to be studied out, but a thing usable on the spot, a thing to be leaned on day by day. A lamp is for walking by; a treasure is for living by. The precept as teacher is just such a living thing, to be leaned on day by day for what to take up and what to set down. This is the *precept as teacher* this paper takes for its pivot.

One misreading must first be barred. "Taking the precept as one's teacher" is in no way a discarding of the teacher, a turning against transmission. What the sūtra speaks of is the daily function the precept assumes *after the teacher has passed*—when the human teacher one could question face to face is gone, the precept itself can still day by day point out what to take and what to leave, can still regulate the going forward and the holding back, as a lamp met in the dark, as a treasure found in poverty. It fills the vacancy left by the teacher's passing; it does not negate the lineage of transmission itself. What this paper takes from "the precept as teacher" for its sealing pivot is exactly this layer: the precept can serve as a daily teacher because it can be kept day by day, re-enacted day by day in present action—and that is just where the two words "daily life" come to land. A human teacher is a teacher because he can point the way day by day; the precept can assume the teacher's office after the teacher's passing because it too can point the way day by day. The office of teacher lies not in personhood but in that function of being "leanable, day by day, for going forward and holding back"; the precept happens to possess that function, and so it can, after the teacher's passing, carry the office on.[^6]

The same sūtra has another line that joins the precept naturally to concentration: "grounded in this precept, the concentrations arise, and the wisdom that extinguishes suffering," and it calls the precept "the foremost dwelling-place of peace and merit."[^7] The sequence by which precept gives rise to concentration and concentration to wisdom, and the mechanism by which the precept serves as the load-bearing ground of concentration, were settled in this volume's earlier papers;[^8] here it is sounded only as a closing echo, and its load-bearing interface is not re-argued—see chapter VI below. The sūtra has, besides, a figure that gathers the restraint of body and mind: "let this mind go and it loses a man's good works; restrain it to one point and nothing is left undone,"[^9] a line held to the very end of the sealing paper, set there as a signpost pointing toward the volume on *Samādhi*.

## III. The Core Argument: Sīla as Daily Life

This chapter is the sealing paper's main field, in three sections: first, gathering the prior twenty-nine papers into one *daily art*; second, drawing together the whole of the perfected precept as shown by the three sūtras; third, taking up the "daily-life division of labor" the preceding paper left over, by which the two failure modes find their resolution in daily practice. All three are gatherings; they point back to earlier papers and do not restate what those papers established.

### (1) Sīla as a Repeatable Daily Art

Set this volume's doctrines out in a row and one sees that they are not, in fact, several juxtaposed teachings but different faces of one and the same thing—and that thing is something to be *done*, day by day. The sealing paper can fold twenty-nine papers into a single sentence on just this strength: they were all pointing, all along, at one place to stand.

First, the precept has its three axes of *intention, reception, restraint*: the root of the precept is in the volition of mental karma; the precept must be received in order to be established; the precept, in present action, restrains and stops wrongdoing.[^10] These three are not three stages of theory but three faces turning together within daily life—every act of going forward or holding back in accordance with the dharma is intention setting it in motion, the precept-substance established by reception in present operation, restraint taking effect on the spot. The three keep no order of before and after, and none may be lacking: lacking intention, the precept becomes a mindless husk; lacking reception, the precept has no substance to rest on; lacking restraint, the precept fails to land on the present choice of what to take and leave. In daily life the three axes move all at once.

Second comes the division of the twin precepts, reception and conduct: the precept must first be received, and thereafter, in the keeping of each day, be conformed to and guarded.[^11] Reception is a one-time founding; conduct is the day-after-day re-enactment. This distinction is of the first importance for the two words "daily life"—it tells us that the precept is not a thing settled once and for all on the day of ordination; reception is only the beginning, and the precept truly dwells in that word *conduct*, in the guarding of every day after reception. A person who has received the precept and never conducts it is scarcely different from one who never received it at all; the whole weight of "daily life" bears down on that one word, *conduct*.

Third comes the very substance of the precept—Daoxuan's perfected-teaching judgment that the ultimate of precept-substance is the wholesome seed within the ālaya, the storehouse consciousness; and this volume read out, with the matured Yogācāra account of seed, perfuming, manas, and the turning of the basis, the deep form and ultimate orientation of that precept-seed.[^12] If the precept-substance is a wholesome seed perfumed into being at the moment of reception and abiding thereafter, then the meaning of "keeping the precept" at the level of substance is not the guarding of an external clause but the daily re-perfuming of that seed into present action. A seed long out of operation is like grain long unplanted: the seed-nature is there, but its force grows daily fainter. The daily conduct is the daily perfuming; "daily life" at the level of substance is keeping the precept-seed never apart from present action. This answers, too, a question often overlooked: why must the precept be kept day by day and not once for all? Because the precept-substance is a living seed, not a dead contract—a contract signed is valid forever; a seed must go into the field season by season.

Last, and most crucial, is the sign-less keeping established by the Prajñā line: *keep in full, grasp no sign*.[^13] The precept must be done with full effort day by day, yet without grasping, in the act, at the sign of "I am keeping the precept," "I am already pure." This layer keeps the "daily art" from souring into two adulterations: souring into mechanical repetition—done day by day with the heart absent, the precept becomes an empty-turning ritual; or souring into self-satisfied merit-hoarding—done day by day with merit secretly tallied, the precept becomes a hotbed of conceit. The sign-less keeping bars both faces: it asks you to keep in full day by day (barring the first slackness) and to keep without abiding in the sign (barring the second conceit). The daily precept is both kept in full and kept without abiding; the two do not obstruct each other but are the inside and outside of one keeping—keeping in full is its *daily-life* face, grasping no sign is its *art* face (an art being that which, mastered to the utmost, forgets it is an art, done without the doer being aware of doing).

Take these four faces together: the precept is a thing rooted in mental karma (intention), established by reception (reception), restraining wrong in present action (restraint), re-enacted in daily conformity (conduct), whose substance is a wholesome seed awaiting daily perfuming (precept-substance), and which is done without grasping its sign (sign-less). It is not a doctrine settled once "understood through," but an art to be done anew day by day, and which can be done anew day by day. The prior twenty-nine papers seem each to speak of one matter, but gathered up they describe different faces of one daily art: some papers speak of its root (intention), some of its founding (reception, precept-substance), some of its guarding (conduct, restraint), some of its temper (sign-less). This is what this paper means by "sīla as daily life": the doctrinal terminus of the precept is a daily art, not a body of knowledge.

### (2) The Three-Sūtra Perfected Precept Gathered: Basis, Gate, Mode, Stage

This volume's Part VIII shows the whole of the precept through three great sūtras. The sealing paper gathers it here in a single phrase, the four faces seen together, without restating each sūtra in turn—the detailed treatment of each was borne separately by this volume's earlier papers.[^14]

First, the *Śūraṅgama* shows the precept's *basis* and *gate*. The precept is the load-bearing ground of concentration—the Four Clear Instructions on Purity are the foundation that precedes samādhi; and the precept is itself one gate among the twenty-five modes of perfect penetration, ranking with the rest—keeping the precept is itself a path of perfect penetration to be entered directly, not merely a preparation for other gates. One thing bears two senses at once: as basis it consents to lie beneath other dharmas; as gate it is of itself a path of direct entry. Second, the *Lotus* shows the precept's *mode*. The precept in this gate is "the precept of the practice gate": its governing verb is "one should abide in," not "one should keep away from"—the precept does not stop at the negative departing from evil but is the positive conforming-cultivation toward the way, so that even its prohibitive face is turned out, from within, into practice. Third, the *Huayan* shows the precept's *stage*. The precept has its "position-stage" within the bodhisattva path: though it is especially heightened in the Stage of Freedom from Defilement (the second bhūmi), it pervades all ten bhūmis, deepening or shallowing with the practitioner's strength and stage—the precept is not a fixed quantity set once at reception, but a living quantity that deepens with the ground.

The four faces fold into one phrase: the perfected precept is at once the *basis* of concentration and itself a *gate* of perfect penetration (Śūraṅgama); its *mode* is the precept of the practice gate, a conforming-cultivation toward the way (Lotus); its *depth* is the precept of position-stage, deepening with the ground (Huayan). And this answers exactly to the "daily art": basis and gate say it can serve as a daily point of entry—it can be guarded daily as the basis of other dharmas, and entered daily as a gate in itself; the mode of the practice gate says it is conforming-cultivation and not mere departure from evil—the daily doing is a going-forward practice, not only a stepping-back avoidance; the stage of position says it deepens with the daily doing—today's precept and the precept of ten years hence are the same in quantity but not in depth, and that increment of depth is just what the daily doing wins. The whole of the perfected precept, brought down into daily life, is an art that deepens as it is done and can be entered anew, day by day.

### (3) The Daily-Life Division of Labor: The Two Failure Modes Resolved in Daily Practice

This volume's earlier papers diagnosed two failure modes in the keeping of precepts and left their "resolution" expressly to the sealing paper.[^15] It is gathered here now.

The two failure modes are rooted alike in drawing the precept out of its living frame and keeping it as a dead thing—either grasping the precept-sign as ultimate (taking the regulation itself for the way, the disease of clinging to rule-and-vow) or stripping out the precept's root in mental karma and keeping only the husk. Their resolution lies not in erecting one more new doctrine but in turning the aforesaid living frame of "intention–reception–precept-substance" over, alive, day by day—this is the "daily-life division of labor": within daily life, intention sets in motion the root of mental karma, the precept-substance established by reception re-enacts in present action, restraint calibrates on the spot the choice of what to take and leave, the three each keeping their office and collaborating day by day. When the precept is thus turned over alive day by day, it will not congeal into a dead sign available for grasping (the disease of clinging to rule-and-vow has nothing to attach to—for the precept, in its daily living turn, never has time to set into a graspable sign), nor be stripped into a thoughtless empty husk (the root of mental karma is always present—for intention is in motion day by day, and the husk is never once parted from its root).

Here one can see a plain conclusion of the sealing paper: the true antidote to the two failure modes is not more doctrine but the setting of the existing frame back into daily life. Doctrine slips into failure not, as a rule, because it is insufficient, but because it is left on the plane of *understanding* and never set back onto the plane of *doing* to be turned over alive. Once it is set back into daily life, intention, reception, precept-substance, and restraint each return to their office and collaborate day by day, and the failure modes have nowhere to attach. This is the living sense of the phrase "sealing without legislating anew": what the sealing paper can do is not to hand the reader one more set of new tools, but to ask the reader to take up the tools already in hand and use them, day by day.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Engagement: Three "Practice-as-Life" Approaches and What Each Lacks

Set "sīla as daily life" against the contemporary scholarly field and one finds three neighboring approaches that likewise stress "practice as life," yet each lacking one face—and so throwing into relief what is distinctive in the precept this paper reads. The three below are all fresh references for the sealing paper; each is taken for one face, in brief.

First, Pierre Hadot on ancient philosophy as a "way of life."[^16] He shows that the core of ancient philosophy lay not in a theoretical system in the head but in daily-exercised disciplines of self-cultivation, by which a doctrine was turned into a transformed life—a doctrine that stays in text and disputation is not yet philosophy; only when one exercises it daily into a life does it count. This chimes exactly with the import of "daily art": the doctrine must be turned into a daily doing to be real, or else it falls into the "understanding without doing" this paper's first chapter rebuked. Yet the discipline he describes is the self-therapy of an autonomous philosopher upon himself—an exercise the philosopher designs for himself and administers to himself; it has no category of "after the teacher's passing, the precept stands in for the teacher." The daily precept is not a discipline the philosopher autonomously designs, but the constant re-enactment, after the teacher's passing, of a precept that was received—its office of teacher belongs to the precept, not to the practitioner's own autonomous planning. Hadot's discipline keeps the philosopher himself as its subject throughout; this paper's daily precept has for its subject that "teacher-substituting" precept. The dimension of "taking the precept as teacher" Hadot's frame cannot register.

Second, Justin McDaniel on the turn modern Buddhist studies should make toward "everyday Buddhism."[^17] He argues for shifting the object of study from doctrine and canon to the practices people actually carry out day by day—not only to read in the sūtras and treatises "how one ought," but to look at how the faithful actually "do, day by day." This accords with this paper's direction, "the doctrine comes to rest at last in daily doing": both move the center of gravity from text to daily life. Yet his "everyday Buddhism" is an ethnographic description, deliberately suspending the evaluation of norm and liberation, recording only how people actually do without asking whether the doing is in accord with the dharma, whether it draws near the way; it does not single the precept out as an art with an inner cultivational logic of its own. This paper reads the daily precept as an art of self-cultivation that bears a norm and opens toward concentration—it has right and wrong (in accord with the dharma or not), it has direction (toward concentration, toward wisdom)—not as one value-neutral descriptive item among the practices a field-worker finds. McDaniel sees the "daily," but suspends the norm and the direction that the "art" contains.

Third, Saba Mahmood on ethical formation as an embodied, repeated, disciplinary practice.[^18] She shows that virtue is not first an inner thing later expressed in action but the reverse—the outward, repeated performance is itself the place where inner virtue is generated. This answers to this paper's "the daily doing is the cultivation itself," and chimes too, faintly, with the not-abiding face of the "sign-less keeping": for her as for this paper, the doing is itself the cultivation, not the expression of it. Yet her frame is a double model of "habituation plus traditional authority"—virtue habituated through bodily repetition, direction given by the authority of tradition. This model lacks the dimension of emptiness that this volume's sign-less line brings in: the daily precept both keeps in full and grasps no sign, and this double of "both keeping in full and grasping no sign" her habituation-authority model cannot register at once. It can account for how repeated performance shapes virtue (the keeping-in-full face it has), but cannot say how "keeping in full while grasping no sign of the keeping" is possible (the grasping-no-sign face it lacks)—in a pure habituation-authority model, the longer one does, the deeper the sign of "I am a rule-keeping person" only grows, whereas the sign-less keeping wants exactly the reverse: the more practiced the doing, the fewer the traces of "I am doing" it leaves.

The three each draw one step near and each lack one face. Pull the lens back: scholarship's handling of the precept does not, on the whole, exceed two readings—it reads the precept either as a system of rules (the Vinaya-studies approach—the precept is clauses, and the question is the clauses' provenance and force) or as an ethical theory (the virtue-debate approach—the precept is a moral claim, and the question is its standing in ethics). Even the three who turn toward "practice as life" give, respectively, the self-therapy of an autonomous philosopher (Hadot), the ethnographic inventory of practices (McDaniel), or the bodily habituation under traditional authority (Mahmood). Set these approaches side by side and a common vacancy appears: no one reads the precept as *this* thing—a daily art received after the teacher's passing, re-enacted day by day, taken as one's teacher, and at once kept in full and grasping no sign. The rule-system reading gives the precept its "clause-character" but loses its "daily-character"; the ethical-theory reading gives the precept its "norm-character" but loses its "doable-day-by-day-character"; the three of practice-as-life give the precept its "daily-character" but lose, respectively, "the precept as teacher," "the inner cultivational logic," and "the sign-less." The precept this paper reads is just the thing that joins these several faces in one body. The place this reading occupies is exactly the vacancy this volume's sealing means to mark.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

Several deviations easily arise at a volume's close; each is corrected here in turn.

❌ **Mistaking "daily art" for a term the canon itself holds.** "Daily art" and "daily precept" are analytic terms this paper coins, set up by analogy with the Western *askesis* line only (so marked in chapter I). What scripture says is "take the precept as your teacher" and "grounded in this precept the concentrations arise"; it does not say "the precept is an art." The layer this paper reads out must be kept clearly apart from what scripture plainly states: what scripture plainly states is quoted directly; what this paper reads out must be marked with the locutions "this paper holds," "this paper reads as"—the two are not to be mixed.

❌ **Misreading "the precept as teacher" as discarding the teacher, turning against transmission.** The sūtra locks the context tight at "after my passing": what the precept stands in for is the daily teacher's office *after the teacher's passing*, not the cancellation of the lineage of transmission. While a teacher may be questioned, take the teacher as teacher; after the teacher's passing, take the precept as teacher—the two are the same single practice borne under different circumstances, not mutually exclusive. This paper in no way hints that a practitioner may discard the teacher and go his own way; on the contrary, "the precept as teacher" is precious precisely because the teacher is hard to meet and is not always present, while the precept can accompany one day by day.

❌ **Misreading "sīla as daily life" as a manual of precept-keeping.** This paper carries forward the prior two papers' discipline of "diagnosing without prescribing," turning at the close to "sealing without legislating anew." It lays out no steps for "how to keep precepts day by day," erects no normative procedure of keeping—that would overstep this volume's descriptive limit set from the start, and would violate the sealing paper's proper office. The work of the close is to set the established frame back into daily life, not to erect new law; to point out that "the precept comes to rest in daily doing" is one thing, to prescribe "how you should do it day by day" is another, and this paper does only the former.

⚠️ **The precept-gives-rise-to-concentration passage is sounded as an echo only, not gathered as an argument.** The *Bequeathed Teaching Sūtra* carries the line "grounded in this precept, the concentrations arise," which readily tempts one to re-argue, at the close, the load-bearing mechanism of the precept as the ground of concentration. But that mechanism was settled in this volume's earlier papers; the sealing paper sounds it here only as a closing echo, points the direction toward the volume on *Samādhi*, and does not re-argue its load-bearing interface. See the following chapter.

## VI. Conclusion: Sealing the Volume, and the Opening Toward Concentration

This volume's thirty papers are sealed here.

To look back over the whole road: from the faculty-structure of the Pāli Vinaya, to the undertaking and observance of the three sectarian recensions; from the bodhisattva precept received as a gate, to the perfected-teaching precept-substance as the wholesome seed of the ālaya; from the perfuming of the precept's fragrance, to the sign-less keeping's "keep in full, grasp no sign"; and on to the worry, under modern diagnosis, of the root in mental karma absent—all that this road has established, the sealing paper gathers into one sentence: the doctrinal terminus of the precept is not in *understanding* but in *daily doing*. What the prior twenty-nine papers established is gathered into a daily art that can be re-enacted day by day, is taken as one's teacher, and at once keeps in full and grasps no sign. After the teacher's passing, when the Buddha no longer abides in the world, the precept itself stands in the teacher's place and serves as the practitioner's daily master—the precept as teacher. This is the native sense of the word *practice* in the *Six Practice Gates* as it lands on the gate of the precept: that the precept is a "practice gate" lies not in how much principle it expounds, but in that it asks one, in the end, to do it day by day, and can be done day by day.

The precept being the fourth gate, its place in the sequence of the six gates is thereby set: the precept receives from above the new precept-substance cleansed by the gate of repentance (the karmic obstructions purified, one can then receive the new precept in accordance with the dharma), and opens below toward the gate of concentration.[^19] This place in the order the sealing paper touches in a single phrase, without unfolding the remaining gates.

And the daily precept opens of itself toward concentration. The *Bequeathed Teaching Sūtra* says "grounded in this precept, the concentrations arise," and gathers the restraint of body and mind in a figure: "let this mind go and it loses a man's good works; restrain it to one point and nothing is left undone."[^20] The daily life of the precept day by day gathers the scattered body and mind toward one point; once this work of "restraining to one point" is mastered, the far side of the threshold is concentration. The load-bearing mechanism of the precept as the ground of concentration was established in this volume's earlier papers—the sealing paper only lays this interface-brick as the last course and points the direction toward the volume on *Samādhi*; as for how, on the far side of the threshold, one "restrains it to one point and leaves nothing undone," that is not for this volume to draw in advance.[^21]

Here the sealing closes. The doctrine of the precept is exhausted; what remains is to do it, day by day—and that is already the work of "restraining to one point," belonging to the next gate.
