# Sīla across the Ten Bhūmis
**Stage-Allocation, the Stainless Ground, and the Ten Courses of Good Action**

## Abstract

Is the precept, on the bodhisattva path, a threshold crossed once at the entrance and thereafter handed off to concentration and wisdom—or a vein that deepens at every ground and runs the whole length of the way? Taking the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*'s "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" as its warrant, this paper offers a third answer: the precept has a **stage-allocation** (分位). Each of the ten grounds takes one perfection as *presiding*; the perfection of *sīla* (*śīla-pāramitā*) presides at the second **Stainless Ground**, which is named precisely for "departing the defilement of broken precepts." Yet the sūtra appends to the close of every ground the same refrain—"the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure"—declaring that all the perfections are cultivated at every ground, and that the precept too pervades the other nine grounds according to capacity and measure: the precept is **predominant yet pervasive**, not *confined to one ground*. Deeper still, one and the same "highest-grade ten courses of good action"—a single precept-essence—yields, by the depth of the stage at which it is cultivated, four distinct fruits: the śrāvaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, the bodhisattva's vast course, and all the Buddha-dharmas. The precept's height lies not in the count of its rules but in the stage of its cultivation. Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyā* opens the pure precept of this ground, by way of "two purities," into "three precepts" ascending "higher… higher," and, with the alum-and-gold refining simile, glosses "presiding" as "advancing in purity," terminating in "the purity of the nature-precept"—so that the precept is graded layer by layer *within* the Stainless Ground, while at the same time being defined as *pervasively presiding* across the grounds. Carrying forward the preceding paper, *The Four Peaceful Practices of the Lotus*, in which the Lotus practice-precept was disclosed as the **horizontal posture of circumstance**, the present paper shows the Huayan Ten-Bhūmi precept to be the **vertical ladder of stage-allocation**: the two axes complete each other, and together display the whole of the round precept.

## I. The Problem: How Is the Precept Situated on the Vertical Axis?

The preceding paper, *The Four Peaceful Practices of the Lotus* (SĪLA P26), left at its close a thread pointing forward. The precept set out in the *Lotus*'s "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" is a *posture* held by the dharma-preacher of a hostile age as he meets his situation—it answers the *horizontal* question, "in what circumstance, and in what attitude, is the precept kept." How the precept is *situated* on the *vertical axis* of the bodhisattva path, how it ascends as realization deepens, the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" did not address head-on. The present paper begins here. Granted that the precept is one gate among the gateways of perfect penetration (P25), and that the precept within that gate is a practice-precept (P26)—what, then, is its place along the line that runs from shallow to deep across the bodhisattva path? Is it a threshold fixed at the starting point, or a vein that deepens ground by ground?

The question is no scholastic nicety; it touches a widespread misconception of practice. Many understand precept-keeping as the entrance "threshold": one receives the precepts, holds the rules, and is thereby "through"; from then on the work is to be moved over to concentration and wisdom, the precept receding into the background as something already completed and no longer needing care. On this understanding the precept is the *lowest rung* of the vertical axis, while concentration and wisdom are the ladder one climbs above it.

The picture given by the *Avataṃsaka*'s "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" overturns precisely this. There the precept is not a rung left behind but a living vein running the length of the ten grounds, *advancing in purity* at every ground; it *predominates* and presides at the second Stainless Ground, yet "according to capacity and measure" it runs through the other nine, and under Vasubandhu's commentary it is finely *graded by stage*—one and the same precept-essence reaching, by the depth of its cultivation, as low as the śrāvaka and as high as buddhahood. This is the paper's thesis: the *Avataṃsaka*'s "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" gives the precept a structure of **stage-allocation**—a being-set-in-place by station and a deepening ground by ground. "Stage-allocation" is the word the present paper uses to name this structure; below it will rigorously distinguish *what the scripture plainly states* from *what the paper names*, lest the reader take "stage-allocation" for the letter of the text or for an external frame imposed upon it.

A change of scripture, not a change of theme: passing from the *Lotus* to the *Avataṃsaka*, the question of the precept turns from "the horizontal posture of circumstance" into "the vertical ladder of stage-allocation." Below: first the scriptural basis (§II), then the core argument (§III), then a conversation with contemporary scholarship (§IV), and finally the middle-way corrections (§V) and the conclusion (§VI).

## II. The Scriptural Basis: The "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" and the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyā*

### 2.1 The Fixed Formula of the Ten Grounds: Presiding Does Not Mean Confinement

The "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis," recounting the conduct of the bodhisattva across the ten grounds, has one fixed turn of phrase running through the whole chapter: with each ascent to a ground, it says that this ground, among the ten perfections, takes some one perfection as "presiding" or "predominant," and at once appends the line, "the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure." This formula is itself the evidential backbone of the precept's stage-allocation. Set the first, Joyful Ground beside the second, Stainless Ground:[^1]

> The Joyful Ground: "Among the ten perfections, the perfection of giving presides; the remaining perfections are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure."

> The Stainless Ground: "Among the ten perfections, precept-keeping predominates; the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure."

Set side by side, the structure is plain: each ground has one presiding perfection, while "the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure" declares expressly that the remaining perfections are cultivated at that ground too, their measure merely set according to capacity. The precept (the perfection of *sīla*) "predominates" and presides at the second Stainless Ground—yet that very passage goes straight on to say "giving and precept-keeping are pure and fully accomplished": giving and precept-keeping stand here together, together accomplished. Plainly "precept-keeping predominates" is in no way "precept-keeping alone." The full context at the Stainless Ground reads:[^2]

> "Over measureless hundreds of thousands of millions of *nayutas* of *kalpas*, because this bodhisattva has departed far from the defilements of stinginess, envy, and broken precepts, his giving and his precept-keeping are pure and fully accomplished. As true gold, placed in alum and refined according to method, departs from every defilement and turns the brighter and purer… Sons of the Buddha! For this bodhisattva, among the four means of gathering, loving speech predominates; among the ten perfections, precept-keeping predominates; the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure."

"Precept-keeping predominates" and "the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure" appear in one and the same place: the precept, at the Stainless Ground, is "predominant" (presiding), while it is at the same time affirmed that the remaining perfections are universally cultivated. This is the textual ground of what the present paper calls "predominant yet pervasive."

### 2.2 The Stainless Ground Is Named by the Precept

The second ground is named "Stainless," and the "stain" it departs is precisely "the defilement of broken precepts." The passage cited above—"because it has departed far from the defilements of stinginess, envy, and broken precepts, giving and precept-keeping are pure and fully accomplished"—already marks the naming of this ground as bound up directly with the precept. Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyā*, glossing the ground's name, is more explicit still:[^3]

> "Because it departs from the defilements of the afflictions—such as the deluded mind that gives rise to the breaking of precepts—and the pure precept is fully accomplished, it is named the Stainless Ground."

One sees that the assignment of the second ground as the "precept-ground" is no accident of arrangement but a *naming by the precept*: the ground is called Stainless precisely because its distinguishing feature is the full accomplishment of the pure precept and the departure from the defilements of the afflictions, such as the deluded breaking of precepts. The precept here is not one item among many; it is the very core that defines this ground as this ground.

### 2.3 The Precept-Essence Is the Ten Courses of Good Action, and It Is a Nature-Precept

What, then, is the *body* of the precept at the Stainless Ground? The "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" takes the ten courses of good action as the precept-essence of the Stainless Ground, and prefixes each course with the word "by nature," marking it as a **nature-precept** and not a restraint-precept:[^4]

> "Sons of the Buddha! A bodhisattva abiding on the Stainless Ground, by his very nature departs from all killing of living beings: he keeps no knife or staff, harbours no enmity or hatred, possesses conscience and shame, is furnished with benevolence and forbearance, and toward every living being that has breath ever gives rise to a mind of benefit and loving thought… by his very nature does not steal… by his very nature does not engage in sexual misconduct…"

"By his very nature departs," "by his very nature does not steal," "by his very nature does not engage in sexual misconduct"—the word "nature" recurs at the head of each clause, meaning that this precept is not a matter of refraining only with effort under outward prohibition, but of departing of itself, out of one's very nature. Vasubandhu glosses in the same key, calling the precept of the Stainless Ground a *nature-precept already accomplished*, "for whatever is to be done is done naturally":[^5]

> "As for the ten upright minds: in dependence on the pure precept, the upright mind's nature-precept is accomplished, for whatever is to be done is done naturally [of itself]."

The phrase "done naturally" is decisive: the precept-keeping of the Stainless Ground has shifted from the first ground's **precept guarded by deliberation** (which must be guarded by the force of deliberation, and only so not transgressed) to the **nature-precept** (which, in whatever is to be done, does not transgress of itself). This shift, as §III will argue, is the very inward engine of the *ascent of stage-allocation*. (The archetype of the ten courses of good action at the Āgama, karmic-path stratum has its own treatment in SĪLA P06; the present paper takes only the *aspect* under which the *Avataṃsaka*'s "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" reads the ten courses as the nature-precept of the Stainless Ground, a context distinct from the Āgama stratum and not reopened here.)[^6]

### 2.4 The Highest-Grade Ten Courses of Good Action: One Precept-Essence, Four Stage-Allocations

The most penetrating passage on the precept in the "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" is its graded assessment of the "highest-grade ten courses of good action." The sūtra states that one and the same "highest-grade ten courses of good action," according to the depth of the *stage* at which it is *cultivated*, accomplishes four fruits of differing height. This is the key textual warrant for what the present paper means by "the precept's depth lies in the stage of cultivation, not in the count of its rules":[^7]

> "Sons of the Buddha! This bodhisattva-mahāsattva further reflects thus: 'The ten unwholesome courses of action are the causes for rebirth in the hells, among animals, and among hungry ghosts; the ten courses of good action are the causes for rebirth among humans, among gods, and on up to the Summit of Existence. Again, this highest-grade ten courses of good action, cultivated with wisdom—because the mind is narrow and base, because one dreads the three realms, because great compassion is wanting, and because understanding comes through hearing the voice of another—constitutes the śrāvaka vehicle. Again, this highest-grade ten courses of good action, cultivated and purified—because one awakens of oneself, not through another's teaching, because the skillful means of great compassion are not fully furnished, and because one comprehends the profound dharma of dependent arising—constitutes the pratyekabuddha vehicle. Again, this highest-grade ten courses of good action, cultivated and purified—because the mind is vast and measureless, because compassion is fully furnished, because it is embraced by skillful means, because it gives rise to the great vow, because it does not abandon beings, because it longs for the great wisdom of the buddhas, because it purifies all the bodhisattva grounds, and because it purely cultivates all the perfections—constitutes the bodhisattva's vast course. Again, this highest-grade ten courses of good action, because it is pure in every mode, and on up to the realization of the ten powers and the four fearlessnesses, brings all the Buddha-dharmas to accomplishment.'"

Note that the phrase "again, this highest-grade ten courses of good action" occurs four times: all four passages treat one and the same "highest-grade ten courses of good action"—the precept-essence does not change. What changes is the fivefold difference of *stage* in the cultivating of this one precept-essence: whether the scope of mind is narrow and base or vast and measureless; whether great compassion is wanting or fully furnished; whether awakening comes through hearing another or arises of oneself; whether there is a great vow or none; whether one longs for the great wisdom of the buddhas or not. The same precept-essence, by the depth of stage along these five axes, accomplishes severally the śrāvaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, the bodhisattva's vast course, and on up to the buddha-ground where "all the Buddha-dharmas are brought to accomplishment."

The insight here is vivid in the extreme: the height of the precept turns not on how many rules one has received and held, but on with what scope of mind, what compassion and vow, what wisdom one *cultivates* this one same precept-essence. One precept-essence runs through four stage-allocations and reaches up to the buddha-ground.

### 2.5 Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyā*: The Precept Graded Layer by Layer from Within

If the "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" raises the textual skeleton of stage-allocation with its fixed formula and its assessment of the ten courses, then Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyā* supplies, *within* "the precept of the Stainless Ground," the fine reasoned flesh upon that skeleton.[^8] Vasubandhu treats the pure precept of the Stainless Ground through a two-tiered framework. First, the "two purities" and the "three precepts" opened from them, set in an ascending "higher… higher" sequence:[^9]

> "This pure precept has two purities: first, the purity of arising; second, the purity of self-nature… As for the purity of self-nature, it has three precepts: first, the purity of the precept of desisting; second, the purity of the precept of gathering wholesome dharmas; third, the purity of the precept of benefiting beings. The purity of the precept of desisting means the ten courses of good action, from desisting from killing on up to right view, and is also called the purity of received precept. The purity of the precept of gathering wholesome dharmas is higher than the purity of the precept of desisting… The purity of the precept of benefiting beings is higher than the precept of gathering wholesome dharmas…"

The precept of desisting (the ten courses of good action—that is, ceasing from evil) → the precept of gathering wholesome dharmas (amassing good, "higher" than the precept of desisting) → the precept of benefiting beings (the benefit of sentient beings, "higher" than the precept of gathering wholesome dharmas). The word "higher" marks an ascending order of stage: the precept is here opened into a structure internally graded and capable of advance. (These three precepts are the early commentarial prototype of the later "three-fold pure precepts"; SĪLA P12 treats that framework in its own right, and it is not unfolded here.)[^10]

Second, Vasubandhu confronts directly a possible difficulty: if the first ground says giving presides and the second says the precept presides, does this mean that the remaining perfections at each ground are "inferior," or even uncultivated? Vasubandhu's answer is the very commentarial ground of the present paper's claim that "presiding does not mean exclusive":[^11]

> "If that be so, why does the first ground say that the perfection of giving presides, the rest not its equal? It is because those very perfections—giving and the rest—advance to a higher purity at this ground. Because it departs from the defilements of stinginess, envy, broken precepts, and the like, this ground is accordingly named 'Stainless.' At the first ground the gold is refined by fire alone, removing the coarse defilements of outward greed and the like—hence one speaks of the purity of refined gold. Now at this ground it is further boiled in alum, removing the defilement [that clouds] its own luminous substance, so that its own-nature is truly pure—hence one speaks of the sense of the purity of the nature-precept."

With the simile of refining gold in alum, Vasubandhu glosses "presiding" as "advancing in purity": the first ground is like refining gold by fire, removing "the coarse defilements of outward greed and the like"; the second ground further boils it in alum, removing "the defilement of its own luminous substance" until, at "the truly pure own-nature," one speaks of "the purity of the nature-precept." The perfections pervade every ground; there is no ground at which they are uncultivated. The precept, at the Stainless Ground, "advances in purity" and so shows itself as presiding, and for that the ground is named "Stainless." In other words, "presiding" is "becoming the more pure," not "monopolizing" or "the other perfections going uncultivated."

With this, Vasubandhu has given us two faces. Outwardly, the precept is "pervasively presiding" (the perfections pervade every ground; the precept advances at the second). Inwardly, the precept is "graded layer by layer" (the two purities open the three precepts, ascending "higher… higher," on up to the natural conduct of the nature-precept). These two faces are exactly the outer and inner of one and the same structure of stage-allocation. (The *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyā*'s extensive exposition of the first, Joyful Ground—the doctrine of the six characteristics, great compassion as the head, the *ālaya* consciousness, and so on—has already been developed at length in DĀNA P24, *Giving on the Joyful Ground*; the present paper reads only the one passage on "the precept of the Stainless Ground," a different fascicle and a different ground, and cites the Joyful-Ground matters without unfolding any of them.[^12] Likewise, Nāgārjuna's relevant discussions of the ten grounds were treated in their own right in SĪLA P13, and are not reopened here.[^13])

## III. The Core Argument: The Precept's Stage-Allocation Is "Predominant yet Pervasive"

### 3.1 "Stage-Allocation" Is the Present Paper's Naming, Not the Letter of the Scripture

Before entering the argument, one strict distinction must be drawn—and it is the point at which this paper is most liable to be misread.

**What the scripture plainly states** is twofold. First, the fixed formula of the ten grounds—"such-and-such perfection presides / predominates; the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure"—which declares expressly that each ground has one presiding perfection and that the rest are universally cultivated (§2.1). Second, "again, this highest-grade ten courses of good action… constitutes the śrāvaka vehicle / the pratyekabuddha vehicle / the bodhisattva's vast course… brings all the Buddha-dharmas to accomplishment," which declares expressly that one and the same precept-essence accomplishes four fruits according to the depth of its cultivation (§2.4). These are stated in the scripture in so many words.

**What the present paper names**, by contrast, is this: it uses the word "stage-allocation" to name the "predominant-yet-pervasive" structure above—that whole configuration in which the precept both presides at the Stainless Ground and, according to capacity and measure, runs through all ten grounds, and in which one and the same precept-essence has a vertical depth of four fruits according to the depth of its cultivation. To call this configuration the precept's "stage-allocation" is the present paper's interpretive act—a naming of a reading—for which the paper must answer in its own person, and which may not be passed off as the letter of the scripture.

But a clause must be added at once: the paper's naming is a *reframing*, not a *fabrication*. The sense of "stage-allocation" is no alien frame laid over the scripture from outside; it is *internal* to the scripture's own fixed formula ("the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure" is itself the sense of "pervasive") and to Vasubandhu's gloss "advancing in purity" ("advancing" is itself the sense of "deepening ground by ground"). What the paper does is only to fix a clear name upon a structure already latent within the scripture and the commentary, so that it may be recognized and discussed.

### 3.2 Presiding Does Not Mean Exclusive

This understood, the first criterion to hold fast is that "presiding" must never be mistaken for "exclusive."

The Stainless Ground says "precept-keeping predominates," but the same sentence sets beside it "giving and precept-keeping are pure and fully accomplished," and goes straight on to "the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure." Vasubandhu, further, states expressly that the perfections pervade every ground and that the precept at this ground merely "advances in purity" (§2.5). On this footing, every reading that says "the precept exists only at the second ground," "the precept belongs exclusively to the Stainless Ground," or "one only begins to keep precepts at the second ground, and need not keep them beyond it" is a misreading directly contrary to the paper's claim. The precept's "predominance" at the Stainless Ground is "a special purity and a special exertion at this ground" (presiding), not "an absence of the precept at the other grounds" (exclusivity). Presiding is "prominence in stage"; pervasiveness is "constancy through the whole vertical run"—the two run together without contradiction.

### 3.3 One Precept-Essence, Four Stage-Allocations: Depth Is Not the Count of Rules

The second criterion comes from the assessment of the "highest-grade ten courses of good action" in §2.4. What most repays reflection in that passage is that it decouples, utterly, "the height of the precept" from "the number of its rules."

What the four fruit-grounds cultivate is one and the same "highest-grade ten courses of good action"—the precept-essence has neither gained a rule nor lost one. The ten courses of good action held by the śrāvaka and the ten courses of good action accomplished at the buddha-ground are the same ten courses of good action. What differs is only the depth of *stage*—in scope of mind, in compassion and vow, in awakening, in wisdom—at which this precept-essence is *cultivated*. The śrāvaka cultivates it with "a narrow and base mind, a dread of the three realms, a want of great compassion, an understanding by hearing another," and so halts at the śrāvaka vehicle; the bodhisattva cultivates it with "a vast and measureless mind, compassion fully furnished, the arising of the great vow, the longing for the great wisdom of the buddhas, the pure cultivation of all the perfections," and so accomplishes the vast course; and at "purity in every mode," "all the Buddha-dharmas are brought to accomplishment."

Hence: the depth of the precept is the vertical depth of one and the same precept-essence in the *stage* of its cultivation, not the length or shortness of a checklist of rules. This is the most exact reference of the word "stage-allocation"—not "the more precepts kept, the higher," but "keeping the same precept, the deeper the cultivation, the higher the station."

### 3.4 The Inner and the Outer Faces: The Precept Is Graded, and It Is Defined as Pervasively Presiding

The third level gathers Vasubandhu's two-tiered framework (§2.5) into the inner and outer faces of the structure of stage-allocation.

**Inwardly**, the precept is graded layer by layer. Vasubandhu's "two purities" opening into "three precepts," in their "higher… higher" ascent, are clear proof that the precept is *already* graded *within* the Stainless Ground: above ceasing-from-evil (the precept of desisting) stands amassing-good (the precept of gathering wholesome dharmas), and above amassing-good stands benefiting-beings (the precept of benefiting beings). Add to this the surpassing of "the precept guarded by deliberation" by the "nature-precept" (natural conduct): with the same precept-keeping, the first ground must guard by deliberation, while the second departs of its very nature and, in whatever is to be done, conducts itself naturally (§2.3). The ascent of stage-allocation just *is* the precept-keeping becoming ever more natural, ever more effortless. This is the dynamics of stage-allocation: not a switching to some other set of precepts to keep, but the same precept kept ever more purely, ever less by strain.

**Outwardly**, the precept is defined as pervasively presiding. The gloss "advancing in purity" (§2.5) shows that the perfections run through every ground, that the precept is not the exclusive possession of the second ground but only "advances to a higher purity" there, and so shows itself as presiding.

The inner "graded layer by layer" and the outer "pervasively presiding" are the two faces of one structure: the precept is both especially prominent at one stage of the vertical axis (the Stainless Ground) and constantly present, according to capacity and measure, along the whole vertical run, while at every stage it may itself be cultivated one layer deeper. This whole is what we call the precept's "stage-allocation."

### 3.5 The Two Axes Complete Each Other: The Horizontal Posture of Circumstance, the Vertical Ladder of Stage-Allocation

Finally, to answer the thread taken up at the paper's opening. What the preceding paper, *The Four Peaceful Practices of the Lotus*, disclosed was the "posture of circumstance" with which the dharma-preacher of a hostile age keeps the precept in the *Lotus*'s "Chapter on Peaceful Practices"—and that is *horizontal*: with what posture of body, speech, mind, and vow one keeps the precept under a given circumstance. What the present paper discloses, the precept of the Huayan ten grounds, is *vertical*: the precept is set in place at a particular stage of the bodhisattva path (presiding at the Stainless Ground) and advances and deepens, ground by ground, in its purity. The horizontal posture and the vertical ladder are not mutually exclusive but two complementary dimensions: one and the same matter of precept-keeping has a horizontal face ("how it is kept in this circumstance") and a vertical face ("to what depth it is kept at this stage"). That the round precept is "round" is precisely that it embraces both axes.

By way of an aside: among Samantabhadra's vows, the offerings include "the offering of practising as taught"; precept-keeping is just "to conduct oneself according to the precept as taught," so precept-keeping is itself a measure of dharma-offering—and this serves nicely as an interface between the "practice-precept" (how it is conducted) and the "stage-allocated precept" (to what stage it is conducted).[^14] (The full breadth of Samantabhadra's dharma-offering belongs to the sister papers; the present paper borrows only these few words as a joint, and does not unfold it. Likewise the passage on giving at the Stainless Ground belongs to DĀNA P24, and is here touched in a single clause.)

## IV. A Conversation with Contemporary Scholarship

"Stage-allocation," as a way of reading, is not without neighbours in contemporary scholarship—yet no one has applied it to *the precept* as its object. Three scholars are taken below as interlocutors, the better to mark the gap the present paper fills.[^15]

**First, Dorji Wangchuk on *bodhicitta*.** In his monograph on the concept of *bodhicitta* in Indo-Tibetan Buddhism, Wangchuk reads the core resolve of the bodhisattva path (*bodhicitta*) as a structure "innately possessed, yet maturing by stages": one and the same equipment of *bodhicitta* deepens progressively according to the station of realization, and is not completed at a single acquisition.[^16] This logic of "abiding yet stage-allocated" is, in structure, highly isomorphic with the present paper's claim of "one precept-essence, vertically deepened by stage"—and this is what may be taken from it. Yet Wangchuk applies the logic to *the will* (*bodhicitta*, the resolve), and never to *the precept*; he does not read the precept of the Stainless Ground, does not treat the stage-allocation of the nature-precept against the precept guarded by deliberation, and does not reach the pervasiveness of "according to capacity and measure." What the present paper does is precisely to apply the same logic of "abiding yet stage-allocated" to the *precept-essence* itself.

**Second, Liu Ming-Wood (劉明) on Huayan's positional interpretation.** Studying Huayan doctrinal classification and the doctrine of the three natures, Liu shows that Huayan consistently places its doctrines by "their location within a positional framework"—the weight of a given teaching depends on the place it occupies within the classification of teachings and the scheme of stations.[^17] This shows that a "stage-allocation" style of reading is Huayan's *native* mode of thought, no alien frame imposed from outside—and this confirms the present paper's self-limitation in §3.1, "a reframing, not a fabrication." Yet Liu's analysis of "positional placement" is applied to *doctrines and natures* (the three natures, the classification of teachings), not to *the precept*; he does not read Vasubandhu's precept-framework at the Stainless Ground, nor reach the four-fruit assessment of the highest-grade ten courses of good action. The present paper applies the very Huayan "positional grammar" Liu discloses to the *vertical situating of the precept-essence*.

**Third, Abraham Vélez de Cea on the criteria of goodness.** Studying the criteria of good action in the Pāli Nikāyas, Vélez de Cea argues that one and the same good act may be evaluated along several dimensions, its ethical weight varying with the depth of cultivation and the intention, and not determined by the act in itself alone.[^18] This answers, from afar, to the present paper's insight that "one and the same highest-grade ten courses of good action accomplishes four fruits according to the stage of its cultivation"—and this too is what may be taken from it. Yet Vélez de Cea works in the Nikāyas—that is, in a *non-staged* register—evaluating the height of good action by intention; he has no doctrine of "grounds" or stations, does not say that the precept is placed at one particular ground (the Stainless Ground), and still less reaches Vasubandhu's dynamics of stage between "the nature-precept" and "the precept guarded by deliberation."

**The gap the conversation reveals.** The three stage-allocate one thing each: Wangchuk the *will*, the Huayan scholar (Liu) *doctrine*, the ethicist (Vélez de Cea) *intention*—yet not one reads *the precept itself* as stage-allocated. A genuine "stage-allocation of the precept" must have three things at once: the precept pervading the ten grounds "according to capacity and measure"; the precept-essence graded into four fruits according to the depth of cultivation of the ten courses of good action; and this whole "pervasive-yet-graded" structure driven by Vasubandhu's nature-precept and two-purities framework. This is the gap the present paper fills: supplementing P26's "horizontal posture of circumstance" with a "vertical stage-allocation," so that together they complete the two axes of the round precept.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

Following this study's custom, this section marks several misreadings to be guarded against (❌) and interpretive boundaries to be kept (⚠️).

> ❌ **Misreading one: the precept exists only at the second ground; once acquired, it is done.** This is a reading directly contrary to the paper's claim. Its error is to read "precept-keeping predominates at the Stainless Ground" as "the precept belongs exclusively to the second ground," and so to infer "once past the second ground the precept is completed and may be handed off to concentration and wisdom." The correction: "the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure" declares expressly that the precept pervades the ten grounds; Vasubandhu's "the perfections advance to a higher purity at this ground" declares expressly that the precept is present at every ground and merely advances at the second. The precept is no threshold on the vertical axis to be crossed once and finished with, but a constant vein that deepens ground by ground.

> ❌ **Misreading two: stage-allocation means the adding or subtracting of rules; the more precepts kept, the higher.** Its error is to equate the depth of the precept with the length of a checklist of rules. The correction: in the four fruits of the "highest-grade ten courses of good action" (§2.4), what is cultivated is one and the same ten courses of good action; the precept-essence is neither added to nor diminished, and the height lies solely in the depth of *stage*—in scope of mind, compassion and vow, and wisdom—at which it is cultivated. The vertical depth of the precept is in the *cultivation*, not in the *items*.

> ⚠️ **Boundary one: "stage-allocation" is the present paper's naming, not the letter of the scripture, nor an external frame.** As distinguished in §3.1: what the scripture plainly states is "presiding / according to capacity and measure" and "one good-action course, four fruits"; to name this "predominant-yet-pervasive" structure "stage-allocation" is the paper's interpretive act, for which the paper must answer. At the same time it must be affirmed that this is a *reframing*, not a *fabrication*—the sense of stage-allocation is internal to the fixed formula and to Vasubandhu's gloss "advancing in purity." The reader should keep a clear sense of the distinction between "faithful to the scripture" and "the paper's naming."

> ⚠️ **Boundary two: "the nature-precept, conducted naturally" is not "there is no precept to keep" or "one need not keep precepts."** That the precept of the Stainless Ground shifts from "the precept guarded by deliberation" to "the nature-precept, conducting itself naturally in whatever is to be done" is easily misread as "since it is now natural, there is no precept-mark to keep, no precept to keep." The correction: "conducted naturally" is precept-keeping *matured to effortlessness*—the precept kept ever less by strain—and is in no way "the abolition of the precept" or "transgression of it." The ascent of stage-allocation makes precept-keeping ever more natural; it does not make the precept dispensable. This line is to be kept especially tight at the level of the round precept.

## VI. Conclusion

**In essence.** The *Avataṃsaka*'s "Chapter on the Ten Bhūmis" gives the precept a structure of stage-allocation: the precept (the perfection of *sīla*) presides at the second Stainless Ground, which is named for "departing the defilement of broken precepts" and takes the ten courses of good action as the body of its nature-precept—yet "the rest are not unpractised, but according to capacity and measure," so that the precept runs through all ten grounds. The precept is "predominant yet pervasive," not "confined to one ground." Deeper still, one and the same "highest-grade ten courses of good action," according to the depth of the stage at which it is cultivated, divides into the four fruits of the śrāvaka, the pratyekabuddha, the bodhisattva's vast course, and all the Buddha-dharmas: the precept's depth lies not in the count of its rules but in the stage of its cultivation. Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyā*, with its "two purities" opening into the "higher… higher" ascent of "three precepts" and its alum-and-gold simile glossing "presiding" as "advancing in purity" and terminating in "the purity of the nature-precept," establishes both the inner (graded layer by layer) and the outer (pervasively presiding) faces of this structure of stage-allocation. Carrying forward the preceding paper's disclosure of the Lotus practice-precept as "the horizontal posture of circumstance," the present paper shows the Huayan Ten-Bhūmi precept to be "the vertical ladder of stage-allocation."

**Guidance for practice.** For practice, the gist is to undo a common misplacement: not to treat precept-keeping as a threshold crossed once at the entrance and thereafter shelved. The precept is a vein running the length of the vertical axis and deepening ground by ground—one and the same precept-essence may pass from being guarded by deliberation to departing of its very nature, conducting itself naturally in whatever is to be done; from ceasing-from-evil to amassing-good to benefiting-beings. At every stage the practitioner keeps one and the same precept, and what differs is only the scope of mind, the compassion and vow, the wisdom with which it is cultivated. Precept-keeping is itself a cultivation at a stage, and is itself a path reaching up to the buddha-ground.

**Open questions and the thread forward.** This Part (Part VIII) rounds the precept through three scriptures and treatises: the *Śūraṅgama* takes the precept as the ground of concentration (P24), the *Lotus* takes the precept as practice (P26), and the *Avataṃsaka* takes the precept as stage-allocation (the present paper). Ground, practice, and stage-allocation issue severally from the three scriptures; they are not three parallel theses but seem to have a higher governing structure that would gather "what the precept is (the ground of concentration)," "how the precept is practised (the practice-precept)," and "how the precept is situated on the vertical axis (stage-allocation)" into a single round precept. This governing synthesis of the round precept through the three scriptures is left to be addressed head-on at the close of this Part; the present paper does no more than point in this direction, and does not unfold it in advance.
