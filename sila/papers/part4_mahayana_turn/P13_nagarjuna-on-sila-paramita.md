## Abstract

This paper takes the *śīla-pāramitā* chapter of Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509, translated by Kumārajīva during the Later Qin Hongshi reign, 401–406), spanning fascicles 13 and 14, as its primary corpus and argues that the chapter exhibits a dual-anchor architecture. The first anchor is the single sūtra-line that opens fascicle 13 — "*Because transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, one should perfect the* śīla-pāramitā" — which installs the *anupalabdhi* (unobtainability) layer of *prajñāpāramitā* as the precept's prior orientation. The second anchor is the narrative concretization of fascicle 14: the poison-dragon's voluntary submission unto death by ant-bite (a *jātaka* freshly narrated in fascicle 14) and King Sutasoma's refusal to abandon his vow even at the cost of body and life (a back-reference to the complete narrative housed in fascicle 4). The two anchors are not joined by external commentary; T1509 itself textually self-glosses the dual-anchor architecture, first at fascicle 14 line 158 (a bridge passage that recursively cites fascicle 13's opening sūtra-line and pairs it with the bodhicitta-motivated reframing through a parallel "復次" construction) and again at fascicle 14 line 219 (a chapter-closing recursive citation of the same line in declarative form). Placed within the 5th–6th-century co-emergence of the *pāramitā*-framing across the Pāli and Northern lineages — both of which thematize *śīla* as a *pāramī(tā)* only in their post-canonical strata — Nāgārjuna's unique structural contribution is identified not as the *pāramitā*-framing itself but as the *addition of the anupalabdhi layer* to it, by which "unobtainable" (*paramārtha*) and "unbreakable" (*saṃvṛti*) stand as two truths simultaneously rather than as stages of a linear teleology. The *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* (T1521, attributed to Nāgārjuna and translated by Kumārajīva), in its five-chapter *śīla* sequence in fascicles 16–17, supplies a sister-corpus confirmation that the *anupalabdhi* layer is a distributed feature of the Nāgārjuna-attributed corpus rather than a localized peculiarity of T1509. A three-way dialogue with Étienne Lamotte, Paul Williams, and Damien Keown situates the paper's contribution within the sparse English-language scholarship on T1509.

**Keywords:** *śīla-pāramitā*; *anupalabdhi*; dual-anchor architecture; self-gloss bridge; two-truth simultaneity; poison-dragon *jātaka*; King Sutasoma; *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*; *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*; Nāgārjuna; Kumārajīva; *pāramitā*-framing co-emergence; Mahāyāna ethics

## 摘要

本文以龍樹《大智度論》（鳩摩羅什 401–406 譯，T1509）卷十三、十四之尸羅波羅蜜章為主場，論證此章為一雙錨架構：第一錨為卷十三章首之單句經文「罪、不罪不可得故，應具足尸羅波羅蜜」，以般若不可得層消解戒之相執；第二錨為卷十四之兩個本生故事——毒龍持戒墮蟻死（卷十四新出）與須陀須摩王不惜身命以全禁戒（卷十四回指卷四完整敘事）。此雙錨之內在連結並非後人疏釋——卷十四第一五八行之自我釋義橋接句與第二一九行之章末遞迴引用，於 T1509 文本自身已完成兩錨之並立。將此論述放回公元五、六世紀巴利與北傳兩傳統共同興起之波羅蜜框架中對讀，龍樹之獨有結構性貢獻並非波羅蜜框架本身，而在於將「不可得」一層加入戒之論述，使「不可得」與「不可破」二諦並立，成為戒之波羅蜜化標誌。本文以《十住毘婆沙論》（T1521）卷十六、十七之姊妹語料補正此判斷，並與當代三位學者——拉莫特、威廉斯、基翁——之相關研究進行對話。

## I. Introduction: From the Three-Cluster Architecture to *Śīla-Pāramitā*

The previous paper in this volume (P12) closed by reconstructing the three-cluster architecture of bodhisattva *śīla* — *saṃvara-śīla*, *kuśala-dharma-saṃgrāhaka-śīla*, and *sattvārtha-kriyā-śīla* — as the three-dimensional functional design articulated by Asaṅga in the *Bodhisattvabhūmi* and later mapped by Zhiyi (538–597) onto the three bodies of Buddhahood (*trikāya*).[^1] That paper supplied the structural map of *what* the bodhisattva precepts are. It did not address a further question: *how do the bodhisattva precepts become a* pāramitā *— a completed perfection of the bodhisattva path?* The classical answer is supplied by Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509; hereafter the *Śāstra*), translated by Kumārajīva in the Later Qin (Hongshi 3–8, 401–406), whose fascicles 13 and 14 are devoted to the *śīla-pāramitā* (尸羅波羅蜜) — the perfection of moral conduct. The thesis of this paper proceeds in two layers. The first layer is structural: T1509's *śīla-pāramitā* chapter exhibits a *dual-anchor architecture*. The first anchor is the single sūtra-line that opens fascicle 13: 「罪、不罪不可得故，應具足尸羅波羅蜜」 — "because transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, one should perfect the *śīla-pāramitā*."[^2] This line installs the *anupalabdhi* (不可得, "unobtainability") layer of *prajñāpāramitā* as the precept's prior orientation: unobtainability *first*, perfection *after*. The second anchor is the narrative concretization of fascicle 14, where two *jātaka*s — the poison-dragon's voluntary submission to ant-bite (fascicle 14 lines 24–45, freshly narrated) and King Sutasoma's refusal to break his vow even unto death (fascicle 14 lines 21–24, back-referencing the complete narrative housed in fascicle 4) — demonstrate that *anupalabdhi* does *not* amount to *non-keeping*: precisely *because* transgression is unobtainable, the precept becomes *unbreakable* at the threshold of bodily survival. The second layer of the thesis concerns the *internal connection* of the two anchors. That connection is not an external commentarial overlay. T1509 itself textually self-glosses the architecture. At fascicle 14 line 158, a bridge passage reads: 「復次，若菩薩於罪、不罪不可得故，是時，名為尸羅波羅蜜」 — "Furthermore, when the bodhisattva [knows] that transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, that itself is named *śīla-pāramitā*." The line recursively cites fascicle 13's opening sūtra-line and pairs it, by a parallel 「復次」 ("furthermore") construction, with the bodhicitta-motivated reframing of the precepts. At fascicle 14 line 219, the chapter closes with a final recursive citation: 「以是故言『於罪、不罪不可得故，應具足尸羅波羅蜜』」 — "Therefore it is said: 'Because transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, one should perfect the *śīla-pāramitā*.'" The chapter is thereby sealed within a three-layer recursive frame — opening (fascicle 13), midpoint (fascicle 14 line 158), close (fascicle 14 line 219) — that is the *Śāstra*'s own internal marker of its architectural design. The dual-anchor reading therefore does not require a "the present paper proposes" qualifier; it is T1509's own textual self-gloss, and this paper's subtitle (*The Dual-Anchor Architecture*) is named for the *Śāstra*'s self-glossing, not for an interpretive overlay imposed from outside the text.[^3] A second, historically stronger claim is built on the first. Within the 5th–6th-century co-emergence of the *pāramitā*-framing of *śīla* across the Pāli and Northern lineages — both of which thematize *śīla* as a *pāramī*(*tā*) only in their post-canonical strata — Nāgārjuna's unique structural contribution is *not the* pāramitā-*framing itself* (a shared post-canonical thematization across both lineages) but the *addition of the anupalabdhi layer* to it: making the unobtainable (*paramārtha*) and the unbreakable (*saṃvṛti*) stand as two truths simultaneously rather than as stages of a linear teleology. The paper proceeds in six chapters. Chapter II reads the chapter-opening *anupalabdhi* axis of fascicle 13. Chapter III reads the narrative concretization of fascicle 14, with philological attention to the Sutasoma back-reference and the *jātaka*-sources. Chapter IV examines the *Śāstra*'s self-gloss bridge at lines 158 and 219, and the *anupalabdhi*-dialectic at lines 164–169 that runs between them. Chapter V places Nāgārjuna's architecture within the 5th–6th-century co-emergence of the *pāramitā*-framing, supplements the analysis with the *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* (T1521) sister-corpus evidence, and stages a three-way dialogue with Étienne Lamotte, Paul Williams, and Damien Keown. Chapter VI concludes by identifying the *anupalabdhi* layer as Nāgārjuna's unique structural contribution and points forward to the *Bodhisattvabhūmi*'s nine-fold *śīla* taxonomy, the subject of the next paper in this volume.

## II. The Chapter-Opening *Anupalabdhi* Axis (*Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* Fascicle 13)

### 2.1 The Single Sūtra-Line and the Priority of *Anupalabdhi*

Fascicle 13 of the *Śāstra* opens not with a definition, not with a typology, and not with a survey of the precepts' merits, but with a single sūtra-line that the commentary will then expound:

> 【經】「罪、不罪不可得故，應具足尸羅波羅蜜。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "Because transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, one should perfect the *śīla-pāramitā*.")*[^2]

The line's syntax is minimal but its sequence is significant. A causal joint links two clauses: "because transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable" is the antecedent, "one should perfect the *śīla-pāramitā*" the consequent. The peculiarity is that the antecedent is not an affirmative proposition but a *double negation*: transgression (罪 *zuì*) is unobtainable, and non-transgression (不罪 *bù-zuì*) is equally unobtainable. What is *anupalabdhi*? The Sanskrit term, glossed in the *Pañcaviṃśati-sāhasrikā-prajñāpāramitā* and elsewhere by the cognate phrase *anupalambha-yoga* ("the practice of non-apprehension," 無所得行), does not deny the conventional reality of transgression or non-transgression. Killing remains a conventionally evil act; precept-keeping remains a conventionally meritorious one. What *anupalabdhi* denies is that transgression and non-transgression can be apprehended as *self-existent natures* (*svabhāva*, 自性) — independently subsisting, ostensively identifiable, graspable as such. Under the gaze of *prajñā*, neither is "obtainable" in this sense. From the standpoint of ultimate truth (*paramārtha*, 勝義), both are unobtainable. The structural fact to register is that *anupalabdhi* is not added *to* the precepts as a later prajñā-flavored interpretation. The sūtra itself sets *anupalabdhi* as the precepts' *prior* orientation. Kumārajīva's translation-bureau, at the chapter's opening, does not first set out precept-categories, precept-essences, or precept-merits and then qualify them by *anupalabdhi* as a later overlay. The single sūtra-line is permitted to stand first — a deliberate authorial sequence that registers an internal verdict of the *prajñāpāramitā* literature: perfection (*pāramitā*) of the precepts, without passing through the filter of *anupalabdhi*, remains the accumulation of conventional merit and has not yet entered the territory of perfection proper.[^4]

### 2.2 「秦言性善」 — A Translation-Bureau Gloss, Not Nāgārjuna's Metaphysics

Immediately after the sūtra-line, the *Śāstra* offers a brief lexical gloss:

> 「尸羅（秦言性善），好行善道，不自放逸，是名尸羅。或受戒行善，或不受戒行善，皆名尸羅。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "*Śīla* — [in the Qin tongue, 'natural goodness'] — is the disposition to delight in good conduct and to refrain from heedlessness; this is named *śīla*. Whether one cultivates the good through receiving the precepts or without receiving them, all is named *śīla*.")*[^5]

The parenthetical phrase 「秦言性善」 ("in the Qin tongue, 'natural goodness'") deserves philological care. The bracketed gloss is *not* part of Nāgārjuna's Sanskrit; it is a translator's note inserted by the Kumārajīva translation-bureau in the Later Qin. The *Śāstra* contains numerous formulae of the structure "秦言 X" — for example, the same fascicle 13 elsewhere glosses the term *uposatha* (布薩) as 「秦言共住」 ("in the Qin tongue, 'cohabitation'"), with identical syntax.[^6] The "Qin tongue" (秦言) refers to the Later Qin (姚秦) dynasty during which Kumārajīva worked; the formula is a translation-bureau convention for marking a transliterated Sanskrit term and supplying a Chinese semantic gloss. The philological consequence is consequential. When a modern reader meets the phrase 「尸羅，秦言性善」, the reader is not encountering Nāgārjuna's metaphysical definition of *śīla* but the Later Qin translation-bureau's *semantic choice* — Kumārajīva and his collaborators (Sengrui 僧叡, Sengzhao 僧肇, and others) selecting *xìng-shàn* 性善 ("natural goodness") as a Chinese rendering for the Sanskrit *śīla*. This distinction matters substantively. The term *xìng* 性 in Chinese carries a strong essentialist resonance — it can be read as *nature* in an own-being sense, suggesting that *śīla* names a *natural essence of goodness* in the bodhisattva. But Nāgārjuna's overall position, articulated across the *Mūlamadhyamaka-kārikā* and the *Śāstra* itself, is that all *dharmas* lack *svabhāva* (自性). If *xìng-shàn* were read as *natural essence of goodness*, it would directly contradict Nāgārjuna's foundational doctrinal stance. The stable reading is therefore to treat *xìng-shàn* as a *translation-bureau idiom* whose intended sense is closer to *habituated goodness* or *disposition to good conduct* — the very content that the *Śāstra*'s next line glosses as 「好行善道，不自放逸」 ("delight in good conduct and refrain from heedlessness") — rather than as a metaphysical claim about an essence of goodness. Two things should thus be read at once. Nāgārjuna's Sanskrit text contains no claim about an *xìng* of *śīla*. Yet the Chinese-language reception of the term *xìng-shàn* has a history of its own, in which the metaphysical resonance is not always dissolved. A modern reading of the *Śāstra* is well-served by distinguishing these layers: Nāgārjuna's Sanskrit *śīla* is unencumbered by such claims; the Chinese *xìng-shàn* is a Later Qin translation-bureau idiom whose subsequent semantic life takes on additional weight in the East Asian reception. A note on translator-register is in order. The body of the present paper follows Kumārajīva's diction throughout, writing *śīla-pāramitā* in Chinese as 「尸羅波羅蜜」 (no 多 suffix) when citing T1509 and T1521. Where Xuanzang's later seventh-century *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* translation (T0220, Tang Xianqing 5 to Longshuo 3, 660–663) supplies the parallel term in the alternative register 「淨戒波羅蜜多」 *jìngjiè-pāramitā(-duō)* — a system-wide replacement across that translation's 599 fascicles — the two are not interchangeable within a single passage. The present paper preserves Kumārajīva's register as the operative body-text vocabulary and notes Xuanzang's register only as a translator-register variation at the footnote level.[^6a]

### 2.3 The Three-Precept Classification: Path-Stage Axis Rather Than Functional Object

After the *anupalabdhi* opening and the *xìng-shàn* gloss, the *Śāstra* proceeds to a three-fold precept classification. The taxonomy is introduced through a brief question-and-answer:

> 「問曰：是不殺戒何界繫？答曰：⋯⋯以實言之，應有三種：或欲界繫，或色界繫，或無漏。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "Question: To which realm does the precept of non-killing belong? Answer: ... In truth, there are three: bound to the *kāma-dhātu* [the realm of desire], bound to the *rūpa-dhātu* [the realm of form], or undefiled.")*[^7]

The three precepts named here — *saṃvara-śīla* (律儀戒, the precepts of restraint received by ordinary beings in the realm of desire), *dhyāna*-generated *śīla* (定共戒, precepts arising in tandem with the *rūpa-dhātu*'s meditative absorptions), and undefiled *śīla* (無漏戒, precepts correlated with undefiled *jñāna*) — share two structural features. First, the discriminating criterion is *the stage of the path*. The progression is vertical: from the ordinary precepts of the desire-realm, through the *dhyāna*-generated precepts of meditative absorption, to the undefiled precepts of liberative wisdom. The same fascicle elsewhere makes the ladder explicit: the five precepts → the novice precepts → the full *saṃvara* → the *dhyāna* precept → the undefiled precept. Second, the criterion is *not* functional object. The classification does not say "this precept restrains harm, that one cultivates merit, the third one benefits sentient beings." Whatever each precept "does" with respect to the field of conduct is not what differentiates them; what differentiates them is the *stratum of mind* on which each precept rests. A philological observation here secures cross-paper continuity with P12. T1509's three-precept classification (by path-stage axis) and the T1581 *Pusa dichi jing* three-cluster classification (the *saṃvara* / *kuśala-dharma-saṃgrāhaka* / *sattvārtha-kriyā* taxonomy by functional-object axis) share the same Chinese phrase "three-fold precepts" (三戒) but are organized along *incommensurable axes*. The fact that both treatises are read as canonical Mahāyāna *śīla*-sources does not warrant treating them as redundant statements of one taxonomy; they are two distinct architectures that happen to count to three. The cross-paper analysis of P12 is the proper locus for the three-cluster framework; the present paper limits itself to recording the path-stage architecture of T1509 and its non-interchangeability with the three-cluster scheme.[^8] The overall shape of fascicle 13's opening is now visible. *Anupalabdhi* first; *should perfect* second; *xìng-shàn* gloss third; *three precepts by path-stage* fourth. The order is not accidental. The chapter installs the prajñā-prior orientation before any taxonomic apparatus is mounted. Whatever taxonomic and merit-related content fascicle 13 will subsequently supply is supplied *within* the *anupalabdhi* frame, not before it. The *unobtainable* and the *unbreakable* — both demanded by the chapter-opening sūtra-line — receive their narrative concretization in fascicle 14, to which the analysis now turns.

## III. Narrative Concretization (*Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* Fascicle 14): The Poison-Dragon and the Sutasoma Back-Reference

### 3.1 The Poison-Dragon Narrative (Fascicle 14, lines 24–45)

Fascicle 14's *śīla-pāramitā* chapter centers a single freshly narrated *jātaka*:

> 「如菩薩本身，曾作大力毒龍。⋯⋯是龍受一日戒，出家求靜，入林樹間思惟；坐久，疲懈而睡。⋯⋯獵者見之驚喜，⋯⋯以杖按其頭，以刀剝其皮。龍自念言：『我力如意，傾覆此國，其如反掌；此人小物，豈能困我？我今以持戒故，不計此身，當從佛語！』於是自忍，眠目不視，閉氣不息。⋯⋯既以失皮，赤肉在地，⋯⋯諸小蟲來食其身，為持戒故，不復敢動。自思惟言：『今我此身以施諸蟲，為佛道故，今以肉施，以充其身；後成佛時，當以法施，以益其心。』⋯⋯爾時毒龍，釋迦文佛是；⋯⋯菩薩護戒，不惜身命，決定不悔，其事如是，是名尸羅波羅蜜。」

> *(Translator's rendering, summary form: "In a former life of the bodhisattva, he was a powerful poison-dragon. ... The dragon, having received the one-day precepts, left [his lair] in search of stillness and entered the forest to meditate. After long sitting, he grew weary and slept. ... A hunter, seeing him, was delighted, ... pressed his head with a staff and flayed his skin with a knife. The dragon thought: 'My strength is such that to overturn this kingdom is as easy as turning my hand; this small man — how could he trouble me? But because I am holding the precepts, I will not take account of this body; I shall accord with the Buddha's word.' He endured it himself, closing his eyes and not looking, holding his breath without breathing. ... With his skin gone, the raw flesh lay on the ground, ... and small worms came to eat his body. For the sake of keeping the precepts, he dared not stir again. He reflected: 'Now I give this body to these worms — for the sake of the Buddha-path, I offer now this flesh to fill their bodies; when I become a Buddha in the future, I shall offer the Dharma to benefit their minds.' ... At that time, the poison-dragon was Śākyamuni Buddha. ... The bodhisattva guards the precepts, not sparing body or life, resolved and without regret; such are the events. This is named *śīla-pāramitā*.")*[^9]

The narrative may be analyzed as a five-beat structure. *Beat 1: Reception.* The dragon — a being of formidable strength — voluntarily takes upon himself the one-day precepts (一日戒, an early designation of what would later be standardized as the *aṣṭāṅga-uposatha* or eight-fold abstinence precepts).[^10] Two terms stand in revealing juxtaposition: *voluntary* and *strength*. The recipient is not one who keeps the precepts because he lacks the power to break them; he keeps them because, having ample power, he chooses not to exercise it. *Beat 2: Threat.* A hunter sees the dragon, presses his head with a staff, and flays his skin with a knife. The threat is then redoubled at a lower register: with the skin gone, swarms of insects come to feed on the exposed flesh. Two stages of threat are deliberately structured: the human threat (motivated by economic interest in the skin) and the biological threat (the unmotivated, undirected feeding of insects). The escalation removes intentional opposition from the equation: the dragon's persistence cannot be a response to *malice*; the insects mean nothing by their eating. *Beat 3: Inner deliberation.* The first hinge of the narrative is the dragon's interior speech: "My strength is such that to overturn this kingdom is as easy as turning my hand; this small man — how could he trouble me? But because I am holding the precepts, I will not take account of this body." The structure of the speech is exact. First, *acknowledge the available power*: I could destroy this kingdom in a moment. Second, *refuse to exercise it*: because I am holding the precepts, I will not. The configuration is the *willed restraint of a strong agent*, not the passive endurance of a weak one. At the perfection of *śīla*, what matters is not whether one *can* kill but whether, *being able to kill, one chooses not to*. *Beat 4: The dual vow.* The second hinge: "Now I give this body to these worms — for the sake of the Buddha-path, I offer now this flesh to fill their bodies; when I become a Buddha in the future, I shall offer the Dharma to benefit their minds." Here the dragon converts the passive structure of *being eaten* into the active structure of *giving*. The body is given in the present as flesh-*dāna* to fill the worms; the Dharma will be given in the future as Dharma-*dāna* to nourish their minds. The dual vow performs a structural transformation: what was a threat to the body becomes, in the same instant, an act of bodhisattva conduct. *Beat 5: The* jātaka-*identification and the doctrinal closure.* "At that time, the poison-dragon was Śākyamuni Buddha. ... The bodhisattva guards the precepts, not sparing body or life, resolved and without regret; such are the events. This is named *śīla-pāramitā*." The *jātaka*-frame supplies the conventional identification (Śākyamuni's previous life), and the doctrinal closure supplies the chapter's normative claim: *this entire narrative is* what *śīla-pāramitā* is. The narrative's argumentative work is to demonstrate that *śīla-pāramitā* is not the technical mastery of many precepts nor the avoidance of error within a precept-list; it is the integrity of the precept *at the threshold of bodily survival*. When the cost of precept-keeping is pushed to its uttermost limit (the loss of one's own body), the perfection of *śīla* discloses what it actually is. This is the content of 「不惜身命」 ("not sparing body or life") as a Mahāyāna technical phrase.

### 3.2 The Sutasoma Back-Reference: Fascicle 14 → Fascicle 4

Immediately after the poison-dragon narrative, the *Śāstra* records a single brief phrase:

> 「如上《蘇陀蘇摩王經》中說，不惜身命以全禁戒。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "As stated above in the *Sūtra of King Sutasoma*: without sparing body or life, in order to keep the precepts whole.")*[^11]

The grammar is unambiguous: 「如上⋯⋯中說」 ("as stated above in ...") indicates *back-reference*, not fresh narration. The Sutasoma narrative is not retold in fascicle 14. The full narrative resides instead in T1509 fascicle 4, where King Sutasoma's story is given in detail across roughly sixty lines. A philological correction is owed here, since the secondary literature occasionally treats fascicle 14 as the locus of the Sutasoma narrative. The complete narrative is in fascicle 4, lines 724–781, where the transliteration of the king's name is 須陀須摩 *Xūtuó-xūmó* (rather than fascicle 14's variant transliteration 蘇陀蘇摩 *Sūtuó-sūmó*), and the kidnapper-king is named 鹿足 *Lùzú* — "Spotted-Foot," corresponding to Sanskrit *Kalmāṣapāda*.[^13] The narrative's hinge passage in fascicle 4 is a verse:

> 「實語第一戒，實語昇天梯，實語小而大，妄語入地獄。我今守實語，寧棄身壽命，心無有悔恨。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "Truthful speech is the foremost precept; truthful speech is the ladder ascending to heaven; truthful speech, small in form, is great in substance; false speech enters the hells. I now hold to truthful speech; I would rather discard body and life — my heart has no regret.")*[^12]

The narrative tells of King Sutasoma — a king known for truthful speech (*satya* / *saccavāca*) — who is captured by a band-leader of demonic kings (the *Kalmāṣapāda* of the Sanskrit-Pāli sources, the *Lùzú* of T1509 fascicle 4). Before his capture, Sutasoma had pledged a gift to a Brahmin; he asks his captor to release him temporarily so that he may keep his vow to the Brahmin, swearing to return after fulfilling it. Having kept the pledge, Sutasoma — true to his oath — returns voluntarily to his captor's domain. The narrative's pivot, made explicit by the verse, is the willingness to "discard body and life" rather than betray the commitment to truthful speech. The argumentative work of the Sutasoma narrative is structurally identical to the poison-dragon's: at the threshold where the cost of precept-keeping reaches its uttermost limit (body and life), the bodhisattva does not break. What differs is the *content* of the precept tested — non-killing in the dragon's case, the integrity of truthful speech in Sutasoma's. The shared structure is "*could break and yet does not*; *could decline to keep and yet keeps*."

### 3.3 A Lexical Coinage and the Philological Discipline of *Jātaka*-Sources

Two cautions must accompany the analysis of these narratives. The first is a lexical caution concerning the thematic naming of the Sutasoma-virtue. The compound 信戒 *xìnjiè* ("Faith-Precept") that one might naturally use to name the narrative-derived virtue concretized in the Sutasoma *jātaka* — fidelity to vow even unto death — *does not appear in T1509 itself*. T1509 fascicle 4's operative vocabulary is 實語 *shíyǔ* ("truthful speech," *satya*) and 不失信 *bùshī xìn* ("not breaking trust," *saccavāca*); neither compound itself forms a fixed term *xìnjiè*. Where this paper hereafter uses the term *xìnjiè* / "Faith-Precept" in the contextualized sense of *the fidelity-to-vow virtue concretized by the Sutasoma jātaka*, it does so as the present paper's own thematic naming, not as a term harvested from T1509. The compound is the paper's framing; the textual operatives 實語 and 不失信 remain T1509's lexicon.[^14] The second is a philological caution concerning *jātaka*-sources. The two *jātaka*s narrated in T1509 fascicle 14 are *not* Nāgārjuna's invention. Étienne Lamotte's source-critical work in volume III (1970) of his *Le Traité de la Grande Vertu de Sagesse de Nāgārjuna* documents their lineage: the Sutasoma narrative is traceable to the Pāli *Mahāsutasoma-jātaka* (No. 537), the Sanskrit *Jātakamālā* of Āryaśūra (No. 31, fourth century CE), and the Chinese *Liù dù jí jīng* (《六度集經》 T0152, translated by Kang Senghui during the Wu kingdom in the mid-third century, fascicle 4); the poison-dragon narrative has a possible antecedent in the Pāli *Bhūridatta-jātaka* (No. 543), the tale of a precept-keeping *nāga*.[^15] The narratives are thus *Mahāyāna re-deployments of widely shared Indian Buddhist jātaka heritage*, not original Nāgārjuna compositions. The disclosure of this source-historical fact does not diminish the paper's central claim: Nāgārjuna's distinctive structural contribution is not the *invention* of the narratives but their *placement* within the dual-anchor architecture in which *anupalabdhi* and *unbreakability* stand simultaneously. The two *jātaka*s of fascicle 14, taken together, supply what may be called the *narrative concretization* anchor of the *Śāstra*'s *śīla-pāramitā* chapter. What they demonstrate is that the first anchor — *anupalabdhi* — does *not* amount to non-keeping. Far from it: at the threshold where the conventional cost of precept-keeping is maximized, the precept becomes *unbreakable* precisely because the bodhisattva's self-grasping at the body has dissolved. *Anupalabdhi* and *unbreakability* are not at odds — they are, on the *Śāstra*'s reading, the two faces of one perfection.

## IV. The Self-Gloss Bridge: *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* Fascicle 14 Lines 158 and 219

### 4.1 The Self-Gloss Bridge at Line 158

The dual-anchor reading presented in §II and §III, considered as the sum of two structurally distinct anchors, might still be challenged: "Why are these two anchors counted as a single architecture? Does Nāgārjuna *himself* link them?" The *Śāstra*'s own answer is supplied at fascicle 14 line 158:

> 「復次，菩薩持戒不以畏故，亦非愚癡，非疑、非惑，亦不自為涅槃故持戒；但為一切眾生故，為得佛道故，為得一切佛法故。如是相名為尸羅波羅蜜。**復次，若菩薩於罪、不罪不可得故，是時，名為尸羅波羅蜜**。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "Furthermore, the bodhisattva keeps the precepts not out of fear, nor out of folly, not from doubt nor from confusion, nor for the sake of his own *nirvāṇa*; but for the sake of all sentient beings, for the sake of attaining the Buddha-path, for the sake of attaining the entire Buddha-Dharma. Such a configuration is named *śīla-pāramitā*. **Furthermore, when the bodhisattva [knows] that transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, that itself is named *śīla-pāramitā*.**")*[^16]

The syntax repays careful attention. The passage contains *two* 「復次」 ("furthermore") clauses, each of which terminates in the identical predicate 「如是相名為／是時名為尸羅波羅蜜」 ("[this configuration / this moment] is named *śīla-pāramitā*"). The first 復次 describes the *motivational layer* of the bodhisattva's precept-keeping: not from fear, not from ignorance, not from doubt, not from self-directed *nirvāṇa*-seeking — but oriented toward all sentient beings, toward the Buddha-path, toward the totality of the Buddha-Dharma. The configuration arising *under that motivation* is named *śīla-pāramitā*. The second 復次 describes the *prajñā layer*: when *anupalabdhi* with respect to transgression and non-transgression is in force, *that itself* is named *śīla-pāramitā*. Two operations are performed in a single passage. First, the second 復次 *recursively cites* fascicle 13's opening sūtra-line, with only the grammatical mood altered: where fascicle 13 reads "*should perfect*" (應具足, imperative), fascicle 14 reads "*that itself is named*" (是時名為, declarative). The line of fascicle 13 returns as a positively recognized configuration of *śīla-pāramitā*. Second, the parallel 復次 construction — bodhicitta-motivation and *anupalabdhi*, identically predicated — declares the two as parallel facets of one *śīla-pāramitā*, neither subordinated to the other. This is the *Śāstra*'s own textual self-gloss of the dual-anchor architecture; no external commentary is needed to establish the link. The line stands at fascicle 14's midpoint and bridges the preceding narrative concretization to the *anupalabdhi*-dialectic that immediately follows.

### 4.2 The *Anupalabdhi*-Dialectic at Lines 164–169

Immediately after the bridge, the *Śāstra* stages a question-and-answer that secures *anupalabdhi* against a predictable objection:

> 「問曰：若捨惡行善，是為持戒，云何言『罪、不罪不可得』？答曰：**非謂邪見、麁心言『不可得』也；若深入諸法相，行空三昧，慧眼觀故，罪不可得；罪無故，不罪亦不可得**。復次，眾生不可得故，殺罪亦不可得；罪不可得故，戒亦不可得。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "Question: If to abandon evil and to cultivate good is precept-keeping, how can one say 'transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable'? Answer: It is *not* a saying of 'unobtainable' from wrong views or coarse mind; rather, when one enters deeply into the marks of dharmas, practices the *śūnyatā-samādhi*, and sees with the eye of wisdom — *therefore* transgression is unobtainable; transgression being absent, non-transgression too is unobtainable. Furthermore, because sentient beings are unobtainable, the transgression of killing is unobtainable; because transgression is unobtainable, the precept too is unobtainable.")*[^17]

The questioner's concern is intelligible: if both transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, has the *Śāstra* not effectively dissolved the precepts? The *Śāstra*'s answer is in two layers. *First*, *anupalabdhi* is not the careless or nihilistic claim that there is "nothing to be obtained." It is the dharma-eye's *seeing*, which arises only "when one enters deeply into the marks of dharmas and practices the *śūnyatā-samādhi*." *Anupalabdhi* is *vision*, not slogan. *Second*, *anupalabdhi* unfolds internally: transgression's own-nature is not obtainable; therefore transgression is, in the ultimate sense, not graspable; non-transgression, defined by contrast to transgression, loses its contrastive ground and thereby is also not graspable. The next step deepens the analysis still further: because the *sentient being* against whom an act of killing might be committed is itself unobtainable, the *transgression of killing* is unobtainable; and because the transgression is unobtainable, the corresponding *precept* (the precept of non-killing) is unobtainable in the same ultimate sense. The final sentence has a structural echo worth noting: "because sentient beings are unobtainable, the transgression of killing is unobtainable" prefigures the later *trimaṇḍala-pariśuddhi* ("three-circle purity," 三輪體空) analysis of *śīla*, in which the agent of killing, the patient of killing, and the act of killing are all seen to be empty. The present paper does not develop the three-circle analysis here; it is the territory of a subsequent paper in the volume.[^18] The present point is more modest: the *anupalabdhi*-dialectic of lines 164–169 is *not* an external prajñā-overlay imposed onto an otherwise self-standing precept; it is the *Śāstra*'s own unfolding of how the precepts look in the gaze of *prajñā*.

### 4.3 The Chapter-Closing Recursive Citation at Line 219

The *śīla-pāramitā* chapter of fascicle 14 closes with a single sentence:

> 「以是故言『於罪、不罪不可得故，應具足尸羅波羅蜜』。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "Therefore it is said: 'Because transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, one should perfect the *śīla-pāramitā*.'")*[^19]

The line is brief, but its location performs a structural function. The chapter's argumentation — narrative concretization, self-gloss bridge, *anupalabdhi*-dialectic — is brought to a close *not* by a new conclusion but by an explicit citation of fascicle 13's opening sūtra-line, prefixed by 「以是故言」 ("therefore it is said"). The whole chapter is thereby sealed within a three-layer recursive frame: ``` Fascicle 13 opening: 「罪、不罪不可得故，應具足尸羅波羅蜜」 ↓ Fascicle 14 midpoint (line 158): 「若菩薩於罪、不罪不可得故，是時，名為尸羅波羅蜜」 ↓ Fascicle 14 closing (line 219): 「以是故言『於罪、不罪不可得故，應具足尸羅波羅蜜』」 ``` This three-layer recursive citation is the *Śāstra*'s own internal architectural marker. The dual-anchor reading is therefore not an interpretive overlay; it is the *Śāstra*'s self-glossing of its own structural design, and the present paper's contribution lies in *reading the marker* rather than in *proposing* it.

### 4.4 Two-Truth Simultaneity as the Mark of *Pāramitā*-Ization The *Śāstra*'s *śīla-pāramitā* chapter operates on two truths simultaneously. At the conventional level (*saṃvṛti*, 世俗), the precept is *unbreakable*: the poison-dragon refuses to use his strength even at the cost of being eaten alive; King Sutasoma refuses to break his vow even at the cost of returning to his captor's death-bound domain. At the ultimate level (*paramārtha*, 勝義), the precept is *unobtainable*: transgression's own-nature, non-transgression's own-nature, the sentient being, and the precept itself are all unobtainable under the gaze of *prajñā*. The decisive structural point is that these two truths are *not sequential*. Nāgārjuna's *śīla-pāramitā* is *not* the doctrine that "first one keeps the unbreakable precept and *then*, later, realizes its unobtainability." It is the doctrine that the two are *simultaneously* in force in the same act of precept-keeping. In a single act of keeping the one-day precept, the dragon is both unbreakably faithful (at the conventional level) and free from grasping at transgression's nature (at the ultimate level). The simultaneous standing of *unbreakable* and *unobtainable* is, in the *Śāstra*, the *mark* of *śīla-pāramitā* as such. The argument is therefore: - *Unbreakable* alone (without *anupalabdhi*) is ordinary precept-keeping, not yet *pāramitā*.
- *Unobtainable* alone (without *unbreakable*) is the wrong-view nihilism that the *Śāstra* explicitly rules out at lines 164–169.
- *Unbreakable* and *unobtainable* simultaneously is *śīla-pāramitā*. This is the structural conclusion that fascicle 14 closes upon, recursively citing fascicle 13's opening sūtra-line as the *Śāstra*'s own sealing of the chapter.

## V. Cross-Traditional Positioning: Pāli Linear Teleology vs. Madhyamaka Two-Truth Simultaneity

### 5.1 The Pāli Linear Teleology: AN 5.213 and AN 11.1

To sharpen the contour of Nāgārjuna's *śīla-pāramitā*, two classical Pāli formulations of *sīla*'s doctrinal weight may be set alongside it. The first is *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 5.213, the *Sīlasutta*, which enumerates five benefits (*ānisaṃsa*) of moral conduct: the precept-keeper (i) acquires great wealth (*bhogakkhandha*), (ii) gains a good reputation (*kalyāṇa-kittisadda*), (iii) enters any assembly without fear (*visārada*), (iv) dies unconfused in mindfulness (*asammuḷha*), and (v) after death is reborn in a fortunate destination (*sugati-saggaloka*).[^20] These five are not the totality of *sīla*'s significance — but they constitute a stable core of the early Buddhist account: the precept's meaning is laid out along the axis of *the consequences it brings about* — wealth, reputation, fearlessness, clarity at death, a good rebirth. The second is AN 11.1, the *Kimatthiyasutta*, which articulates an eleven-link chain of conditioning. The chain begins with *sīla* and proceeds through *avippaṭisāra* (absence of remorse), *pāmojja* (gladness), *pīti* (joy), *passaddhi* (tranquility), *sukha* (ease), *samādhi* (concentration), *yathābhūta-ñāṇadassana* (knowledge-and-vision of things as they are), *nibbidā* (disenchantment), *virāga* (dispassion), and culminates in *vimutti-ñāṇadassana* (knowledge-and-vision of liberation).[^21] The *sutta*'s closing line is the volume's recurring touchstone: > *Iti kho, ānanda, kusalāni sīlāni anupubbena aggāya parentī*ti. > *(Translator's rendering: "Thus, Ānanda, wholesome moral conduct progressively leads up to the highest.")* The decisive terms are *anupubbena* ("step by step") and *aggāya parentī* ("leading up to the highest"). The structure of *sīla*'s significance is *linear teleology*: *sīla* is the starting point of an eleven-link chain in which the realization of each link conditions the next, and the chain as a whole proceeds *step-by-step* toward the final liberative state. *Sīla*'s perfection consists in the chain's capacity to unfold in full sequence.

### 5.2 The Co-Emergent *Pāramitā*-Framing of the 5th–6th Centuries

A philological observation requires a corrective claim here. The *thematization of sīla as a* pāramī(tā) — i.e., as a *completable independent virtue* within a list of perfections — is *post-canonical in both lineages*. In the Pāli, the four principal Nikāyas (DN, MN, SN, AN) thematize *sīla* extensively as an independent moral category, but they do not isolate *sīla-pāramī* as a thematized compound. The compound surfaces only in the late stratum of the Khuddaka — in the *Buddhavaṃsa* (which lists the ten *pāramī*s) and the *Cariyāpiṭaka* (whose first chapter is devoted to *sīla-pāramī*) — and is systematized in the commentarial layer of Dhammapāla's *Cariyāpiṭaka-aṭṭhakathā* (c. 6th century) and the *Jātaka-nidānakathā* attributed to the circle around Buddhaghosa (c. 5th century).[^22] In the Northern Mahāyāna, *śīla-pāramitā* as a thematized chapter-heading emerges in the *Aṣṭasāhasrikā-prajñāpāramitā* (2nd–3rd century CE) and is given its sustained treatise-length treatment in T1509 (3rd–4th-century composition, Kumārajīva's Chinese translation 401–406). The two lineages' systematic thematizations of the *pāramī(tā)* of *śīla* are thus *contemporaneous*. The Pāli *sīla-pāramī* receives its sustained commentarial development in the 5th–6th centuries, in Sri Lanka, within the Mahāvihāra lineage of the Theravāda; the Northern *śīla-pāramitā* receives its sustained treatise-length development in the 3rd–5th centuries, in northwestern India and Central Asia, within the *prajñāpāramitā* lineage. They are *concurrent post-canonical developments*, not a unilateral inheritance. The structural consequence is decisive. *If* one looks for Nāgārjuna's unique structural contribution by asking *which tradition first thematized śīla as a pāramī(tā)*, one finds nothing distinctively Nāgārjunian: the thematization is a *shared post-canonical phenomenon across both lineages*. The unique structural contribution must be located elsewhere. The proposal of this paper is that Nāgārjuna's unique structural contribution lies *not* in the *pāramitā*-framing itself but in *the addition of the anupalabdhi layer to it*. The Pāli *pāramī*-framing (as systematized in the *Cariyāpiṭaka-aṭṭhakathā* and the *Jātaka-nidānakathā*) preserves the linear teleology of the canonical *sīla*-doctrine: *sīla-pāramī* is one of ten *pāramī*s in a series that progresses step-by-step toward Buddhahood. The Northern *śīla-pāramitā* of Nāgārjuna *adds* a layer that the Pāli *sīla-pāramī* literature does not develop: the prajñā-prior *anupalabdhi* layer, which makes the perfection of *śīla* simultaneously *unobtainable* (at the ultimate level) and *unbreakable* (at the conventional level). The axis-difference can be summarized in two formulas: - Pāli *sīla-pāramī*: **linear teleology** — *sīla* → *avippaṭisāra* → *pīti* → *samādhi* → *paññā* → *vimutti*, progressing step-by-step (*anupubbena aggāya parentī*).
- Northern *śīla-pāramitā* of T1509: **two-truth simultaneity** — at the same instant of precept-keeping, the precept is conventionally *unbreakable* and ultimately *unobtainable*. These are not opposed structures. The linear teleology is *not* rejected by T1509; the poison-dragon narrative explicitly maintains the teleological orientation ("for the sake of the Buddha-path, ... when I become a Buddha in the future"). What T1509 *adds* is an orthogonal layer that runs through every link of the teleology: the ultimate-level *anupalabdhi* of *śīla*. The addition, this paper argues, is the *mark* of Nāgārjuna's *pāramitā*-ization of *śīla*.

### 5.3 The *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* Sister Corpus

The *anupalabdhi* layer of T1509 is not a local peculiarity of fascicles 13–14. It is distributed across the broader Nāgārjuna-attributed corpus. The strongest evidence is supplied by the *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* (T1521 *Shízhù pípóshā lùn* 《十住毘婆沙論》, attributed in the Chinese transmission to Nāgārjuna and translated by Kumārajīva), whose fascicles 16–17 contain a five-chapter sequence devoted to *śīla*: chapter 31 護戒品 (T26n1521_p0107c25), chapter 32 解頭陀品 (p0111b26), chapter 33 助尸羅果品 (p0116a27), chapter 34 讚戒品 (p0120a07), and chapter 35 戒報品 (p0121a19). T1521 frames *śīla-pāramitā* within the *daśabhūmi* scheme — the second ground (the *vimalā* or "Stainless" ground, 離垢地) is the bodhisattva-stage at which *śīla-pāramitā* is the presiding perfection.[^23] The register-difference between T1509 and T1521 is real but not contradictory. T1509 fascicles 13–14 are a *standalone treatise*-mode treatment of *śīla-pāramitā* — the *pāramitā*-chapter of a *prajñāpāramitā* commentary, organized topically. T1521 fascicles 16–17 are a *staged-progression* treatment — the *śīla*-chapter within the *daśabhūmi* commentary, organized along the path-stages of the ten grounds. The Nāgārjuna-attributed corpus can speak in both registers without contradiction. The cross-corpus evidence for the distribution of the *anupalabdhi* layer comes at T1521 chapter 33 (T26n1521_p0116b02–12):

> 「此人深樂空故，於第一義中而亦不得護三業法。」

> *(Translator's rendering: "Because this person deeply delights in emptiness, even at the level of ultimate truth, he does not obtain the dharma of guarding the three karmas.")*

The phrase 「於第一義中而亦不得護三業法」 ("even at the level of ultimate truth, he does not obtain the dharma of guarding the three karmas") is a direct cross-corpus parallel to T1509's *anupalabdhi*-dialectic. The guarding of the three karmas (身 body, 口 speech, 意 mind) is, in conventional terms, the very content of *śīla*-keeping; the ultimate-level *non-obtaining* of this guarding is the *anupalabdhi* of *śīla* in a slightly different vocabulary. The distribution of the *anupalabdhi* layer across two distinct treatises in the Nāgārjuna-attributed corpus stabilizes the layer as a structural feature of the corpus, not a local rhetorical flourish of T1509 fascicle 14. An authorship-discipline note is warranted at this point. T1521's attribution to Nāgārjuna has been questioned in modern scholarship — Étienne Lamotte's 1944 volume I introduction registers reservations, and Hirakawa Akira's 1974 *History of Indian Buddhism* gives extended attention to the question — with the possibility that T1521 is a later compilation incorporating Nāgārjuna's school's interpretive material. The Chinese transmission's attribution reads 「聖者龍樹造、姚秦三藏鳩摩羅什譯」 ("composed by the Holy One Nāgārjuna, translated by Tripiṭaka-master Kumārajīva of Yao Qin") at T26n1521_p0107c23–24. The present paper takes a neutral position by speaking of "the Nāgārjuna-attributed corpus" — honoring the operative reference of the Chinese transmission while acknowledging the open scholarly question.[^24] Whatever the resolution of the authorship question, the *thematic* continuity of the *anupalabdhi* layer across T1509 and T1521 is a textual fact independent of authorship-attribution.

### 5.4 Three Modern Interlocutors: Lamotte, Williams, Keown

Contemporary English-language scholarship on T1509 fascicles 13–14 is sparse. Three modern interlocutors warrant focused dialogue, each operating along a distinct scholarly axis. **Étienne Lamotte** (1903–1983), the Belgian Buddhist scholar whose five-volume *Le Traité de la Grande Vertu de Sagesse de Nāgārjuna* (Louvain, 1944–1980) remains the only sustained book-length Western-language treatment of T1509, devotes volume III (1970) to the *pāramitā*-chapters that include fascicles 13–14. *Concession.* Lamotte's three-layered reading of T1509 fascicles 13–14 — conventional *Vinaya* continuity, Mahāyāna *bodhicitta*-motivated reframing, and Madhyamaka *anupalabdhi*-deconstruction — is the direct intellectual antecedent of the structural analysis in §III–§IV above. His philological documentation of the *jātaka*-sources for the poison-dragon and Sutasoma narratives — *Bhūridatta-jātaka*, *Mahāsutasoma-jātaka*, *Jātakamālā*, T0152 *Liù dù jí jīng* — is the most thorough available in any Western language. *Extension.* The present paper's positioning of T1509's *anupalabdhi* layer differs in one detail of historical placement. Lamotte's 1970 reading aligns the *anupalabdhi* passages tightly with the later doxographic Madhyamaka school of Candrakīrti and Bhāviveka. The present paper reads T1509's *anupalabdhi* layer instead as an *internal* gloss within the *prajñāpāramitā* commentarial lineage — a development *of* the *Pañcaviṃśati-sāhasrikā*'s own *anupalambha-yoga* rather than an anticipation of later Madhyamaka doxographic identity. The distinction is one of *placement within intellectual genealogy*, not of substantive disagreement on the content of the *anupalabdhi*-passages. **Paul Williams**, *Mahāyāna Buddhism: The Doctrinal Foundations* (Routledge, 2nd edition, 2009), is the most reliable contemporary English-language entry-point to the *bodhicitta*-motivated reframing of *śīla* in Mahāyāna. Williams's chapter 10, "The Ethics of the Bodhisattva," synthesizes the *prajñāpāramitā* and Madhyamaka treatments of Mahāyāna ethics and has substantially updated the older taxonomies of Edward Conze's 1960s framework.[^25] *Concession.* Williams's account of *bodhicitta*-motivated *śīla* maps closely onto T1509 fascicle 14 line 158's first 復次 clause — the bodhicitta-orientation of the bodhisattva's precept-keeping ("for the sake of all sentient beings, for the sake of attaining the Buddha-path, for the sake of attaining the entire Buddha-Dharma"). Williams is correctly read as a synthesis of the contemporary doctrinal-historical consensus on this layer. *Extension.* Williams's treatment is textbook-synthesis-level rather than monograph-specialist-level on T1509 itself: T1509 receives roughly a single-page overview in chapter 10, with a Lamotte-pointer for further detail. The present paper extends Williams's Mahāyāna-ethics synthesis by returning to T1509's textual evidence directly, with particular attention to the second 復次 clause at line 158 (the *anupalabdhi* layer) and to the chapter-closing recursive citation at line 219 (the structural seal of the dual-anchor architecture). **Damien Keown**, *The Nature of Buddhist Ethics* (Palgrave, 1992; paperback 2001), has developed the most sustained virtue-ethics reading of Buddhist ethics in the contemporary English-language literature. *Concession.* Keown's virtue-ethics framework, drawing on Aristotelian-Aquinas register, fits T1509's *anupalabdhi* layer better than a consequentialist reading. *Anupalabdhi*'s apophatic structure — neither transgression nor non-transgression is graspable as a self-existent entity — falls outside the calculational framework of any consequentialist ethics. But it falls within a virtue-ethics framework whose central category is *character-formation*: under the *anupalabdhi* gaze, the bodhisattva's precept-keeping is *not* the calculation of consequences nor the mechanical observance of rules but the maturation of character toward a state in which the precept is held *without grasping at the precept-relation as such*. *Extension.* Keown's virtue-ethics framework was developed primarily from Pāli and early-Mahāyāna sources, with limited direct engagement of T1509. The present paper extends Keown's framework into T1509's *anupalabdhi* layer by proposing that *anupalabdhi* does *not* dissolve *śīla* but *deepens* it into a non-grasping character-formation. The apophatic dimension is not a corrosive of virtue but a maturation of it — an extension beyond Keown's own explicit treatment of the Pāli and early-Mahāyāna corpora. A brief negative-selection note may be added here. Charles Goodman's consequentialist reading (*Consequences of Compassion*, Oxford University Press 2009), the subject of P12's interlocutor dialogue, is set aside in the present paper because the *anupalabdhi*-axis of T1509 is structurally orthogonal to consequentialist calculation: the apophatic non-graspability of transgression's own-nature cannot be entered into any calculus-of-consequences ledger without remainder. Edward Conze's *Buddhist Thought in India* (Allen & Unwin, 1962) is treated only as a historical reference, having been substantially updated by Williams 2009. Jan Nattier's *A Few Good Men* (University of Hawai'i Press, 2003) addresses the *Ugraparipṛcchā* rather than the *prajñāpāramitā* commentary lineage and does not engage T1509 fascicles 13–14 directly. Christopher Gowans's *Buddhist Moral Philosophy* (Routledge, 2015) is a textbook genre and does not pursue the *anupalabdhi*-section in depth.

### 5.5 Honest Disclosure: Sixty Years of Sparse Coverage

A frank disclosure is in order. *No English-language monograph has appeared in the sixty years since Lamotte's volume III taking T1509's* śīla-pāramitā *chapter as its sustained subject*. Article-length treatments scattered across *JIABS* (*Journal of the International Association of Buddhist Studies*), the *Journal of Buddhist Ethics*, and similar venues invariably cite Lamotte's volume III as their secondary anchor without independent textual analysis of the *anupalabdhi*-passages. The structural analysis of §III–§V above — *anupalabdhi* axis, narrative concretization, self-gloss bridge, two-truth simultaneity — is offered as a contemporary English-language scholarly supplement on this specific corpus, in conscious awareness of the thinness of the existing English-language coverage. The paper does not claim to fill the gap; it claims only to add one analytic contribution along a single dimension, in the hope that the sparseness of the field invites further work.

## VI. Conclusion: Nāgārjuna's Unique Contribution Is the *Anupalabdhi* Layer

The paper's central argument can now be drawn together. T1509's *śīla-pāramitā* chapter, across fascicles 13 and 14, exhibits a dual-anchor architecture. The first anchor — the *anupalabdhi* axis — is installed by the single sūtra-line that opens fascicle 13: "Because transgression and non-transgression are unobtainable, one should perfect the *śīla-pāramitā*." This line places the prajñā-prior orientation of *unobtainability* ahead of every taxonomic, motivational, or merit-related treatment of the precepts. The second anchor — the narrative concretization — is supplied by fascicle 14's poison-dragon *jātaka* (a fresh narration) and the back-reference to King Sutasoma's complete narrative in fascicle 4. Together the two *jātaka*s demonstrate that *anupalabdhi* is not *non-keeping*: precisely because the bodhisattva is free of self-grasping at the body, the precept becomes *unbreakable* at the threshold of bodily survival.

The internal connection of the two anchors is not an interpretive overlay. T1509 itself textually self-glosses the architecture. At fascicle 14 line 158, a bridge passage parallels the bodhicitta-motivated layer and the *anupalabdhi* layer through a doubled 復次 construction, with each layer identically predicated as *śīla-pāramitā*. At fascicle 14 line 219, the chapter closes by recursively citing fascicle 13's opening sūtra-line in declarative form. The three-layer recursive citation — opening, midpoint, close — is the *Śāstra*'s own structural marker, and the present paper's dual-anchor reading is named for the *Śāstra*'s self-glossing rather than for any external claim about it.

Placed within the 5th–6th-century co-emergence of the *pāramitā*-framing across the Pāli and Northern lineages, three structural propositions follow:

- The thematization of *śīla* as a *pāramī(tā)* is a *shared post-canonical phenomenon* of both lineages — emerging in the Khuddaka's late stratum (*Buddhavaṃsa*, *Cariyāpiṭaka*) and its commentarial layer (*Cariyāpiṭaka-aṭṭhakathā*, *Jātaka-nidānakathā*) on the Pāli side, and in the *Aṣṭasāhasrikā*-and-*Śāstra* corpus on the Northern side — not the unilateral invention of either tradition.
- Within that shared framing, Nāgārjuna's unique structural contribution is the *addition of the anupalabdhi layer* to *śīla* — the doctrine that, in the ultimate sense, transgression, non-transgression, the sentient being, and the precept are equally unobtainable.
- The *mark* of Nāgārjuna's *pāramitā*-ization of *śīla* is *two-truth simultaneity*: at the same instant of precept-keeping, the precept is conventionally *unbreakable* (the *jātaka* threshold) and ultimately *unobtainable* (the *anupalabdhi* analysis).

The Pāli *sīla-pāramī*'s linear teleology (*sīla* → *avippaṭisāra* → *samādhi* → *paññā* → *vimutti*, *anupubbena aggāya parentī*) is *not* rejected by T1509. The teleological orientation is preserved in the poison-dragon's "for the sake of the Buddha-path" and "when I become a Buddha." What T1509 adds is an orthogonal layer that runs through every link of the teleology: the ultimate-level *anupalabdhi* of *śīla*. This addition, the paper argues, is the structural core of Nāgārjuna's *śīla-pāramitā* discussion.

T1509 fascicles 13–14 are the master architectural treatment of *śīla-pāramitā*, but the *functional* differentiation of *śīla* within the bodhisattva path is not unfolded there. The nine-fold taxonomy of bodhisattva precepts — the *navavidha-śīla* (種性戒, 一切戒, 難行戒, 一切門戒, 善士戒, 一切種戒, 遂求戒, 此世他世樂戒, 清淨戒) — is supplied instead by the *Bodhisattvabhūmi* chapter on *śīla* within the *Yogācārabhūmi* (T1579), translated by Xuanzang during the Tang in 646–648, which is the subject of the next paper in this volume (P14).[^26] The dual-anchor architecture established here is the prior condition; the functional differentiation of the nine-fold taxonomy is the subsequent unfolding. The *pāramitā*-ization of *śīla*, on the present reading, draws its prajñā-axis from Nāgārjuna (where the *anupalabdhi* layer is the structural core) and its functional differentiation from the Maitreya / Asaṅga corpus (where the nine-fold taxonomy is the structural articulation). The two together — *anupalabdhi* and *navavidha-śīla* — supply the complete face of Mahāyāna bodhisattva *śīla* as it stabilizes through the 5th and 6th centuries. The present paper has treated the former; the latter is reserved for the paper that follows.

[^23]: T1521 fascicle 16 chapter 31 ("Guarding the Precepts," 護戒品), T26n1521_p0107c25 onward, and fascicle 16 chapter 33 ("Auxiliaries to the Fruit of *Śīla*," 助尸羅果品), T26n1521_p0116b02–12. The *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*'s framing of the bodhisattva stages locates *śīla-pāramitā* as the presiding *pāramitā* of the second bhūmi (the *vimalā* "Stainless" ground, 離垢地), in continuity with the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra*'s own structural assignment of each *pāramitā* to its corresponding ground.

[^24]: T1521's attribution to Nāgārjuna has been questioned in modern scholarship. Étienne Lamotte's 1944 *Le Traité* volume I introduction registers reservations, and Hirakawa Akira's 1974 *Indo bukkyō shi* 『印度佛教史』 (*History of Indian Buddhism*, Tokyo: Shunjūsha, vol. 1) gives extended attention to the question, with the possibility that T1521 is a later compilation incorporating Nāgārjuna's school's interpretive material. The Chinese transmission's attribution at T26n1521_p0107c23–24 reads 「聖者龍樹造、姚秦三藏鳩摩羅什譯」 ("composed by the Holy One Nāgārjuna, translated by Tripiṭaka-master Kumārajīva of Yao Qin"). The present paper takes a neutral position by speaking of "the Nāgārjuna-attributed corpus" — honoring the operative reference of the Chinese transmission while acknowledging the open scholarly question.

[^25]: Paul Williams, *Mahāyāna Buddhism: The Doctrinal Foundations*, 2nd edition (London: Routledge, 2009), particularly chapter 5 (*Prajñāpāramitā*), chapter 6 (Madhyamaka), and chapter 10 ("The Ethics of the Bodhisattva"). Williams's 2nd edition substantially updates the framework of Edward Conze's earlier surveys (Conze, *Buddhist Thought in India*, London: George Allen & Unwin, 1962) to reflect scholarship from the 1990s–2000s. The present paper engages Williams through paraphrase + page-range references; Williams's monograph is in-copyright (Routledge) and is not blockquoted.

[^26]: The nine-fold *śīla* taxonomy (*navavidha-śīla*, 九種戒相) of the *Bodhisattvabhūmi* chapter of the *Yogācārabhūmi* (T1579, Xuanzang's translation, dated 646–648) reads: 種性戒 (clan-precepts, *gotra-śīla*), 一切戒 (all-precepts, *sarva-śīla*), 難行戒 (difficult-to-keep precepts, *duṣkara-śīla*), 一切門戒 (gate-of-all-purposes precepts, *sarvato-mukha-śīla*), 善士戒 (good-person precepts, *satpuruṣa-śīla*), 一切種戒 (all-kinds precepts, *sarvākāra-śīla*), 遂求戒 (desire-fulfilling precepts, *vighātārthika-śīla*), 此世他世樂戒 (precepts for happiness in this and the next world, *ihaparalokika-sukha-śīla*), and 清淨戒 (purity-precepts, *pariśuddha-śīla*). The next paper in this volume, SILA-P14 *The Bodhisattvabhūmi Chapter on Sīla*, takes up the systematic treatment of these nine categories.
