## Abstract

A Western reader first meeting the East Asian monastic tradition encounters a puzzle that the tradition itself never bothered to flag, because to insiders it was obvious. The operative rulebook of Chinese, Korean, Japanese, and Vietnamese monasticism for roughly thirteen centuries was not the canonical monastic code — the sixty-fascicle *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* (the *Sifenlü*, 四分律) translated at the turn of the fifth century — but a seventh-century *digest* of it: Daoxuan's (596–667) *Sifenlü shanfan buque xingshichao* (四分律刪繁補闕行事鈔), the *Vinaya Compendium for Conducting Affairs*. Why should an abridgement outrank the canon it abridged? This paper argues that the premise of the puzzle is wrong: the *Xingshichao* is not an abridgement at all but a *change of genre*. Daoxuan re-architected the entire Dharmaguptaka corpus along a single load-bearing spine — the *two precepts of receiving and following* (受 *shòu* and 隨 *suí*). *Receiving* is the once-and-for-all founding of the precept-substance at ordination, "the vow framed in intention"; *following* is the lifelong restraint-and-cultivation that fulfils that vow, "the practice that fulfils the vow"; and the two "must mutually sustain each other for there to be any attainment at all." The compendium's canonical authority, the paper contends, rests on two pillars: its *architecture* (Daoxuan's self-declared three-fascicle design and his "cut-the-verbose, supply-the-deficient" editorial method) and its *doctrinal classification* (the *three-school judgment* and the *five senses of partial accord with the Greater Vehicle*, transmitted in Yuanzhao's *Zizhiji* from Daoxuan's lost *Karman-Commentary*, which place the Dharmaguptaka as a Nominalist text that annexes the Realists below and converges on the Perfect Vehicle above). The ontological trilemma of the precept-substance proper — *what is the thing received?* — belongs to the previous paper (P16); here the substance appears only as one structurally placed column. The central thesis is that the *proximate* cause of a millennium of authority was not any single doctrinal verdict but the **sustainable operability** that the receiving–following scheme itself secured: a manual any generation could run, whatever its metaphysics of the substance.

**Keywords:** the *Xingshichao*; Daoxuan; the receiving-and-following precepts (*shòu-suí*); precept-substance (*jietǐ*); doctrinal classification (*pànjiào*); the three-school judgment (*sānzōng pàn*); the five senses of partial accord (*wǔyì fēntōng*); "cut the verbose, supply the deficient"; the *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya*; the Nanshan Vinaya school; Yuanzhao

## I. The Problem: Why the Compendium, Not the Code, Became the Standard

Picture a novice holding two books. The first is the *Sifenlü*, the sixty-fascicle *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* rendered into Chinese by Buddhayaśas and Zhu Fonian in the Later Qin. Open it and you find a chronicle. It begins with royal genealogies — some forty-odd generations of kings down to Śuddhodana, the future Buddha's father, and Rāhula; it then narrates the supersession of four successive methods of conferring full ordination (ordination by seeing the truth, by "come, monk," by the threefold formula, and finally by the formal saṅgha act), each introduced with the phrase "from this day forward" that retires the method before it; and the precepts themselves accrete one at a time, each prefaced by the occasion on which some monk did something, followed by the Buddha's consequent legislation, followed by later case-by-case supplements. It is a faithful record of a code *laid down as occasions arose* — rich in the *events* of ordination and transgression, but never organized into a map a monk could consult in daily practice.

The second book is Daoxuan's *Xingshichao*. Open it and the reader is told at once how the whole work is divided, which chapters treat the communal procedures of the saṅgha, which the individual's keeping and transgressing of precepts, which the miscellaneous conduct that faces the lay world. Why receiving is receiving, why transgression is transgression — each has a stated rubric one can follow.

History's verdict is unambiguous. The book that East Asian monastic communities took as their working standard, that later commentators glossed in stratum upon stratum, that the monk Jianzhen (Ganjin) carried to Japan to found an entire school there, was not the sixty-fascicle root code but this excised-and-supplemented digest. Three Vinaya houses arose in the Tang — Nanshan (Daoxuan), Xiangbu (Fali), and Dongta (Huaisu) — yet only Nanshan endured, unbroken, for a millennium.[^13] So the question sharpens to a point: how does a "summary" come to outrank the very text it summarizes and hold the position of canonical standard for thirteen centuries?

This paper's answer is that the *Xingshichao* became the standard precisely *because it is not a summary*. It is a transformation of genre. Daoxuan took a code laid down occasion by occasion and, using the *two precepts of receiving and following* as his spine, rebuilt it into an *operable manual*: receiving founds the substance, following completes the conduct, and the two sustain each other to accomplish a whole life of disciplined practice. The argument proceeds by treating, in turn, the compendium's architecture and its textual foundations (§II), the core claim that the architecture *is* the proximate cause of its authority (§III), a dialogue with contemporary scholarship (§IV), the disciplinary corrections that keep the reading honest (§V), and a synthesizing conclusion (§VI).

One line of continuity with the previous paper must be drawn at the outset. P16, *Three Schools on the Nature of Precept-Substance*, asked an *ontological* question: when one is ordained, *what, exactly, is the thing received?* — hence its trilemma of the material, the neither-form-nor-mind, and the seed. The present paper asks a different question: not *what is received* but *what kind of building houses that receiving and organizes the whole code around it?* For that reason, the three-school ontology that stood at the center of P16 enters here only once, as the *structural placement* of the "substance-received" (受體) inside the compendium; its ontological dispute is not re-argued (for that, see P16). And Daoxuan's own "Perfect-Teaching seed-substance," which closed P16, is here re-situated: it is no longer treated as an ontological position but as the *content of the third school* in Daoxuan's doctrinal classification, read from the angle of architecture and placement.

## II. Textual Foundations: Architecture, the Receiving–Following Spine, and the Doctrinal Classification

This section presents the first-hand textual basis of the *Xingshichao* in three strata, under one citation discipline that governs the whole paper and must be stated before anything else. Everything bearing on the *architecture* of the work, the *receiving–following* spine, and the *four-fold rubric* of the opening chapter comes from Daoxuan's own autograph *Xingshichao* (*Taishō* vol. 40, No. 1804) and may be quoted directly as Daoxuan's words. Everything bearing on the *systematized* formulae of doctrinal classification — the "three-school judgment," the "five senses of partial accord," the "Perfect-Teaching seed" — comes instead from the Song-dynasty *Zizhiji* of Yuanzhao (*Taishō* vol. 40, No. 1805), which transmits Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary* (*Yeshu* / *Jiemoshu*); such material must always carry that byline and may never be quoted as Daoxuan's autograph. §V corrects the common mis-assignment in detail.

### II.1 Daoxuan's Self-Declared Three-Fascicle Architecture and the "Cut-and-Supply" Method

The architecture of the *Xingshichao* is not an accident of arrangement; it is a design Daoxuan announces in his own preface:

> 然一部之文，義張三位。上卷則攝於眾務，成用有儀；中卷則遵於戒體，持犯立懺；下卷則隨機要行，託事而起。
> *(Translator's rendering: "The text of this one work unfolds, in meaning, across three positions. The upper fascicle gathers the communal affairs of the saṅgha, giving their performance due form; the middle fascicle keeps to the precept-substance, establishing the keeping-and-transgressing of precepts and the rites of repentance; the lower fascicle treats the essential conduct that meets circumstances, arising as occasions require.")*[^1]

These three clauses are Daoxuan's own declared taxonomy of governance. The upper fascicle gathers the communal acts of the saṅgha and gives them ritual form (twelve chapters); the middle fascicle keeps to the core of the precept-substance and establishes the procedures of keeping-and-transgressing and of repentance (four chapters); the lower fascicle handles the miscellaneous, circumstance-driven conduct by which the saṅgha meets the lay world (the remaining chapters). Thirty chapters across three fascicles, in clean order. What deserves notice is the asymmetry: the middle fascicle has the *fewest* chapters yet the *highest* doctrinal density — the substance, the keeping-and-transgressing, and the repentance are all concentrated there, while the upper and lower fascicles spread across the procedural surface. The center of gravity of the whole work falls on the individual's reception of the substance and the subsequent waxing and waning of keeping and transgressing.

And why did Daoxuan write a new book at all? His preface states the diagnosis as a *deficiency to be supplied*: the records left by earlier vinaya masters stopped at the rise and fall of textual commentaries and at digests of questions-and-answers; as for "manifesting conduct in actual affairs and laying down a track for those who come after" — that is, rendering the code into concrete, followable practice and giving later students a rule they could actually walk — there was, he charges, "not one book in a hundred" fit for the purpose.[^2] Hence his resolve to "cut away the verbose and the corrupt, supply what was dropped and missing." The title itself — *shanfan buque*, "cutting the verbose and supplying the deficient" — names the method: excise what is tangled and redundant, supply what is absent and incomplete, gathering the reliable texts of other schools, the incidental teachings of the scriptures, the legacy of the Indian sages and the records of native worthies, and "sifting and weighing their agreements and differences, pursuing each to the bottom."[^3] The four characters *shanfan buque* are at once the book's name and the editorial principle that made the compendium possible.

### II.2 The Four-Fold Rubric of the Opening Chapter: The Conceptual Engine That Runs from Receiving to Following

The work's first chapter, "Stating the Aim, Manifesting the Virtue" (標宗顯德篇), functions as a programmatic preamble. There Daoxuan sets out four headings to comprehend the whole of the precepts:

> 且據樞要，略標四種：一者戒法，二者戒體，三者戒行，四者戒相。……三言戒行者。既受得此戒，秉之在心，必須廣修方便，撿察身口威儀之行……持心後起，義順於前，名為戒行。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Taking the essentials, I outline four kinds: first the precept-dharma; second the precept-substance; third the precept-conduct; fourth the precept-characteristics. … Third, as for precept-conduct: having received this precept and held it in the mind, one must broadly cultivate the means and examine the deportment of body and speech … this keeping-mind that arises thereafter, conforming in meaning to what came before, is called precept-conduct.")*[^4]

Of the four, the *precept-substance* (戒體) is the foundation founded at ordination, "the root from which all conduct is born"; the *precept-conduct* (戒行) is the conduct that arises *after* one has received that precept — broadly cultivating the means, examining bodily and verbal deportment, "in conformity with the originally received substance." This "precept-conduct" is precisely what the later text calls *following* (隨行). And here a move of real importance: when Daoxuan touches the precept-substance in this chapter, he *deliberately brackets the ontological question*. Were one to argue in full, he says, one would have to specify the karmic substance that ordination produces; but for the present purpose of plain exposition, he will "simply set forth the character of the mind that can receive."[^5] In other words, in the *Xingshichao* Daoxuan takes only the single facet of "the mind that receives" at the moment of ordination, and sets explicitly to one side the dispute over whether the karmic substance produced is material, neither-form-nor-mind, or seed.

This paper therefore cites the phrase "what is received and retained in the mind is called the precept-substance" *only* to show how the compendium structurally houses the moment of receiving — not to reopen the ontology of the substance. The three-school ontological dispute is P16's scene; the finer mechanics of the substance-received (the seed, the perfuming, and so on) belong to P18; the present paper digs into neither. The four headings — precept-dharma, precept-substance, precept-conduct, precept-characteristics — are invoked here purely as the *conceptual engine* that runs from receiving to following: the dharma is the rule received, the substance is the foundation receiving founds, the conduct is the following that conforms to the substance, and the characteristics are the determinate boundaries of that conduct.

### II.3 The Receiving–Following Spine: The Building-a-Residence Simile and the Functional Verdict (Daoxuan's Autograph)

The most central text in the whole compendium is Daoxuan's simile of *building a residence*, by which he fixes the relation of receiving and following. This image is where the paper's spine is set:

> 然則，受是要期思願，隨是稱願修行。譬如築營宮宅，先立院牆周匝，即謂壇場受體也；後便隨處營構，盡於一生，謂受後隨行。若但有受無隨，直是空願之院，不免塞露之弊。若但有隨無受，此行或隨生死，又是局狹不周；譬同無院室宇，不免怨賊之穿窬也！必須受隨相資，方有所至。
> *(Translator's rendering: "And so: receiving is the vow framed in intention; following is the practice that fulfils the vow. It is like building a residence. First one raises the perimeter wall all around — this is the substance received at the ordination platform. Afterward one builds out as occasion allows, to the end of one's life — this is the following-conduct after receiving. If there is receiving but no following, it is a courtyard of empty vows, not spared the ills of an open roof. If there is following but no receiving, the conduct may drift with birth-and-death, cramped and incomplete — like rooms with no surrounding wall, not spared the thief who tunnels in. Receiving and following must sustain each other; only then is anything reached.")*[^6]

The image is exact. *Receiving* is like first raising the perimeter wall on the ordination platform, enclosing the ground all around — a once-and-for-all apprehension; the moment one receives, the substance stands. *Following* is like what comes after the wall is up: building out within it, place by place, to the end of one's life — the day-by-day labor of keeping and transgressing. A wall with no construction inside it is a courtyard of empty vows, exposed to wind and sun; construction with no wall around it is conduct that scatters without support, cramped and incomplete, open to the burglar. Hence the two "must sustain each other; only then is anything reached."

Daoxuan then fixes their priority by a second image, of root and branch:

> 受為根本；隨依受起，故曰枝條。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Receiving is the root; following arises in dependence on receiving, and is therefore called the branches.")*[^7]

Receiving is the root, following the branches; the branches arise from the root. And yet — this is the point worth dwelling on — although Daoxuan makes receiving the *root*, he places the *weight of actual attainment* squarely on the side of following. On the respective functions of the two he is blunt:

> 受是緣助，未有行功；必須因隨，對境防擬，以此隨行，至得聖果，不親受體。……若但有受，無隨行者，反為戒欺，流入苦海，不如不受，無戒可違。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Receiving is an enabling condition; it has not yet any merit of conduct. One must, through following, guard against the situations one meets; it is by this following-conduct that the fruit of sagehood is reached, not by the substance-received directly. … If there is only receiving and no following-conduct, the receiving turns into a deceit about the precepts, and one drifts into the sea of suffering — worse than not having received at all, for then there would be no precept to violate.")*[^8]

Two extraordinarily weighty verdicts sit here. First, ordination *in itself* is merely an "enabling condition" (緣助), with no real merit of conduct yet; what actually guards against wrongdoing in the face of circumstance and finally reaches the fruit of sagehood is the lifelong *following* — and the attaining of the fruit is "not by the substance-received directly," meaning that the accomplishment of sagehood rides on the *accumulation of following*, not on the single moment of receiving the substance. Second, and more severe: if one receives but does not follow, the receiving itself becomes a "deceit about the precepts" and a descent into suffering — better, then, "not to have received at all," for then there would be no precept to break. In one breath Daoxuan raises receiving to the status of root and, without the least equivocation, lays the weight of actual realization on following. Receiving founds the root; following completes the conduct; and a root with no conduct becomes a deceit. This is the receiving–following spine that runs through the whole compendium.

### II.4 The Doctrinal Classification: The Three-School Judgment, the Five Senses of Partial Accord, and the Nominalist-to-Perfect Hinge (Yuanzhao's *Zizhiji* Transmitting Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary*)

What lets the *Xingshichao* seat the *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* within the whole doctrinal map of Buddhism, and assign it a definite classificatory place, is a system of *doctrinal judgment* (判教). It must be said again: this *systematized* three-school judgment and the five senses of partial accord are *not* found in Daoxuan's autograph *Xingshichao*; they appear in Yuanzhao's *Zizhiji*, transmitting Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary* (the *Yeshu* / *Jiemoshu*). Yuanzhao's text self-attributes at every turn — "following the three schools of the *Karman-Commentary*," "thus the *Karman-Commentary* says," "the *Commentary* lists five senses in all" — so that the attribution is verified *internally* by the text itself: the framework is Daoxuan's, transmitted by Yuanzhao. It is cited here under that byline.

First, the *three-school judgment* comprehends the substance and conduct of all vinaya study under three schools:

> 且依《業疏》三宗，以示一家處判。……一者實法宗。即薩婆多部。彼宗明體則同歸色聚，隨行則但防七支……此即當分小乘教也。二者假名宗。即今所承曇無德部。此宗論體則強號二非，隨戒則相同十業……此名過分小乘教也。三者圓教宗。……故考受體，乃是識藏熏種；隨行即同三聚圓修……此名終窮大乘教也。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Following the three schools of the *Karman-Commentary*, I set out one lineage's classification. … First, the Realist school — that is, the Sarvāstivāda. That school, in clarifying the substance, returns it to the form-cluster, and in following-conduct guards only the seven branches of body and speech … this is teaching that is wholly within the Lesser Vehicle. Second, the Nominalist school — that is, the Dharmagupta lineage we now inherit. This school, in discussing the substance, strains to name it 'the two negations' [neither form nor mind], and in its following-precepts is the same as the ten courses of action … this is named teaching that goes beyond the Lesser Vehicle. Third, the Perfect-Teaching school … here, examining the substance-received, it is a seed perfumed into the store-consciousness; and following-conduct is the same as the perfect cultivation of the three comprehensive [precepts] … this is named the teaching that exhausts the Greater Vehicle.")*[^9]

The three schools: the *Realist school* is the Sarvāstivāda, which judges the precept-substance to be a cluster of *form* and has following-conduct guard only the seven branches of body and speech — "wholly within the Lesser Vehicle." The *Nominalist school* is the Dharmagupta lineage now inherited (the home school of the *Sifenlü* itself), which strains to call the substance "neither form nor mind" and likens its following-precepts to the ten courses of wholesome action — teaching that "goes beyond the Lesser Vehicle." The *Perfect-Teaching school*, which Daoxuan himself establishes, takes the substance-received to be "a seed perfumed into the store-consciousness" and its following-conduct to be the perfect cultivation of the three comprehensive precepts (of restraint, of wholesome dharmas, of beings) — "the teaching that exhausts the Greater Vehicle." Note carefully: the phrase "a seed perfumed into the store-consciousness" is cited here *only* as the label-content of the Perfect-Teaching school; its ontology — how a seed-substance could actually be constituted — is P16's matter, not developed here.

Second, and most consequential, is the placement of the *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* within the three schools, which has *two tiers*, the "Nominal" and the "Perfect":

> 然今《四分》，正當假宗。深有兼淺之能，故旁收有部；教蘊分通之義，故終會圓乘。是則大小通塞假實淺深，一代雄詮，歷然可見。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Now the *Sifenlü* falls squarely within the Nominalist school. Having the depth that can also take in the shallower, it annexes the Sarvāstivāda alongside; and because its teaching harbors a meaning that partially opens onto [the Greater Vehicle], it ultimately converges on the Perfect Vehicle. Thus the great and the small, the open and the blocked, the nominal and the real, the shallow and the deep — the grand exposition of a whole age — are all plainly to be seen.")*[^10]

This is the subtlety of Daoxuan's placement of the *Sifenlü*. As to its actual operation, it "falls squarely within the Nominalist school" — it is the Nominalist home text; but it reaches *downward*, "having the depth that can also take in the shallower," so it annexes the strengths of the Realist (Sarvāstivāda) school; and it reaches *upward*, with a "teaching that harbors a meaning of partial accord," so it ultimately converges on the Perfect Vehicle. A single vinaya is thereby seated as a hinge that swings both ways, threading through the Lesser and the Greater alike.

Third, the "partial accord with the Greater Vehicle" is established by *five senses*. Yuanzhao, transmitting Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary*, lists them:

> 《疏》中總列五義云：相召為佛子，施生成佛道，沓婆厭無學，捨財用非重，塵境非根了，此皆誠例也。
> *(Translator's rendering: "The *Commentary* lists five senses in all: that [the monks] address one another as 'children of the Buddha'; that [the merit is] dedicated to beings, to accomplish the Buddha-path; that Dravya wearied of the fruit of the adept; that in relinquishing goods the use [of mind] is not the grave [graspingly calculating] kind; and that sense-objects are cognized by consciousness, not by the faculty alone — these are all genuine instances.")*[^11]

These five are examples Daoxuan draws from the text of the *Sifenlü* to prove its "partial accord with the Greater Vehicle": the monks address one another as "children of the Buddha"; there is language of dedicating merit to beings with the vow to accomplish the Buddha-path; there is the episode of Dravya-Mallaputra wearying of the arhat's fruit and turning toward the great practice; there is the handling of forfeited goods in which the state of mind is not the purely Lesser-Vehicle kind of grasping calculation; and there is the sense that objects are cognized *in consciousness, not in the faculty alone* — a consciousness-leaning epistemology. It must be flagged that the fifth sense reads *chénjìng fēi gēn liǎo* (塵境非根了, "sense-objects are not [merely] cognized by the faculty"), *not* the variant *chénjìng fēi gēn jìng* (塵境非根境); a one-character distinction with doctrinal weight, corrected in §V.

What most deserves notice is that Daoxuan's classification is a *measured* argument, not a wholesale elevation. In his day, the lineage of Huiguang ("Master Guang") judged the *Sifenlü* to be *Mahāyāna outright*, taking it simply as a Greater-Vehicle vinaya. Daoxuan corrected this by appeal to old precedent, holding Huiguang's verdict "too sweeping and diffuse" — his citations had some warrant, but the meaning could not be taken whole; for if one says flatly "the *Sifenlü* is Mahāyāna," then "what is left to be the Lesser?" Yuanzhao records Daoxuan's sense: "what the patriarch established is measured in its phrasing; its meaning, being one of partial accord, deeply matches the import of the teaching."[^12] What Daoxuan establishes is *partial accord*, not *full identity* — the *Sifenlü* partakes of the Mahāyāna's reach without being wholly Mahāyāna. That measure is exactly where the rigor of his classification lies.

### II.5 The Root-Vinaya Baseline: The "Events" of Receiving Are Complete, but the "Rubric" of Receiving-and-Following Is Not Yet Set

To see what the compendium's architecture *adds*, one must look back at the root text it works from, the *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* (*Taishō* vol. 22, No. 1428). A descriptive caution must come first: the root code and the *Xingshichao* are two things of *different genre* — the former a code laid down as occasions arose, the latter an operable manual. Their difference is one of genre, *not* a matter of the root being "primitive" or "deficient." The comparison below means only to show that the *organizing principle* of receiving-and-following is Daoxuan's compilation-layer innovation; it does not claim the root code falls short.

The root vinaya's chapter on ordination (受戒揵度) stands at fascicles thirty-one through thirty-five.[^13] Its account of ordination is governed by *event* and *history*: it opens with the forty-odd generations of royal genealogy — not a definition, not a statement of aim; it then narrates the sequence of the four ordination methods, so that the establishment of the formal saṅgha act, for instance, is marked by the diachronic phrase "from this day forward," which carries forward the prior method (ordination by the threefold formula) only to retire it:

> 自今已去，捨三語授具足戒……白四羯磨，當如是授具足戒。
> *(Translator's rendering: "From this day forward, set aside conferring full ordination by the threefold formula … by a single motion and three proclamations [the formal saṅgha act], full ordination is to be conferred thus.")*[^14]

This is a *diachronic* narrative of one method superseding another. As for the precepts, they all run through the three beats of *occasion — legislation — supplement*: first the occasion on which something happened, then the Buddha's consequent framing of the precept, then later case-by-case additions. Even what comes closest to a "statement of aim" — the *ten purposes* for which precepts are laid down — appears *embedded within* the occasion of legislation, as the *rationale for legislating*:

> 自今已去，與諸比丘結戒，集十句義：一、攝取於僧……十、正法得久住。欲說戒者當如是說……
> *(Translator's rendering: "From this day forward, I lay down a precept for the monks, gathering ten purposes: first, to hold the saṅgha together … tenth, that the true Dharma may long endure. One who would recite the precepts should recite thus …")*[^15]

The ten purposes are solemn, but their *position* is as the *reason for legislating*, not as a statement of aim for the whole code; and they carry no architecture of a *receiving / following* division.

So the picture is this: within the root vinaya, the *events* of receiving (the four ordination methods) and the *materials* of following (the precept-characteristics, transgression by transgression) are already present and abundant — but the *receiving / following binary as a framework* is not in the root code. To distill receiving and following into a single mutually sustaining organizing spine, and to rebuild the whole code along that spine, is Daoxuan's compilation-layer contribution. To "cut the verbose" is to cut the royal genealogies, the retired ordination methods, the redundant procedures; to "supply the deficient" is to supply the statement of aim, the receiving-following organizing principle, and a consultable, followable thematic manual. This is a transformation of genre — from *a code laid down as occasions arose* into *an operable manual*.

(A note in passing: this kind of editorial gesture — distilling a vast vinaya canon into a compendium — is not Daoxuan's alone. Buddhaghosa's *Samantapāsādikā* in the southern tradition performs a comparable move of genre. The canon-to-digest passage is, in that sense, pan-Buddhist. Daoxuan's distinctive signature lies in *the particular principle* by which he organizes the whole code: the *receiving-and-following* pair joined to *doctrinal classification and statement of aim*. The southern material is engaged here only by paraphrase for comparison, never quoted.)[^20]

## III. The Core Argument: The Receiving–Following Architecture as the Proximate Cause of a Millennium's Authority

The two foregoing sections set out the textual basis. This section advances the paper's own argument: that the receiving–following organizing spine is the *proximate cause* of the *Xingshichao*'s millennium-long standing as canonical compendium — and that any *conclusion* about the ontology of the precept-substance is, by contrast, only one load-bearing column inside the building, not its focus.

### III.1 Receiving-and-Following as the Spine That Organizes a Field into an Operable Manual

For a vinaya to become a community's working standard, it must answer a thoroughly practical question: from the day a monk is ordained to the end of his life, *how does his relationship to "the precepts" unfold over time?* The root code can tell him the occasion and the boundary of each individual precept, but it gives him no single thread running the length of a life. What Daoxuan supplies, through the receiving-and-following pair, is exactly that thread. Receiving is "the vow framed in intention" — at the single moment of ordination, with one all-encompassing resolve, the substance is apprehended once and the perimeter wall raised. Thereafter receiving is not repeated; once founded, the substance is borne in the mind for life. Following is "the practice that fulfils the vow" — the ongoing, lifelong work of keeping and transgressing, situation by situation, continually answering back to the original vow. A monk's entire disciplined life is thereby organized into a clear *temporal* structure: a once-and-for-all founding (receiving) and a lifelong building-out (following); the former establishes what makes him a monk, the latter accomplishes his actually *being* one.

The genius of this organization is that it gathers the root code's scattered, *event*-indexed precept-materials into an operating frame indexed to *a single human life*. The four aspects of keeping-and-transgressing — keeping by refraining, keeping by acting, transgressing by acting, transgressing by refraining — can all be understood and consulted under one unifying question: *how does the following-conduct answer back to the substance-received?* A sixty-fascicle canon is thereby compressed and recomposed into a map one can follow for a lifetime. This is what makes a *compendium* a compendium: not a *reduction* of content, but an *increase* of organization.

### III.2 Why Receiving-and-Following, Not the Precept-Substance, Is the Proximate Cause of Authority

Here the paper must mark its distance from P16. That paper made the ontological trilemma its focus: is what is received at ordination material, neither-form-nor-mind, or seed? A deep and genuinely valuable speculative question. But a fact deserves notice: monastic communities through the centuries did not take the *Xingshichao* as their daily standard because it had picked the winning side in the ontology of the substance. A *conclusion* about the substance's ontology gives no direct operational guidance on how a monk is to keep and transgress day by day, how he is to repent, how he is to guard against wrongdoing in a given situation.

What actually gave the *Xingshichao* its everyday authority is the *sustainable operability* the receiving–following architecture itself provides. Daoxuan has already made the point for us: receiving is an enabling condition with no merit of conduct yet; what guards against situations and finally reaches the fruit is *following*, and that "not by the substance-received directly." In Daoxuan's own design, the accomplishment of the fruit rides directly on the accumulation of following, not on any verdict about the *nature* of the substance-received. The nature of the substance may be left unsettled — Daoxuan in the opening chapter precisely *leaves* it unsettled — and yet, so long as the architecture of mutually sustaining receiving and following stands, the disciplined life can run.

Hence the paper's claim: the architecture of the receiving-and-following precepts is the *proximate cause* of the *Sifenlü*'s standardization through the *Xingshichao*. The three-school ontology of the substance is, to be sure, one load-bearing column in the building — without *some* "substance-received" to found, the receiving–following architecture could not even be stated — but it is not the focus on which the building's millennium of standing depends. The focus is the receiving–following operating frame that *any* generation, whatever its position on the substance, could run all the same. A book that can be held in common *across* disagreement about ontology is exhibiting one of the defining marks of a canonical standard.

### III.3 How the Classification Consolidates the Standard: Seating the *Sifenlü* as a Two-Way Hinge

If the receiving–following architecture supplies operability *inward*, the doctrinal classification supplies placement-power *outward*. Through the three-school judgment transmitted from Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary*, the *Sifenlü* is seated unambiguously at a hinge between the Lesser and Greater Vehicles: squarely the Nominalist home text, annexing the strengths of the Realist (Sarvāstivāda) school below, and ultimately converging on the Perfect Vehicle above. The cleverness of this placement is that it neither demotes the *Sifenlü* to a purely Lesser-Vehicle vinaya (which would have cost it standing in an East Asia saturated with Mahāyāna sensibility) nor inflates it into a wholly Greater-Vehicle one (which would have forfeited vinaya rigor and collapsed into the "too sweeping and diffuse"). The measure of "partial accord" — *sharing in* the Mahāyāna's reach without *being* Mahāyāna — gives the *Sifenlü* an identity that can both stand its ground in a Mahāyāna world and keep its disciplinary strictness.

Daoxuan's correction of Huiguang's "the *Sifenlü* is Mahāyāna" repays attention here. A classification that can *limit itself* — that says plainly "the meaning is one of partial accord," not "wholly Greater" — can bear the authority of a vinaya more durably than one that elevates without restraint, precisely because it survives the retort "then what is left to be the Lesser?" The *rigor* of the classification is, paradoxically, the *guarantee* of its authority.

### III.4 The Genre Transformation in Review: A Compendium Is Not a Summary

Putting §II's texts together with this section's argument yields a single verdict: the relation of the *Xingshichao* to the *Sifenlü* is not "less" to "more" (summary to original) but "another genre" to "the original genre." The root code is a historical record of a discipline laid down as occasions arose, and its value is *fidelity*; the *Xingshichao* is an operable manual organized by receiving-and-following, and its value is *workability*. Daoxuan accomplishes the transformation by his method of "cutting the verbose and supplying the deficient": cutting the historical, the retired, the redundant; supplying the organizational, the aim-stating, the consultable. The receiving-and-following precepts are the spine of that transformation; the three schools of the classification are its placement-power; and the precept-substance is one structurally placed, load-bearing column in the building — *not* a column this paper sets out to take apart again.

## IV. Dialogue with Contemporary Scholarship

The reading offered here — the *Xingshichao* as an architectural compendium, with the receiving-and-following precepts as the proximate cause of its authority — must be calibrated against the current state of vinaya studies. Three scholars are taken below, each representing one approach; each contribution is granted, and the paper's advance over each is then specified.

### IV.1 The Daoxuan-Commentary and Classification Approach: Newhall

The most precise living reader of Daoxuan's commentaries in English is Thomas Newhall. His study of the concept of *jietǐ* (precept-substance) in Daoxuan's vinaya commentaries shows that Daoxuan's reading of the Dharmagupta school was not passive inheritance but a deliberate, "strong" interpretation — that Daoxuan *reads* the *Sifenlü* as partially according with the Mahāyāna, rather than treating it as inherently Mahāyāna by nature; his lecture title, "Partially in Accord with the Greater Vehicle," names exactly this hermeneutic.[^16] The present paper grants this finding wholesale: it establishes with precision the *agency* of Daoxuan's classification and its sense of measure — partial accord, not full identity.

The paper's advance is a *shift of register*. Newhall's attention falls mainly on the doctrinal and conceptual stratum (how the *jietǐ* concept is constructed, how the classificatory reading unfolds); the present paper raises the question from that stratum to the *architectural* one, asking: as a *compendium*, how does the *Xingshichao*'s receiving-and-following pair organize the whole vinaya field into an operable manual, and how did *that architecture* confer a millennium of authority on the Nanshan school? Put another way: Newhall illuminates *how Daoxuan judges*; this paper goes on to ask *how Daoxuan builds*, and argues that the labor of *building* is the proximate cause of the canonical standing. (Newhall served as the principal English interlocutor for the *jietǐ* doctrine in P16; he is re-anchored here on the classification-and-architecture axis, engaging the "partial accord" thesis rather than the three-school *jietǐ* framing of P16 — a topic-driven re-use disclosed in the footnote, justified because P17 is *literally about Daoxuan's commentaries*, Newhall's own published subject, with no equal English substitute.)

### IV.2 The Chinese-Vinaya and Dharmaguptaka-Institutional Approach: Heirman

Ann Heirman's work on the Dharmaguptaka rules for nuns is the standard English authority on *why the Dharmaguptaka vinaya became the standard in China*; it centers Daoxuan as the hinge of the standard understanding and grounds the basic institutional-historical fact at the level of ordination procedure and rule-content.[^17] The present paper grants this placement: it is precisely this institutional-historical line that establishes the basic fact of the Dharmaguptaka's standard status in East Asia.

The paper's advance is from the stratum of *rule-content and ordination procedure* to the stratum of *Daoxuan's compilation-architecture*. What Heirman details is the *content* of the vinaya; what this paper asks after is the *act itself* by which Daoxuan, using the receiving-and-following organizing principle, recomposed that content into a compendium — and it argues, further, that this very act of compendium-making constitutes the proximate cause of the *Sifenlü*'s standardization. A standard of content needs an operable vehicle to carry it, and the *Xingshichao* is that vehicle.

### IV.3 The Vinaya-School *Longue-Durée* Approach: Wang Jianguang

Chinese-language scholarship has a systematic account of the Vinaya school as a *school* across the long term, well represented by Wang Jianguang's *A Comprehensive History of the Chinese Vinaya School*.[^18] The book traces the diachronic spine of the Vinaya school as a tradition: the formation of the Nanshan lineage, the flourishing of its sixty-odd Tang-and-Song commentators on the *Xingshichao*, and its several later revivals — placing Daoxuan on a transmission-hinge that spans a millennium. The present paper grants this long-term picture.

The paper's advance is from the *survey-historical* dimension to the *structural-diagnostic* one. What Wang narrates is the history of *what happened* to the Vinaya school; what this paper asks is the *structural* question — *what feature of the* Xingshichao *itself* made it the irreplaceable operating manual (the answer remaining: the receiving-and-following architecture). On this reading, the flourishing of sixty-odd later commentaries is not merely a transmission history; it is *evidence of the architecture's completeness*: precisely because the *Xingshichao*'s architecture was complete enough to operate, later students gathered around it to gloss it stratum upon stratum and argue endlessly over it.

### IV.4 The Thinness of Attention: A Gap Still to Be Filled

One scholarly gap should be stated honestly. There is, to date, no English-language monograph that treats the *Sifenlü xingshichao* as an *architectural, authoritative compendium in its own right*. Anglophone attention clusters on the *jietǐ* concept (Newhall), on biography and visionary records (the Shinohara line), and on the institutional history of Dharmaguptaka ordination (Heirman); systematic treatment of the compendium's *architecture* and the *generation of its authority* survives mainly in Chinese (Wang Jianguang) and Japanese (the line of Satō Tatsugen, 1986). Taking the receiving-and-following two-precepts structure as its spine, this paper is an attempt to supply that thin place — to read the *Xingshichao* as a *building*, rather than merely as a container for a concept or a stretch of transmission-history.

## V. Disciplinary Corrections

A methodological hallmark of this project is to mark, in each paper, the red lines of scope — to forestall over-extension and mis-attribution. This paper touches a byline stratification, a textual emendation, and a division of labor with its neighbors; the traps are unusually many, so each must be corrected in turn.

**❌ The mis-attribution of the byline (the most important).** The gravest hazard is to quote the systematized classificatory formulae — the "three-school judgment," the "five senses of partial accord," the "Perfect-Teaching seed" — directly as Daoxuan's autograph *Xingshichao*. In fact, *Taishō* vol. 40, No. 1804, the *Xingshichao*, is Daoxuan's own composition (its preface signed "composed by the śramaṇa Daoxuan of Chongyi Monastery in the capital"), while No. 1805, the *Sifenlü xingshichao zizhiji*, is the work of Yuanzhao of the Song (its preface signed "composed by the śramaṇa Yuanzhao of Yuhang under the Great Song") — different authors, different eras. The systematic statement of those classificatory formulae is *not* found in Daoxuan's autograph *Xingshichao*; it is found in Yuanzhao's *Zizhiji*, transmitting Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary* (the *Yeshu* / *Jiemoshu*); and Yuanzhao's text self-attributes ("following the three schools of the *Karman-Commentary*," and so on). Therefore any citation of the classificatory formulae must carry the byline "Yuanzhao's *Zizhiji*, transmitting Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary*"; only the architecture and the receiving–following spine may be quoted directly as Daoxuan's *Xingshichao*. A further note: Daoxuan's *Commentary on the Precept-Text* (the *Jieshu* / *Han-zhu jieben shu*) and his *Karman-Commentary* (the *Yeshu*) survive in full as *Manji*-canon texts X0714 (fused with Yuanzhao's *Xingzong ji*) and X0728 (fused with Yuanzhao's *Jiyuan ji*) respectively — *not* as *Taishō* "T" numbers; older references that mis-labeled T1805 as Daoxuan's *Han-zhu jieben shu* are in error, and that mis-assignment (which a previous paper, P11, also propagated) is corrected here and not to be repeated.[^19]

**❌ The wording of the five senses of partial accord.** The fifth of the five senses reads *chénjìng fēi gēn liǎo* (塵境非根了, "sense-objects are cognized by *consciousness*, not by the faculty alone"), *not* *chénjìng fēi gēn jìng* (塵境非根境). The full order and text of the five is: addressing one another as children of the Buddha; dedicating merit to beings to accomplish the Buddha-path; Dravya's wearying of the adept's fruit; relinquishing goods with a state of mind that is not the graspingly calculating kind; and sense-objects cognized by consciousness, not by the faculty alone (Yuanzhao's *Zizhiji*, transmitting Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary*, T40, No. 1805, p. 0220b16–18). This one-character difference bears on the doctrine and must not be treated carelessly.

**⚠️ The firewall on the ontology of the substance.** Wherever this paper cites the phrases "received and retained in the mind," "a seed perfumed into the store-consciousness," or "the substance received at the ordination platform," it does so *only* as the structural placement of receiving, or as the label-content of the Perfect-Teaching school in the classification — never to reopen the three-school ontological dispute (material, neither-form-nor-mind, seed). That ontological debate is P16's scene; here it is signaled only by a "(cf. P16)" pointer. The finer Yogācāra mechanics — the six characteristics of seeds, the perfuming of perfumer and perfumed, the afflicted mind (*manas*), the transformation of the basis — belong to P18, and are strictly not dug into here. Where the term "non-acting" (*avijñapti*, 無作) appears in the receiving-following passages, it is taken only as a term for the *persistence* of the substance-received and the following-conduct, and not as a thread into seed-theory.

**⚠️ No verdict on the root vinaya's merit.** The root code, the *Sifenlü*, and the *Xingshichao* are two things of different genre — the former a code laid down as occasions arose, the latter an operable manual. In calling the receiving-following division Daoxuan's contribution, the paper means that this *organizing architecture* was established at Daoxuan's compilation layer; it does *not* mean that the root code is "primitive" or "deficient." The root code's faithful record has its own irreplaceable value; a difference of genre is not a judgment of worth.

**⚠️ No ranking of the three houses.** Of the three Tang Vinaya houses — Nanshan (Daoxuan), Xiangbu (Fali), Dongta (Huaisu) — this paper relates only, in a descriptive register, the historical *survival and continuity* of the Nanshan lineage; it passes no judgment on the relative merit of the three, and does not itself endorse Daoxuan's classificatory conclusion about his own school. Historical survival is one thing; doctrinal superiority is another; the paper relates only the former.

## VI. Conclusion: Building the Residence — A Thirteen-Century Dwelling

**The argument restated.** The *Sifenlü xingshichao* became the canonical compendium of East Asian vinaya study for thirteen centuries not because it is a polished summary but because it is a transformation of genre: Daoxuan, with the receiving-and-following precepts as his spine, rebuilt a code laid down as occasions arose into an operable manual. Receiving founds the root; following completes the conduct; the two sustain each other. And the standing and endurance of that architecture rested in turn on Daoxuan's self-declared three-fascicle design, on his "cut-the-verbose, supply-the-deficient" editorial method, and on the doctrinal classification transmitted from his *Karman-Commentary* by Yuanzhao (the three-school judgment and the five senses of partial accord, placing the *Sifenlü* as Nominalist home text, annexing the Realists below and converging on the Perfect Vehicle above). The three-school ontology of the substance is, in this building, one structurally placed, load-bearing column — not the focus on which its millennium of standing depends.

**Why could this architecture become a canonical standard?** Three converging proofs close the case. *First, a self-declared supersession*: Daoxuan's preface charges that earlier writers had left "not one book in a hundred" usable for actual practice, and so by "cutting the verbose and supplying the deficient" he set himself up as an unprecedented operating manual — and a book that consciously declares itself a supersession of its predecessors, and is then in fact venerated by later generations, is at the threshold of becoming a standard. *Second, a competing stratigraphy of commentary*: after the Song, rival Nanshan sub-lineages formed around the *Xingshichao* — Yuanzhao's "Zichi" line against Yunkan's "Huizheng" line — and what later students contested was *how to read* the *Xingshichao*, never *whether to use* it; when a book becomes the object everyone must confront, and over which one can only differ in interpretation, it has already become a standard. *Third, a complete transmission and downstream*: Daoxuan's "three great works" (the *Xingshichao*, the *Jieshu*, the *Yeshu*) and Yuanzhao's matched "three records" (the *Zizhiji*, the *Xingzong ji*, the *Jiyuan ji*) survive in full, and were carried to Japan by Jianzhen (Ganjin) to found a school there; of the three Tang Vinaya houses, only Nanshan endured a millennium unbroken. Taken together, the three proofs make the standard's formation well-attested.

**What it teaches about disciplined practice.** Daoxuan's simile of building a residence still gives concrete guidance today: ordination is like first raising the perimeter wall — a once-and-for-all founding; but what actually makes a person into a keeper of precepts is the lifelong, place-by-place building-out within the wall. A founding with no building is a courtyard of empty vows; Daoxuan even says that to receive and not follow turns the receiving into a deceit about the precepts, "worse than not receiving at all." This severe line reminds every recipient that the dignity of receiving lies not in the moment of receiving but in a whole life of following thereafter; that the point of an architecture is not the raising of the frame but the *sustainable dwelling* within it. That a compendium can become a millennium's canonical standard, and that a person's disciplined life can become a lifelong dwelling, run on one and the same principle — both turn on "receiving and following sustaining each other; only then is anything reached."

**An open question.** Throughout, this paper has taken the precept-substance only on its structural side, as the "substance-received" that ordination founds, and has explicitly left to a later paper the *mechanics* of its ontology — above all, how the Perfect-Teaching school's "seed perfumed into the store-consciousness" could actually be constituted within the Yogācāra frame of seeds, perfuming, and the transformation of the basis. How can a substance-received be "apprehended once and borne for a lifetime"? What is the mechanism of its persistence? That is the question P18 will take up and dig into. This paper raises the architecture; the next probes the mechanism. The perimeter wall now stands all around — let us see how the building goes up within it.

The work is open-access; it is engaged here through close paraphrase of its load-bearing finding (that Daoxuan *reads* the Dharmagupta vinaya as-if partially Mahāyāna, a deliberate "strong reading"). **Cross-paper disclosure:** Newhall was the principal English interlocutor on the *jietǐ* doctrine in SILA-P16; he is re-anchored here on the classification-and-architecture axis — engaging the "partial accord" thesis, not the three-school *jietǐ* framing of P16. The re-use is topic-driven and justified: P17 is literally about Daoxuan's commentaries, Newhall's own published subject, and no equal English substitute exists.

[^17]: Ann Heirman, *Rules for Nuns according to the Dharmaguptakavinaya*, 3 vols. (Delhi: Motilal Banarsidass, 2002). In copyright; engaged through paraphrase and page-reference citation. Individual article venues to be verified before citing.

[^18]: Wang Jianguang (王建光), *Zhongguo lüzong tongshi* (中國律宗通史, *A Comprehensive History of the Chinese Vinaya School*) (Nanjing: Phoenix Publishing House, 2008). In copyright; engaged through paraphrase. (Title verified; not *Zhongguo lüzong sixiang yanjiu*.)

[^19]: Philological note on the byline. T1805 is Yuanzhao's *Zizhiji* (Song), not Daoxuan's autograph and not his *Han-zhu jieben shu*. Daoxuan's *Jieshu* (*Han-zhu jieben shu*, *Commentary on the Precept-Text*) survives as X0714 (with Yuanzhao's *Xingzong ji*), and his *Yeshu* (*Jiemoshu*, *Karman-Commentary*) as X0728 (with Yuanzhao's *Jiyuan ji*) — both in the *Manji* (Xuzangjing) canon, the ultimate locus of the three-school judgment and the five senses of partial accord. The framework is Daoxuan's; the extant witness is Yuanzhao's transmission. An earlier paper in this series (P11) mis-attributed *Zizhiji* content to Daoxuan's *Han-zhu jieben shu*; corrected here and not propagated.

[^20]: The southern parallel — Buddhaghosa's *Samantapāsādikā* (whose partial Chinese parallel is the *Shanjianlü piposha*, T1462) — is engaged here through paraphrase only, never quoted, both for corpus-licensing reasons and because it serves only to attest that the canon-to-digest move is pan-Buddhist. Daoxuan's signature is the *particular* organizing principle (receiving-and-following plus classification-and-aim). For Japanese-language groundwork on the compendium and the Vinaya school, see Satō Tatsugen (佐藤達玄), *Chūgoku bukkyō ni okeru kairitsu no kenkyū* (*Studies on the Vinaya in Chinese Buddhism*; Tokyo: Mokujisha, 1986); engaged through paraphrase.

## Series Cross-References

### Within Volume III SĪLA, preceding

- **SILA-P15** *Receiving as Gate* — P15 treats the *bodhisattva-precept* receiving-architecture (the three grades of receiving, self-ordination, the auspicious sign, Mahāyāna self-ordination); the present paper treats the *Dharmaguptaka monastic* ordination (the chapter on assembling the conditions of ordination, the five conditions completing reception, the formal saṅgha act) only as a structural placement inside the compendium. Different receiving registers — not to be conflated; P17 does not import P15's self-ordination material.

Newhall moved from P16's *jietǐ*-doctrine slot to P17's classification-and-architecture slot (explicit cross-reference, footnote 16). The T1805 byline erratum lock carries forward here.
- **SILA-P09 / P10 / P11** (the Part III vinaya papers) — P10 / P11 cited Daoxuan as an indigenous-reception anchor; P17 is the dedicated treatment of his compendium. (P11's T1805 byline mis-attribution is corrected in this paper.)
