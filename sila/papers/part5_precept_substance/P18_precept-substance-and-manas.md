## Abstract

A Western reader who follows Buddhist ethics even a little way meets a verdict that can sound perverse. Keeping the precepts is good — and yet the mature scholastic tradition insists that a precept *kept* is still a "tainted" good (有漏, *sāsrava*): meritorious, productive of fortunate rebirth, but not, by itself, liberating. Why? And if even a well-kept precept does not free us, what, in the end, is the precept *for*? This paper answers those two questions through the third of the three classical Chinese theories of the precept-substance (戒體, *jietǐ*) — the "seed" theory of Daoxuan's (596–667) Nanshan Vinaya school in its Round-Teaching (圓教) classification, which reads the substance founded at ordination as **a wholesome seed perfumed into the store-consciousness** (阿賴耶識, *ālaya*): "the formal act of receiving perfumes the deluded mind and forms a wholesome seed within the underlying store-consciousness; this is the precept-substance." But the paper's spine is an honesty problem it refuses to hide. The Round-Teaching precept-substance corpus — Daoxuan's *Xingshichao* (T1804), Yuanzhao's *Zichiji* (T1805), and the autograph *Yeshu* (X0728) — contains *zero* occurrences of "manas" (末那) and *zero* of "transformation-of-the-basis" (轉依, *āśraya-parāvṛtti*); the store-consciousness it invokes is the *Awakening-of-Faith*–lineage "true-and-deluded-combined consciousness" (真妄和合識), not Xuanzang's pure-deluded ālaya. The full Yogācāra deep-dive offered here — the six meanings of a seed, perfuming, manas, the transformation of the basis — is therefore an *openly constructed synthesis*: a reading of the Round-Teaching seed-fragment *through* the lens of mature Xuanzang Yogācāra, which this paper owns at every step and never passes off as Daoxuan's or Yuanzhao's own doctrine. Read in that frame, the precept-seed acquires a determinate shape (newly-perfumed, not innate; storable only in the eighth consciousness; perfumed by the wholesome volition of receiving; continuous yet modifiable), a deepest obstruction (the manas's standing self-grasping, which the *Cheng weishi lun* says renders even a wholesome dharma tainted — it names *giving* as exactly such a case), and a final orientation (the transformation of the basis, in which the manas's content flips from self to no-self; precept-keeping is the long perfuming that bends the seed *toward* that flip, not the flip itself, and conscience is its safest bridge). Precept-substance and manas: the deepest substance meets the deepest obstruction. With this, Part V closes by turning the whole question of the precept-substance toward transformation.

**Keywords:** precept-substance (*jietǐ*); manas (the afflicted mind); store-consciousness (*ālaya*); wholesome seed (*bīja*); perfuming (*vāsanā*); transformation-of-the-basis (*āśraya-parāvṛtti*); the Round-Teaching school; the Nanshan Vinaya school; tainted good (*sāsrava*); conscience and shame; constructed synthesis; the true-and-deluded-combined consciousness

## 摘要

## I. The Problem: What, Exactly, Is Received at Ordination

When a person is ordained, what is received?

The question is not rhetorical. The East Asian Vinaya tradition has a technical name for the *thing* that is gained at ordination, guarded in keeping, and lost in transgression: the precept-substance (戒體, *jietǐ*). It is conceived as a real bearer — at the moment of ordination something genuinely changes in body-and-mind and leaves a residue; that residue works silently while the recipient keeps the precepts, so that one "naturally turns away from fault," is damaged when a precept is broken, and departs at renunciation of the vows or at death. So the puzzle is: what is this much-discussed but invisible, intangible *thing* in its substance? Where is it stored? Can it grow or shrink? And where, finally, is it bound?

Chinese Buddhist thought gave three answers, and the sixteenth paper of this series laid out that trilemma in full: the Sarvāstivāda judged the precept-substance to be *form* (色法) — a kind of subtle, invisible matter; one line within the Satyasiddhi tradition judged it to be *neither form nor mind* (非色非心) — a special existent that is neither material nor mental; and Daoxuan's Nanshan school, in its Round-Teaching classification, judged it to be a *seed perfumed into the store-consciousness* — a wholesome seed (*bīja*) within the ālaya. This paper does not re-litigate the first two ontologies (that is P16's scene); it takes only the third — the seed school — and gives it the full ontological excavation P16 deferred.

The reason for singling out this one school is that it connects the question of the precept-substance to the most finely-worked machinery in all of Mahāyāna mind-theory. Once one says the precept-substance is "a wholesome seed in the store-consciousness," then every question Yogācāra had already worked out in minute detail — how a seed arises, abides, is perfumed into being, waxes and wanes, and is finally transformed — becomes an extension of the question of the precept-substance. And following that line into its depth, one meets a question that Western readers and Chinese readers ask alike, and that is too often passed over:

If the precept-substance is a wholesome seed, and the person keeping the precept still has every manner of self-grasping and affliction, is that "wholesome" seed *actually pure*? In other words: does keeping the precepts free anyone from self-grasping at the root, or not?

Yogācāra has a cold and exact answer to this, and the hinge of that answer is a consciousness the Round-Teaching precept-substance corpus *never once mentions*: the manas (末那), the seventh consciousness — the afflicted mind that grasps, perpetually and at a subtle depth, at a "self." The *Cheng weishi lun* says plainly that *because* the manas perpetually generates the grasping at a self, even wholesome dharmas are tainted (有漏); the text, in the very same passage, names *giving* (施) as such a case.[^1][^2] Precepts are wholesome dharmas; so is giving. So the precept-substance, this wholesome seed, while the manas is still grasping at a self, can only be a *tainted good* — a cause of fortunate human and heavenly fruit, not a cause of liberation. The "bondage of consciousness" is not yet undone — not until the transformation of the basis.

This is why the two terms in the title stand together. The **precept-substance** is the deepest substance of the thing received at ordination; the **manas** is the deepest obstruction that that substance meets. The argument to follow will show that the Round-Teaching seed-substance, read through the Yogācāra machinery, orients toward nothing other than the *transformation of the basis* (轉依, *āśraya-parāvṛtti*) — and that precept-keeping is the long perfuming that bends the precept-seed toward that turning, but is *not* the turning itself.

Before any of that can be argued, one thing must be set down honestly, for it is the methodological backbone of the entire paper and cannot be left vague.

## II. Textual Foundations: The Round-Teaching Seed-Substance, and the Yogācāra Machinery This Paper Borrows

### II.1 The Vinaya Line: The Precept-Substance as "the Deluded Mind Perfumed by the Formal Act of Receiving, Forming a Wholesome Seed in the Store-Consciousness"

The Round-Teaching school's definition of the precept-substance in its final sense is transmitted in Yuanzhao's *Zichiji*, conveying Daoxuan's *Yeshu* (the Karman-Commentary) — whose own autograph survives as *Manji*-canon X41, No. 0728 (the *Jiyuanji*).[^3] The definitional sentence reads:

> 故《業疏》云：智知境緣，本是心作；不妄緣境，但唯一識；隨緣轉變，有彼有此；欲了妄情，須知妄業；故作法受還熏妄心，於本藏識，成善種子，此戒體也。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Thus the *Karman-Commentary* says: wisdom knows that objects-and-conditions are originally mind-made; not falsely taking objects as objects, there is only the one consciousness; following conditions it transforms, and there is 'this' and 'that'; wishing to comprehend deluded feeling, one must know deluded karma; therefore the formal act of receiving turns back and perfumes the deluded mind, and within the underlying store-consciousness forms a wholesome seed — this is the precept-substance.")*[^4]

The sense is this: the formal act of receiving (作法受) turns back to perfume the deluded mind and accomplishes, within the underlying store-consciousness, a single wholesome seed — and that seed *is* the precept-substance. The Round-Teaching verdict within the three-school judgment says as much directly:

> 三者圓教宗。即用涅槃開會之意，決了權乘同歸實道。故考受體，乃是識藏熏種；隨行即同三聚圓修，微縱妄心，即成業行。此名終窮大乘教也。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Third, the Round-Teaching school. It uses the *Nirvāṇa*-Sūtra's sense of disclosing-and-gathering to determine that the provisional vehicles all return to the real path. Thus, examining the substance-received, it is a seed perfumed into the store-consciousness; the following-conduct is the same as the perfect cultivation of the three comprehensive precepts, and the least indulgence of the deluded mind constitutes karmic conduct. This is named the teaching that exhausts the Greater Vehicle.")*[^5]

The phrase "examining the substance-received, it is a seed perfumed into the store-consciousness" fixes the precept-substance founded at ordination as a seed perfumed into the store-consciousness. Yuanzhao further pictures the seed's relation to the store-consciousness through the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s simile of the ocean and its waves:

> 即明梨耶隨緣變造，含藏種子……如《楞伽》中，識海識浪；浪從海起，還復海中；浪無別浪，還即海水；能造所發，全體是識，更無別法。
> *(Translator's rendering: "This clarifies that the ālaya, transforming according to conditions, holds and stores the seeds … as in the *Laṅkāvatāra*: the consciousness-ocean and the consciousness-waves; the waves rise from the ocean and return into the ocean; there is no wave apart from the wave — it is just ocean-water; the constructing and the constructed are wholly consciousness, and there is no separate dharma.")*[^6]

The wholesome-root seed is held by the store-consciousness, "ceaseless as the mind"; the constructing waves and the held seeds are wholly consciousness, with no separate dharma besides. With this the substance of the precept-substance — a wholesome seed held by the store-consciousness — is established.

One byline matter must be cleared up at once, since it is easily misread. The sentences quoted above are found in *Taishō* vol. 40, No. 1805, and the author of that work, the *Sifenlü xingshichao zizhiji*, is Yuanzhao of Lingzhi (1048–1116) of the Song — *not* Daoxuan. The whole vocabulary of the Round-Teaching seed (a seed perfumed into the store-consciousness; the deluded mind perfumed by the formal act of receiving into a wholesome seed; the true-and-deluded-combined consciousness) comes from Yuanzhao's stratum *transmitting* Daoxuan's *Yeshu* (the Karman-Commentary). When cited, it must be labeled "Yuanzhao's *Zichiji*, transmitting Daoxuan's *Karman-Commentary*," or "Daoxuan's *Yeshu* (X0728 autograph)" — never as the autograph wording of Daoxuan's *Xingshichao* (T1804). For Daoxuan's *Xingshichao* itself contains not one occurrence of "manas," "ālaya," "store-consciousness," or "transformation-of-the-basis"; its seven instances of "seed" all denote *plant-seeds* (used to figure the precept-clauses), and the entire seed-substance vocabulary sits at the *Yeshu* layer.[^7]

### II.2 The Backbone of the Whole Paper: Zero Manas, Zero Transformation-of-the-Basis — and a *True-and-Deluded-Combined* Ālaya

Now the textual fact that forms the backbone of the entire paper.

A full-text search of the Round-Teaching precept-substance corpus (T1804, T1805, X0728) returns *zero* occurrences of "manas" and *zero* of "transformation-of-the-basis." Conversely, a search of Xuanzang's *Cheng weishi lun* corpus (T1585, T1586, T1594) returns *zero* occurrences of "precept-substance" — the *Cheng weishi lun* never discusses the precept-substance at all. This means: **the link between precept-substance and manas, and the link between precept-substance and the transformation of the basis, do not exist in any extant text; they are this paper's constructed synthesis, achieved by reading Xuanzang Yogācāra into the Round-Teaching seed-theory — and they are not the native doctrine of either textual side.**

A further stratum: the "store-consciousness" on which the Round-Teaching relies is *not* Xuanzang's pure-deluded consciousness. Yuanzhao's *Jiyuanji* autograph states its basis outright:

> 今依《楞伽》《起信》《唯識》《攝論》以明藏識……與生滅和合，非一非異，名阿賴耶，即真妄和合識……轉趣諸根為第七，名為傳送，亦名染汙……
> *(Translator's rendering: "Here, relying on the *Laṅkāvatāra*, the *Awakening of Faith*, the Yogācāra [treatises], and the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*, I clarify the store-consciousness … combined with arising-and-ceasing, neither one nor different, it is named the ālaya — that is, the true-and-deluded-combined consciousness … turning toward the faculties, it is the seventh, named the conveyer, also named the defiled …")*[^8]

The phrase "true-and-deluded-combined consciousness" is the consciousness-conception of the *Awakening of Faith* lineage; it sets the *Laṅkāvatāra*, the *Awakening of Faith*, the Yogācāra treatises, and the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* side by side and takes "true-and-deluded-combined" as the nature of the ālaya — *not* the *Cheng weishi lun*'s maturation-consciousness (異熟識), perfumed by defiled-class dharmas and deluded in nature. Most telling: this text names the seventh consciousness by its *functional office* ("conveyer, defiled," 傳送/染汙), not by Xuanzang's transliteration "manas" — a trace of the pre-Xuanzang, Paramārtha-and-Dilun-period eight-consciousness scheme.

Therefore the frame of the whole paper must be laid as follows, and must be owned explicitly at every point where manas or the transformation of the basis is applied:

**This paper takes mature Xuanzang Yogācāra (the six meanings of a seed, perfuming and its perfumer-and-perfumed, the manas, the transformation of the basis) as its analytic lens, and reads it *into* — constructively interprets onto — the Round-Teaching seed-substance; this synthesis is the paper's own operation, not a doctrine native to Daoxuan or Yuanzhao.** Whatever is text-self-attesting — the seed-substance definition (cited in II.1), the three-school judgment, the claim that the manas renders wholesome dharmas tainted (cited in §I), the definition of the transformation of the basis (below) — this paper states directly. Whatever is the paper's application of the Yogācāra machinery to the Round-Teaching seed-theory — the relation of the precept-substance to the manas, of the precept-substance to the transformation of the basis, conscience as the bridge, precept-keeping as not-subduing the manas — this paper marks throughout with "this paper argues" or "read through the Yogācāra frame." That asymmetry is the integrity backbone of the whole paper. **This paper never claims "Daoxuan founded the precept-substance on the *Cheng weishi lun*."**

### II.3 The Yogācāra Machinery This Paper Borrows: Seed, Six Meanings, Newly-Perfumed, the Four Marks of Perfuming

On that premise, the Yogācāra machinery the paper takes as its lens must first be set out in its own sense.

First, a seed, as a seed, is not a particle of real substance but a *stored function that, met by conditions, can manifest*. The *Cheng weishi lun* defines it:

> 此中何法名為種子？謂本識中親生自果，功能差別。
> *(Translator's rendering: "What here is named a seed? It is the differentiated capacity, within the root consciousness, that directly produces its own fruit.")*[^9]

"Differentiated capacity" (功能差別) is the key: a seed is the latent capacity, in the root consciousness, that can directly produce its own fruit. To understand the precept-substance *as* a wholesome seed is precisely this — it is a kind of differentiated capacity, accomplished at ordination, stored by the store-consciousness, that on meeting conditions (that is, facing a situation in which a precept might be broken) can manifest the power of "naturally guarding against fault."

Second, a seed has six meanings, lacking any one of which it would not be a seed:

> 然種子義，略有六種。一、剎那滅……二、果俱有……三、恒隨轉……四、性決定……五、待眾緣……六、引自果。
> *(Translator's rendering: "The meaning of a seed has, in brief, six kinds: first, momentary cessation; second, co-existence with its fruit; third, continuous flow; fourth, determinacy of nature; fifth, dependence on the assembly of conditions; sixth, the producing of its own fruit.")*[^10]

(One philological note: the third meaning reads "continuous flow" (恒隨轉) — a long, single-class continuity up to the final stage — *not* "continuous bondage," two phrasings easily confused but distinct in sense.)

Third, on the *source* of seeds, the *Cheng weishi lun* (fascicle two) records the views of the innate-seed school, the newly-perfumed school, and Dharmapāla's reconciliation of the two. Dharmapāla divides seeds into "seeds abiding in their own nature" (本性住種, innate) and "seeds formed by habituation" (習所成種, newly-perfumed and arisen):

> 二者始起，謂無始來，數數現行熏習而有……此即名為習所成種。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Second, the arisen: that is, from beginningless time, what comes to be through repeated active perfuming … this is named the seeds formed by habituation.")*[^11]

Fourth, how does perfuming form a seed? The perfumer and the perfumable each have four marks; and what is capable of *being perfumed* (the perfumable) is the eighth, maturation consciousness alone:

> 何等名為所熏四義？一、堅住性……二、無記性……三、可熏性……四、與能熏共和合性……唯異熟識具此四義，可是所熏，非心所等。
> *(Translator's rendering: "What are the four marks of the perfumable? First, stability; second, neutrality; third, perfumability; fourth, the capacity to combine with the perfumer … only the maturation consciousness possesses these four marks and can be the perfumable — not the mental factors and so on.")*[^12]

The causal mechanism of perfuming the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* clarifies through the simile of sesame perfumed with flowers — perfumer and perfumable arise together and cease together, cause and effect simultaneous, not a transfer in sequence:

> 如苣蕂中有花薰習，苣蕂與花俱生俱滅，是諸苣蕂帶能生彼香因而生……阿賴耶識薰習道理，當知亦爾。
> *(Translator's rendering: "As sesame is perfumed by flowers — the sesame and the flowers arising together and ceasing together, so that the sesame comes to bear the cause that can produce that fragrance … the principle of the perfuming of the ālaya should be understood likewise.")*[^13]

These four pieces of machinery — the functional definition of a seed, the six meanings, the newly-perfumed-and-arisen, and the four marks of perfuming — are the whole of the toolkit by which this paper will read out the determinate shape of the precept-seed. The next section is that reading-in.

## III. The Core Argument: The Seed's Shape, Its Obstruction, and Its Telos

This chapter is the paper's original synthesis. It bears repeating: everything below that applies the Yogācāra machinery to the Round-Teaching seed-theory is this paper's *constructed* reading, not language native to the Round-Teaching corpus.

### III.1 The Determinate Shape of the Precept-Seed

Read through the Yogācāra frame, the Round-Teaching's "deluded mind perfumed by the formal act of receiving, forming a wholesome seed" gives the precept-seed four determinate features.

**First, it falls on the "newly-perfumed / arisen" side, not the "innate" side.** The Round-Teaching states outright that the precept-substance is formed "by the perfuming of the formal act of receiving" — it is perfumed into being at the moment of ordination, by the perfuming of the formal act; it is not beginningless, innate, and ready-made in everyone. In Dharmapāla's bifurcation, the precept-seed belongs to the "seeds formed by habituation" (what comes to be through repeated active perfuming), not to the "seeds abiding in their own nature." This yields a contrast worth marking: Daoxuan's Round-Teaching "forming a seed by the formal act" is a *newly-perfumed* route, distinguishable from the "everyone innately possesses Buddha-nature, the precept-substance is innate" kind of formulation (the innate-seed route). The Round-Teaching precept-substance is not ready-made; it is *received out*, *perfumed out*.

**Second, only the eighth consciousness is capable of holding it.** By the four marks of the perfumable, only the maturation consciousness (the eighth) possesses stability, neutrality, perfumability, and the capacity to combine with the perfumer — it "can be the perfumable, not the mental factors and so on." Read through this teaching, the precept-seed, if it is to be *held*, can be held by the eighth consciousness alone. And this is precisely the textual joint behind the title's pairing of "precept-substance and manas": *the manas cannot be the perfumable*. The seventh, the manas, though it cogitates ever and deliberatively, does not possess the four marks of the perfumable (it is *obscured-neutral* and perpetually conjoined with the four afflictions, not the stable, pure-neutral perfumable body); neither the perfuming of the wholesome precept-seed nor its holding belongs to the manas. What the precept-substance ultimately rests on, at the depth, is the eighth consciousness; and what the eighth consciousness is, at the depth, accompanied by, is exactly the manas that grasps it as "self." The deepest home of the precept-substance and its deepest obstruction inhabit one and the same place.

**Third, it is perfumed into being by the strong wholesome volition of receiving.** One of the four marks of the perfumer is "having a dominant function"; the strong, resolute, lifelong wholesome *volition* (思, *cetanā*) of the ordination moment is exactly such a dominant-function perfumer (belonging to the seven evolving consciousnesses). The eighth, maturation consciousness is itself "feeble in force": it receives perfuming but cannot perfume. So a closed loop forms: the seven evolving consciousnesses (including the strong wholesome volition of receiving) perfume; the eighth is perfumed and holds the seed. The *forming* of the precept-substance is the strong wholesome volition perfuming the eighth and leaving a seed; the *holding* of the precept-substance is the eighth spontaneously bearing that seed unbroken.

**Fourth, it "flows continuously" and "can increase or decrease," and so can be augmented or diminished by keeping and transgressing.** Among the six meanings of a seed is "continuous flow" (long continuity); among the four marks of the perfumer is "increase-and-decrease" (it can grow or shrink, gathering habituated energies). Read along this line, keeping and breaking the precept are the increase and the diminishment of this seed: keeping is the repeated manifestation of homogeneous wholesome volition, repeatedly augmenting the perfuming, so the seed's force grows day by day; breaking is its converse, a diminishment. Here the levels of evidence must be carefully marked. What the corpus gives *directly* is the framework "the seed can increase or decrease," together with one concrete instance of *diminishing* a seed — Yuanzhao cites the *Saṃyukta-abhidharma-hṛdaya*: Devadatta, committing a deed of immediate retribution, "destroyed the wholesome seed" and fell into the Avīci hell.[^14] The "keeping augments the perfuming" side accords directly with the framework; the "breaking diminishes the perfuming" side is this paper's reasonable inference *within* that framework, drawn in reverse from the Devadatta case of destroying a wholesome seed — not a word-for-word datum. (The Round-Teaching has its own separate set of *ten* meanings of a seed — discussed below — among which "spontaneous flourishing" and "successive continuity" likewise echo this reading of increase-and-diminishment.)

### III.2 The Load-Bearing Finding: Why a Kept Precept Is Still Tainted

With the shape of the precept-seed established, the genuinely load-bearing question follows: is this wholesome seed *pure*?

Yogācāra's answer is cold: no. So long as the manas remains, it is not pure. The paper's opening already cited the *Cheng weishi lun*'s grounding statement — "because there is the manas perpetually generating the grasping at a self, it comes about that wholesome dharmas are tainted." The nature of the manas (the seventh consciousness) is defined in the *Thirty Verses*:

> 次第二能變，是識名末那，依彼轉緣彼，思量為性相。四煩惱常俱，謂我癡、我見、并我慢、我愛……阿羅漢、滅定、出世道無有。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Next, the second transformer: this consciousness is named manas; depending on that [the eighth] and taking that as its object, cogitation is its nature and mark. Four afflictions are always conjoined with it — self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, and self-love … in the arhat, in the cessation-attainment, and in the supramundane path, it does not obtain.")*[^15]

The manas cogitates ever and deliberatively, perpetually conjoined with the four afflictions of self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, and self-love; at the floor of consciousness it grasps, silently and without interruption, at a "self." For just this reason, every wholesome dharma of the ordinary being — giving included, precept-keeping included — carries the floor-level taint of "*I* am doing good," "*I* am the keeper of precepts," and so "it comes about that wholesome dharmas are tainted." The verse on the afflicted mind says it more bluntly still:

> 如是染污意，是識之所依，此意未滅時，識縛終不脫。
> *(Translator's rendering: "Such is the afflicted mind, the support of consciousness; while this mind is not yet extinguished, the bondage of consciousness is never undone.")*[^16]

Here a textual boundary must be marked honestly. What the *Cheng weishi lun* says in this passage is "wholesome dharmas" and "giving"; it does *not* say "precept-substance" — "precept-substance" is this paper's word, not the *Cheng weishi lun*'s. The paper can carry the verdict over to the precept-substance only on two grounds: first, the universality of wholesome dharmas — a precept is one branch of the wholesome (precept ⊂ wholesome dharmas), so whatever holds of the tainted-ness of wholesome dharmas holds of the precept too; second, the *Cheng weishi lun* in the same passage explicitly names *giving* as its example, and giving and precept-keeping are paired exemplars of Mahāyāna wholesome practice, of comparable standing. So this paper argues: the precept-substance, this wholesome seed, while the manas still grasps at a self, is a tainted good — a cause of fortunate human and heavenly fruit, not a cause that tends toward liberation. It is real, it is potent, it can guard against fault — but it has not yet touched the manas grasping at "I" at the floor of the mind.

And so the pivot of the paper's thesis is set: **what precept-keeping must ultimately counter is exactly the manas's grasping at a self; yet precept-keeping is not, in itself, the dissolution of that grasping.** The precept-substance is a real wholesome seed — but a wholesome seed still bound by the bondage of consciousness.

### III.3 The Telos: Transformation-of-the-Basis, and the Real Place of Precept-Keeping Within It

When, then, is the bondage of consciousness undone? At the transformation of the basis.

The ultimate orientation of the precept-seed, this paper argues, is the transformation of the basis (轉依, *āśraya-parāvṛtti*). The *Cheng weishi lun* defines it:

> 轉謂二分，轉捨、轉得……由轉煩惱得大涅槃，轉所知障證無上覺。
> *(Translator's rendering: "'Transformation' means two aspects, transforming-away and transforming-into … by transforming the afflictions one attains great nirvāṇa; by transforming the cognitive obstruction one realizes unsurpassed awakening.")*[^17]

(One emendation: the original reads "by transforming the afflictions one attains great nirvāṇa; by transforming the cognitive obstruction one realizes unsurpassed awakening" — there is *no* neat parallel couplet of the form "transforming the affliction-obstruction one attains great nirvāṇa, transforming the cognitive obstruction one attains great bodhi"; that later tidy formulation is a subsequent summary and must not be ascribed to the *Cheng weishi lun*'s wording.) The ultimate of the precept embeds most naturally on the "transforming the afflictions one attains great nirvāṇa" side — for what restraint-discipline counters is exactly the active arising of the afflictions.

At the transformation of the basis, the manas does *not* perish; rather, its content flips:

> 能審思量名末那故。未轉依位，恒、審思量所執我相；已轉依位，亦審思量無我相故。
> *(Translator's rendering: "It is because it deliberatively cogitates that it is named manas. In the pre-transformation stage, it ever and deliberatively cogitates the grasped mark of a self; in the post-transformation stage, it still deliberatively cogitates — but now the mark of no-self.")*[^18]

Before the transformation, what the manas deliberatively cogitates is the "mark of a self"; after the transformation, the manas still deliberatively cogitates, but what it cogitates is now the "mark of no-self." The seventh, the manas, turns into the wisdom of equality (平等性智), beholding self and all beings as equal.

Here surfaces a structural parallel this paper finds powerful (and must mark clearly: this parallel is the paper's construction, not a statement of the texts). Just as the manas, in the transformation, does not perish but has its content flipped (from grasping-self to no-self), the precept-seed likewise does not perish but has its function purified (from tainted good to undefiled); both are "continuity-with-transformation," not "annihilation-and-fresh-start." Read along this line, **precept-keeping is the long, repeated perfuming that bends the precept-seed toward that flip; but precept-keeping is not the flip itself.**

That sentence must be stated with the greatest possible precision, because it is the one most easily overstated. The paper must hold to a boundary Yogācāra sets for itself:

> 諸有漏道，不能伏滅；三乘聖道，有伏滅義。真無我解，違我執故。
> *(Translator's rendering: "No tainted path can subdue [the manas's afflictions]; the noble path of the three vehicles has the power to subdue them — for the genuine insight of no-self runs counter to the grasping at a self.")*[^19]

What can counter and subdue the manas's grasping at a self is the "genuine insight of no-self" (the no-self realization of the three-vehicle noble path), not a tainted path. And precept-keeping — so long as the keeper is still an ordinary being, still possessed of self-grasping — *is* a tainted path. The afflictions of the manas are *innate* (俱生), not severed by the path of seeing; they are cut only when the adamantine samādhi appears. So the paper's thesis must be exact: **precept-keeping cannot subdue the manas; what runs counter to the grasping at a self is the no-self insight alone; precept-keeping is the long preparation made *for* that no-self insight, not that no-self insight itself.**

Is there, then, a reliable bridge between precept-keeping and the transformation of the basis? There is — but one most dangerous mis-join must be avoided.

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* does say that "as the perfuming progressively increases through low, middle, and high grades … the basis is transformed."[^20] At first glance, "unceasing perfuming, progressive increase, culminating in the transformation of the basis" seems to describe precept-keeping exactly. But this is a misreading against which a firewall must be built: the perfuming that increases *there* is the *hearing-perfuming* (聞熏習) — which the treatise expressly judges to be "comprised within the dharma-body and the liberation-body," "not the ālaya," i.e. *supramundane, undefiled* perfuming. In substance it is utterly distinct from the *tainted* wholesome seed formed by an ordinary being's precept-keeping. **Precept-keeping (a tainted good) must on no account be equated with this supramundane, undefiled hearing-perfuming.** Between precept and hearing-perfuming one may draw, at most, a *formal* analogy (both have the structure of gradual perfuming and gradual increase), while marking simultaneously that their substances differ — the one tainted, the other undefiled — and may not be conflated.

What, then, is the safest *substantive* bridge? This paper holds it to be *conscience* — shame-before-oneself and shame-before-others (慚愧). The *Cheng weishi lun*, treating the six transformations of the basis, makes conscience and shame the motive force of the first, the "diminishing-the-force-and-augmenting-the-capacity transformation":

> 一、損力益能轉：謂初、二位，由習勝解及慚、愧故，損本識中染種勢力，益本識內淨種功能……亦名為轉。
> *(Translator's rendering: "First, the diminishing-the-force-and-augmenting-the-capacity transformation: in the first and second stages, by cultivated resolve and by conscience-and-shame, one diminishes the force of the defiled seeds in the root consciousness and augments the capacity of the pure seeds within it … this too is named a transformation.")*[^21]

Conscience and shame "diminish the force of the defiled seeds in the root consciousness and augment the capacity of the pure seeds within it." They are restraint-class wholesome mental factors — precisely the psychology the precept-keeper raises moment by moment in keeping: ashamed before oneself, abashed before others, and so guarding against fault and stopping evil. They are at once the inner motive of precept-keeping and, by the *Cheng weishi lun*'s explicit listing, the motive of the *first stage* of the transformation of the basis. Conscience thus becomes the safest bridge between precept and transformation — it sidesteps the undefiled/tainted conflation of the hearing-perfuming, since conscience already stands on the *tainted-good* side of the restraint discipline, while being identified by the treatise itself as the motive of the "diminishing-the-force-and-augmenting-the-capacity transformation." The precept-keeper's daily conscience is exactly the "diminishing of defiled seeds, augmenting of pure seeds" that bends the precept-seed, inch by inch, toward that far-off turning.

With this, the full span of the question of the precept-substance has been laid open: the precept-substance is a wholesome seed in the store-consciousness (substance); under the manas's grasping at a self it remains tainted (obstruction); its ultimate orientation is the transformation of the basis, while precept-keeping is the long perfuming that bends toward the turning, with conscience as its bridge (orientation). Precept-substance and manas — the deepest substance meeting the deepest obstruction — is exactly the height at which Part V comes to rest.

## IV. Dialogue with Scholarship: Yogācāra Grammar, the Seed-Theory's Genealogy, and the Intra-Tradition Critique

This chapter sets the paper's scholarly position through dialogue with three modern scholars.

### IV.1 The Yogācāra Grammar of Ālaya and Transformation: Dan Lusthaus

One of the most rigorous expositions in English of the Yogācāra machinery of ālaya, seeds, and the transformation of the basis is Dan Lusthaus's *Buddhist Phenomenology* (2002).[^22]

*What is granted.* Lusthaus argues that classical Yogācāra (the *Cheng weishi lun*) is not a metaphysical "idealism" but a phenomenological-epistemological analysis of suffering, no-self, impermanence, and liberation; the ālaya is a karmically-conditioned ground whose seeds bear dispositions, and the transformation of the basis is its soteriological terminus. This exposition legitimizes the paper's basic reading: to understand the precept-substance as "a wholesome seed in the ālaya" is to understand it as a *stored function within a conditioned consciousness, whose terminus is that consciousness's own transformation*. Lusthaus supplies the most reliable Yogācāra grammar.

*Where the paper advances.* Lusthaus keeps strictly within the orthodox Xuanzang frame — the pure-deluded ālaya, the transformation of the basis as the system's *internal* terminus. What this paper advances toward is exactly where that frame does not reach: Daoxuan's Round-Teaching *appropriates* the seed-mechanism but (i) lodges it in an *Awakening-of-Faith*–lineage, true-and-deluded-combined ālaya rather than the orthodox pure-deluded one, and (ii) its own corpus has zero manas and zero transformation-of-the-basis. The two items "manas-as-obstruction" and "transformation-as-terminus" are therefore *absent* on the Round-Teaching side; this paper supplies them through the *Cheng weishi lun*'s machinery, which is just a constructed extension onto the Round-Teaching fragment. Lusthaus gives the orthodox grammar; this paper demonstrates a Vinaya-school dialect that bends it.

### IV.2 The Genealogy of Daoxuan's Seed-Theory: Thomas Newhall

Where Daoxuan's precept-substance seed-theory actually comes from is settled, independently, in a short 2017 article by Thomas Newhall in the *Journal of Indian and Buddhist Studies*.[^23]

*What is granted.* Newhall shows that Daoxuan's store-consciousness seed-theory derives *not* from Xuanzang's newly-translated Yogācāra but from the pre-Xuanzang *Awakening of Faith* and Huiyuan's *Dasheng yizhang* (the *Compendium of the Great Vehicle's Meanings*) — that is, from the Paramārtha-and-Dilun stratum, the consciousness-conception flavored by the *Awakening of Faith*.

*Why this paper credits and advances it.* This independent philological determination accords exactly with the finding this paper reaches from the Round-Teaching corpus itself (that the Round-Teaching ālaya is the true-and-deluded-combined consciousness, not Xuanzang's deluded consciousness), and so can be cited as external corroboration of the paper's core philological claim. Newhall's piece, however, is a four-page genealogical note: it establishes the source-stratum but does not develop its systematic consequence. What this paper advances is precisely that consequence — that since the Round-Teaching ālaya descends from the true-and-deluded-combined line and its corpus has zero manas and zero transformation-of-the-basis, the full "manas-and-transformation" reading must be *reconstructed* as a synthesis, and that synthesis carries a determinate doctrinal cost: a wholesome precept remains tainted under the manas, and the transformation of the basis is the only exit. This paper supplies the deep-dive Newhall's note opens but does not walk.[^24]

### IV.3 The Intra-Tradition Critique of the Round-Teaching Precept-Substance: Shih Chao-Hwei

In Chinese-language scholarship, the most directly relevant interlocutor is Shih Chao-Hwei's (釋昭慧) 2013 article.[^25]

*What is granted.* Shih Chao-Hwei lays out the Nanshan Round-Teaching precept-substance as a self-coherent doctrinal construction *within the living Chinese Vinaya tradition* (set in the context of Hongyi's "exclusively transmitting Nanshan"), giving a searching account of why the native tradition theorized the precept-substance as it did.

*Where the paper advances.* Shih Chao-Hwei, from the standpoint of dependent origination (緣起), relativizes the Round-Teaching precept-substance with a critique (a *normative*, intra-tradition theological move: has the precept-substance been over-substantialized?). This paper's direction differs — it is *descriptive and philological*, not normative: it does not adjudicate whether the Round-Teaching precept-substance is doctrinally right or wrong, but only shows *how* its ālaya-seed reading is a constructed synthesis (a corpus with zero manas and zero transformation, an ālaya that is true-and-deluded-combined) and *what* its manas-frame entails. So the paper grants Shih Chao-Hwei's exposition and registers her dependent-origination critique as *one* response from within the living tradition, while declining to take her normative side — keeping to this series' consistent descriptive-historical register and passing no verdict on a living tradition.

### IV.4 The Thinness of Attention: A Gap Still to Be Filled

It should be said plainly: to date there is no English-language monograph (nor even a single article) that takes "the precept-substance as ālaya wholesome-seed" — the Round-Teaching Vinaya–Yogācāra synthesis — as its own systematic subject. Anglophone work splits into three non-intersecting strands: the precept-substance concept and the Daoxuan commentaries (Newhall — article-length, never developing manas or the transformation of the basis); the systematic study of the Yogācāra ālaya-and-transformation (Lusthaus, Waldron — never touching the precept-substance); and the study of the *Awakening of Faith*'s true-and-deluded-combined ālaya (never connected to the Vinaya). This paper is the first to (i) take "the precept-substance as ālaya wholesome-seed" as a systematic object, (ii) join these three orphaned strands, and (iii) openly own that "the manas-and-transformation reading is a constructed synthesis." On the Japanese side, Satō Tatsugen's *Studies on the Vinaya in Chinese Buddhism*, chapter eight, treats "Daoxuan and the precept-substance"[^26] — of service to the historical context, but it likewise does not reach the Yogācāra-ontological synthesis that is this paper's concern. With this paper, the three papers of Part V fill that least-treated seam.

## V. Disciplinary Corrections

This chapter marks the mis-readings most likely to occur, with ❌ (a logical error) and ⚠️ (a heuristic analogy, not a theoretical equivalence) as flags.

**❌ "Daoxuan founded the precept-substance on the *Cheng weishi lun*."** This is the mis-reading the paper most needs to forestall. The Round-Teaching precept-substance corpus contains zero manas and zero transformation-of-the-basis; its ālaya is the true-and-deluded-combined consciousness, not Xuanzang's deluded consciousness; and Newhall has independently determined that Daoxuan's seed-theory derives from the pre-Xuanzang *Awakening of Faith* and the *Dasheng yizhang*. Hence every link between precept-substance and manas, and between precept-substance and the transformation of the basis, is this paper's constructed synthesis — reading Xuanzang Yogācāra into the Round-Teaching seed-theory — and is not Daoxuan's native doctrine. The correct statement is: this paper uses the *Cheng weishi lun*'s machinery as a lens to read the Round-Teaching's seed-fragment.

**❌ "*Taishō* No. 1805 is Daoxuan's book."** The author of No. 1805, the *Sifenlü xingshichao zizhiji*, is Yuanzhao of the Song, not Daoxuan. The whole vocabulary of the Round-Teaching seed comes from Yuanzhao transmitting Daoxuan's *Yeshu* (the Karman-Commentary, X0728); Daoxuan's *Xingshichao* (T1804) itself has zero Yogācāra-ontological vocabulary, its seven instances of "seed" all denoting plant-seeds. Citations must carry the byline "Yuanzhao's *Zichiji*, transmitting Daoxuan's *Yeshu*," and may not be made directly as Daoxuan's *Xingshichao* autograph. (The Round-Teaching's own *ten* meanings of a seed are Yuanzhao's *Jiyuanji* gloss, distinct from Yogācāra's *six* meanings — itself evidence of appropriation, not verbatim import.)

**❌ "Precept-keeping is just the gradual increase of hearing-perfuming, and perfumed to the end it becomes the transformation of the basis."** The perfuming the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* describes as "gradually perfumed, gradually increasing → the basis transformed" is the *hearing-perfuming*, which the treatise expressly judges to be comprised within the dharma-body and the liberation-body, *not* the ālaya — i.e. supramundane and undefiled. What an ordinary being's precept-keeping forms is a tainted wholesome seed, utterly distinct in substance. The two admit, at most, a formal analogy, and may not be equated. The safest *substantive* bridge between precept and transformation is conscience, which the *Cheng weishi lun* does list as the motive of the "diminishing-the-force-and-augmenting-the-capacity transformation," and which already stands on the tainted-good side of the restraint discipline, committing no undefiled/tainted conflation.

**❌ "Precept-keeping can subdue or sever the manas's grasping at a self."** Yogācāra states plainly: "no tainted path can subdue [them]; the noble path of the three vehicles has the power to subdue them — for the genuine insight of no-self runs counter to the grasping at a self." What can counter and subdue the manas's self-grasping is the no-self realization of the three-vehicle noble path alone; precept-keeping (at the ordinary-being stage) is a tainted path and cannot subdue the manas. Precept-keeping is the long preparation made *for* the no-self insight, not that no-self insight itself. Wherever this paper says "precept-keeping counters the manas," it means this sense of *preparation*, not "direct subduing-and-severing."

**⚠️ The structural parallel of "the manas not perishing but flipping / the precept-seed not perishing but purifying" is a heuristic analogy, not a theoretical equivalence.** The paper uses this parallel to disclose the real place of precept-keeping (the long perfuming that bends toward the flip); its heuristic power is that it points at once to the precept-substance's potency (it really forms a seed, can be augmented) and its limit (under the manas it remains tainted). But it must be marked clearly: this parallel is the paper's construction, not a statement of the texts; it is a scaffold for reading the question of the precept-substance, not a doctrinal proposition that stands on its own. Readers should take it up in that sense.

## VI. Conclusion: The Deepest Substance Meets the Deepest Obstruction — Part V Turns the Whole Question Toward Transformation

**The argument restated.** The precept-substance — the thing gained at ordination and retained after keeping — is judged by the Round-Teaching school, in its ultimate sense, to be a wholesome seed in the store-consciousness: the formal act of receiving perfumes the deluded mind, forming a wholesome seed in the underlying store-consciousness. But the Round-Teaching corpus has zero manas and zero transformation-of-the-basis, and its ālaya is the true-and-deluded-combined consciousness; so this paper, reading mature Xuanzang Yogācāra into the Round-Teaching seed-theory as an openly constructed synthesis, reads out: the precept-seed is newly-perfumed-and-arisen, held by the eighth consciousness alone, perfumed by the strong wholesome volition, flowing continuously and able to increase or decrease (shape); the manas perpetually grasps at a self, leaving even a wholesome precept tainted (obstruction); its ultimate orientation is the transformation of the basis, while precept-keeping is the long perfuming that bends toward the turning, with conscience as its safest bridge — and is not the turning itself (orientation). Precept-substance and manas: the deepest substance of the precept-substance meeting its deepest obstruction.

**What it teaches about disciplined practice.** The one most intimate reminder this doctrine offers the precept-keeper is: do not think the precept-substance too *mysterious*, and do not think precept-keeping too *shallow*. Not mysterious — the precept-substance is no occult, once-gained-forever "real object"; it is a functional seed that, met by conditions, can guard against fault, growing with keeping and diminishing with transgression, alive, able to be nourished and able to be lost. Not shallow — the importance of precept-keeping lies not in accruing a "*I* kept the precepts" stock of merit (that is exactly the tainted good of the manas grasping at a self), but in its day-after-day diminishing of the defiled and augmenting of the pure through conscience, bending the whole of body-and-mind, inch by inch, toward that fundamental turning. In other words, the true worth of precept-keeping is not in "*I* have become a keeper of precepts," but in its silent, long labor of paving the way for *no-self*. The moment one treats precept-keeping as a medal of the self, it remains only a tainted good under the manas's self-grasping; only when one keeps the precept without grasping at a self who keeps it, and feels conscience without preening on the merit of conscience, is the precept-seed truly being bent toward the transformation of the basis.

**An open question.** This paper has shown that the ultimate orientation of the precept-seed is the transformation of the basis, and that precept-keeping is the long perfuming that bends toward it. But it leaves a question unanswered: since precept-keeping depends so heavily on conscience and on long perfuming, *what conditions make it possible for one person to keep the precepts continuously while another cannot?* Whether the precept-seed can be repeatedly augmented evidently turns on more than the wholesome volition of the ordination moment — it turns also on the recipient's body, faculties, food, clothing, lifespan, and circumstances of gender and society, the whole array of supporting conditions. That question — the supporting conditions of precept-keeping — is what Part VI takes up, after Part V comes to rest.

The question of the precept-substance — from early Buddhism, where no such independent question was yet posed (the historical arc P16 traced), to the three Chinese answers (P16), to Daoxuan's judgment of the seed as ultimate and his establishment of the *Xingshichao* as a millennium's standard (P17), to this paper's reading-in of Yogācāra and its seeing the substance oriented toward the transformation of the basis — is, with this, complete in Part V's three papers: P16 resolving the trilemma's impasse, P17 establishing its paradigmatic architecture, and this paper filling its least-treated ontological seam. The deepest substance of the precept-substance meets its deepest obstruction; and its way out points toward that one transformation of the basis, in which the grasping at a self flips into no-self.
