## Abstract

The present paper continues the categorical thesis advanced in P05 — that the three ethical limbs of the noble eightfold path (*sammā-vācā*, *sammā-kammanta*, *sammā-ājīva*) are constitutive path-elements rather than a pre-path constraint-list — and applies it, at the second paper of Part II ("The Āgama View"), to the specific structure of the five precepts (*pañca-sīla*). When the canonical record speaks at length about the rationale of each precept individually, the justification register changes from precept to precept across five distinct layers: the precept of abstaining from killing receives an *abhaya-dāna* (gift of fearlessness) framing keyed to the *mahā-dāna* discourse at *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.39; the precept of abstaining from taking what is not given is keyed to the practitioner's bodily-perceptual purity as an identitarian register; the precept of abstaining from sexual misconduct turns on the asymmetry between monastic *brahmacariya* and the lay-side *kāmesumicchācāra*, whose canonical definition is structurally underdetermined in the Pāli sūtra layer and completed only at the Mahāyāna lay-precept stratum of *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* fascicle 6; the precept of abstaining from false speech is articulated as the formation of five positive verbal virtues — *saccavādī*, *saccasandho*, *theto*, *paccayiko*, *avisaṁvādako* (truthful, reliable, trustworthy, dependable, non-deceiving); and the precept of abstaining from intoxicants is given a consequentialist register, in the six failings of *Sigālovāda* DN 31 §§27–28, together with the self-explanatory analytic surface of its own canonical name *surāmeraya-majja-pamāda-ṭṭhāna* — "liquors, wines, and intoxicants that are the basis of negligence." The reception ritual of the five precepts is cross-traditionally uniform; the canonical scope of each precept is cross-precept differential. "Same ritual, different scope" is the architectural observation that constitutes the present paper's thesis. The intoxicant precept stands outside the four *kamma-courses* (killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying) at the Āgama layer; outside the ten *kamma-courses* in the Yogācāra abhidharmic analysis; and outside the conditional-opening provisions in the Mahāyāna bodhisattva-precept framework — its protecting-the-path status as a derivative precept securing the cognitive layer of *paññā* receives canonical-internal expression at the *Sigālovāda*'s phrase *paññāya dubbalikaraṇī* ("weakening of wisdom"), and prefigures the *kammaṭṭhāna* layer of the next volume. Ten Pāli loci directly relevant to the present paper's argument are absent from the present author's working corpus and are accordingly cited by PTS reference only, without verbatim Pāli text, in line with the discipline carried over from P04 and P05.

**Keywords:** the five precepts (*pañca-sīla*), training-rule (*sikkhāpada*), precept (*sīla*), great gift and gift-of-fearlessness (*mahā-dāna*, *abhaya-dāna*), chaste-life (*brahmacariya*), positive five-fold verbal virtues, basis of negligence (*pamāda-ṭṭhāna*), weakening of wisdom (*paññāya dubbalikaraṇī*), protecting-the-path precept, nature-precepts and protective-precepts (*prakṛti-śīla*, *pratikṣepaṇa-śīla*), *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* fascicle 6, *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 13.

## I. The Problem

P05 opened Part II of the present volume by advancing a categorical thesis: the three ethical limbs of the noble eightfold path — right speech, right action, right livelihood — are constitutive path-elements internal to the path's emergence, not a pre-path constraint-list of prohibitions that the practitioner satisfies before stepping onto the path proper.[^1] But the path-element character of these limbs is not a homogeneous shape. What concrete canonical scope does each precept carry? The five precepts — abstention from killing, from taking what is not given, from sexual misconduct, from false speech, from intoxicants — appear in the Pāli canon and in the Chinese *Āgama* corpus alike under a single grammatical formula, *veramaṇī*, "abstaining-from." On the surface, the five formulas present themselves as a homogeneous list of five negative restraints of the same kind. But when one looks closely at how the canon actually argues *why* each precept stands, the justification register changes from precept to precept across five distinct layers. The first precept is framed by the *mahā-dāna* gift-architecture — by abstaining from killing the noble disciple gives countless sentient beings freedom from fear, enmity, and ill-will. The second is framed by the practitioner's bodily-perceptual purity — the recluse Gotama, having given up taking what is not given, takes only what is given, expects only what is given, and dwells in unstealing purity. The third turns on a definitional asymmetry between monastic *brahmacariya* (chaste-life, the absolute abstention from sexual conduct constitutive of monastic life as such) and the lay-side *kāmesumicchācāra* (sexual misconduct), where the canonical definition of "misconduct" at the lay layer is structurally underdetermined. The fourth is framed by the five positive verbal virtues *saccavādī saccasandho theto paccayiko avisaṁvādako*. The fifth is framed by the *Sigālovāda*'s six concrete consequences of intoxication, and by the self-explanatory analytic of its own canonical name. **The reception formula of the five precepts is uniform across traditions; the canonical scope of each precept is differential across precepts.** This architectural observation — "same ritual, different scope" — is the present paper's thesis. Within that thesis, the intoxicant precept occupies a structurally distinctive position across three layers of the tradition. At the Āgama layer it is set apart from the four *kamma*-courses (killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying); at the Yogācāra abhidharmic layer the ten-*kamma*-course analysis of the *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā* does not include intoxicants; and at the Mahāyāna bodhisattva-precept layer the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s conditional-opening provisions, which permit the four core precepts to be set aside under specified welfare-of-sentient-beings conditions, do not extend to intoxicants — the intoxicant precept is held at an absolute deontic level even within the bodhisattva framework. The cross-traditional convergence of this finding — that the intoxicant precept is not a fifth core precept on a par with the four, but a derivative protective precept whose function is to safeguard the path itself — receives canonical-internal expression in the *Sigālovāda*'s phrase *paññāya dubbalikaraṇī*, "weakening of wisdom." The intoxicant precept's protecting-the-path status is the present paper's strongest single architectural finding, and it furnishes a lay-side preparation for the next volume's *kammaṭṭhāna* layer. Before the per-precept exposition proceeds, one terminological distinction internal to the *Āgama* layer must be flagged. The dual register of *pañca-sikkhāpada* (the five training-rules) and *pañca-sīla* (the five precepts) is not a synonymy between two names for the same thing but a coexistence of two registers. The *sikkhāpada* register belongs to the vinaya reception-formula context — the three Chinese vinaya recensions each preserve the formula "abstaining from killing, I undertake the training-rule"[^20] — and to the *Khuddakapāṭha*'s *Dasa-sikkhāpada* (registered in the PTS-only disclosure below).[^8] The *sīla* register belongs to the path-element context: the *Saṁyutta Nikāya* 55th *Saṁyutta*, *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.39, *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 5.174, the *Cūḷasīla* section of *Dīgha Nikāya* 1.[^3][^4][^5] The present paper follows the discipline carried over from P05: the *sīla* register, as path-element, is taken as primary; the *sikkhāpada* register, as reception-ritual, is brought in only as ritual-side corroboration. A canonical-internal expression of the problem can be drawn from *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 5.178, the *Rāja-sutta*. In Bhante Sujato's public-domain rendering: "rulers don't arrest someone and punish them for abstaining from killing; they arrest and punish them for killing."[^2] The canonical sentence is structurally revealing. The precept's significance is not constituted by its quiet observance but is made visible by its breach. If a list of prohibitions were what the precepts amount to, then non-violation would be the precepts' principal evidential surface. The canonical text moves in the opposite direction. Non-violation is not a positive evidential fact a king can inspect; violation is. This sharpens the P05 categorical thesis at one further turn: precepts, as path-elements, presuppose a background of non-violation as the routine condition against which "how non-violation contributes to the development of the path" becomes the structurally posable question. The per-precept exposition that follows is the present paper's concrete answer to that question. ---

## II. Canonical Sources

The evidence base of the present paper draws on two axes: the Pāli axis (the *Aṅguttara* five-precept cluster, *Dīgha Nikāya* 1's *Cūḷasīla* section, and *Dīgha Nikāya* 31 *Sigālovāda*) and the Chinese axis (*Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicles 32–33, *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicles 11, 13, 14, and 15, and *Madhyama-āgama* fascicle 45). The two axes do not distribute symmetrically across the relevant material. On the Pāli side, the *Aṅguttara*'s five-precept cluster (AN 5.171 through AN 5.179) yields six suttas in the present author's working corpus (AN 5.171, 5.174, 5.175, 5.176, 5.178, 5.179), with three absent (AN 5.172, 5.173, 5.177). On the Chinese side, the present paper relies for *Saṃyukta-āgama* on two principal anchors, sūtra 916 and sūtra 927, rather than on a continuous cluster of sūtras 916–927: the latter framing, suggested by some earlier project notes, is a structural over-reading that requires correction to "sūtra 916 as the *kamma*-framing anchor and sūtra 927 as the reception-formula anchor."

### 2.1 The Pāli axis

The Pāli axis's central material is positioned for the present paper as follows. The *Abhisanda-sutta* at *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.39 supplies the killing precept's *mahā-dāna* anchor. In Sujato's public-domain rendering, the noble disciple who abstains from killing gives countless sentient beings freedom from fear, freedom from enmity, freedom from ill-will (*abhayaṁ deti averaṁ deti abyābajjhaṁ deti*).[^3] The sutta reframes each of the five precepts in turn as a concrete gift directed at others — the abstention from killing as the gift of fearlessness regarding life, the abstention from stealing as the gift of fearlessness regarding property, the abstention from sexual misconduct as the gift of fearlessness regarding marital relations, the abstention from false speech as the gift of fearlessness regarding speech, the abstention from intoxicants as the gift of fearlessness regarding clarity of intention. The discourse frames the five precepts together as the *mahā-dāna* (great gift) architecture. P05 has already drawn on this discourse for its eightfold-path application; the present paper develops it at fuller depth in §III.1 below as the killing-precept's principal canonical scope. The *Vera-sutta* at *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 5.174 states the psychological consequence of violating the five precepts — the five enmities (*pañca-vera*) — as a reflexive effect on the practitioner.[^4] The breach of the precepts is not a violation of an external norm alone; it is a specific damage to the practitioner's own mental field. Conversely, the upholding of the precepts is not primarily a satisfaction of external norms but a concrete settling of the practitioner's mental field. AN 5.174 and AN 8.39 form a pair of negative and positive same-content discourses — five enmities and five gifts of fearlessness — locating the canonical content of the five precepts simultaneously at the negative consequence of breach and at the positive emergence of gift. The *Cūḷasīla* section of the *Brahmajāla-sutta* at *Dīgha Nikāya* 1, §§1.8–1.12, supplies the cluster of positive virtues at the heart of the killing-precept's exposition. In Sujato's public-domain rendering of the killing-precept formula: the recluse Gotama, having given up killing living creatures, refrains from killing them, with rod and weapon laid aside, conscientious, kindly, sympathetic to all living beings (*nihita-daṇḍo nihita-sattho lajjī dayāpanno sabba-pāṇa-bhūta-hitānukampī*).[^5] The canonical structure of this passage places "having given up killing" (the negative abstention) alongside four positive virtues — rod laid aside, weapon laid aside, conscience, sympathy with all living beings. The exposition of a precept is not exhausted by its negative abstention; the canonical content includes a cluster of positive virtues as its concrete substance. The categorical thesis of P05 receives, in this passage, its strongest single-passage canonical support: a precept is exposited as the holding-together of a refraining and a positive virtue-cluster. The *Sigālovāda-sutta* at *Dīgha Nikāya* 31, §§27–28, supplies the principal anchor for the intoxicant precept. Sujato's public-domain rendering of the relevant passage gives the six drawbacks of addiction to liquor, wines, and intoxicants that are the basis of negligence: loss of wealth in the present life, increase in quarrels, growth of illness, loss of good repute, indecent exposure, and the weakening of wisdom (*paññāya dubbalikaraṇī*).[^6] The discourse's canonical scope for the intoxicant precept is not a prescriptive "should abstain" but a consequentialist "six concrete harms" exposition. The same discourse lists, separately, the six gateways-of-loss (*apāyamukha*) for the householder — gambling, untimely roaming, frequenting shows, addiction to intoxicants, bad company, and laziness, with addiction to intoxicants heading the list. The intoxicant-six-failings and the wealth-loss-six-gateways stand as two distinct architectural frames within the same discourse: the former is the intoxicant precept's own dedicated justification register; the latter is the householder's economic-domain analysis in which intoxication figures as one of six causes of ruin. *Sigālovāda* does not collapse the two frames into one; the present paper preserves the distinction in §III.5 below. The *Vitthata-uposatha-sutta* at *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.42 is brought in only as a brief cross-reference for the eight-limb *uposatha* observance, indicating its structural relation to the five-precept baseline; the full exposition of *uposatha* eight-precept observance is reserved for P07.[^7] An honest disclosure of vault absences. The following Pāli loci are absent from the present author's working Pāli corpus and are accordingly cited by PTS reference only, without verbatim citation of the Pāli text: *Vaṇijjā-sutta* (AN 5.177, the five-trades formula); *Ambalaṭṭhikā-rāhulovāda-sutta* (MN 61, the false-speech five-virtues anchor); *Mahācattārīsaka-sutta* (MN 117, already disclosed at P05); *Vibhaṅga-sutta* (SN 45.8); *Cunda-kammāraputta-sutta* (AN 10.176, the ten-wholesome-kamma-course formula); *Sāleyyaka-sutta* (MN 41); *Methuna-sutta* (AN 7.50, the seven bonds of sexual conduct); *Pāṭaliya-sutta* (SN 42.13); and *Dasa-sikkhāpada* (*Khuddakapāṭha* 2). The ten absences are not incidental gaps but represent a structural feature of the present author's working corpus, disclosed at P05 and reconfirmed in the course of preparing the present paper.[^8] No verbatim Pāli reconstruction from these absent loci is undertaken; the canonical scope of the precepts at issue is documented through working-corpus material and through Chinese-side parallels where these exist.

### 2.2 The Chinese axis

The Chinese axis's central material is positioned as follows. *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle 33, sūtra 927 (T0099), translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅) during the Yuan-jia (元嘉) reign-period of the Liu-Song, c. 435–443 CE: "what is meant by the lay disciple being accomplished in the five precepts? The lay disciple in white clothes abstains from killing, from taking what is not given, from sexual misconduct, from false speech, and from intoxicants, taking no pleasure in these."[^9] The passage is the single strongest enumeration of the five precepts as a complete reception-formula on the Chinese side. The discursive setting is the dialogue with Mahānāma (摩訶男), city-ruler of the Śākyas, and the reception-ritual register of the passage parallels the *Saṁyutta Nikāya* 55th *Saṁyutta*'s stream-entry materials. *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle 32, sūtra 916 (T0099, Guṇabhadra trans.) records Mahānāma's statement: "World-honored One, I have been brought under your guidance. From this day forth, I will abstain from killing, from taking what is not given, from sexual misconduct, from false speech, and from intoxicants, taking no pleasure in these."[^10] The passage is the single strongest articulation of the five precepts in the *kamma*-framing register on the Chinese side. The discursive setting is the Buddha's response to a Nigaṇṭha (尼乾子) interlocutor whose challenge concerns the deterministic character of karma over against the active role of intention (*cetanā*); the Buddha's response frames the undertaking of the five precepts not as a passive submission to karmic determinacy but as the active settling of intention. The passage carries on the Chinese side the path-element thesis of P05 at the five-precept layer. *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 11, sūtra 16, the *Sigāla-sūtra* (T0001), translated by Buddhayaśas (佛陀耶舍) and Zhu Fonian (竺佛念) during the Hong-shi (弘始) reign-period of the Later Qin, c. 412–413 CE, in the Dharmaguptaka school's translation lineage, names six failings of intoxication: first, loss of wealth; second, the rise of illness; third, the rise of quarrels; fourth, the spread of ill repute; fifth, the outbreak of rage; sixth, the daily diminishment of wisdom.[^11] The passage is the Chinese-side full exposition of the intoxicant-six-failings, and stands in cross-translation, cross-recension doctrinal parity with DN 31's Pāli formulation. The Chinese discourse similarly lists, separately, the six causes of wealth-loss (with intoxication heading the list), in parallel with the Pāli framing. The structural feature observable on the Chinese side as on the Pāli side — the intoxicant precept set apart from the four *kamma*-courses, articulated through its own dedicated consequentialist scope — appears already in the two-frame architecture of this single discourse. *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 14, sūtra 21, the *Brahmajāla-sūtra* (T0001, Buddhayaśas and Zhu Fonian trans.) preserves the killing- and stealing-precept positive virtue-clusters: the recluse Gotama gives up killing, refrains from killing, takes up no rod or weapon, dwells with conscience, sympathises with all living beings; he gives up taking what is not given, takes only what has been given, takes nothing ungiven, dwells constantly inclined to giving.[^12] The passage is the Chinese-side parallel to DN 1's *Cūḷasīla* section, with the positive virtue-cluster preserved intact across the Chinese translation. *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 13, sūtra 20, the *Ambaṭṭha-sūtra*, together with *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 15, sūtras 22 and 23, the *Soṇadaṇḍa-sūtra* and *Kūṭadanta-sūtra* (all Buddhayaśas and Zhu Fonian trans.), form the Chinese-side cluster of stepwise-training pericope materials, paralleling *Dīgha Nikāya* 2 *Sāmaññaphala-sutta*'s gradual-training exposition.[^13] The present paper draws on these three texts as the Chinese-side reservoir of stepwise-training exposition material. *Madhyama-āgama* fascicle 45, sūtra 175, the *Pratigraha-sūtra* (T0026), translated by Saṅghadeva (僧伽提婆) during the Lóng-ān (隆安) reign-period of the Eastern Jin, c. 397–398 CE — **not** during the Tai-yuan (太元) reign-period, as some earlier project notes had it: the present paper makes the translation-court correction explicitly.[^14] The discourse supplies the four-*kamma*-and-ten-wholesome-*kamma*-course framework on the Chinese side. The ten-wholesome-*kamma*-course framing furnishes the Chinese-side anchor for the *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā*'s ten-kamma-course analysis taken up in §V.2 below, providing a cross-Āgama and cross-abhidharmic homologous registration of the framework. A doctrinal-philological correction concerning the so-called three-fold formula ("not engaging oneself, not instructing others, not praising the conduct"). On a careful review, the *Brahmajāla-sūtra* at *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 14 contains only the Buddha's direct first-person exemplar framing of the five precepts and the three speech-related *kamma*-courses; **the three-fold formula is not an Āgama-layer canonical formulation**. The formula's developmental trajectory traces as follows: absent at the Āgama layer; partially formed in the *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* (T1488, Dharmakṣema's translation of 426 CE); completed in the bodhisattva-precept system (*Pusa yingluo benye jing*, *Brahma-net Sūtra*, *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* fascicles 40–41). When the present paper handles the canonical scope of the precepts on the Chinese side, it locates each layer of this trajectory in its proper place and does not attribute the three-fold formula to the Āgama layer.[^15] ---

## III. Per-Precept Canonical Scope

The present chapter is the architectural core of the paper, exposititing the canonical scope of each precept in turn. Each precept's exposition follows a three-layer structure: Pāli anchor, Chinese parallel, cross-precept differential.

### 3.1 The killing-precept: the gift-frame

The canonical scope of the killing-precept is principally framed by the *mahā-dāna* gift-architecture. *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.39 reframes each of the five precepts as a concrete gift directed at others — by abstaining from killing the noble disciple gives countless sentient beings freedom from fear, freedom from enmity, freedom from ill-will.[^3] The architectural depth of this reframing is twofold. First, the killing-precept is not the negative judgment "killing is wrong and abstention is its absence" but the positive act of giving — the recipients are "countless sentient beings" (*aparimāṇānaṁ sattānaṁ*), and the gift is articulated in three parallel registers (*abhayaṁ deti averaṁ deti abyābajjhaṁ deti* — gives fearlessness, gives non-enmity, gives non-ill-will). Second, the canonical syntax is active: "the noble disciple *gives*" (*deti*), not "the noble disciple *refrains*." Layered upon this gift-architecture, the *Cūḷasīla* passage at DN 1 expands the killing-precept's canonical content through a four-fold positive virtue cluster: "rod laid aside" (*nihita-daṇḍo*, the concrete behavioural cessation), "weapon laid aside" (*nihita-sattho*, paired behavioural cessation), "conscience" (*lajjī*, the inner psychological dimension), "sympathy" (*dayāpanno*, the emotional disposition), and "compassion-with-benefit toward all living beings" (*sabba-pāṇa-bhūta-hitānukampī*, the universalising intentional dimension).[^5] The canonical scope of the killing-precept on the Pāli side is exhibited fully through this four-fold positive virtue cluster — not as a minimum "no killing equals adequate" requirement but as a layered ethical formation in which the laying down of rod and weapon is the floor on which conscience, sympathy, and universalising compassion are built. The Chinese side preserves the formation intact: *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 14 *Brahmajāla-sūtra*'s exposition — gives up killing, refrains from killing, takes up no rod or weapon, dwells with conscience, sympathises with all living beings — presents the same four-fold virtue sequence as its Pāli parallel.[^12] The killing-precept's canonical scope is, on both the Pāli and Chinese sides, the abstention from killing held together with a four-fold positive virtue cluster. A note on the killing-precept's ten-*kamma*-course handling. *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 10.176 (cited only by PTS reference AN v 263) is the principal anchor for the ten-wholesome-*kamma*-course formula in its complete form.[^8] The Chinese-side parallel is the *Madhyama-āgama Pratigraha-sūtra*, fascicle 45, whose ten-*kamma*-course exposition opens with killing, taking what is not given, sexual misconduct, false speech, divisive speech, harsh speech, idle talk, covetousness, ill-will, and wrong view — the killing precept is positioned at the head of the list.[^14] The development of the ten-*kamma*-course framework belongs primarily to the abhidharmic layer and is taken up at §V.2 below.

### 3.2 The stealing-precept: bodily-perceptual purity

The canonical scope of the stealing-precept is keyed to the practitioner's own bodily-perceptual purity. The *Cūḷasīla* passage at DN 1, in its second precept formulation, places the recluse Gotama as one who, having given up taking what is not given, takes only what is given, expects only what is given, and dwells in unstealing purity.[^5] The structure parallels the killing-precept formula: behaviour-layer cessation ("having given up taking what is not given"), restriction-layer formulation ("takes only what is given"), psychological-layer formulation ("with a heart of unstealing"), and identitarian-layer formulation ("dwells in purity"). The canonical scope of the stealing-precept here exceeds the minimum "no stealing equals adequate" formulation; the phrase "dwells in purity" registers the precept's specific axis as the practitioner's own bodily-perceptual identity. The Chinese side: the *Brahmajāla-sūtra* at *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 14 introduces an additional positive element — the recluse Gotama dwells constantly inclined to giving — placing the stealing-precept's positive face in explicit verb-form alongside its abstentive face, articulating the precept as a "no-taking-plus-constant-giving" double-faced structure.[^12] Beyond the *Āgama* layer's framing of the stealing precept, the *Pāṭaliya-sutta* (SN 42.13, cited only by PTS reference SN iv 340) supplies one of the strongest Pāli-side debate exchanges between the Buddha and an external interlocutor on the fine grain of moral law.[^8] In light of the present author's vault absence, the social-economic dimension of the stealing-precept's canonical scope at this depth is not unpacked here; the vinaya-layer case-law adjudication of stealing is in any case reserved for P09 and P10.

### 3.3 The sexual-misconduct precept: chaste-life and lay definitional gap

The canonical scope of the sexual-misconduct precept is asymmetric between its monastic and lay sides. The monastic side is anchored on the strict sense of *brahmacariya* (chaste-life) at the *Cūḷasīla*'s third precept formulation: the recluse Gotama, having given up unchaste conduct, lives the chaste-life, dwelling apart, refraining from the vulgar practice of sexual intercourse (*abrahmacariyaṁ pahāya brahmacārī ārācārī virato methunā gāmadhammā*).[^5] The monastic-side scope is not a "no sexual misconduct" restriction but the absolute prohibition of unchaste conduct — the term *brahmacariya* in early canonical usage denotes "chaste-conduct," "holy living," not merely sexual continence, designating a way of life set wholly apart from sexual entanglement. The lay side is keyed by contrast to "sexual misconduct" (*kāmesumicchācāra*) — but the definition of this term in the extant early Pāli sūtras is not complete. What counts as "misconduct" in distinction from licit sexual conduct does not receive in the *Āgama* layer the kind of enumerated articulation that the killing, stealing, and false-speech precepts each receive. The *Methuna-sutta* at *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 7.50 takes the precept up under the heading of seven bonds of sexual conduct,[^8] but the depth of its analysis, as far as the discipline of the present paper's vault permits one to register, does not approach the precision attained for the killing, stealing, or false-speech precepts. The lay-side definitional gap of the sexual-misconduct precept is not an artefact of the present author's working-corpus limitations but a structural feature of the *Āgama* layer's own canonical formulation — the present paper makes this disclosure honestly, without compensatory inference. It is the *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* fascicle 6, at the Mahāyāna lay-precept stratum (Dharmakṣema, trans.; Northern Liáng, Xuán-shǐ 426 CE; T1488 — cited from the CBETA XML edition only) that completes the definition. The text articulates the lay-side scope of the sexual-misconduct precept through a three-fold delimitation: one's own spouse, not another's spouse, not at improper time or place or in improper manner. In the same fascicle, the text records the architectural observation that, of the five precepts, the sexual-misconduct precept is the unique one that cannot be self-recovered in a future life.[^15] The architectural depth of this observation is striking. Where the breach of the killing, stealing, false-speech, and intoxicant precepts in a future birth may simply not recur if the appropriate conditions are not reactivated, the breach of the sexual-misconduct precept — given that sexual desire is a root *kleśa* whose karmic conditions recur — must be received and held in the present life. The trajectory of the sexual-misconduct precept's canonical scope thus extends from the Āgama-layer definitional gap, through the *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra*'s fascicle-6 completion, to the bodhisattva-precept system's three-fold formula — three developmental layers within the Chinese-side textual record.

### 3.4 The false-speech precept: the formation of five positive verbal virtues

The canonical scope of the false-speech precept is principally framed by the formation of five positive verbal virtues. The principal Pāli anchor is the *Ambalaṭṭhikā-rāhulovāda-sutta* at *Majjhima Nikāya* 61 (cited only by PTS reference MN i 414). In this discourse, the Buddha teaches Rāhula through the metaphor of clear water, articulates the ethical injury of "doing whatever for the sake of one lie," and names the five positive verbal virtues — *saccavādī* (the truth-speaker), *saccasandho* (the reliable), *theto* (the trustworthy), *paccayiko* (the dependable), *avisaṁvādako* (the non-deceiving).[^8] The canonical scope of the false-speech precept is not the minimum "no lying equals adequate" requirement but the concrete formation of these five positive verbal virtues as the precept's substantive content. The *Cūḷasīla* passage at DN 1, in its false-speech formulation: the recluse Gotama, having given up false speech, refrains from false speech, speaks the truth, dwells in the truth, is reliable, trustworthy, not deceiving toward the world.[^5] The Chinese-side parallel at *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 14 — gives up false speech, with full sincerity not deceiving, not deluding others — preserves the core three of the five positive virtues (truthfulness, trustworthiness, dependability), in accordance with the doctrinal-uniformity pattern between the early Pāli and the early Chinese translations.[^12] A point on Chinese-side lineage already made in §2.2: the three-fold formula ("not engaging oneself, not instructing others, not praising the conduct") is not an Āgama-layer formulation but emerges through the staged development of the Mahāyāna lay-precept stratum and the bodhisattva-precept system.[^15] When the present paper handles the false-speech precept, it preserves the layer-distinction between the Āgama-side five positive verbal virtues and the bodhisattva-side three-fold formula.

### 3.5 The intoxicant precept: consequentialist six-failings and protecting-the-path derivative status

The canonical scope of the intoxicant precept extends across three layers: the consequentialist six-failings exposition; the self-explanatory analytic of the precept's own canonical name; and the cross-traditional protecting-the-path position observable across the Āgama, Yogācāra-abhidharmic, and Mahāyāna bodhisattva-precept layers. **The six failings.** *Dīgha Nikāya* 31 *Sigālovāda* §§27–28 names six drawbacks of addiction to liquor, wines, and intoxicants that are the basis of negligence — loss of wealth in this very life, increase in quarrels, growth of illness, loss of good repute, indecent exposure, and the weakening of wisdom.[^6] The Chinese parallel at *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 11 *Sigāla-sūtra* records six failings — loss of wealth, rise of illness, rise of quarrels, spread of ill repute, outbreak of rage, daily diminishment of wisdom[^11] — in cross-translation, cross-recension doctrinal parity with the Pāli. Of the five precepts, only the intoxicant precept receives the canonical structure of six specific harms; the four other precepts do not receive this homologous structure. The intoxicant precept's structural distinctness within the *Āgama* layer is already exhibited in the six-failings architecture. **The name's self-explanatory analytic.** The intoxicant precept's full Pāli reception formula is "I undertake to refrain from liquors, wines, and intoxicants that are the basis of negligence" — *surāmeraya-majja-pamāda-ṭṭhānā veramaṇī*. The phrase *pamāda-ṭṭhāna*, literally "basis of negligence" or "dwelling-place of negligence," carries the precept's reason for being inside its own canonical name: why one abstains from intoxicants is given not because they are intrinsically evil but because they are the *basis of negligence* — a derivative reason rooted in their function as a substrate for unmindfulness. This self-explanatory analytic is not paralleled in the canonical formulations of the other four precepts. The reception formulas for killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, and false speech are bare verb-phrases — "abstaining from killing," "abstaining from taking what is not given," and so on — without an embedded "why" within the formula itself. The intoxicant precept's name is unique among the five in carrying its derivative-protective status as a lexical feature of its own canonical surface. **The cross-traditional protecting-the-path position.** At the *Āgama* layer, the intoxicant precept is set apart from the four *kamma*-courses (killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying) within the *Sigālovāda* itself — the discourse's own architecture separates "abstaining from the four corrupt actions" and "the six failings of intoxication" into two distinct frames. At the Yogācāra abhidharmic layer, the *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā* fascicle 7's ten-*kamma*-course analysis (Xuanzang's translation) names the *kleśa*-culmination of each course — killing-as-kamma-course culminates in hatred; taking-what-is-not-given and sexual misconduct culminate in greed; lying culminates in any of the three poisons; intoxicants are not included in the ten *kamma*-courses at all.[^18] The ten *kamma*-courses, as the abhidharmic formalisation of the four-body-three-speech-three-mind framework, do not extend to intoxicants. At the Mahāyāna bodhisattva-precept layer, the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s *Bodhisattvabhūmi-śīla-paṭala* (fascicles 40–42, Xuanzang's translation) sets out conditional-opening provisions under which the four core precepts — killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, false speech — may be set aside for the benefit of sentient beings (the bodhisattva may, for instance, kill to save the lives of many, or take what is not given to save others from financial ruin); the intoxicant precept is *not* covered by this conditional-opening provision and is maintained at the absolute deontic level even within the bodhisattva framework.[^17] The convergence across the three layers — Āgama outside the four *kamma*-courses, Yogācāra-abhidharmic outside the ten *kamma*-courses, Mahāyāna bodhisattva-precept outside the conditional-opening — is a cross-traditional architectural fact: the intoxicant precept is not a fifth core precept on a par with the four, but a derivative precept whose function is the protection of the path itself. The cross-traditional architecture finds its canonical-internal expression at the *Sigālovāda*'s phrase *paññāya dubbalikaraṇī*, "weakening of wisdom." The substantive content of the intoxicant precept's rationale is not that intoxication directly harms the body or mind (the first five of the six failings articulate indirect harms, mediated through wealth-loss, illness, quarrel, reputation, and indecency); it is that intoxication damages the cognitive layer on which the path itself unfolds. The sixth failing — "the weakening of wisdom" — names the underlying mechanism of which the other five are surface manifestations. The intoxicant precept's canonical reason, at its deepest level, is the protection of the *paññā*-layer of the path. **The *prakṛti-śīla* / *pratikṣepaṇa-śīla* distinction.** Zhiyi (智顗, late 6th century) in the *Mohe-zhiguan* fascicle 4-upper, within the exposition of the first of five preparatory conditions ("the purity of the precepts"), distinguishes nature-precepts (*prakṛti-śīla*, 性戒 — those whose substance is intrinsic ethical wrongness regardless of legal context: the four core precepts plus the three speech-related *kamma*-courses, divisive speech, harsh speech, and idle talk) from protective-precepts (*pratikṣepaṇa-śīla*, 遮戒 — those whose substance is protective enactment: the intoxicant precept, in that intoxication is legally permitted in the social-economic field but is constituted as a precept on account of its function as the basis of negligence).[^19] The Chinese commentarial tradition's distinction between nature-precepts and protective-precepts, registered at the Tiantai layer, is homologous in architectural function with the Āgama-layer's four-*kamma*-courses framing, the Yogācāra-abhidharmic layer's ten-*kamma*-courses framing, and the Mahāyāna bodhisattva-precept layer's conditional-opening framing — across four canonical and commentarial layers, the intoxicant precept consistently receives a "protecting-the-path" structural placement. ---

## IV. Scholarly Dialogue

The present chapter continues the three-cast format inherited from P01 through P05 — Bhikkhu Anālayo, Rupert Gethin, and Bhikkhu Bodhi — each engaged through the Concede + Extend two-step structure debuted in P04 and continued through P05. Peter Harvey is brought in as a secondary scholarly voice, not enrolled in the three-cast, for systematic Western-academic corroboration at the false-speech and intoxicant precept sections.[^22]

### 4.1 Bhikkhu Anālayo

Bhikkhu Anālayo's *Saṃyukta-āgama Studies* (Dharma Drum Publishing, 2015) provides the cross-traditional documentary baseline for the present paper. Anālayo's documentary work establishes the textual stability of *Saṃyukta-āgama* sūtras 916 and 927, the *Aṅguttara* five-precept cluster (AN 5.171–5.179), and AN 8.39 across translation-courts (sources reaching back behind sectarian division) and across the doctrinal schools (Sarvāstivāda and Theravāda before their separation) — the five-precept reception formula is preserved across translation-court and school as a pre-sectarian doctrinal architecture, in the documentary sense Anālayo's project sustains for the *Āgama* materials throughout (a point already taken up at P05 §IV.1). **Concede.** Anālayo's cross-traditional documentary work establishes the philological ground for the present paper's claim that "the five-precept reception ritual is uniform across traditions," and rules out the historical-revisionist hypothesis that the five-precept reception formula is a late sectarian construction. **Extend.** Anālayo's cross-traditional stability analysis terminates at the reception-formula layer. The present paper extends the analysis at one further step. Within the cross-traditionally uniform reception formula, the canonical scope of each precept exhibits cross-precept differential structure — killing-as-gift, stealing-as-bodily-purity, sexual-misconduct-as-chaste-life-plus-lay-definitional-gap, false-speech-as-virtue-formation, intoxicants-as-consequentialist-plus-protecting-the-path — and this cross-precept differential is itself cross-traditionally shared, not a late commentarial overlay. The reception ritual's cross-traditional uniformity and the per-precept scope's cross-precept differential are two coexisting structural layers, and the present paper's exposition of this coexistence constitutes its specific advance over Anālayo's cross-traditional documentary baseline.

### 4.2 Rupert Gethin

Rupert Gethin's *The Foundations of Buddhism* (Oxford University Press, 1998), at chapters 7 and 8, situates the five precepts within the layered architecture of lay and monastic ethical formation — the five precepts are not a freestanding list of prohibitions but the entry layer of a layered architecture extending through the novice ten precepts to the *bhikkhu* and *bhikkhunī pāṭimokkha* and onward to the bodhisattva precepts. The present paper inherits Gethin's layered framework for the Mahāyāna Chinese stratum's later treatment, supplying Western-academic systematic articulation of what the Chinese commentarial tradition arrives at through different routes (P05 §IV.2 already drew on Gethin's layered architecture in the analogous role). **Concede.** Gethin's layered architecture situates the five precepts at the lay-entry layer of a progressive sequence, registers their structural relation with the ten novice precepts, the *pāṭimokkha*, and the bodhisattva precepts, and supplies a clear systematic frame for understanding their place within the larger trajectory. **Extend.** Gethin's layered architecture terminates at "the five precepts as the lay-entry layer." The present paper extends one further step. Even within the single lay-entry layer, the canonical scope of the five precepts exhibits cross-precept differential structure across five distinct registers. Gethin's homogenising treatment of the lay-entry layer is extended, in the present paper, into a five-fold register-differentiation internal to that layer.

### 4.3 Bhikkhu Bodhi

Bhikkhu Bodhi's *Going for Refuge & Taking the Precepts* (BPS Wheel 282/284, 1981) is the direct subject-matter monograph precedent for the present paper. Part 2 of Bodhi's monograph treats each of the five precepts in a dedicated subsection, framing the precepts as "training-rules plus moral foundation" — a dual identity. Bodhi's *Noble Eightfold Path* (BPS, 1984; Wisdom 1994), already cited at P05 §IV.3, embeds the four behaviour-precepts within the eightfold-path articulation of right speech and right action, supplying the strongest Western-academic precedent for the three-ethical-limbs categorical thesis of P05. The present paper's three-cast §IV places Bodhi as the single strongest anchor across the present volume so far (P05 §IV.3 similarly placed Bodhi as its strongest anchor through *The Noble Eightfold Path*); Bodhi's *Numerical Discourses of the Buddha* (Wisdom, 2012) editorial introductions to the relevant chapters of AN 5 and AN 8 supply further direct textual support. **Concede.** Bodhi's dual identity — "training-rules plus moral foundation" — supplies a precise Western-academic articulation of the multi-layered character of the canonical scope of the five precepts, and parallels the present paper's distinction between the *sikkhāpada* register and the *sīla* register at §I. **Extend.** Bodhi's dual identity terminates at the recognition that the precepts carry both faces. The present paper extends one further step. The "moral foundation" face — which is the *sīla* register, the path-element register — exhibits cross-precept differential structure across the five precepts (killing-as-gift, stealing-as-bodily-purity, and so on); the "training-rules" face — which is the *sikkhāpada* register, the reception-formula register — is cross-precept uniform across the five. The same ritual carries five distinct scopes. Bodhi's monograph is the present paper's starting point; its specific advance is the cross-precept differentiation of Bodhi's "moral foundation" face into its five distinct registers.

### 4.4 Peter Harvey (secondary voice; not enrolled in the three-cast)

Peter Harvey's *An Introduction to Buddhist Ethics: Foundations, Values and Issues* (Cambridge University Press, 2000), at chapter 3, supplies the most complete single-chapter Western-academic treatment of the five precepts. Harvey's framing is a dual-axis reading: the five precepts cover five distinct moral domains (life, property, sexuality, speech, mindfulness) and at the same time function as trainings in individual ethical development. Harvey's dual-axis reading overlaps in part with Bodhi's dual-identity framing, with the additional contribution of articulating the five moral-domain dimensions in a manner systematically intelligible within the Western philosophical-ethics tradition. The present paper cites Harvey's chapter 3 at the false-speech precept's virtue-formation register and at the intoxicant precept's protecting-the-path register, in the role of Western-academic systematic corroboration.[^22] Harvey is not enrolled in the chapter's Concede + Extend three-cast structure, the three-cast inheritance being a Part I and Part II discipline traceable through P01–P05.

### 4.5 Closing the three-cast

Across the three voices — Anālayo's cross-traditional documentary baseline, Gethin's layered architecture, Bodhi's dual identity — the shared underlying assumption is that the five precepts constitute a homogeneous type. The present paper advances at one further step. The canonical scope of the five precepts, cross-traditionally shared as it is, is also cross-precept differential at five distinct registers — killing as gift-frame, stealing as bodily-perceptual purity, sexual misconduct as chaste-life plus lay-definitional gap, false speech as positive virtue formation, intoxicants as consequentialist six-failings plus self-explanatory name plus protecting-the-path derivative status. The intoxicant precept's protecting-the-path status, cross-traditional across the Āgama, Yogācāra-abhidharmic, and Mahāyāna bodhisattva-precept layers, constitutes a cross-layer architectural finding distinctive of the present paper. The three-cast's shared underlying base is reception-formula uniformity, layered architecture, and dual-identity — three Western-academic systematic registrations of what the present paper extends into cross-precept differential canonical scope. Harvey's secondary voice supplies Western-academic systematic corroboration at the false-speech and intoxicant junctures. ---

## V. Integration: Abhidharmic Layer, Mahāyāna Lay-Precept Monograph, Vinaya, and Commentarial Stratum

The present chapter integrates the five-precept canonical scope across four further layers: abhidharmic redaction (*Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 13; *Abhidharma-samuccaya*; *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā*); the Mahāyāna lay-precept monograph (*Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* fascicle 6); the vinaya cross-witness (three Chinese vinaya recensions, brief mention only); and the Chinese commentarial tradition (Zhiyi's nature-precept / protective-precept distinction).

### 5.1 The *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* fascicle 6: five-precept chapter

The *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* (Dharmakṣema, trans.; Northern Liáng, Xuán-shǐ 426 CE; T1488 — drawn from the CBETA XML edition)[^15] is the single strongest Mahāyāna lay-precept monograph precedent on the Chinese side. Fascicle 6's five-precept chapter contains four architectural elements relevant to the present paper. (i) The partial-reception architecture. The five precepts are not an all-or-nothing absolute reception but admit five graded receptions: one-precept, two-precept, three-precept, lesser-precept (covering four), and full-precept reception. The architectural existence of partial reception presupposes the cross-precept differential of canonical scope; if the five precepts were a homogeneous type, partial reception would be conceptually unmotivated. The cross-precept differential of canonical scope is the architectural ground on which partial reception stands. (ii) The six grave precepts. Beyond the five precepts, T1488 fascicle 6 names three additional grave precepts: great-lying (about supramundane attainments), disclosing the faults of the fourfold *saṅgha*, and selling intoxicants. The Mahāyāna lay-precept stratum's addition is structurally revealing: selling intoxicants (酤酒) is enumerated as a separate grave precept distinct from the intoxicant precept itself, registering the social-economic dimension of intoxicant production-and-distribution as architecturally separable from the individual practitioner's reception. (iii) Case-law granularity. The criteria for adjudicating violations of the killing, stealing, sexual-misconduct, and false-speech precepts receive detailed case-law treatment in fascicle 6 (the full unfolding of casuistic logic is reserved for P09 and P10; the present paper notes only the existence of fascicle 6's case-law layer). (iv) The five-great-gifts equation. The five precepts are framed in fascicle 6 as the five great gifts (*pañca-mahā-dāna*) — abstention from killing as the gift of fearlessness, abstention from stealing as the gift of security of property, abstention from sexual misconduct as the gift of marital safety, abstention from false speech as the gift of credibility, abstention from intoxication as the gift of clarity of intention. This framing matches the *mahā-dāna* architecture of *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 8.39, exhibiting cross-layer doctrinal homology between the *Āgama* and Mahāyāna lay-precept strata. The *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra*'s own opening makes a striking self-positioning claim: the lay-disciple precepts are extremely difficult to undertake, since these precepts function as the foundation of the novice ten precepts, the *bhikkhu* great-precepts, the bodhisattva precepts, and ultimately *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi*.[^15] The text places the five precepts at the foundation of the entire precept architecture — a positioning homologous with Gethin's layered architecture and with Buddhaghosa's *catukka-sīla* (four-fold-precept) layering of the *Visuddhimagga*, with the additional articulation of the five precepts as the foundation of all subsequent precept strata.

### 5.2 Abhidharmic-layer treatment of the intoxicant precept

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (Kumārajīva [鳩摩羅什], trans., Later Qin Hong-shi 401–413 CE) at fascicle 13's five-precept chapter expands the intoxicant precept into thirty-five failings,[^16] a further-articulated abhidharmic redaction beyond the *Sigālovāda*'s six. The thirty-five failings extend across six broad domains — present-life material loss, bodily illness, domestic rupture, social reputation, psychological state, and rebirth consequence — articulating the intoxicant precept's protecting-the-path status with detailed doctrinal precision against the cross-school background of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s Sarvāstivāda-Sautrāntika synthesis. The same *śāstra*'s fascicle 14 brings in the *prajñā*-pāramitā register's double negation — "neither grasping the precepts nor abandoning the precepts" — articulating the Madhyamaka standpoint on the ultimate-truth-layer status of precepts as neither to be grasped nor to be abandoned, anticipating the bodhisattva-stratum *prajñā*-treatment reserved for P12. The *Abhidharma-samuccaya* (Asaṅga; Xuanzang, trans.) at fascicles 4–5 sets out the three kinds of restraint (*saṃvara*) — the *prātimokṣa* restraint, the *dhyāna* restraint, and the *anāsrava* restraint — and the architectural distinction "violation is named by failure of achievement," in which non-achievement of a precept constitutes its violation.[^18] This architecture parallels the *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra*'s partial-reception framework at a different abhidharmic site: the five precepts are receivable in stages and constitute graded accomplishments, not all-or-nothing absolute receptions. The *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā* (Sthiramati; Xuanzang, trans.) at fascicles 7–8 supplies the ten-*kamma*-course *kleśa*-culmination analysis already noted at §III.5: the killing *kamma*-course culminates in hatred; the taking-what-is-not-given and sexual-misconduct *kamma*-courses culminate in greed; the false-speech *kamma*-course culminates in any of the three poisons; the remaining six *kamma*-courses (divisive speech, harsh speech, idle talk, covetousness, ill-will, wrong view) lack direct correspondence with the five precepts; intoxicants do not enter the ten *kamma*-courses at all.[^18]

### 5.3 The *Yogācārabhūmi*'s conditional-opening provision

The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (Xuanzang, trans., Tang Zhenguan 646–648 CE) at fascicles 40–42, the *Bodhisattvabhūmi-śīla-paṭala*, articulates the nine aspects of the bodhisattva's *śīla* together with the *tri-vidha-śīla-skandha* (the three pure precept-aggregates — the restraint precepts, the wholesome-dharma precepts, and the sentient-being-welfare precepts) and the bodhisattva-distinctive conditional-opening provisions. The conditional-opening provisions cover the four core precepts (killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, false speech) under specified welfare-of-sentient-beings conditions; **the intoxicant precept is not covered by the conditional-opening provision and is maintained at the absolute deontic level even within the bodhisattva framework**.[^17] This treatment, taken together with the Āgama-layer four-*kamma*-course framing and the Yogācāra-abhidharmic ten-*kamma*-course framing, constitutes the third layer of the cross-traditional architectural finding registered at §III.5 above. The present paper handles the *Yogācārabhūmi* at the level of single architectural finding only — the full unfolding of the nine bodhisattva-precept-aspects and the *tri-vidha-śīla-skandha* architecture is reserved for P12 and P14.

### 5.4 The vinaya cross-witness (brief mention only)

The five-precept reception formula is preserved across the three Chinese vinaya recensions — *Mahīśāsaka-vinaya* fascicle 15 (T1421, Mahīśāsaka school); *Dharmaguptaka-vinaya* fascicle 32 (T1428, Dharmaguptaka school); *Mūla-sarvāstivāda-vinaya* fascicle 7 (T1442, Mūlasarvāstivāda school) — each preserving the homologous reception-formula structure "abstaining from killing, from taking what is not given, from sexual misconduct, from false speech, from intoxicants." Together with the Theravāda Pāli vinaya's reception formula, the four-school cross-witness establishes the reception-formula's pre-sectarian doctrinal uniformity.[^20] The present paper notes the four-school cross-witness as architectural fact only; the per-precept casuistic adjudication of the vinaya traditions is reserved for P09 and P10.

### 5.5 Zhiyi's nature-precept / protective-precept distinction (brief mention only)

Zhiyi (智顗) at the *Mohe-zhiguan* fascicle 4-upper, in the section on the first of five preparatory conditions ("the purity of the precepts"), articulates the nature-precept (*prakṛti-śīla*, 性戒) / protective-precept (*pratikṣepaṇa-śīla*, 遮戒) distinction.[^19] The killing, stealing, sexual-misconduct, and false-speech precepts are nature-precepts (intrinsically ethical, holding force regardless of social-legal framing); the intoxicant precept is a protective-precept (intoxication is socially-legally permissible activity, constituted as a precept on account of its function as the basis of negligence and the consequent need for derivative protective enactment). The Chinese commentarial tradition's distinction homologously registers the cross-layer architectural finding of the intoxicant precept's protecting-the-path status, in parallel with the Āgama-layer four-*kamma*-course framing, the Yogācāra-abhidharmic ten-*kamma*-course framing, and the Mahāyāna bodhisattva-precept conditional-opening framing — across four layers of canonical, scholastic, and commentarial tradition, the intoxicant precept consistently receives the same protecting-the-path placement. The present paper handles Zhiyi at the level of single-sentence reference only; Zhiyi's four-teaching architecture (*Tripiṭaka* / shared / separate / perfect — 藏通別圓), the twenty-five preparatory means, and the threefold contemplation-and-cessation system are reserved for the Tiantai-tradition main scene. On the *Visuddhimagga* (Buddhaghosa, 5th century), chapter 1 *Sīla-niddesa*'s five-precept passage: Buddhaghosa treats *pañca-sīla* within the *catukka-sīla* (four-fold precept) architecture, placing the five precepts at the lay foundation layer of a graded sequence (lay five precepts → novice ten precepts → *bhikkhu* and *bhikkhunī pāṭimokkha*). The present paper references Buddhaghosa's treatment only at the chapter-section name level; the *Visuddhimagga*'s chapters 1 and 16–22 are absent from the present author's working corpus (a structural feature reconfirmed across P04, P05, and P06), and the GRETIL edition is licensed CC-BY-SA (paraphrased here, not quoted).[^21] Paragraph-number citations are deferred pending print collation against Ñāṇamoli (Buddhist Publication Society 4th edition). ---

## VI. Conclusion

Part II of the present volume opened at P05 with the categorical thesis of the three ethical limbs as constitutive path-elements; the present paper exposits the per-precept canonical scope of the five precepts under that thesis — the killing precept's gift-frame, the stealing precept's bodily-perceptual purity, the sexual-misconduct precept's chaste-life and lay definitional-gap, the false-speech precept's positive virtue formation, the intoxicant precept's consequentialist six-failings together with its self-explanatory canonical name and its protecting-the-path derivative status. The cross-traditional uniformity of the reception ritual and the cross-precept differential of the canonical scope coexist as two structural layers; "same ritual, different scope" is the architectural observation that orients the present paper. The next two papers carry the structure forward. P07 takes up the *uposatha* eight-precept observance as the cyclic intermittent-monasticism mechanism — the five-precept baseline supplemented by three further precepts (no high or large bed, no untimely eating, no song-dance-music-cosmetics) — with *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 5.213, 4.245, 11.1, and 8.41–8.43 as the four principal anchors. P08 takes up the scent-of-virtue (*sīla-gandha*) as the social-domain effect of the precepts, anchored at *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 3.79. Both papers proceed on the architectural ground that the present paper has surveyed: the five precepts are not a set of prohibitions but five distinct registers of the path's developmental emergence. The intoxicant precept's protecting-the-path status carries a distinctive structural position at the seam between the present volume and the next. *Paññāya dubbalikaraṇī*, the *Sigālovāda*'s name for what the intoxicant precept guards against, names the cognitive-layer protection on which the path itself rests; in the architectural connection between the SĪLA volume and the SAMĀDHI volume, the protection of *paññā* on the lay side prefigures the *kammaṭṭhāna* operational layer of the next volume. The intoxicant precept's lay-side preparatory function constitutes a concrete advance preparation from the SĪLA volume for the SAMĀDHI volume to come. ---

### Cross-volume relations

- **DĀNA volume** — the *mahā-dāna* (great gift) anchor at AN 8.39 is shared canonical material between the killing-precept's gift-of-fearlessness reading (the present paper §III.1) and the *dāna* foundation of the DĀNA volume. The present paper treats this internally as an architectural argument for the killing-precept's path-element status, rather than as a cross-volume polyphony anchor footnote (per the cross-volume polyphony discipline of RAG_BRIEF_SOP §11).
- **SAMĀDHI volume forward** — the intoxicant-precept's *paññāya dubbalikaraṇī* ("weakening of wisdom") canonical phrase functions as an architectural bridge from SĪLA to SAMĀDHI: the lay-layer protection of the cognitive faculty of *paññā* prefigures the *kammaṭṭhāna* (meditation-object) operational layer of the next volume.

### Octalogy (sister series) relation

- The octalogy (八部曲) series treats *sīla* doctrinally as one of the three trainings (*tri-śikṣā*); the SĪLA volume of the Six Practice Gates treats *sīla* developmentally — the path-oriented (行) counterpart of the doctrinal (見 / 慧學) treatment. The present paper's per-precept canonical-scope differential argument is path-oriented; the octalogy's pañca-sīla treatment is by-and-large at the doctrinal-overview register.
