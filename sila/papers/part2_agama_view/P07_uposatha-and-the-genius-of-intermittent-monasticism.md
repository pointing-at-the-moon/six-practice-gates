**Five Lay Precepts and Three Monastic Borrowings, One Day and One Night**

## Abstract

The eight precepts of the *aṭṭhaṅga-uposatha* — abstention from killing, from taking what is not given, from unchastity, from false speech, from intoxicants, from eating at the wrong time, from dancing and singing and music and shows, garlands and perfumes and cosmetics, and from high and luxurious beds — appear on the canonical surface within a single *veramaṇī* formula and present to the casual reader as a quantitative arithmetic: five lay precepts with three additional restrictions stacked on top. The present paper, the third of Part II of the SĪLA volume, asks whether this surface arithmetic is the architecture, or whether the eight-factor formula encodes a categorically different design. The argument advanced is that the eight precepts of the uposatha are not a quantitative extension of the five but a "five lay precepts plus three monastic borrowings" *borrow-and-return architecture*: the lay practitioner, on the lunar observance day, temporarily adopts three monastic restraints (full celibacy upgraded from the lay-side *kāmesumicchācāra*; abstention from food after midday; and the bodily-and-sensory triad of restraints) and returns them at the appearance of dawn (*aruṇuggamana*) the following day. The one-day-and-one-night time-bound observance is not a concession to lay weakness but a *canonical structural design feature*, and the uposatha is — as a canonical category — what the present paper names *intermittent monasticism*. Four lines of evidence converge on this reading. First, the eight-factor formula exhibits an architectural stability across six independent witnesses spanning two-and-a-half centuries: AN 8.41 *Saṅkhitta-uposatha-sutta* in Pāli; *Madhyama-āgama* 202 *Pratigraha-sūtra* (trans. Saṅghadeva 僧伽提婆, Eastern Jin Lóng-ān, 397–398 CE); *Ekottara-āgama* fascicle 16 *Sānzhāi-pǐn*; *Zhāi-jīng* (T0087, trans. Zhi Qian 支謙, Wu 3rd century — attribution corrected from the traditional ascription to An Shigao per the byline at CBETA T01n0087); *Upāsika-uposatha-sūtra* (T0088, anonymous, appended to the Sòng catalogue, linguistically dated to the 2nd–3rd century after Nattier 2008); and *Aṣṭāṅga-uposatha-sūtra* (T0089, trans. Juqu Jingsheng 沮渠京聲, Liu Song, 5th century). Across all six the content of the eight precepts is fully stable; only the position of the abstention from eating at the wrong time is permuted between the sixth and the last factor; no witness adds, removes, or modifies any precept. Second, AN 3.70 *Uposatha-sutta* supplies the canonical-internal philological key in its three-typology of uposatha (*gopālaka-uposatha*, the cowherd's; *nigaṇṭha-uposatha*, the Jain's; *ariy-uposatha*, the noble one's) and in the *ariy-uposatha*'s central reception formula *imañca rattiṁ imañca divasaṁ arahataṁ anukaromi · iminā ca me aṅgena uposatho upavuttho bhavissati* ("for this day and this night I imitate the Arahants; by this factor the uposatha will have been observed"): the verb *anukaromi* is a first-person-singular active indicative, with the lay practitioner herself as the grammatical subject and *arahataṁ* (genitive plural, "of the Arahants") as the object of imitation. Third, the *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā* (T1606, trans. Xuanzang 玄奘, 646–648 CE) at fascicle 7 places the uposatha within the eight categories of pratimokṣa-restraint as the *ahorātrika-saṃvara* — the day-and-night restraint, the *near-dwelling restraint* (近住律儀) — sitting architecturally between the seven lay-monastic categories, and supplies the doctrinal justification "but the day-and-night near-dwelling restraint is prescribed in order that they may train gradually together" (但制日夜近住律儀,為令漸漸俱修學故): the uposatha's *propaedeutic function* as the bridge from lay to monastic ethical formation. Fourth, the *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* (T1488, trans. Dharmakṣema 曇無讖, Northern Liáng, Xuán-shǐ 15 / 426 CE) at fascicle 7 poses the architectural question to which the borrow-and-return reading supplies the functional answer: "for what reason are the five precepts received for the duration of life, but the eight-precept fasting observance for one day and one night?" (何緣五戒盡形壽受,八戒齋法一日一夜?), together with the time-bound's canonical formulation "when dawn appears, then [the precepts] are released" (明相出時,是時則失). On the commentarial side, Dàoxuān 道宣 (Tang 7th century) at the *Sifenlü-xingshichao* (T1804) fascicle 下, section "Three Modes of the Eight-Precept Procedure" (三明八戒法, T40 p.140b), codifies the uposatha reception into three modes — including a self-administered "mental-utterance" option (心念口言) with no Pāli parallel, a Chinese-vinaya innovation that architecturally maximises lay accessibility. Zhiyi 智顗 (Sui 6th century) at the *Mohe-zhiguan* (T1911) contains zero references to the uposatha, an *architecturally significant absence* that registers the Chinese disciplinary division of labour between Tiantai's perfect-and-sudden precept programme and the vinaya school's lay-precept oversight. The propaedeutic function of the one-day-and-one-night observance prefigures the *sīla-gandha* social-field phenomenon developed in P08 and the SĪLA → SAMĀDHI developmental gradient consummated in P24. Ten Pāli loci directly relevant to the present paper's argument are absent from the present author's working corpus and are accordingly cited by PTS reference only, without verbatim Pāli text, in line with the firewall carried over from P04, P05, and P06.

**Keywords:** the eight-precept uposatha (*aṭṭhaṅga-uposatha*), one-day-and-one-night time-bound observance, intermittent monasticism, borrow-and-return architecture, "I imitate the Arahants" (*arahataṁ anukaromi*), the three-typology of uposatha (cowherd / Jain / noble disciple, AN 3.70), the day-and-night restraint (*ahorātrika-saṃvara*, 近住律儀), propaedeutic function (T1606), the explicit thesis question (T1488 fascicle 7), Dàoxuān's "mental-utterance" self-administration (T1804), the *Mohe-zhiguan*'s architecturally significant absence (T1911), six-witness cross-recension architectural stability, five lay precepts and three monastic borrowings, dawn-appearance terminator (*aruṇuggamana* · 明相出時).

## I. The Problem

The preceding paper of the present volume's Part II (P06, *The Five Precepts and Their Canonical Scope*) advanced a categorical claim about the *pañca-sīla*: the five precepts are not a homogeneous list of lay-side prohibitions but a set of five constitutive path-elements, each carrying a different canonical scope under a single reception ritual — what was named there the "same ritual, different scope" architecture.[^1] But the structure of *sīla* in the lay practitioner's developmental trajectory is not exhausted by the standing five precepts. At periodic intervals during the lunar month — on the eighth, the fourteenth, and the fifteenth days, or, in the cross-traditional variant attested in T1442 *Mūla-sarvāstivāda-vinaya*, on the four quarterly observance days (eighth, fifteenth, twenty-third, and month-end), and in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s extension calendar at T1509 fascicle 13, on the six monthly observance days — the lay practitioner adopts a different formula: the *aṭṭhaṅga-uposatha*, the eight-factor uposatha, in which the standing five precepts are joined by three further restraints. The eight precepts appear on the surface within a single *veramaṇī* formula and read at first glance as a quantitative arithmetic: five plus three equals eight. The central question of the present paper is whether this arithmetic is the architecture: **are the eight precepts of the uposatha a quantitative extension of the five, or do they encode a categorically different structural design?** The answer to this question is supplied within the canon itself by AN 3.70 *Uposatha-sutta*, in a passage of striking architectural precision. The discourse opens with a three-fold typology of uposatha observance. The first is the *gopālaka-uposatha* — the cowherd's uposatha — in which the lay practitioner outwardly observes the precepts but inwardly attends to the consumption to come, as a cowherd attending the herd in pasture attends to the richer grass and the better water on tomorrow's route: formal observance with the mind on tomorrow's pasture. The second is the *nigaṇṭha-uposatha* — the Jain's uposatha — in which abstention from killing is configured ritualistically by spatial demarcation: one abstains from killing living beings within a delimited radius, but the precept does not extend beyond it; the formal scope of abstention is bound to a perimeter rather than to a categorical commitment, and the observance is therefore ritualistic rather than transformative. The third is the *ariy-uposatha* — the noble one's uposatha — in which the practitioner undertakes the six-fold recollection prelude (*cha anussatiyo*: of the Buddha, the Dhamma, the Saṅgha, *sīla*, *cāga*, and *devatā*), the eight-fold precept formula, and the central reception line *imañca rattiṁ imañca divasaṁ arahataṁ anukaromi · iminā ca me aṅgena uposatho upavuttho bhavissati* — "for this day and this night I imitate the Arahants; by this factor the uposatha will have been observed."[^2] The canonical status of the formula *arahataṁ anukaromi* — "I imitate the Arahants" — deserves particular emphasis. It is **not** a later commentarial gloss imposed on the suttas, nor is it a monastic exhortation directed at the laity, nor is it a programmatic ideal supplied by the modern interpreter. It is the canonical reception line of the *ariy-uposatha* itself, embedded within the sutta's own articulation of what the noble disciple's uposatha *is*. Its grammar is decisive: *anukaromi* is a first-person-singular active indicative, taking the lay practitioner as its grammatical subject; *arahataṁ* is a genitive plural, "of the Arahants," supplying the object of imitation. The lay practitioner — herself, in the first person, in the act of undertaking the uposatha — declares her imitation of the Arahants for the duration of the day and the night. The formula is the canon's own emic structural description of what uposatha observance is in the noble-disciple register: the periodic, time-bound adoption by the lay practitioner of the Arahant's mode of dwelling. Read in this canonical light, the eight precepts of the uposatha decompose into a *borrow-and-return architecture*. The first five precepts retain the surface form of the lay *pañca-sīla*, but at the third precept a decisive categorical shift occurs. The lay third precept — *kāmesumicchācārā veramaṇī*, abstention from sexual misconduct — is keyed in its lay scope to behaviour outside the licit marital relation. The uposatha third precept — *abrahmacariyā veramaṇī*, abstention from unchastity — is keyed instead to the monastic-grade categorical commitment *brahmacārī ārācārī virato methunā gāmadhammā*: "chaste, dwelling apart, refraining from the vulgar practice of sexual intercourse," in which *methuna* (sexual intercourse) under the rubric of *gāma-dhamma* (the "village practice") names all sexual conduct without internal distinction. The uposatha third precept is not a strengthening of the lay third precept but a borrowing of the monastic third precept in its place. The latter three precepts — abstention from eating at the wrong time, abstention from high and luxurious beds, and the long-compound seventh precept of abstention from dancing and singing and music and shows, from garlands and perfumes and cosmetics, from jewellery and ornaments — are not lay precepts at all; they are direct borrowings from the monastic restraint repertoire. The lay practitioner on the uposatha day adopts these monastic restraints for one day and one night and returns them at dawn. The temporal terminator is the *aruṇuggamana*, the "dawn appearance," whose Chinese canonical formulation at T1488 fascicle 7 reads "when dawn appears, then [the precepts] are released" (明相出時,是時則失): the borrowed restraints are released, the standing five-precept discipline resumes, and the uposatha is concluded. The architecture is what the present paper names *intermittent monasticism*: a canonical design in which the lay practitioner periodically adopts the Arahant's mode of dwelling, samples it for one day and one night, and returns it, without the permanent renunciative commitment that the going-forth (*pabbajjā*) requires. This architectural reading receives further confirmation in the philological record. The eight-factor formula is attested across six independent witnesses spanning approximately two-and-a-half centuries of transmission and translation: AN 8.41 *Saṅkhitta-uposatha-sutta* (Pāli, the *Mahāsaṅgīti* CC0 edition); *Madhyama-āgama* 202 *Pratigraha-sūtra* (T0026 fascicle 55, trans. Saṅghadeva, Eastern Jin Lóng-ān 397–398 CE);[^8] *Ekottara-āgama* fascicle 16 *Sānzhāi-pǐn* (T0125, Gautama Saṅghadeva and the Buddhayaśas 佛陀耶舍 circle, 4th–5th centuries);[^9] *Zhāi-jīng* (T0087, trans. Zhi Qian, Wu, 3rd century — attribution corrected from the traditional ascription to An Shigao 安世高 per the byline at CBETA T01n0087);[^12] *Upāsika-uposatha-sūtra* (T0088, anonymous, appended to the Sòng catalogue, linguistically dated to the 2nd–3rd century after Nattier 2008);[^13] and *Aṣṭāṅga-uposatha-sūtra* (T0089, trans. Juqu Jingsheng, Liu Song, 5th century).[^14] Across all six the content of the eight precepts is fully stable; the only variation is in the position of *vikāla-bhojanā veramaṇī* (the abstention from eating at the wrong time), which occurs either as the sixth or as the last of the eight; no witness adds, removes, or substantively modifies any precept. This 250-year, six-witness *architectural stability* across two canonical-language traditions and multiple independent translation-courts is itself the strongest philological argument that the eight-factor formula is a canonical structural design, not an occasional liturgical assembly.[^24] The present paper's architectural thesis is grounded, accordingly, in a four-axis evidence chain: (1) the six-witness cross-recension stability of the eight-factor formula across two-and-a-half centuries; (2) the canonical-internal *arahataṁ anukaromi* formula at AN 3.70, with its grammatical decisiveness in placing the lay practitioner as subject and the Arahants as object of imitation; (3) the abhidharmic doctrinal articulation at *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā* fascicle 7, which classifies the uposatha as the *ahorātrika-saṃvara*, the eighth of the eight pratimokṣa-categories, and supplies the propaedeutic justification "in order that they may train gradually together"; and (4) the explicit thesis question posed in the Chinese Mahāyāna lay-precept stratum at *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* fascicle 7 — "for what reason are the five precepts received for the duration of life, but the eight-precept fasting observance for one day and one night?" The four axes intersect at a single architectural conclusion. The uposatha is the canon's own design of a periodic, time-bound, lay-practitioner adoption of the Arahant's mode of dwelling, and the one-day-and-one-night time-bound is a *canonical structural design feature* rather than a concessive accommodation. ---

## II. Canonical Sources

### 2.1 The Pāli axis: the three-typology, the eight-factor formula, and the dual register of the benefits of *sīla*

The Pāli anchor of the present paper is AN 3.70 *Uposatha-sutta*, which supplies, in a single discourse, the three-typological discrimination of uposatha observances, the six-fold recollection prelude of the *ariy-uposatha*, and the central reception formula *imañca rattiṁ imañca divasaṁ arahataṁ anukaromi · iminā ca me aṅgena uposatho upavuttho bhavissati*.[^2] The three-typology is not a rhetorical contrast but a categorical discrimination of what counts as uposatha at all. The cowherd's uposatha (*gopālaka-uposatha*) fails not by lapse of the outward observance — the formal precepts are kept — but by the internal disposition of the mind, which attends to consumption rather than to renunciation. The Jain's uposatha (*nigaṇṭha-uposatha*) fails not by lapse of the outward observance — the formal abstention from killing is performed — but by the spatial ritualisation of the precept, which configures abstention as a geographical perimeter rather than a categorical commitment. Only the noble one's uposatha, in which the six-fold recollection prelude, the eight-factor precepts, and the *arahataṁ anukaromi* emulation framework converge as a tripartite whole, qualifies as the noble disciple's observance. The discourse's three-typological discrimination is therefore not merely a taxonomy but the canon's own internal answer to the question "what makes an uposatha a true uposatha." This three-typology functions as the structural foundation of §III.3 below. AN 8.41 *Saṅkhitta-uposatha-sutta* supplies the eight-factor formula in its full canonical articulation: abstention from killing, from taking what is not given, from unchastity (with the categorical upgrade to *brahmacārī ārācārī virato methunā gāmadhammā*), from false speech, from intoxicants, from eating at the wrong time (*vikāla-bhojanā*), from dancing and singing and music and shows, from garlands and perfumes and cosmetics, from jewellery and ornaments (the long-compound seventh precept *nacca-gīta-vādita-visūkadassana-mālā-gandha-vilepana-dhāraṇa-maṇḍana-vibhūsana-ṭṭhānā*), and from high and luxurious beds (*uccā-sayana-mahā-sayanā*).[^4] Two philological clarifications concerning the immediately adjacent suttas merit notice. First, AN 8.42 *Vitthata-uposatha-sutta* and AN 8.43 *Visākhā-uposatha-sutta* are not two further independent eight-factor formulae; both abbreviate the eight-factor content with the elision marker *…pe…* and then append, respectively, the comparison with the sixteen great realms together with a six-tiered cosmological articulation (AN 8.42), and an extended instruction directed to the laywoman Visākhā (AN 8.43); the eight-factor content of both is identical to AN 8.41.[^4] Second, AN 9.18 *Navaṅguposatha-sutta* records a nine-factor variant — the eight precepts together with a ninth, mindful loving-kindness directed in all directions; this discourse is absent from the present author's working corpus and is cited by PTS reference (PTS AN iv 388) only.[^7] The nine-factor variant is documented for completeness; the eight-factor formula remains the canonical centre, as the philological consensus across the six witnesses confirms. The canonical articulation of the benefits of *sīla* is given in two complementary registers within the Pāli axis. AN 5.213 *Sīlasutta* sets out the worldly-result register through its 5+5 enumeration: the five drawbacks of breach of precepts (dissipation of wealth, evil reputation, loss of confidence in social gatherings, confused death, and rebirth in a state of woe) and their five corresponding benefits in the opposite ethical state (prosperity, good reputation, confidence in gatherings, unconfused death, and rebirth in a heavenly state).[^3] This enumeration is the canonical articulation of *sīla*'s consequences in the worldly result-register, both in the present life and at the time of death. AN 11.1 *Kimatthiyasutta* sets out, in complementary register, the soteriological-trajectory expansion of *sīla*'s benefits as an eleven-link developmental chain: *sīla → avippaṭisāra → pāmojja → pīti → passaddhi → sukha → samādhi → yathābhūta-ñāṇadassana → nibbidā → virāga → vimuttiñāṇadassana* — ethical conduct, non-remorse, gladness, rapture, tranquillity, bliss, concentration, knowledge and vision of things as they are, disenchantment, dispassion, and the knowledge and vision of liberation.[^6] The first seven links of this chain — from *sīla* through *samādhi* — articulate the developmental gradient by which ethical conduct unfolds naturally into concentration. The two-fold register matters specifically for the uposatha. The day-and-night observance's immediate fruits fall under the AN 5.213 worldly-result register; the cumulative trajectory of repeated periodic observance unfolds along the AN 11.1 soteriological chain, with the lay practitioner experientially sampling the *sīla → samādhi* gradient at each observance day. The full eleven-link unpacking is reserved for the SĪLA → SAMĀDHI handoff at P24; the present paper cites only the first seven links as the canonical baseline for the lay-side rehearsal. A third Pāli register supplements the dual benefit-frame. AN 4.245 *Sikkhānisaṃsa-sutta* articulates the *sikkhā* / *sikkhāpada* register — training and training-rule — through the formula "this celibate life is led for the benefit of training, with wisdom as its summit, with liberation as its core, with mindfulness as its sovereign" (*sikkhānisaṁsam idaṁ brahmacariyaṁ vussati paññuttaraṁ vimuttisāraṁ satādhipateyyaṁ*).[^5] *Sikkhā* denotes the dynamic register of cultivation, distinct from the static register of *sīla* as established ethical character. The uposatha's reception formula names its eight constituents as *aṭṭhaṅga-sikkhāpada* — eight training-rules — and the choice of register itself carries the doctrinal content of "graduated cultivation," consonant with the propaedeutic function articulated at T1606.

### 2.2 The Chinese axis: four independent translations and the strongest *Āgama*-side anchor

The Chinese-side anchor of the present paper is *Madhyama-āgama* 202 *Pratigraha-sūtra* (持齋經, MA 202 at T0026 fascicle 55), translated by Saṅghadeva 僧伽提婆 in the Lóng-ān reign-era of the Eastern Jin (397–398 CE).[^8] The discourse records a laywoman undertaking the eight-factor observance and the Buddha articulating the reception formula in the Chinese-side counterpart of *arahataṁ anukaromi*: "for this reason, laywoman, having fully undertaken the eight-factor fast, on the day of the eight-factor fast you are like an Arahant, having of yourself crossed over birth and death. I likewise, on this present day and night, also practise the precepts, also practise the fast." The Chinese-side phrase "on this present day and night, like an Arahant" corresponds, by independent transmission within the same late-fourth-century horizon, to the Pāli formula *arahataṁ anukaromi*. The convergence of the Pāli and Chinese transmissions at the same canonical formula, in independent translation contexts, supplies the strongest single piece of evidence for the cross-traditional architectural stability of the emulation framework.[^24] Four further independent Chinese translations of dedicated uposatha sūtras are extant, three of them from the early translation period. **The earliest extant Chinese translation of a dedicated uposatha sūtra is *Zhāi-jīng* (T0087), translated by Zhi Qian 支謙 in the third century during the Wu kingdom period.** A philological correction is required at this point. The traditional Chinese catalogue tradition long ascribed T0087 to An Shigao of the second-century Later Han, but the byline at the head of the text in the CBETA Electronic Tripiṭaka (T01n0087) explicitly identifies the translator as Zhi Qian; the present paper follows the CBETA byline-ascription, treating the Zhi Qian attribution as the philologically supported one.[^12] The discourse contains a three-fold classification of fasting observances — "cowherd's fast" (牧牛齋), "Nigaṇṭha's fast" (尼揵齋), and "Buddha-Dharma fast" (佛法齋) — structurally identical to the Pāli AN 3.70 three-typology. T0087 is the principal *Āgama*-side anchor for the three-typological discrimination on the Chinese side, and predates the Saṅghadeva translation of MA 202 by approximately one hundred and fifty years. *Upāsika-uposatha-sūtra* (T0088, 優陂夷墮舍迦經) is anonymous; the text is appended to the Sòng catalogue (Sēng Yòu 僧祐's *Chū-sānzàng-jì-jí*) but neither the translation-court nor the translator is reconstructible. Linguistic dating after Nattier 2008 places the translation in the 2nd–3rd-century early-translation horizon, but the precise date and the translator remain unrecoverable.[^13] *Aṣṭāṅga-uposatha-sūtra* (T0089, 八關齋經) was translated by Juqu Jingsheng 沮渠京聲 in the Liu Song period of the 5th century, and supplies what is the most complete reception-formula articulation among the independent Chinese translations: "I, so-and-so, take refuge in the Buddha, take refuge in the Dharma, take refuge in the Saṅgha. For one day and one night, holding the mind as a True Person; not killing, not stealing, not engaging in unchastity, not lying, not drinking intoxicants, not eating at the wrong time, not dancing or singing, not lying on high beds."[^14] The phrase "holding the mind as a True Person" (一日一夜,持心如真人) bears particular philological weight: 真人 ("True Person") is the principal Chinese rendering of *arahant* in the Han–Six-Dynasties translation tradition, and the formula is therefore the strongest single-line Chinese rendering of the Pāli *arahataṁ anukaromi*. The Pāli and the Chinese transmissions converge on the same architectural formula in independent witnesses. Further *Āgama*-side evidence includes *Ekottara-āgama* fascicle 16 *Sānzhāi-pǐn* (T0125, the "Three Observance-Days Chapter"), which supplies the three observance-day calendar of the eighth, fourteenth, and fifteenth lunar days;[^9] *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 20 *Lokaprajñapti-sūtra* (T0001 *Shìjì-jīng*), trans. Buddhayaśas and Zhu Fonian 竺佛念, which records the three observance-day table within a cosmological frame articulating the Four Heavenly Kings' inspection of human conduct;[^9] and *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle 40 and fascicle 50 sūtra 1325 (T0099, trans. Guṇabhadra 求那跋陀羅), which provide, respectively, Indra's verses praising the uposatha and a cautionary narrative of breach of the observance.[^10] One philological clarification is required: *Dīrgha-āgama* fascicle 11 *Sigāla-sūtra* (DA 16), occasionally cited as a Chinese-side support for the eight-precept uposatha, contains only the six wealth-loss + six intoxicant-failings articulations and does **not** contain the eight-factor reception formula. DA 16 is the *Āgama*-side anchor for the intoxicant precept of P06's exposition; it is **not** a source for the uposatha, the *Dīrgha-āgama*-side uposatha anchor being instead DA fascicle 20 *Lokaprajñapti-sūtra*. The present paper preserves the distinction explicit in P06.[^11]

### 2.3 Cross-recension architectural stability: six witnesses, 250 years, eight precepts stable

Synthesising the Pāli and Chinese witnesses — AN 8.41 (Pāli); MA 202 (Eastern Jin 397–398 CE); *Ekottara-āgama* fascicle 16 (Eastern Jin 4th–5th centuries); T0087 (Wu 3rd century); T0088 (early translation period 2nd–3rd centuries); and T0089 (Liu Song 5th century) — the eight-factor formula's content is **fully stable** across the six witnesses; the only divergence is in the position of *vikāla-bhojanā veramaṇī*, which appears as the sixth or as the last of the eight; **no witness adds, removes, or substantively modifies any of the eight precepts**. The philological finding is of particular weight. From the 2nd to the 5th century, across the Pāli and the Chinese transmissions, across multiple independent translation-courts (Zhi Qian's southern China, Saṅghadeva's Xiangyang and Jiangnan, Juqu Jingsheng's Liu Song / Gaochang), the eight-factor formula maintains its architectural identity, with variation restricted to the order of two factors. **This 250-year, six-witness architectural stability is itself the strongest philological argument that the eight-factor uposatha formula is canonical structural design rather than occasional liturgical assembly.**[^24] A note on citation discipline is required before proceeding. The present paper engages, at §III and §V, with passages of Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* concerning *sīla* and its recollection (chapter I *Sīla-niddesa* and chapter VII §4 *Sīlānussati-kathā*). Chapter I has been confirmed across multiple checks to be a structural absence from the present author's working corpus of Pāli texts; chapter VII §4, though present in the working corpus, carries a licensing setting (GRETIL CC-BY-SA with **) that does not permit verbatim quotation. The present paper accordingly cites the *Visuddhimagga* in paraphrase at the chapter-section level, **without paragraph numbers**, pending print collation against Ñāṇamoli's translation (BPS 4th edition).[^22] By the same discipline, nine further Pāli loci of relevance — AN 9.18 *Navaṅguposatha-sutta*, AN 8.44 *Vāseṭṭha-sutta*, AN 8.45 *Bojjhā-sutta*, AN 7.50 *Methuna-sutta*, AN 5.176 *Pīti-sutta*, AN 5.179 *Gihī-sutta*, *Khuddakapāṭha* 2 *Dasa-sikkhāpada*, *Khuddakapāṭha* 5 *Maṅgala-sutta*, AN 4.61–62, AN 10.46 *Sakkasutta*, and *Sutta Nipāta* 2.14 *Dhammika-sutta* — are absent from the working corpus and are cited by PTS reference only, without verbatim Pāli text.[^7] ---

## III. The Eight-Factor Architecture: Five Lay Precepts and Three Monastic Borrowings

### 3.1 The first five precepts: the categorical upgrade at the third

The first five precepts of the *aṭṭhaṅga-uposatha* correspond, on the surface, to the five lay precepts of the standing *pañca-sīla*. But the apparent surface-correspondence conceals a decisive categorical shift located at the third precept. The lay third precept is *kāmesumicchācārā veramaṇī* — abstention from sexual misconduct — whose canonical scope, as P06 §3.3 set out at length, is keyed to behaviour outside the licit marital relation: sexual conduct with a partner who is not one's spouse counts as breach; sexual conduct within the licit marital relation does not fall under the precept's prohibition. The uposatha third precept is by contrast *abrahmacariyā veramaṇī* — abstention from unchastity — whose canonical reception line is *brahmacārī ārācārī virato methunā gāmadhammā*: "chaste, dwelling apart, refraining from the vulgar practice of sexual intercourse."[^4] The decisive lexical term is *methuna* (sexual intercourse) under the rubric of *gāma-dhamma* (the "village practice" or "vulgar practice"), which functions in the canonical idiom as a categorical designation for all sexual conduct **without** any internal distinction between licit and illicit forms. The uposatha third precept, on the day of observance, brings the lay practitioner's licit marital sexual conduct **also** under the scope of the precept. This is a categorical upgrade, not a quantitative extension: the social register of *brahmacārī*, in the canonical idiom, is the principal lexical marker of the monastic identity. The architectural significance of this upgrade is decisive for the central thesis of the present paper. Were the uposatha third precept a quantitative extension of the lay third precept (a "stronger sexual-misconduct precept"), the "5 + 3" arithmetic reading would be correct. But the uposatha third precept does not extend the canonical scope of *kāmesumicchācāra*; it substitutes the monastic-grade *brahmacariya* scope in its place. The displacement is categorical, not gradational. The remaining four of the first five precepts — abstention from killing, from taking what is not given, from false speech, and from intoxicants — retain their lay canonical scope on the uposatha day, identical with their scope under the standing *pañca-sīla* discipline. The asymmetry is itself the philological evidence: only at the third precept does the canonical scope shift from lay to monastic. The first five precepts of the uposatha are therefore not a "strengthened five-precept set" but a "four lay precepts retained plus one monastic precept borrowed in place of the corresponding lay precept" — the *borrow-and-return* mechanism already operating within the first five precepts. The reading clarifies two persistent misreadings of the uposatha third precept. The first misreading interprets the upgrade as a tightening of the *kāmesumicchācāra* scope (a "stricter sexual-misconduct precept for the observance day"), neglecting the categorical force of the term *brahmacārī* as a monastic-identity marker. The second misreading interprets the upgrade as the lay practitioner's "becoming a monastic for a day," in the sense of taking on the full monastic discipline; but the eight-factor uposatha is not the complete monastic *pātimokkha*, which comprises 227 rules in the Theravāda recension or 250 in the *Dharmaguptaka-vinaya* tradition. What the lay practitioner adopts on the uposatha day is **selective and time-bound**: not a complete monastic discipline, but eight specific restraints, three of which are categorically monastic in scope. The first misreading underestimates the categorical force of the upgrade; the second misreading overestimates the completeness of the adoption. The architectural reading that the present paper advances is more precise: the uposatha is a *selective and time-bound monastic borrowing*, neither a strengthened lay discipline nor a complete monastic ordination.

### 3.2 The latter three precepts: selective borrowing from the monastic restraint repertoire

The three precepts that distinguish the *aṭṭhaṅga-uposatha* from the standing *pañca-sīla* — abstention from eating at the wrong time (*vikāla-bhojanā veramaṇī*), abstention from high and luxurious beds (*uccā-sayana-mahā-sayanā veramaṇī*), and the long-compound seventh precept of abstention from dancing and singing and music and shows, garlands and perfumes and cosmetics, jewellery and ornaments (*nacca-gīta-vādita-visūkadassana-mālā-gandha-vilepana-dhāraṇa-maṇḍana-vibhūsana-ṭṭhānā veramaṇī*) — are taken in their entirety from the monastic restraint repertoire.[^4] The selection is not arbitrary. Within the novice *dasa-sikkhāpada* and the monastic *pātimokkha*, these three correspond respectively to the sixth, the integrated seventh-and-eighth, and the integrated ninth-and-tenth restraints in the standard novice precept enumeration. The selection covers three specific monastic-life dimensions that interface most directly with lay daily life and are therefore the natural site for lay-side periodic sampling. **The sixth precept, abstention from eating at the wrong time.** The monastic discipline structures the day around one meal taken before noon and abstention from food after midday. The restraint directly addresses the lay practitioner's choice of *when to eat*. The lay practitioner who undertakes the uposatha takes no further food after midday, for one day and one night, and thereby samples the basic dietary structure of monastic life. **The seventh precept, the long-compound abstention from entertainments and adornments.** The monastic discipline forbids participation in worldly entertainment (dance, song, instrumental music, theatrical spectacles), the wearing of garlands and perfumes, and bodily ornamentation. The restraint addresses the lay practitioner's social life and outward self-presentation. The lay practitioner on the uposatha day abstains from entertainment, perfume, and ornament, sampling the monastic withdrawal from the public sphere of social-aesthetic display. **The eighth precept (or the eight-precept set's terminal precept), abstention from high and luxurious beds.** The monastic discipline forbids lavish furnishings of repose. The restraint addresses the lay practitioner's material conditions of *how to rest*. The lay practitioner on the uposatha day sleeps on a low bed or on the floor, sampling the material simplicity of the monastic dwelling. The three precepts together sample three specific dimensions of monastic daily life — **diet, social-aesthetic presentation, and material conditions of repose** — through a minimal set of restraints covering the most lay-accessible monastic interfaces. The selection is the canon's minimum sampling of the monastic life-form: a small number of restraints covering the largest accessible cross-section. The eight-factor uposatha is therefore the two-layer architecture of (a) the standing five precepts with one categorical upgrade at the third precept, and (b) the selective borrowing of three monastic restraints across the three principal monastic-life dimensions — and not the one-layer architecture of "five precepts with three additional restraints" that the surface arithmetic would suggest. The two-layer architecture is the borrow-and-return architecture.

### 3.3 The *arahataṁ anukaromi* formula: the canonical-internal articulation of intermittent monasticism

The exposition of §§3.1 and 3.2 has set out the eight precepts as a "five lay precepts plus three monastic borrowings" architecture in its philological articulation. The architectural reading requires, however, a canonical-internal warrant — an explicit statement, in the canon's own voice, that the lay practitioner on the uposatha day undertakes the Arahant's mode of dwelling. This warrant is supplied by the reception formula of AN 3.70's *ariy-uposatha*: *imañca rattiṁ imañca divasaṁ arahataṁ anukaromi · iminā ca me aṅgena uposatho upavuttho bhavissati* — "for this day and this night I imitate the Arahants; by this factor the uposatha will have been observed."[^2] The canonical weight of this formula deserves articulation at two levels. The first is the grammatical level. The verb *anukaromi* is a first-person-singular active indicative; the subject is the lay practitioner herself, in the act of undertaking the observance. The object of imitation is supplied by *arahataṁ*, the genitive plural "of the Arahants" — the Arahants as the canonical category of the liberated, the noblest taxonomic identity in the Pāli canonical typology. The grammatical structure of the formula directly aligns the lay practitioner's first-person undertaking with the Arahants' mode of dwelling. The alignment is not metaphorical, not comparative, not aspirational; it is grammatical, and grammatical at the heart of the reception line itself. The formula does not say "may I become like the Arahants" or "as if I were an Arahant" or "in remembrance of the Arahants" — it says "I imitate the Arahants," in the active indicative, for the duration of this day and this night. The second is the level of canonical placement. The formula is not located within the post-canonical commentarial layer or within a hermeneutical reading imposed on the text; it stands within the reception formula of the *ariy-uposatha* itself, embedded in the canonical articulation of what the noble disciple's uposatha *is*. The *raison d'être* of the lay practitioner's uposatha is not, on this canonical articulation, simply the temporary intensification of restraint or the accumulation of merit; it is the alignment of the lay practitioner's conduct, for the duration of one day and one night, with the Arahant's mode of dwelling. The significance of this alignment becomes clearer against the three-typological backdrop of AN 3.70. The cowherd's uposatha fails because the formal observance is undertaken with the mind on tomorrow's pasture: *arahataṁ anukaromi* is absent as an internal disposition, and the observance is reduced to outward form. The Jain's uposatha fails because abstention is ritualised by spatial boundary: the categorical commitment of the Arahant's mode of dwelling is reduced to a geographical perimeter, and the observance is reduced to ritualistic precision. Only the noble one's uposatha, in which the six-fold recollection prelude, the eight-factor precepts, and the *arahataṁ anukaromi* internal alignment converge as a tripartite whole, qualifies as the canonical noble-disciple observance. The eight-factor precepts in this canonical context are not formal restraints under a quantitative description but the practical articulation of the Arahant's mode of dwelling across the dimensions of diet, sexual conduct, social-aesthetic display, and material conditions — the four sampled dimensions of intermittent monasticism. The reading clarifies, finally, that "intermittent monasticism" is not an extrinsic interpretive category imposed on the canonical material from outside; it is the direct philological articulation of what AN 3.70 says the *ariy-uposatha* is. The lay practitioner on the uposatha day enacts, for one day and one night, the periodic emulation of the Arahant's mode of dwelling. The uposatha is, in this canonical articulation, the Arahant's mode of dwelling in its lay-side periodic return.

### 3.4 The one-day-and-one-night time-bound: canonical structural design

The remaining question concerns the time-bound of the observance: why is the *aṭṭhaṅga-uposatha* limited to one day and one night, terminating at dawn the following morning? The question is not a modern interpretive overlay; it is posed explicitly within the canonical literature itself, at the Mahāyāna lay-precept stratum of *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* fascicle 7 (T1488, Dharmakṣema, Northern Liáng, Xuán-shǐ 15 / 426 CE): "for what reason are the five precepts received for the duration of life, but the eight-precept fasting observance for one day and one night?" (何緣五戒盡形壽受,八戒齋法一日一夜?).[^15] The same fascicle supplies the time-bound's canonical formulation: "when dawn appears, then [the precepts] are released" (明相出時,是時則失) — at the *aruṇuggamana*, the canonical dawn-appearance, the borrowed restraints are released and the standing five-precept discipline resumes. The time-bound is therefore not a matter of pastoral practice or local custom but a canonical structural feature of the observance. The doctrinal articulation of why this time-bound is structurally designed (rather than concessively granted) is supplied at the abhidharmic stratum. Sthiramati's *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā* (T1606), translated by Xuanzang in 646–648 CE, at fascicle 7, places the uposatha within the eight categories of *pratimokṣa-saṃvara* (the eight pratimokṣa-restraint categories) — *bhikṣu*, *bhikṣuṇī*, *sikkhamānā*, *sāmaṇera*, *sāmaṇerī*, *upāsaka*, *upāsikā*, and as the eighth category the *ahorātrika-saṃvara*, the day-and-night restraint or *near-dwelling restraint* (近住律儀). The category is named by its time-bound: *ahorātrika* renders, in Sanskrit, "of the day and the night"; the Chinese equivalent 近住律儀 carries the gloss of "dwelling near [the monastic life]." The architectural placement is decisive: the uposatha sits as the eighth of eight pratimokṣa-categories, **between** the seven lay-monastic ordination categories — neither a pure lay-restraint nor a pure monastic-ordination category, but the canonical category of "dwelling near the monastic life for the day and the night." The same fascicle then supplies the doctrinal justification: "but the day-and-night near-dwelling restraint is prescribed in order that they may train gradually together" (但制日夜近住律儀,為令漸漸俱修學故).[^18] The phrase 漸漸俱修學 — "training gradually together" — articulates the uposatha's *propaedeutic function* as the architectural bridge from lay to monastic ethical formation. The lay practitioner cannot, without warrant, undertake the permanent commitments of the going-forth; but the lay practitioner can, on the periodic observance day, sample the Arahant's mode of dwelling without that permanent commitment. The sampling is itself the gradual training; the periodic return is the canonical design of the gradient. The reading lifts the one-day-and-one-night time-bound from the position of a concessive accommodation to the position of a *canonical structural design feature*. The time-bound is not granted to the lay practitioner because she cannot sustain the Arahant's mode of dwelling; it is designed for the lay practitioner because the periodic adoption is itself the canonical mechanism of gradual training toward the full monastic life — for those who would so progress — and the canonical mechanism of repeated sampling without permanent commitment for those who would remain at the lay-side periodic return. The architectural reading reframes the uposatha, in consequence, from a "lay-side compromise" to a "canonical design of intermittent monasticism." The treatment of *śīla*-substance theory at P16 and the unpacking of Dàoxuān's vinaya commentary at P17 will resume the present paper's architectural articulation at deeper levels. ---

## IV. Scholarly Dialogue

The present chapter continues the three-cast format inherited from P01 through P06 — Bhikkhu Anālayo, Rupert Gethin, and Bhikkhu Bodhi — each engaged through the *Concede + Extend* two-step structure debuted in P04 and continued through P05 and P06. Peter Harvey is brought in as a secondary scholarly voice, not enrolled in the three-cast, for systematic Western-academic corroboration on the dual-axis lay-monastic ethical reading.[^23]

### 4.1 Bhikkhu Anālayo: cross-recension stability → architectural functional reading

Bhikkhu Anālayo's *Saṃyukta-āgama Studies* (Dharma Drum Publishing, 2015) is the methodological template for contemporary cross-recension comparative studies. His method foregrounds the parallel between the Pāli Nikāyas and the Chinese Āgamas, treating cross-recension comparison as the primary evidentiary axis for observing the stability and variability of canonical passages across translation traditions. The "six-witness 250-year architectural stability of the eight-precept uposatha formula" demonstrated in §2.3 of the present paper is a direct application of Anālayo's method. **Concede:** Anālayo's cross-recension comparative method is the philological foundation of the present paper; without it, the parallel six-witness stability finding could not be established. **Extend:** Anālayo's research, however, contains no dedicated chapter on *uposatha*; his contribution is at the level of methodological template, not substantive interlocutor. The present paper builds on this methodological foundation and advances to **the architectural functional reading**: the cross-recension stability is not merely a philological fact but architectural evidence that the eight-precept formula is canonical structural design rather than occasional liturgical assembly. The key to this advance is that philological stability must be elevated to architectural functional reading in order to answer T1488 fascicle 7's internal question, "Why are the five precepts received for life, but the eight-precept uposatha for [only] one day and one night?" — the answer to this question lies not at the philological layer but at the functional reading that "the one-day-and-one-night is a canonical structural design feature."[^23]

### 4.2 Rupert Gethin: the *catuparisā* monastic-lay interface → the architecture of the lay practitioner's internal status-transposition

Rupert Gethin's *The Foundations of Buddhism* (Oxford University Press, 1998), Chapter 9 "The Buddhist community: monks, nuns, and lay followers," treats the *uposatha* within the *catuparisā* (fourfold assembly) framework: on the 8th, 14th, and 15th of the lunar month, the lay practitioner enters the monastery, receives the precepts, hears the Dhamma, and participates in the communal *uposatha* observance, constituting a monastic-lay interface event between the renunciant and lay assemblies. **Concede:** Gethin's *catuparisā* framework provides an effective analytical architecture for grasping the sociality and ritual character of the *uposatha*; the joint assembly of lay and monastic disciples on observance days is indeed a key interface event in the Buddhist community. **Extend:** Gethin's analysis, however, stops at the externalist description of the "interface event" and **does not address the lay practitioner's internal status-transposition on the uposatha day**. The present paper advances a step further: the *uposatha* is not merely the interface between two assemblies but **the architectural mechanism by which the lay practitioner internally borrows monastic restraints in a periodic status-transposition**. The lay practitioner not only joins the monastic assembly in collective observance but, more decisively, *herself* borrows the monastic restraints for this day and night, *herself* emulates the Arahants' mode of dwelling — this internal status-transposition is the canonical structural dimension that Gethin's framework does not reach.[^23]

### 4.3 Bhikkhu Bodhi: the eight-precept monograph precedent → the "five lay precepts and three monastic borrowings" architectural decomposition

Bhikkhu Bodhi's *Going for Refuge and Taking the Precepts* (Buddhist Publication Society Wheel 282/283/284, 1981) is the **single strongest dedicated-chapter monograph precedent** in Western scholarship on the eight-precept uposatha. Part III of the work, "The Eight Precepts," directly takes the per-precept exposition of the eight restraints as its subject matter, providing per-precept commentary together with a dual framing of the one-day-and-one-night time-bound (training-rules of discipline + foundation of moral life).[^23] **Concede:** Bodhi's monograph treatment is the strongest single Western-scholarly precedent for the present paper's subject; his per-precept commentary plus dual framing supplies concrete reference points for the expositions in §3.1–§3.2 of the present paper. (P05 cited Bodhi's *The Noble Eightfold Path*; P06 cited *Going for Refuge* Part II on the five precepts; the present paper enters Part III of the same series of monograph precedents.) **Extend:** Bodhi's monograph, however, stops at per-precept exposition plus conceptual integration; it **does not enter the "five lay precepts plus three monastic borrowings, with borrow-and-return architecture" decomposition**. The present paper advances a step further: the eight precepts are not a quantitative accumulation but a qualitative selective borrowing of monastic restraints, and this borrowing receives its canonical-internal architectural articulation in AN 3.70's *arahataṁ anukaromi* formula. Bodhi's dual framing of the training-rules + foundation captures the two faces of the precept observance but does not make explicit the canonical mechanism of "the lay practitioner borrowing monastic status for the observance day" — and this is the decisive advance of the present paper over Bodhi.

### 4.4 Peter Harvey: the lay precept system's internal stratification (secondary scholarly voice)

Peter Harvey's *An Introduction to Buddhist Ethics: Foundations, Values and Issues* (Cambridge University Press, 2000), Chapters 3–5, treats lay-monastic ethics together with the *uposatha* as the intermittent peak of the lay precept system.[^23] Harvey's stratification reading positions the *uposatha* as a periodic peak event in the lay practitioner's ethical formation, holding a special location within the lay precept system. This observation is compatible with the present paper's intermittent-monasticism reading and supplies an ethical-philosophical reading of the lay-monastic graded ladder as supplementary background. **Harvey is not enrolled in the §IV three-scholar dialogue cast** (the present paper carries forward the three-cast discipline of Anālayo / Gethin / Bodhi from P01–P06); his citations occur primarily at footnote tier.

### 4.5 Closing of the three-cast

Synthesising the three-cast — Anālayo's cross-recension philological stability + Gethin's *catuparisā* interface framework + Bodhi's per-precept monograph precedent — together with Harvey's stratification reading from ethical philosophy, the present paper advances one step beyond all four: **the intermittent-monasticism architecture of the *uposatha* (five lay precepts + three monastic borrowings + the one-day-and-one-night time-bound as canonical structural design feature) is anchored on three axes** — AN 3.70's *arahataṁ anukaromi* formula + T1606's doctrinal justification "in order to gradually train together" + T1488 fascicle 7's time-bound phrasing "when dawn appears, [the precepts] are released" — and is settled as the **canonical-internal architectural articulation** of the periodic-renunciation mechanism. The significance of the three-axis weaving lies in this: the architectural reading has internal Pāli-canonical articulation (AN 3.70), abhidharmic doctrinal codification (T1606), and Chinese-Mahāyāna lay-precept treatise's internal question (T1488) — a cross-tradition, cross-stratum chain of canonical evidence rather than the isolated claim of a single text.

## V. Abhidharmic Treatises + Vinaya + Chinese Commentarial Integration

### 5.1 The Yogācāra abhidharmic codification: the day-and-night restraint as the eighth of the eight pratimokṣa categories

Asaṅga's *Abhidharma-samuccaya* fascicles 4–5 and Sthiramati's *Abhidharma-samuccaya-vyākhyā* fascicles 7–8 (both translated by Xuanzang during the Tang Zhenguan period, 646–648 CE), within their classification of the *pratimokṣa-saṃvara* (codes of individual liberation through restraint), list the eight-precept uposatha as the **eighth restraint category**, naming it the **day-and-night restraint** (*ahorātrika-saṃvara* · 一日一夜律儀 / 近住律儀).[^18] The eight categories of *pratimokṣa-saṃvara* are, in order: the *bhikṣu* restraint, the *bhikṣuṇī* restraint, the *sikṣamāṇā* restraint (式叉摩那), the *sāmaṇera* restraint, the *sāmaṇerī* restraint, the *upāsaka* restraint (the layman's five precepts), the *upāsikā* restraint (the laywoman's five precepts), and the **day-and-night restraint** (the eight-precept uposatha). This categorical codification of the eight categories itself locates the eight-precept uposatha at the **architectural connecting position** between "lay precepts (the five)" and "monastic precepts (the *sāmaṇera*'s ten and above)" — neither purely lay nor purely monastic, but a periodic structure that the lay practitioner undertakes on observance days. The canonical significance of this codification: the lay-monastic ladder is not a binary opposition but a continuous gradient, and the eight-precept uposatha holds a special transitional function on this gradient.

T1606 fascicle 7's doctrinal explanation, "but the day-and-night restraint is prescribed in order that [practitioners] gradually train together" (*但制日夜近住律儀, 為令漸漸俱修學故*), has been treated in §3.4 and is not repeated here. What requires further note is that T1606's elaboration of "gradually training together" involves the concept of *gradual sampling* of monastic restraints: the lay practitioner cannot bear the full weight of monastic restraints all at once (compulsory imposition would carry an excessive risk of breach), but can sample them on observance days, with sampling developing into formation and formation approaching complete undertaking. This gradualist doctrine is the Yogācāra school's doctrinal articulation of the internal developmental trajectory of the lay-monastic ethical ladder.

### 5.2 The prajñā-Madhyamaka expansion: Kumārajīva's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 13

Kumārajīva's translation of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 13 (translated during the Later Qin Hong-shi period, 401–413 CE), the chapter on the eight-precept uposatha (八戒齋章), is the principal prajñā-Madhyamaka expansion of the eight precepts.[^16] Three aspects of this fascicle's exposition carry particular significance. First, fascicle 13 enumerates the "thirty-five drawbacks of breaking the eight-precept uposatha" (a detailed enumeration of consequences of breach — P06 cited this same framework for the intoxicant precept), providing a fine-grained drawback-versus-benefit matching for the bodhisattva-path framing of the eight-precept observance. Second, Nāgārjuna in his own auto-commentary in this fascicle articulates "no day is intrinsically auspicious or inauspicious" (*日無好惡*), refuting non-Buddhist day-auspiciousness arguments — the view that "the 8th, 14th, and 15th [of the lunar month] carry special auspicious meaning." Nāgārjuna shifts the meaning of the uposatha day from "the auspiciousness of the day itself" to "the meaning of the precepts undertaken." **The canonical meaning of the uposatha day lies not in the day's auspiciousness but in the meaning of the discipline the lay practitioner undertakes on the periodic day**; this shift of meaning is the prajñā-Madhyamaka layer's de-mystification of the uposatha day. Third, fascicle 13 provides the "six monthly observance days" (六齋日) framework — the 8th, 14th, 15th, 23rd, 29th, and 30th of the lunar month — a six-day calendar that exceeds the Pāli tradition's three observance days (8th, 14th, 15th), reflecting the Chinese tradition's expansion of the uposatha cycle.

### 5.3 The Chinese-Mahāyāna lay-precept treatise: Dharmakṣema's *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* fascicles 6 and 7

Dharmakṣema's translation of the *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* (T1488, translated in the Northern Liáng Xuán-shǐ period, year 15 / 426 CE) is the most important Mahāyāna lay-precept treatise in the Chinese translation tradition. The sūtra is seven fascicles in total, of which fascicle 6's chapter on the eight-precept uposatha (八戒齋品) and fascicle 7 constitute the concentrated treatment of the eight-precept observance.[^15] What must be noted regarding this text: the sūtra has no extant Pāli original in the research corpus, and the present study's citations follow the CBETA Electronic Tripiṭaka XML edition of *Taishō* volume 24 No. 1488; the translation court was located in Liángzhou (Northern Liáng). The internal question posed in fascicle 7, "Why are the five precepts received for life, but the eight-precept uposatha for [only] one day and one night?" (何緣五戒盡形壽受, 八戒齋法一日一夜?), has been treated in §3.4 as the present paper's core structural question. Fascicle 7 also contains the explicit time-bound phrasing "when dawn appears, [the precepts] are released" (*明相出時, 是時則失*) — when dawn (*aruṇa*, 明相) appears, the uposatha precepts release automatically, and the lay practitioner's original five-precept discipline naturally resumes. This explicit articulation of the time-bound is the **canonical formulaic statement** of the periodic-renunciation mechanism.

### 5.4 The three vinaya traditions' reception-formula cross-recension witness

The three Chinese vinaya systems exhibit cross-tradition stability in their reception formulae for the eight-precept uposatha. The *Mahīśāsaka-vinaya* fascicle 15 (T1421) provides the Mahīśāsaka school's reception formula; the *Dharmaguptaka-vinaya* fascicle 11 (T1428) provides the Dharmaguptaka school's "monk's reception-and-recitation dual track" for laywomen (「受+說」雙軌); and the *Mūla-sarvāstivāda-vinaya* fascicle 34 (T1442) provides the naming "noble eight-factor near-dwelling training-rule" (聖八支近住學處) together with the cross-tradition variant of four monthly observance days (8th, 15th, 23rd, last day of the lunar month).[^19] The reception formulae of these three vinaya systems are fully identical in the content of the eight precepts (with only slight permutations of order and phrasing), while presenting school-specific differences in the reception ritual (who administers, who receives, the specific verbal phrasing) — this is the vinaya tradition's "identical restraints, differing rituals" cross-recension stability. Detailed case-by-case comparative reading is the main scene of P09 and P10 of the present volume; the present paper offers only a cross-recension cameo to evidence the *uposatha*'s identity across the three vinaya systems.

### 5.5 The Chinese commentarial layer: Dàoxuān's "mental-utterance" self-administration + Zhiyi's significant absence

Dàoxuān's *Sifenlü-xingshichao* (T1804, *Four-Part Vinaya Practical Guide*), fascicle 下, section "Three Modes of the Eight-Precept Procedure" (三明八戒法) at *Taishō* volume 40, p.140b, written in the Tang seventh century, is the strongest codification in the Chinese commentarial layer of the reception procedure for the eight-precept uposatha.[^20] Dàoxuān integrates the reception procedure into three modes: **first, the self-uttered "mental-utterance" mode** (*心念口言*, the self-administration when no monastic is present); **second, kneeling in three-fold going-for-refuge before a monk** (the standard reception formula in which a monk administers to the lay practitioner); **third, the recitation of the seven-fold uposatha precept text** (recitation of the standardised Chinese formulaic text as the form of reception) — and all three "are uposatha" (皆是布薩). Within this three-mode codification, **the "mental-utterance" self-administration clause is a distinctive discovery of the Chinese tradition** — even in the absence of a monastic witness, the lay practitioner may self-administer the uposatha precepts through mental recitation, architecturally maximising the lay-side accessibility of the *uposatha*. This self-administration clause has no parallel in the Southern Theravāda tradition; it is the Chinese vinaya school's institutional innovation for lay-precept observance. Detailed treatment of Dàoxuān's vinaya commentary is the main scene of P17 of the present volume; the present paper offers only a brief mention.

The last item to handle is an architecturally significant absence: **Zhiyi's *Mohe-zhiguan* (T1911), written in the late Sui sixth century, contains zero references to the eight-precept uposatha, the eight precepts, or the term *uposatha*** (八關齋, 八戒, 八關).[^21] This absence has been confirmed repeatedly through CBETA XML grep. The significance of this zero-citation, however, is not that "Zhiyi has no view on the eight-precept uposatha" but rather that "the Tiantai main scene architecturally yields to the vinaya track" — Zhiyi's precept discourse ascends to the perfect-and-sudden precept (圓頓戒) / single-mind precept (一心戒) at the prajñā-Madhyamaka layer, while the concrete unfolding of the lay-realm *uposatha* ritual is yielded to Dàoxuān's vinaya track. This absence itself is architectural evidence of doctrinal division of labour between Chinese Buddhist schools, not Zhiyi's oversight. Zhiyi's *Bodhisattva-precept Commentary* (T1812) contains four brief references to the eight-precept uposatha, all concentrated around the doctrinal emphasis "abstention from eating after noon is the core" (過中食為本); Yongming Yanshou's *Wanshan-tonggui-ji* (T2017, Five Dynasties / early Northern Song) contains one brief reference, "either receive the one-day precept or undertake the eight-precept uposatha" (或受一日戒, 或持八關齋) — this phrasing belongs to the pastoral register of lay-Buddhist practice, reflecting the lay-Buddhist tradition of periodic precept observance in the Five Dynasties / Song period.[^21]

## VI. Conclusion

Part II of the present volume opens at P05 with the path-element categorical thesis, advances at P06 to the differential canonical scope of the five precepts, and now at the present paper advances to the periodic-renunciation mechanism of the eight-precept uposatha. The core finding of the present paper is: **the eight precepts of the uposatha are not a quantitative accumulation atop the five precepts but a "five lay precepts + three monastic borrowings, borrow-and-return" canonical structural design; the "one-day-and-one-night" time-bound is not a concession but a structural design feature, enabling the lay practitioner to sample the Arahant's mode of dwelling without committing to permanent renunciation**. The concrete significance of this architectural reading for the practitioner's path is this: what the lay practitioner undertakes on the 8th, 14th, and 15th (or the six-day extended calendar) of the lunar month is not merely a one-day ethical intensification but the canonical practice of "imitating the Arahants' mode of dwelling for this day and this night" — and the meaning of this practice lies not in the *quantity* of merit accumulated but in the *quality* of the Arahant's mode of dwelling returning in cycle.

The periodic-renunciation mechanism demonstrated in the present paper carries forward-link significance in two directions. The following paper, P08, will take *sīla-gandha* (the scent of virtue) as its subject, with AN 3.79 as its primary anchor, demonstrating that the simile of *sīla-gandha* travelling against the wind is canonical evidence of the social-domain efficacy of precept observance — the collective ritual effect of the eight-precept uposatha among lay assemblies on observance days (the *uposatha-saṅghānusavana*, the lay uposatha assembly) is the concrete social-domain articulation of the *sīla-gandha*. The eleven-link chain of AN 11.1 in its first seven links (*sīla → avippaṭisāra → pāmojja → pīti → passaddhi → sukha → samādhi*) is the natural-arising sequence from precept to concentration; the lay practitioner, in the one day and one night of the uposatha observance, architecturally rehearses these seven links once — *sīla* (the eight restraints) → *avippaṭisāra* (the absence of regret from setting worldly affairs aside) → *pāmojja* → *pīti* → *passaddhi* → *sukha* → *samādhi* (the lay-side preparation of access concentration on the observance day). This seven-link rehearsal is the most direct canonical mechanism for the SĪLA→SAMĀDHI architectural bridge that P24 (the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's four pure-clear admonitions) will consummate at the Mahāyāna level.

The open question of the present paper is this: **if the periodic-renunciation mechanism of the eight-precept uposatha is the canonical design for the lay practitioner to sample the Arahant's mode of dwelling on observance days, then how is this sampling to be re-interpreted under modern institutional conditions** (where there is no monastic Sangha for joint observance, no monastery as a venue of communal practice, no traditional observance-day calendar as a periodic framework)? The institutional dimension of this question concerns the canonical basis for the self-organisation of lay Buddhism in modernity; the concrete unfolding of its contemporary applicability is the subject range of Part IX of the present volume.

## Cross-References

### Within-volume continuity

- **SILA P01–P04** (Part I foundations): the three-axis articulation of *sīla* (P01), *sīla* as the fourth *sotāpattiyaṅga* (P02), *sīla* as an object of recollection (P03 — the *sīlānussati* axis is directly continuous with AN 3.70's six-recollection prelude in the *ariy-uposatha*), and the *ekaggatā* object-structure argument (P04).
- **SILA P05** (Part II #1): the three ethical limbs of the noble eightfold path as constitutive path-elements, not a constraint-list.
- **SILA P06** (Part II #2, immediately prior): the five precepts as five different canonical-scope path-elements ("same ritual, different scope"). The present paper extends this thesis to the lay-renunciate intermittent stratum: same reception ritual, different canonical scope — the *uposatha* is structurally a different observance category from the standing five precepts, not a quantitative extension of them.

### Within-volume forward links

- **SILA P08** (Part II #4): *sīla-gandha* (the scent of virtue) and *sīla* in the social field — the *uposatha*'s lay-assembly social phenomenon (the *uposatha-saṅghānusavana*) prefigures P08's social-domain treatment.
- **SILA P09 + P10** (Part III): the *pāṭimokkha* structure and the three-vinaya-tradition comparative reading — the present paper's vinaya cross-recension cameo (T1421 + T1428 + T1442) is unpacked in detail at this volume stage.
- **SILA P12 + P14 + P16 + P17** (Mahāyāna and vinaya strata): the three pure-precept aggregation (P12), the nine aspects of the bodhisattva precepts (P14), the precept-substance theory (P16), and Dàoxuān's *Four-Part Vinaya Practical Guide* unpacked in full (P17, where the present paper's "three modes of the eight-precept procedure" GOLD anchor is treated at full extent).

### Cross-volume relations

- **DĀNA volume** — P06 carry-forward: the killing-precept's *abhaya-dāna* (gift of fearlessness) reading at AN 8.39 supplies the SĪLA↔DĀNA bridge. The present paper's first precept (abstention from killing) continues this same architectural reading.
- **SAMĀDHI volume forward** — the eleven-link chain of AN 11.1 (*sīla → avippaṭisāra → pāmojja → pīti → passaddhi → sukha → samādhi → ...*) is canonical evidence that *sīla* naturally unfolds into *samādhi*. The lay practitioner's one-day-and-one-night observance is an architectural rehearsal of the first seven links of this chain — the lay practitioner, by adopting monastic restraints for a day, experientially samples the *sīla → ... → samādhi* developmental gradient. The full eleven-link soteriological unfolding is reserved for **P24** (the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's four pure-clear admonitions, where the SĪLA→SAMĀDHI architectural handoff is consummated at the Mahāyāna level).

### Octalogy (sister series) relation

- The Octalogy (八部曲) series treats *sīla* doctrinally as one of the three trainings (*tri-śikṣā*); the SĪLA volume of the *Six Practice Gates* treats *sīla* developmentally — the path-oriented (行) counterpart of the doctrinal (見 / 慧學) treatment. The present paper's intermittent-monasticism architectural argument is path-oriented; the Octalogy treats the *uposatha* (where treated at all) at the doctrinal-overview register.
