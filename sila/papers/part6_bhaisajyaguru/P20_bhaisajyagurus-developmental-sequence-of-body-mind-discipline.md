# Bhaiṣajyaguru's Developmental Sequence of Body-Mind Discipline
**Surface Falsification and the Reconstruction of the Precondition-Field**

## Abstract

This paper takes up a question that looks simple but is overturned at the level of the text itself: do the twelve great vows of the Tathāgata Bhaiṣajyaguru (the Medicine Buddha) encode a developmental sequence of discipline that runs from body to mind, from coarse to subtle? The answer falls into three tiers. **First, of a linear sequence there is none.** Nowhere in the sūtra do the ordering-words "sequence," "only then," or "thereupon able to" fall between the vows; the vow-order is, if anything, reversed — the precept-vow stands fifth, not first, and the most basic conditions of all, food (eleventh) and clothing (twelfth), come last; and the mainstream commentaries (Taehyŏn's *Commentarial Traces*, with its six paired gates, and the two Dunhuang commentaries) all lay the vows out flat as a classification, not as a progression. Any "body-to-mind" ladder-reading is therefore not taken from the vows but read into them. **Second, a parallel field there is — and this is what the vows are by their own nature.** The surface "disorder" is not a blank; it is the very face of a *field* of preconditions for precept-keeping, whose material prerequisites are **laid out in parallel** rather than stacked rung upon rung. The twelve vows are not a stairway climbed rung by rung, but the level ground at the foot of the mountain path; what the ground holds — food, bodily form, the faculties, freedom from the perils that obstruct the Dharma — is set out all at once, not stacked step by step. **Third, an order of depth there is — yet depth is not time.** Lingyao's *Direct Explanation*, reading by the four siddhāntas, judges every bodily reversal alike to be the "shallower benefit," and reserves for bodhi, which is beyond gender, the ultimate sense and the "deeper benefit": this is an order of shallow-versus-deep, not of before-and-after, and so can stand alongside the parallel field without displacing it. Running through all three tiers is a strict distinction between **enabling precept-keeping** (the upstream parallel field that the Medicine Buddha's vows guard) and **the fruition that follows from keeping precepts** (the downstream sequential ladder set out in the *Kimatthiya* discourse and its Pāli parallel). By this distinction the paper marks plainly what it itself constructs, and does not use the downstream ladder to argue back that the upstream field is also a sequence. The eighth vow, "transformation from female to male form," serves as the deepest single case-study under the dimension of "body," handled throughout in a descriptive-historical manner and relativized from within the tradition itself (by Lingyao's verdict of "the shallower benefit," and by the Vimalakīrti's "neither male nor female").

## 摘要

本論文處理一個看似簡單、卻在文本層被推翻的問題：藥師如來十二大願是否編碼一個由身至心、由粗及細之調伏次第？其答可析為三層。其一，**線性之次第無**：全經未見「次第」「乃能」「方能」之序語，願序且反向——持戒願居第五而非居首，最基本之飲食（第十一）、衣服（第十二）反殿於末，主流古註（太賢《古迹》六對門、敦煌二疏）復皆作平列之分類，不讀為進程；任何「由身至心」之階梯讀法，皆非取自願文，而是讀入。其二，**並列之場域有，且此即願文之本然**：表層之「無序」非空白，而正是一持戒前提之場域之本相——其物質前提乃**並列鋪設**而非逐級而疊。譬之，十二願非拾級而上之階，乃使人得立於山徑之口的一片已平之地；地基上之所需（飲食、身相、諸根、離於法難）一時並陳，非一階一階而起。其三，**深淺之序有，然深淺非時間**：靈耀《直解》以四悉檀判諸身體之逆轉同為「淺益」、而以超於性別之菩提為第一義之「深益」，是益之深淺，非時之先後，可與場域並存而不相奪。貫乎三層者，為「使能持戒」（藥師願所守護之上游並列場域）與「持戒得果」（《何義經》及其巴利平行所陳之下游序列階梯）之嚴格區辨。本論文借此分辨明標其所建之讀法，而不以下游之階梯反證上游之場域亦為序列。第八願「轉女成男」作為「身」維度下最深之單例，全程以描述—歷史處之，並由傳統內部（靈耀「淺益」之判、維摩「非男非女」之說）自行相對化之。

## I. Introduction: Why Does the Precept-Vow Not Come First?

In Xuanzang's translation of the *Sūtra of the Original Vows and Merits of Bhaiṣajyaguru, the Tathāgata of Lapis-Lazuli Radiance*, the vow concerning the precepts does not stand at the head of the twelve great vows. It stands fifth:

> 第五大願：願我來世得菩提時，若有無量無邊有情，於我法中修行梵行，一切皆令得不缺戒，具三聚戒。設有毀犯，聞我名已，還得清淨，不墮惡趣。
> *"The fifth great vow: When in a future age I attain bodhi, may all the countless and boundless beings who cultivate the brahmacarya within my Dharma be made, all of them, to obtain the unbroken precepts and to complete the three-encompassing precepts; and should any transgress, then upon hearing my name they shall regain purity and not fall into the evil destinies."*

This placement is worth pausing over. If the twelve vows truly encoded a "foundation-first" ladder — first the body settled, then the mind reached, and the precepts at the last — then the most basic conditions of food and clothing ought to come early, and the precept-vow ought to come *after* the various bodily conditions. The facts run exactly the other way. The precept-vow stands fifth, while the vows of body and matter — the faculties (sixth), the manifold sicknesses (seventh), the circumstance of gender (eighth), peril before the law (tenth), food (eleventh), and clothing (twelfth) — all come after it, with food and clothing, the most basic of all, last in the whole sequence. The question surfaces: do the twelve vows have a sequence? If they do, why this order? And if they do not, does that very "disorder" carry a sense of its own?

The question is not a mere by-product of philology. It points straight at a perplexity in the field of actual practice: why is it that, of two people who have received the same full precepts and hold to the same code, one can keep them and the other cannot? The difference often lies not in the strength of the aspiration but in the conditions of body, mind, and matter that support it — the starving person cannot be settled, the sick person cannot be focused, the imprisoned person has no way to cultivate the brahmacarya at all. Whether a precept *can* be kept rests on an upstream material ground. The preceding paper of this series (P19) mapped these support conditions laterally, setting out a reverse-index of seven dimensions; but what P19 answered was "which conditions are there" — a synchronic index — and it did not yet ask "can these conditions be ordered, and why are they so hard to order." That is the diachronic question the present paper inherits.[^8]

The sixth section of P19 made the reader a promise: that it would take up the question of "whether the disciplining of body and mind has a sequence." The honest answer of the present paper is this: **the sequence is not on the face of the text; it is in the construction.** The twelve vows do not encode a linear, temporal sequence — this can be falsified at the surface; but that surface "disorder" discloses precisely this, that the material preconditions of precept-keeping are laid out as a parallel *field* rather than ordered as a ladder. This paper reads the twelve vows as a **precondition-field** for precept-keeping, and holds that this is the reading the corpus can bear, and the one that can mark honestly its own constructed character. This is not an evasion of the promise P19 made to the reader but its keeping — answered as "not a linear ladder, but a field admitting of construction-by-ordering." The arc of the whole paper runs from "surface falsification" to "the reconstruction of the precondition-field."

## II. The Canonical Basis: Surface Disorder and the Surviving Within-Vow Logic

### II.1 Three Pieces of Surface Evidence

First, **silence at the level of the lexicon.** Within the vow-text itself, no graph capable of marking a sequence or a conditional ordering — "sequence," "only then," "thereupon able to" — falls anywhere between the vows. If the vow-text meant to encode a body-to-mind progression, the total absence of such sequencing vocabulary is itself counter-evidence.

Second, **the reversal of the vow-order.** As already noted, the precept-vow stands fifth, while the vows of body and matter — the faculties, the manifold sicknesses, gender, peril before the law, food, and clothing — all come after it; food (eleventh) and clothing (twelfth), the most basic, come last of all. A "foundation-first" ladder would have to be arranged in the opposite way. The reversal of the vow-order means that any vertical "body-to-mind" reading cannot be taken directly from the vow-text but must be a reading *into* it, not something the corpus gives.

Third, **the laying-flat of the mainstream commentaries.** The Silla monk Taehyŏn, in his *Commentarial Traces on the Original-Vow Bhaiṣajyaguru Sūtra*, lays out the twelve vows by way of "six paired gates," mechanically pairing them two by two as the vow-order runs, with no causal or stage-relation between gate and gate — which is in fact a synchronic classificatory reading, in the same key as P19's index, and not a progression.[^1] The two Dunhuang commentaries (two *Commentaries on the Bhaiṣajyaguru Sūtra*, both unattributed, belonging to a recension prior to Xuanzang's translation) follow an even plainer "name — raising of the vow — dedication" template, and nowhere read the vows by sequence. In other words, both the earliest commentators (Dunhuang) and the mainstream one (Taehyŏn) decline to read the twelve vows as a developmental sequence.

### II.2 The Surviving Within-Vow Logic: The Eleventh Vow

Once the surface has been falsified, what actually survives as load-bearing is not a sequence "between vow and vow" but a causal logic "within a single vow." The clearest case is the eleventh vow:

> 第十一大願：願我來世得菩提時，若諸有情，飢渴所惱，為求食故造諸惡業；得聞我名，專念受持，我當先以上妙飲食，飽足其身；後以法味，畢竟安樂而建立之。
> *"The eleventh great vow: When in a future age I attain bodhi, if there are beings tormented by hunger and thirst who, for the sake of seeking food, commit evil deeds — should they come to hear my name and single-mindedly hold it in mind, I shall first satisfy their bodies with excellent food and drink, and afterward, with the savor of the Dharma, establish them in ultimate peace and joy."*

Here there is an unmistakable micro-order *within* the vow: **first satisfy the body with food, afterward establish it with the savor of the Dharma**; and a cause is given — tormented by hunger and thirst, even to the point of committing evil deeds for the sake of food, one must first be settled in body, and only then can the Dharma, and the precepts, be spoken of. This is the corpus itself supplying the logic that "the material precondition precedes the Dharma." But the paper must mark plainly: this is a logic *within* a vow, a before-and-after "inside a single vow," and it must not be magnified, as if of itself, into a textual proof of sequence "between vow and vow."[^2] On this point the precondition-field reading has a within-vow textual anchor; but what that anchor supports is the *principle* "the priority of the material precondition," not the *conclusion* "the vows constitute a ladder."

### II.3 The Case Enters: The Eighth Vow across Three Recensions

Among the twelve vows, the single case most acute, and most worth excavating, under the dimension of "body" is the eighth vow, "transformation from female to male form." The paper first sets the three recensions side by side, to bring out the layering of their translation-history; the doctrinal sense and the cross-sūtra context are reserved for Chapter IV.

> （達摩笈多，隋·六一五）為婦人百惡所逼惱故，厭離女身，願捨女形……轉女人身成丈夫相，乃至究竟無上菩提。
> *"(Dharmagupta, Sui, 615) Because they are oppressed by the hundred ills of womanhood, they grow weary of the female body and wish to cast off the female form … transforming the woman's body into the marks of a man, on up to ultimate, unsurpassed bodhi."*
>
> （玄奘，唐·六五〇）為女百惡之所逼惱，極生厭離，願捨女身；聞我名已，一切皆得轉女成男，具丈夫相，乃至證得無上菩提。
> *"(Xuanzang, Tang, 650) Oppressed by the hundred ills of being a woman, they grow utterly weary and wish to cast off the female body; having heard my name, all of them obtain transformation from female to male, complete in the marks of a man, on up to the attainment of unsurpassed bodhi."*
>
> （義淨，唐·七〇七）為女眾苦之所逼切，極生厭離，願捨女身；若聞我名，至心稱念，即於現身，轉成男子，具丈夫相，乃至菩提。
> *"(Yijing, Tang, 707) Pressed hard by the many sufferings of being a woman, they grow utterly weary and wish to cast off the female body; if they hear my name and with utmost sincerity invoke it, then in this very body they are transformed into a man, complete in the marks of a man, on up to bodhi."*

The differences among the three lie purely between translators' diction, not in variant readings within a manuscript.[^3] The genealogy is clear: Dharmagupta uses the roundabout "transforming the woman's body into the marks of a man"; Xuanzang forges the terse "transformation from female to male," which becomes the fixed phrase for later tradition; Yijing alters "hundred ills" to "many sufferings," and adds the practice-condition "with utmost sincerity invoke it" and the this-life marker "in this very body." This is descriptive-historical material: Yijing's later adjustment of register (from the more pejoratively colored "hundred ills" toward the more neutral "many sufferings," together with the strengthening of an immediate, this-life efficacy) is itself a phenomenon in the history of translation; the paper only describes its layering and adds no value-judgment.

## III. The Core Argument: Constructing the Precondition-Field, and Internal Commentarial Evidence

### III.1 Constructing the Precondition-Field (an own-it claim)

On the reading proposed here, what the twelve vows guard are the upstream material conditions that make precept-keeping possible at all — food, the bodily faculties, freedom from sickness, freedom from the perils of the law, clothing to cover the body. These conditions are set in place as a **field laid out in parallel**, not ordered as a fixed ladder. That they are parallel can be argued on two grounds. First, an actual life does not ascend by one fixed staircase: this person lacks food, that one lacks a sound faculty, a third is hemmed in by the peril of imprisonment — the obstructions differ from person to person, with no settled before-and-after. A parallel field can meet each person where they stand; a fixed ladder, by contrast, requires everyone to start from the same rung. Second, it is precisely because the preconditions stand parallel, none being a fixed prerequisite of another, that the vow-order's being reversible and laid-flat finds its explanation — the "disorder" of the vow-text is not an oversight but the natural outward appearance of a field-structure.

It must be said again and plainly: this "precondition-field" is the construction of the present paper, not something the sūtra states. The reader should be able to tell apart "what the text says" (the within-vow causation of the eleventh vow) and "what this paper reads out" (that the vows constitute a precondition-field for precept-keeping). The ancient commentaries cited below are adduced precisely so that this construction does not float free as an external imposition but can find internal corroboration within the tradition.

### III.2 Internal Commentarial Evidence, First: Lingyao's Two Tiers of Depth

Lingyao's *Direct Explanation of the Bhaiṣajyaguru Sūtra* (Qing) supplies the most explicit ordering-verdict to be found within the tradition:[^4]

> 與樂中益有淺深之別，根缺者即具諸根，貧窮者即得豐足，女身者即成丈夫，皆敵對相翻，屬對治、為人兩悉檀淺益；若成無上菩提，即第一義，深遠益也。
> *"Within the bestowing of happiness, the benefit admits a difference of shallow and deep: those lacking faculties at once become complete in their faculties, the poor at once obtain abundance, those in a female body at once become men — all of these are reversals-of-opposites, belonging to the shallower benefit of the two siddhāntas of the remedial and of the personal; whereas to attain unsurpassed bodhi is the ultimate sense, the deep and far-reaching benefit."*

Taking the four siddhāntas (the four modes of teaching) as his criterion, Lingyao gathers "completeness of faculty," "abundance for the poor," "the female body become male," and every such bodily and circumstantial reversal of opposites **alike** under the "shallower benefit" of the two siddhāntas of the remedial and of the personal, and reserves "attaining unsurpassed bodhi" for the "deeper benefit" of the ultimate sense (*paramārtha*). The point that matters here is this: what Lingyao gives is a **difference of depth** (shallow/deep), not a difference of time (before/after). This paper reads it as exactly the load-bearing internal evidence the tradition affords — but it is an ordering by depth, not a linear ladder. The two tiers of depth support precisely the structure "a parallel field plus a depth-verdict": the preconditions stand parallel on one plane (all being the "happiness-bestowing" of the shallower benefit), while bodhi forms a tier apart (the deeper benefit of the ultimate sense). This dovetails with P19's synchronic index, but re-read along the axis of depth, the difference comes clear.

### III.3 Internal Commentarial Evidence, Second: Lingyao's Debate on the Vow-Order

The stronger internal evidence is that Lingyao actually stages, against himself, a debate about the vow-order:

> 問：既依《涅槃》及順修行，當以戒學為先，今何先慧？答：今是圓佛所修聖道，貴乎先得權實二智，普應……
> *"Question: Since, following the Nirvāṇa-sūtra and the ordinary course of practice, the training in the precepts should come first, why here does wisdom come first? Answer: This is the holy path cultivated by the Buddha of the round teaching, which prizes first obtaining the two wisdoms, the provisional and the real, so as to respond universally to all …"*

Lingyao asks outright: following the *Nirvāṇa-sūtra* and the ordinary sequence of practice, the training in the precepts ought to come first — why, then, does wisdom come first here? His own answer appeals to the round teaching: the holy path cultivated by the Buddha of the round teaching prizes first obtaining the two wisdoms, provisional and real, so as to respond universally to the capacities of beings. He says further, "Every particular vow is framed in terms of the four truths; a vow that does not rest on the truths is called a deranged vow," taking the four truths as that on which the vows rest. The very existence of this question-and-answer proves that a commentator did indeed treat the vow-order as a problem open to doctrinal dispute — and a dispute not idle but motivated by a doctrinal-classificatory concern. This is the strongest internal commentarial evidence for the present paper's claim that "the sequence is something constructible, and its construction is itself open to debate": not a question the paper imposes from outside, but one Lingyao raised more than three hundred years ago.

### III.4 An Honest Marking: Laying-Flat versus Depth

A mark of honesty must be set down at the last. The Taehyŏn six paired gates and the two Dunhuang commentaries cited above all lay the vows out flat and decline to read them as a progression; only Lingyao has an ordering, and the axis of his ordering is the three trainings, the four truths, and depth — not a body-to-mind ladder of body and mind. The posture of this paper is therefore: **it borrows Lingyao's depth-axis, not his three-trainings axis.** That is, the paper grants that its "precondition-field" reading is a later, constructed one; the support it has found within the tradition is Lingyao's depth-verdict and his debate on the vow-order, not a sequence-doctrine native to the mainstream commentators — because the mainstream commentators read the vows flat to begin with. Only by making this plain can the reader gauge accurately where this paper's contribution lies.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Synthesis: The Structure-Reading, the Gender Case, and the Research Gap

### IV.1 The Spine Axis: Gethin's Numbered-List Structure-Reading

For the structure of Buddhist "numbered lists," modern scholarship offers a mature reading the paper can draw on. In his study of the path to awakening, Gethin observes that Buddhist numbered lists often encode the structure of a path-sequence, but that whatever is a path-sequence ordinarily carries explicit path-markers and an ordinal logic, such that the earlier item conditions the later and the later rests on the earlier.[^9]

Viewed by this method, the paper's central move gains a methodological fulcrum. Apply Gethin's reading to the twelve vows of Bhaiṣajyaguru: between them there is neither path-marker nor ordinal logic, and the vow-order is reversible and lays flat — by this criterion, the twelve vows are not a path-sequence but a parallel field. Apply the same method to the precept-chain of the *Kimatthiya* discourse treated in the next chapter: its path-markers are complete, each item conditioning the next with precision — by the same criterion, it is exactly a ladder. This paper holds that it is **one method, two verdicts**: the vow-text is a field, the precept-chain is a ladder. Especially worth noting, Gethin's own caveat — that a path-sequence ought to carry path-markers — when applied to the Bhaiṣajyaguru vows becomes, precisely through their *lack* of markers, a methodological proof that they are "a non-linear series." This paper therefore uses Gethin not to argue Bhaiṣajyaguru with him (he never treated the Bhaiṣajyaguru material), but to borrow his structural criterion, so that the verdict "the vow-text is not a sequence" can proceed by a method.

### IV.2 The Gender Axis: The Eighth Vow and the Tradition's Self-Relativization

For the modern study of the eighth vow's "transformation from female to male form," Diana Y. Paul's *Women in Buddhism: Images of the Feminine in the Mahāyāna Tradition* serves as one reference. Paul records the ambivalence of female-transformation imagery in the Mahāyāna tradition. This paper grants that the eighth vow does belong to this body of imagery, and that Paul's description is accurate. Yet a further layer must be added that Paul's frame is apt to flatten — the tradition's own self-relativization *from within*. Lingyao, as seen, has already judged "the female body become male," along with the other bodily reversals, to be the "shallower benefit," reserving for bodhi, which is beyond gender, the "deeper benefit" of the ultimate sense (see Chapter III); and in the Vimalakīrti the goddess states outright that "all dharmas are neither male nor female." In other words, the corpus has itself already demoted "female-to-male" to a provisionally posited bestowal of happiness, and held the genderless bodhi to be the ultimate. Here the paper only renders this neutrally, supplying this measure of proportion from within the tradition, and does not set in motion a modern value-critique. Nancy Schuster (Barnes)'s study of the mechanism of the "changing the female body" motif may serve here as corroboration, accounting for the motif's prevalence in Mahāyāna literature and its narrative function.

The doctrinal context of the eighth vow shows its layering still more clearly when read across sūtras:

> （法華·龍女）忽然之間變成男子，具菩薩行……成等正覺。
> *"(Lotus, the nāga-girl) In a single instant she was transformed into a male, complete in the bodhisattva practices … and attained perfect awakening."*
>
> （維摩·天女）是故佛說一切諸法非男、非女……雖現女身，而非女也。
> *"(Vimalakīrti, the goddess) Therefore the Buddha says that all dharmas are neither male nor female … though she manifests a female body, she is not female."*
>
> （無量壽經·第三十五願）厭惡女身，壽終之後復為女像者，不取正覺。
> *"(Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha, the thirty-fifth vow) Should those who loathe the female body, after the end of this life, again take a female form, may I not attain perfect awakening."*

Each of the three has its own register. The Lotus's nāga-girl makes the body's transformation a condition of becoming a buddha — the register of "transformation as condition"; the Vimalakīrti's goddess, with "neither male nor female," directly breaks the substantial reality of gender — the counter-register of "the emptiness of gender," which is the corpus's own act of breaking the former register; and the thirty-fifth vow of the *Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha* is a Pure-Land vow, structurally the nearest to the eighth vow of the Bhaiṣajyaguru. The paper sets the three side by side precisely to show that the tradition itself holds plural readings of this motif, not a single verdict. So too with the eighth vow's ancient commentaries: Taehyŏn frames it as "the inward condition of the suffering of the female body" and cites an ancient view from the *Nirvāṇa-sūtra*;[^7] Lingyao judges it, by the four siddhāntas, to be the "shallower benefit"; the Dunhuang T2767 reads it as "transforming indeterminate karma into determinate-for-the-good karma" and connects it to rebirth; while the Dunhuang T2766 does not gloss the transformation-of-the-female passage at all. The coexistence of plural readings is itself the material; the paper describes it and does not adjudicate it — this is "the excavation comes from an expansion on the side of the evidence, not from an addition on the side of the author's appraisal."[^5]

### IV.3 The Research Gap: Where This Paper's Contribution Lies

So far as modern scholarship goes, no one has read the twelve vows of Bhaiṣajyaguru as a "developmental ordering of the preconditions of precept-keeping." Devotional and encyclopedic treatments mostly stop at listing the vows and offer no structural analysis; and the literature on path-sequences (such as Gethin's) never touches this Bhaiṣajyaguru material. P19 already showed that no one had read the twelve vows as an architecture of support for precept-keeping; the present paper's question of ordering is narrower and thinner still. This negative result itself carries the justification of the paper's contribution, and warrants the whole paper's "own-it" voicing — for a reading with no predecessor to inherit must all the more honestly mark out which is "what the text says" and which is "what this paper reads."

---

## V. Middle-Way Correction: The Field Is the Upstream Phase of the Ladder

### V.1 A Structural Distinction

The point at which this paper is most apt to go wrong is in taking "the precondition-field" and "the gradual ladder" as two competing structures, and then using the gradual chain to argue back that the vow-text is also a sequence. The paper sets down a middle-way correction here: **the field is not a structure competing with the ladder, but the upstream phase of the ladder.** Their relation can be put as a single phase-chain:

> the Bhaiṣajyaguru precondition-field (upstream · parallel · enabling precept-keeping) → the precept → the gradual chain of the fruition that follows from keeping precepts (downstream · sequential · the fruition that follows) → liberation

The downstream gradual chain is, in the Chinese, most aptly led by the *Kimatthiya* discourse (of the Practice-Related section of the Madhyama-āgama):[^6]

> 阿難！持戒為何義？……持戒者，令不悔義……不悔者，令歡悅義……
> *"Ānanda, what is the point of keeping the precepts? … Keeping the precepts has the point of producing non-remorse … non-remorse has the point of producing gladness …"*

Its full chain runs: keeping the precepts → non-remorse → gladness → joy → tranquillity → happiness → concentration → seeing things as they are and knowing them as they truly are → disenchantment → dispassion → liberation; and the discourse marks its principle thus, "dharma benefits dharma, dharma conditions dharma," saying that "this precept-keeping leads on to the highest." Its Pāli parallel is the *Kimatthiyasutta* of the Aṅguttara-nikāya, whose chain of eleven members runs: the precepts → non-remorse → gladness → joy → tranquillity → happiness → concentration → knowledge-and-vision of things as they are → disenchantment → dispassion → knowledge-and-vision of liberation, said to be that which leads "gradually (*anupubbena*) to the highest."

### V.2 A Discipline: The Gradual Chain Is the Foot of the Downstream Ladder, Not Positive Evidence for the Field

A strict discipline must be set down here, and this discipline is just where the paper's integrity rests: **this gradual chain begins from "the precept"; it is a chain of "the fruition that follows from keeping precepts," and the whole of it is downstream of the precept.** This paper therefore does not use the chain to argue that "the vow-text is itself a gradual sequence" — to use it so would be to mistake a plainly downstream ladder for evidence that the upstream field is a sequence. What the twelve vows of Bhaiṣajyaguru guard is the upstream precondition that **brings a person to the foot of the ladder**; what the *Kimatthiya* discourse and its Pāli parallel unfold is the rung-by-rung fruition that comes *after* one has mounted the ladder. The paper cites the chain in order to mark the foot of the ladder and its nature, and **not** to prove that the field too is a sequence. With the distinction so drawn, even the most exacting reader can find no opening to object that "the *Kimatthiya* discourse is plainly a ladder — how can it be used to argue for a field?" — because this paper has never made any such claim.

### V.3 The Pivot: "Enabling Precept-Keeping" versus "the Fruition That Follows from Keeping Precepts"

From this the paper's pivotal distinction lands of itself. The Bhaiṣajyaguru vows = "enabling precept-keeping" (the upstream parallel field, which brings a person to the foot of the ladder); the *Kimatthiya* discourse and its Pāli parallel = "the fruition that follows from keeping precepts" (the downstream sequential ladder, climbed rung by rung). The analogy between the two is structural — both embody the principle that "dharma benefits dharma, dharma conditions dharma" — and not an equation: the vow-order does not amount to the formal three trainings or to the stages of cultivation of the three higher trainings. That the vow-order can be reversed and laid flat is precisely because "becoming a ladder" belongs to what is after the precept, downstream; the upstream field has no rungs to invert, and so no sequence to speak of. This is the exact sense of the words from Chapter I, "the sequence is not on the face of the text but in the construction": what is constructible is the downstream ladder and the two tiers of depth; the surface disorder is the upstream field's own nature.

### V.4 The Proportion-Rule for the Eighth Vow

It remains to restate, as one part of the middle-way correction, the rule for handling the eighth vow. On this case the paper keeps throughout a descriptive-historical posture: the excavation comes from an expansion on the side of the evidence (the three-recension diction, the cross-sūtra motif, the plurality of ancient glosses), not from an addition on the side of the author's appraisal. The relativization from within the tradition — Lingyao's verdict of "the shallower benefit / the ultimate sense," the Vimalakīrti's "neither male nor female" — already suffices to set this imagery in its proportion, and the author need not, and should not, add a modern adjudication of his own. The *Nirvāṇa-sūtra*'s words on the female body that Taehyŏn cites are marked as "an ancient view the commentary cites," not the author's own words; and the institutional background bound up with the eighth vow (the various matters of the Saṅgha and of ordination) has been treated in dedicated papers of this series, and the present paper does no more than give a descriptive pointer to that context, without reopening it here.[^7]

## VI. Conclusion: Guarding the Material Ground of Precept-Keeping, and Opening toward Sign-less Sīla

This has been an inquiry into "the Medicine Buddha's vow-power and the support conditions for the precepts." The one question asked: do the twelve vows of Bhaiṣajyaguru encode a developmental sequence of discipline running from body to mind? The answer falls into three tiers.

First, **of a linear sequence there is none.** If "sequence" means a ladder climbed rung by rung — body first and mind after, and the precepts at the end — then the vow-text can be falsified at the surface: at the level of the lexicon, no ordering-word "sequence," "only then," "thereupon able to"; the vow-order, if anything, reversed — the precept-vow standing fifth, not first, while food and clothing, the most basic, come last of all in the whole sequence; and the mainstream commentaries, from Taehyŏn's six paired gates to the two Dunhuang commentaries, all laying the vows out flat as a classification and not reading them as a progression. With these three pieces of evidence standing together, any "body-to-mind" ladder-reading is not taken from the vow-text but read into it.

Second, **a parallel field there is — and this is what the vows are by their own nature.** The surface "disorder" is not a blank; it is the very face of a *field* of preconditions for precept-keeping. The reason is utterly plain: a life does not wait upon a ladder. The needs of the hungry, the sick, the imprisoned arise all at once, and cannot be parcelled out one before another — the Medicine Buddha does not first heal one's hunger and only after three years cure one's disease. The twelve vows are therefore to be read as one ground laid out in parallel, not as a row of rungs stacked one upon another. To put it in an image: the twelve vows are not a stairway climbed rung by rung, but the level ground at the foot of the mountain path; everything the ground holds — food, bodily form, the faculties, freedom from the perils that obstruct the Dharma — is set out all at once, not stacked step by step. What "enabling precept-keeping" names is exactly the guarding of this upstream field that brings a person to the foot of the precept.

Third, **an order of depth there is — yet depth is not time.** The only order of which the corpus truly admits comes from Lingyao's *Direct Explanation* — and it is a verdict of depth, not a row of before-and-after: the bodily reversals of the faculties, of wealth, of the female body are judged alike to be the "shallower benefit" of the two siddhāntas of the remedial and of the personal, while the genderless bodhi is the "deeper benefit" of the ultimate sense. This order belongs to the depth of the benefit, not to the time of its coming; and so it can stand alongside the parallel field, neither displacing nor confounding it.

Running through all three tiers is a single spine of distinction: "enabling precept-keeping" and "the fruition that follows from keeping precepts." The Bhaiṣajyaguru vows = enabling precept-keeping, the upstream parallel field that brings a person to the foot of the ladder; the precept → concentration → wisdom chain set out in the *Kimatthiya* discourse and its Pāli parallel = the fruition that follows from keeping precepts, the downstream sequential ladder climbed rung by rung. Both embody the principle "dharma benefits dharma, dharma conditions dharma," and the analogy between them is structural, not an equation: that the vow-order can be reversed and laid flat is precisely because "becoming a ladder" belongs to what is after the precept, downstream, while the upstream field has no rung to invert and no sequence to speak of. This is the exact sense of Chapter I's words, "the sequence is not on the face of the text but in the construction" — what is constructible is the downstream ladder and the verdict of depth; the surface disorder is the upstream field's own nature. The eighth vow, "transformation from female to male form," as the deepest single case under the dimension of "body," is handled throughout in a descriptive-historical manner and relativized from within the tradition itself (by Lingyao's verdict of "the shallower benefit," by the Vimalakīrti's "neither male nor female"), the author adding no modern adjudication.

In this way the paper also keeps the promise P19 made to the reader. P19 had asked "whether the disciplining of body and mind has a sequence"; the honest answer of the present paper is neither "there is a plain ladder" nor "there is no order to speak of at all," but: **not a linear ladder, but a parallel field, supplemented by a verdict of depth.** To guard the material ground of precept-keeping is to guard the upstream conditions by which a person "comes to the foot of the precept"; this is the steadiest sense in which the Medicine Buddha's vow-power supports the precepts.

From here, a look ahead. What this paper has treated is the **precondition** of precept-keeping — the upstream field that lets the precept be possible and be kept; and the next part turns, by way of the Prajñā corpus (the Diamond Sūtra and the Larger Prajñāpāramitā), to the **sign** of precept-keeping — the emptiness of the very sign of the precept. From "guarding the precondition of precept-keeping" to "the emptiness of the precept-sign" is just the turn from the ground of the precept to its ultimate.[^10] The upstream once settled, one may go on to ask: the precept-sign one keeps — what, after all, is its own-being?
