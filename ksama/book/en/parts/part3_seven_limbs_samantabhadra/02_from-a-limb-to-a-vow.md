## 3.2 From a Limb to a Vow

The last chapter said that confession is one limb of the sevenfold worship, ending at a completed act — once said aloud, it is concluded.

But the same confession has another face. Among Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, it is no longer merely a limb but a "vow" — the fourth great vow, "the confession of the karmic obstacle."

A limb and a vow — where do they differ? This chapter looks at that difference.

### In the Verses, Confession Ends When Said

The same body of Samantabhadra conduct-and-vows material has given rise to two shapes.

One is verse. Like that most widely circulated stanza, "Whatever evil deeds I have done in the past, all arising from beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion, / born of body, speech, and mind — all of them I now confess" — confession falls within the sequence of the seven limbs as an act done as soon as said: "all of them I now confess," completed, ending there.

The other is long prose — the vow-text in prose form. In the "Chapter of Samantabhadra's Conduct and Vows" of the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra*, confession is the fourth of the Ten Great Vows, and the close of its vow-text carries a sentence the verses do not have at all:

> This confession of mine has no exhaustion; moment after moment it continues, without interruption; in the karmic acts of body, speech, and mind there is no weariness.

The key is exactly here. The verses do not carry this "no exhaustion"; only the long-prose vow-text does.

The same confession, in the shape of a "limb," ends when said; in the shape of a "vow," it is pushed toward "no exhaustion."

The difference is not that confession has changed its content, but that it has been set into a different structure. Once it passes from a limb among the seven to a vow established among the Ten, it takes over the chassis of the "vow" — and a vow has temporal length. This is the exact sense of "from a limb to a vow": not that confession has grown larger, but that confession has been cast into the time of a vow.

### What the Fourth Vow Confesses

First read the fourth vow's text through once, for everything that matters is inside it:

> The bodhisattva reflects: "Throughout the past, beginningless eons, by greed, hatred, and delusion arising in body, speech, and mind, I have done evil deeds measureless and boundless. Had these evil deeds a bodily form, all of empty space could not contain them. I now, with the three karmas made pure, before all the Buddhas and bodhisattvas in every dust-mote land throughout the dharma-realm, with earnest mind confess, henceforth not committing them again, abiding ever in the pure precepts and all their merit."

The vow-text first defines what "the karmic obstacle" is. It does not treat the obstacle as a ready-made stone lodged in the way, to be hauled off; it describes a generative chain: "by greed, hatred, and delusion" is the root (the three poisons); "arising in body, speech, and mind" is the manifestation (the three karmas of body, speech, and mind); "done evil deeds" is the effect. The karmic obstacle is a process continually being produced, from root to manifestation to effect.

One line here is the most easily misread, and care is needed: "Had these evil deeds a bodily form, all of empty space could not contain them" — this is a sentence in the hypothetical mood, "had they … a bodily form," and its premise is precisely that karma has no bodily form. What it is saying is the sheer extremity of fault's quantity — so vast that space itself could not hold it; this is phenomenal repentance's hyperbole for the gravity of fault, not an assertion that "karma is a really existing thing." The line speaks of how much in "quantity," not of whether-it-is in "nature."

Only when what it confesses is understood can how it confesses be understood.

The vow-text's way of countering the karmic obstacle is not to declare it struck off at a stroke, but two turnings that rise together. One is "with the three karmas made pure … with earnest mind confess" — defilement arose by the three karmas of body, speech, and mind, and purity enters back through this same gate: with pure body, speech, and mind, one discloses the deeds formerly done by defiled body, speech, and mind. The other, and the weightier: "henceforth not committing them again, abiding ever in the pure precepts" — the true landing-point of confession is not that "the past karma is wiped away," but "henceforth it is not committed" and "abiding ever in the pure precepts."

This retells confession as a redirect, not an erasure. It does not promise that the debt you owe is struck from the books; what it promises is the turning of your heading.

One sentence must be added: the "before" in "before all the Buddhas and bodhisattvas" is the witnessing field, the field to which one entrusts oneself — not a judge who passes sentence and grants pardon. There is in the vow-text not half a sentence pronouncing "your transgression is absolved"; what can make the karmic obstacle dissolve is, throughout, the confessant's own pure three karmas, and that line "henceforth not committing them again." The Buddha can receive your confession — and to receive is not to absolve. That distinction was established earlier, and it holds here just the same.

### Four "Nevers" That Stretch Confession from a Point into a Line

That "no exhaustion" in the vow-text's second half is where "from a limb to a vow" truly bears its weight. How does it stretch confession from a point into a line?

By an extremely precise construction, which may be called "the four exhaustion-similes":

> Thus: were the realm of space exhausted, the realm of beings exhausted, the karma of beings exhausted, the afflictions of beings exhausted — then my confession would be exhausted. But because the realm of space, and so on down to the afflictions of beings, cannot be exhausted, this confession of mine has no exhaustion.

The way to read it is this. It first sets a condition for "stopping": only when the realm of space, the realm of beings, the karma of beings, and the afflictions of beings — all four — are "exhausted" does "my confession" become "exhausted" with them. Then it at once adds: but these four can never be exhausted.

And so the condition for "stopping" never obtains; the "exhausted" of "then my confession would be exhausted" is never triggered.

Confession has no exhaustion not because the confessant resolves to repent until the end of the world, gritting his teeth and holding out, but because its termination-condition has been constructed so that it can never obtain. It is inexhaustible because it has been established as a vow.

One thing must be seen clearly here, lest the matter be told crooked: this "four exhaustion-similes" is not peculiar to the confession-vow. It is the shared closing chassis of every one of the Ten Great Vows — the homage-vow says "then my homage would be exhausted," the offering-vow says "then my offering would be exhausted," the confession-vow says "then my confession would be exhausted"; the verb changes vow by vow, but the frame of "no exhaustion" is one and the same. So one cannot say "only the confession-vow has inexhaustibility." Yet this point is precisely the best corroboration of "from a limb to a vow": confession is inexhaustible exactly because it has been set into the "vow-form" that shares one inexhaustible chassis with the other nine vows — and that, in itself, is "rising from a limb into a vow."

### Moment After Moment, Not by the Grinding of Willpower

The four similes push "stopping" to where it never obtains; "moment after moment it continues, without interruption … there is no weariness" then spreads confession into an unbroken, stretching line of time.

Here the question comes: unbroken moment after moment — on what strength? On gritted-teeth perseverance?

The patriarch's answer is not what intuition expects. He says that what moment-to-moment continuity rests on is "the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice," not the grinding of willpower. What cannot hold out, what tires, what wearies, is that "mind that rises and falls," that "homage that grasps at outward marks" — do it with a mind that flickers up and down, eyes fixed dead on outward marks, and of course the moments will break. What truly keeps it unbroken is the power of a contemplative practice that does not follow arising-and-ceasing and does not grasp at outward marks.

This is exactly where the weight of the vow-text's words "there is no weariness" lies. An inexhaustible matter, received in the key of "a heavy burden," must move toward fatigue and anxiety; yet the vow-text, right after "no exhaustion," follows at once with "there is no weariness" — it is not describing a suffocating drudgery with no end in sight; it is stating that this aspirational structure carries, of itself, no key of weariness.

The patriarch also gave a fine simile: a room pitch-dark for a thousand years — one lamp comes in, and in an instant the light pervades it. A thousand years of dark, a moment's light, and the two, astonishingly, are equal in measure. The finite single moment has an infinite range. So the inexhaustible confession is not "a debt that can never be paid off," requiring you to carry, in every moment, the weight of measureless eons in repayment; it is a vow that is "sufficient in each moment, and stretching on moment after moment."

### Inexhaustible Is Not Hopeless

A confession established as "never completed" has one place where it is most easily misread, and it must be pointed out squarely here.

When people hear "never to be exhausted," the heart often sinks, sliding in two directions. One direction is "futility" — as if one had been sentenced to a labor that can never be finished, done and done again, never reaching the end. The other direction is "never enough" — as if no amount of confessing could wash one clean, done and done again, never at rest. Both sinkings are misreadings. They must be sorted out one by one.

The first: this is the making of a vow, not the serving of a sentence. The convict's labor is laid on him by another; this inexhaustible confession is an oath the confessant takes himself. One endures passively; the other sets out of its own resolve — the key is different from the very start.

The second: "there is no weariness" by itself refutes the reading of "futility." The suffering of futility lies in fatigue, in aversion; yet the vow-text expressly marks out "there is no weariness." This is not an optimistic consolation; it is the very nature of this aspirational structure — it carries no key of weariness to begin with.

The third: "no exhaustion" is not the same as a task "stuck incomplete." A task that could in principle be finished, yet is jammed and cannot be — that is what disheartens; but the fourth vow's "unfinished" is not a jamming. It is because the object aspired to — beings, afflictions — is boundless from the start. It is a vow that stretches together with the realms, not a goal that cannot be reached.

The fourth, and the most important: the "no end" of this inexhaustible confession rests on "the object aspired to is boundless," not on "this person of mine is never enough." To read the former as the latter is the most common, and the most wounding, of the misreadings — it turns a vigorous, living bodhi-vow into a self-torment of "I can never finish atoning."

With all four said through, that surface resemblance collapses: what the inexhaustible vow stretches is aspiration — not penalty, and not compulsion.

### Walking On

Gather Part Three into one sentence: at Samantabhadra's fourth vow, confession has climbed to the high-water mark of its aspirational form. From an act in the seven limbs that "ends when said," it has risen into a great vow that "continues moment after moment, with no exhaustion" — yet the ground of its inexhaustibility is that the realms are boundless, not that I am never enough; and its way of confessing is the redirect of "henceforth not committing them again, abiding ever in the pure precepts," not the erasure of the old account.

By this point, repentance at the level of the "phenomenal" has walked to its height — from a single plain disclosure in the Vinaya hall, all the way to an inexhaustible, never-wearied vow before the Buddhas of the ten directions. In the part before, we sounded the floor of phenomenal repentance upon the gravest karma; in this part, we have seen its summit at the height of worship. Between floor and summit, the full span of phenomenal repentance has been laid out.

But whether at the floor or at the summit, this has remained repentance at the level of the "phenomenal": the marks of fault stand vivid, disclosure is made to the Buddhas, removal is sought. The deeper question — those real faults, one by one, and that real "I" who seeks repentance: were they ever real? — has still not been asked head-on.

And to take one step forward from this height of phenomenal repentance, a particular threshold must first be crossed. That threshold concerns something more fundamental than "what is confessed" — it concerns "death" and "birth."

That is the matter of the next part.
