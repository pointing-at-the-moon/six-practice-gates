## 3.1 Where Confession Stands Among the Seven Limbs

The Mahāyāna possesses a highly fixed sequence of worship, which the Chinese tradition has come to call "the sevenfold worship" — seven links, in order: homage to the Buddhas, praise of the Tathāgatas, the vast cultivation of offering, confession of the karmic obstacle, rejoicing in merit, requesting the turning of the Dharma-wheel (together with the petition that the Buddhas remain in the world), and universal dedication.

Confession is one limb of it. In this worship it settled into a verse everyone can recite by heart:

> Whatever evil deeds I have done in the past, all arising from beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion, born of body, speech, and mind — all of them I now confess.

These four lines are the "confession of the karmic obstacle" limb among the seven. It is not a solitary business of atonement; it is a position within this string of worship. A person at home in this liturgy will not ask "why repent" — that is too obvious. He will ask a finer question: why is confession placed here, and not elsewhere? This chapter answers that question.

### A Subtraction, Lodged in a String of Additions

Take the seven limbs one by one and look at the "direction" of each, and something interesting comes into view.

Homage, praise, and offering are all outward-tending, augmenting — by body, speech, and mind one turns toward the Buddhas, gathering merit little by little. Rejoicing is gladness at another's merit, and it too augments. Requesting the Buddhas to turn the Dharma-wheel, asking the Buddhas to remain long in the world — these pray that the true Dharma endure. Dedication turns all the merit around and points it toward awakening.

All of these limbs are "adding."

Confession alone is "subtracting." It adds nothing; it removes, it lessens, the obstacle of fault already incurred.

Among the seven limbs, confession is the sole subtraction.

And where has this sole subtraction been placed? A summary verse near the close of the *Samantabhadra-caryā-praṇidhāna* sets the sequence out plainly:

> Homage, offering, and confession of fault; rejoicing in merit, and requesting; whatever merit I have gathered — all of it I dedicate to awakening.

Homage and offering before, confession of fault in the middle, rejoicing, requesting, and dedication after. This sole subtraction lies exactly between "the outward gathering of merit" before it and "the augmenting of merit" after it. It is not first, and it is not last; it stands at the very pivot where "adding" turns to "subtracting," and "subtracting" turns back to "adding."

### Why the Subtraction Is Inserted Among the Additions

Why this order?

Because to heap up merit while the obstacle of fault has not yet been removed is like pouring water into a leaking vessel — the vessel unclean, cracked, what is poured in must in the end drain away.

The wisdom of the seven limbs is to draw near to the Buddhas first by homage, praise, and offering, gathering an initial store of provisions; then at once to clear the obstacle of fault by confession, mending and washing the leaking vessel first; and only when the vessel is clean to fill it with merit through rejoicing, requesting, and dedication.

Purify the vessel first; fill it with merit after.

The slot where confession stands means exactly this. It is the gate of "purify, then increase" among the seven limbs — the merit gathered before must first pass through this gate and be cleared, before the merit that follows can be held. Its position is its meaning.

### The Same Disclosure, a Different Witness

Then this confession, carried up into worship — what act is it, exactly?

Said plainly, it is still the old act that has walked all the way from the Vinaya: disclosure — saying the fault aloud, not covering it.

This becomes clear the moment its provenance is laid open. In the Vinaya, when a monk knows he has a fault and seeks the way of purity, that way is disclosure. The *Daśādhyāya-vinaya* says it most bluntly:

> Having a fault, one should disclose it; disclosing it, he is at ease; not disclosing it, the fault grows deeper.

The set form of disclosure is quite fixed: first call the pure monk who will bear witness, then give one's own name, then state what fault was committed, then declare "I disclose; I do not conceal." Within it there is one crucial position — the position of the witness. Disclosure requires a pure counterpart, present, to bear witness.

Now set this Vinaya formula beside the Mahāyāna scene of worship.

In Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, the bodhisattva is said to perform three events constantly, at the six periods of day and night, and the first of them is disclosure to the Buddhas of the ten directions:

> I, so-and-so, whatever evil karmic faults of body, speech, and mind I have committed in this life or in past lives over measureless eons — these I confess before the present Buddhas of the ten directions, praying that they be extinguished, and I shall not do them again.

Set this passage line by line against the Vinaya's formula of disclosure, and it is almost the same mould: calling the witness (the Vinaya calls a pure monk; here, homage to the Buddhas of the ten directions); giving one's name (I, so-and-so); stating the fault (the evil deeds of body, speech, and mind); disclosing and seeking purity (not concealing; praying that they be extinguished); and the close (I shall not do them again — precisely the terminus of "guarding against recurrence" we met earlier).

Slot for slot, they match. Only one thing has been exchanged: the one who bears witness.

In the Vinaya, the witness is a pure monk before one's eyes, a Saṅgha present in person. In the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, the witness is all the present Buddhas of the ten directions.

The same act of disclosure — only the witnessing object has been exchanged, from the pure Saṅgha to the Buddhas of the ten directions.

And this makes one thing plain: the confession limb of the seven is not a link the Mahāyāna newly added to adorn its worship. It is the plainest act of the Vinaya chapter-house — saying aloud the fault that was hidden — carried up into worship before the Buddhas of the ten directions. The act has not changed; what has changed is the scene, and the one who bears witness. It is the oldest, and the most load-bearing, of the seven limbs.

### Why Confession Must Come First

That confession is set at the front of practice is not only an arrangement of sequence; in doctrine too it has its necessity.

The masters state it completely. First, it is a literal command: the scriptures say the bodhisattva should "first," with earnest sincerity, confess — confession is the task set in front. Second, the purification of fault is the precondition of seeing the Buddhas and of attaining concentration — only when the fault is purified do the Buddhas appear; only having departed from the fault does one attain samādhi. Purify first, and only then does everything else stand. Third, and the most cautionary point: covering over is not merely "not yet purified"; it corrodes in return — turning what might have become good into fault; whereas one who is willing to disclose, willing to confess and remove, becomes faultless and gains joy.

Put the three layers together and the reason confession stands in front comes through: without purity there is no gathering of merit, no seeing the Buddhas, no attaining of concentration. Confession is not the polite overture of worship; it is the causal ground of everything that follows — if the vessel is not washed clean, whatever is poured in afterward leaks.

### What Makes a Recited Confession Count

When the confession limb lands in practice, it becomes a liturgy that can be recited. The most exemplary instance is the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession — the confessant, before the names of the thirty-five Buddhas, in solitude by day and night, with earnest mind confesses, disclosing all at once the many faults of beginningless time — done by himself, caused to be done by others, rejoiced in upon seeing them done — together with those "whether concealed or not concealed," and asks that the Buddhas bear witness to him and hold him in mind.

Here is one place that must be seen clearly. A confession like this — with a fixed text, a fixed order, recitable over and over — what makes it count? The sincerity within the heart, or the reciting of the liturgy correctly?

The answer is that both are needed; neither can be spared.

Form alone will not do — phenomenal repentance requires "the earnest mind." To recite the liturgy through absent-mindedly does not accomplish purity. But neither can one say "it is all in the heart; the form does not matter" — the fixed text, the witness before the Buddhas, the fixed sequence are themselves doing work, helping to gather in and hold up the scattered mind.

Form and heart, both employed together — this is the middle way of confession: neither mere ritual walked through, nor mere mood relied upon. With the fixed text, the Buddhas' witness, and the ordered position holding it up from without, and the earnest mind being earnest within, this recited confession truly counts.

### Walking On

By the time it reaches the sevenfold worship, confession has passed from a single disclosure in the Vinaya hall into a worship-liturgy before the Buddhas of the ten directions, at the six periods of day and night — more solemn now, more colored by the devotion of worship. Yet it is still the same act: saying aloud what was hidden. And it is still repentance at the phenomenal level — what it faces is, throughout, one real fault after another, and a real self that seeks repentance.

And this confession, carried up into worship, can climb one step higher still.

In the next chapter, we watch how it rises from a "limb" among the seven into a "vow" among Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows — a confession established "never to be exhausted."
