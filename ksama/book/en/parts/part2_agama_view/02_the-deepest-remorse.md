## 2.2 The Deepest Remorse

The last chapter said that good remorse closes itself out: the fault seen, disclosed, received — and the ache comes undone. But what we watched there were ordinary faults — two monks in a quarrel of words, one venerable letting a resisting thought arise.

This chapter drives the same blade to its very end.

Ask the sharpest question there is: when what was done is no ordinary fault, but the heaviest deed in the human world — killing one's own father — how much force is left in the blade of remorse?

The Āgamas hold a man who seems made to the measure of this question.

### A King Who Killed His Father

Ajātaśatru, crown prince of Magadha, goaded by his evil friend Devadatta, killed his own father — King Bimbisāra, who ruled the country by the true Dharma. Of the five gravest evils, this is the heaviest.

And his fault does not stop at that one deed. Devadatta, wanting the Buddha dead, laid a plan before him: in the royal city there is a fierce elephant named Nāḷāgiri; make it drunk on strong liquor, and when the ascetic Gautama enters the city at dawn for alms, loose the drunken elephant to trample him to death. Ajātaśatru heard it, and in earnest gave the order. Weapon and decree alike came from his hand. He had also, day after day, set aside five hundred cauldrons of food — drawing on the resources of an entire kingdom — to provision, over the long run, the very Devadatta who was bent on splitting the Saṅgha and murdering the Buddha.

Set these side by side, and the moral weight of this man Ajātaśatru is utterly real. He is not a villain painted on a mask, nor the prodigal of some sentimental drama for whom one turn of the heart parts the clouds and clears the moon. It matters to remember this — precisely because the one the Buddha "received" was him, a man like this, and not someone guilty of a small mistake, can this case weigh out the true measure of phenomenal repentance.

### The Most Complete Remorse

Later, led by the royal physician Jīvaka, on the full-moon night of the fifteenth, King Ajātaśatru went to see the Buddha in Jīvaka's mango grove outside Rājagṛha. After hearing the Buddha teach, he rose from his seat, bowed head and face to the Buddha's feet, and made a disclosure that held nothing back:

> May the World-Honored One receive my repentance. I was mad, foolish, benighted, and without understanding … deluded by the five desires, I truly killed my father the king. May the World-Honored One, in pity and compassion, receive my repentance.

And the Buddha received it:

> One who can now repent the fault within the noble Dharma thereby benefits himself. Out of pity for you, I receive your repentance.

Read the Buddha's wording closely — "one who can repent the fault thereby benefits himself." The benefit comes by way of the repenter himself handing the fault over in accordance with the Dharma; it is not a pardon-decree dropped from the Buddha's side. "Benefiting oneself" is not "being absolved by someone." This distinction is the hinge to which the rest of this chapter returns again and again.

Nor does the format of this disclosure change because the fault is grave. The two monks of the last chapter, contending over who stood higher, walked — when they repented — the very same procedure this patricide king walked: see the fault, disclose it, guard against its recurrence; and they were met with the very same verdict: this is growth in the discipline of the noble ones. An ordinary man repenting a small fault and a patricide repenting the gravest evil use one and the same blade. Good remorse does not lose its force because what it must sever is enormous.

### And Yet, One Thing It Cannot Bring Back

This is the spine of the chapter.

After King Ajātaśatru had taken his leave, the Buddha turned to the monks seated there and said one thing. That saying is the keystone of the whole affair:

> This King Ajātaśatru's offenses are lessened, the grave fault already drawn out. Had King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he would have attained the pure Dharma-eye on this very seat; yet now, repenting the fault himself, his guilt is lessened, the grave fault already drawn out.

Two things here must be held apart.

One is the "lessened," the "grave fault drawn out" — the guilt lightened, the heavy load set down. This, the repentance genuinely did.

The other is that "had he not killed his father, he would have attained the pure Dharma-eye on this very seat" — and this, the repentance did not do, and cannot do. The Buddha names the cost with His own mouth: the disclosure ran its whole course and was received by the Buddha Himself, and still, on that seat, the fruit of the Dharma-eye simply could not arise in Ajātaśatru. Not because his remorse was not real enough, nor because the Buddha's receiving was not sincere enough, but because the deed of killing his father had already hollowed out, from the root, the very soil that fruit requires.

The wording must be kept exact: the sūtra says "lessened," says "the grave fault drawn out" — not "fault extinguished," not "fault removed." To lessen is not to remove.

What, then, was left to Ajātaśatru once the Dharma-eye was blocked? The sūtra keeps the account complete:

> Had this King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he should this very day have won the first fruit of recluseship … Yet even so, he has now gained great good fortune: he has gained rootless faith … Among my lay-followers, the one who has gained rootless faith — that is Ajātaśatru.

Though he could not realize the fruit, he still gained a great good fortune — a "rootless faith." Take care with this term: do not read it as "groundless faith," a faith with no reason behind it. It means faith, without a root — the patricide had severed the root of realization, and what remained was a conviction that had lost the support of the path-root and yet was still genuine. This is already the great good fortune a person of grave fault can reach; the Buddha even urges those who have done wrong by it: seek the means to accomplish this rootless faith. And yet it is not, for all that, the fruit that should have been his on that seat.

The lessening is real; so is the cost.

### The Salt Is Still in the Water

Why can remorse "lessen," yet not "remove"? The Buddha once gave a superb simile:

> As when a man casts an ounce of salt into a little water, making it too salty to drink.

Cast a handful of salt into a small cup of water, and it is too salty to drink; cast the same handful of salt into a great river, and no saltiness can be tasted. Where does the difference lie? Not in the salt — not one grain of it is gone; in the water. The water bearing the salt has grown great.

The karma of the fault is that handful of salt. Repentance, together with the sustained practice that follows it, enlarges the river-like continuum that bears the karma. What it turns is always the karma's trajectory, never the deed already done. The salt is still in the water.

And the Buddha drew a floor beneath all this:

> If one does a deed intentionally, I say one must experience its result — in this life or a later; if one does a deed unintentionally, I say one need not necessarily experience the result.

A deed done with intention must be experienced as its result — in this life, or in a later one. Intentional karma does not get away. Repentance can widen the bearing river, thin the saltiness to where it can be borne, turn a heavy ripening into a light experiencing — but it cannot make that handful of salt vanish into thin air.

This floor must be held, and held steadily. Phenomenal repentance can lessen, can redirect; it cannot erase.

### A Cleaner Example: Devadatta

For this truth — it can lessen and redirect, it cannot erase — there is an example cleaner still: Devadatta himself, the one who goaded Ajātaśatru.

> This was Devadatta's first committing of the five gravest evils … And because, at the very end, in the last moment of his life, Devadatta raised a mind of gladness and uttered "namo," he afterward became a paccekabuddha, named Namo.

He committed the five gravest evils, and fell, in his present body, into the Avīci hell, to undergo it there for a kalpa. Not half a measure of the karma was erased. And yet at the very moment of death he raised one thought of gladness, setting out to utter "namo" — and that one thought planted a seed reaching far ahead: when the kalpas have passed, he will in the end become a paccekabuddha, and his name will be Namo.

A kalpa in hell: the karma not erased. A paccekabuddha at the last: the destination turned. These two stand together in one and the same person — the plainest possible picture of "it can lessen and redirect; it cannot erase."

### The Buddha Can Receive, but Cannot Absolve

Gather this chapter into its one most needful sentence: the Buddha can receive; He cannot absolve.

The difference of that one word must be seen clearly. What does "absolve" mean? It means one party holds the power to cancel another's fault at a stroke — to move the guilt off a person the way one moves a stone away. What the Buddha did for Ajātaśatru was not this.

Karma cannot be borne by proxy, cannot be absolved by proxy: one's own deed, one's own burden. The Buddha did not, and could not, lift the karma of patricide off Ajātaśatru by a decree. What the Buddha "received" was the fault Ajātaśatru had truly seen and truly owned — He took up the act of handing the fault over as a fault; He did not cancel the deed at a stroke.

To receive the repentance is not to absolve the fault.

This is the one-word difference the chapter must hold. Ajātaśatru still pays his price — the Dharma-eye lost on that seat. Devadatta still undergoes his kalpa — the passage through the Avīci hell. The mind was set in order again, the karma's trajectory was turned; but the deed already done was canceled by no one. To "receive" is the Buddha, in compassion, taking up the remorse you hand over, while the road ahead is yours to walk; it was never anyone wiping your account clean for you.

### Walking On

Then let the matter rest here for now: that salt still steeping in the water, that Dharma-eye that will not return to the seat — can the root of a person's karma of fault, in the end, be purified at all? Or is it sealed shut for good?

The Āgamas walk to this point, and stop. At this end they plant a signpost, its arrow pointing toward a door not yet opened. The good blade of remorse can cut, but cannot cut to the root of the karma; the floor ends here, and the door leads to something deeper.

That deeper door will not be pushed open until much further on. For now, we must first carry remorse out of one person's aching heart and into a larger scene — when the act of "handing the remorse over" is no longer made toward people only, but toward the Buddhas of the ten directions, becoming a solemn offering and aspiration, what new weight will it be given?

That is the matter of the next part.
