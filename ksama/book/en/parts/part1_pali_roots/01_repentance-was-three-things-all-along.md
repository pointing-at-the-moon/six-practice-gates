## 1.1 Repentance Was Three Things All Along

When a person has done something wrong, an ache rises in the heart. Bottled up inside, that ache will wake a person in the middle of the night. For dealing with it, the Buddha's house has an ancient method, and we are used to calling it — repentance.

But the Chinese compound for repentance, *chàn-huǐ*, comes off the tongue today as smoothly as a single word. Take it apart, and you find it was stitched together — *chàn* and *huǐ* are of entirely different ancestry.

*Chàn* is short for the transliteration of an old Indian word, *kṣama*, whose root sense is close to "allow, bear, beg to be borne with" — what it asks for is to be received. *Huǐ* translates another word altogether (*kukkucca*), naming that churn of regret in the heart that will not let a person sit still. Two words of separate ancestry were sewn into one compound over the centuries of sūtra translation. And later readers have taken the compound for one undifferentiated mood: feeling sorry for what one has done, and asking to be forgiven.

More important still, the compound hides a third word. In the earliest Vinaya and Āgamas, what actually carries the whole procedure of making good on a fault is not the feeling of remorse but disclosure — saying the fault one has committed, plainly and openly, before one's fellow practitioners.

So repentance is in fact three things: remorse is the feeling within; disclosure is the saying before others; *kṣama* is the being-received. Feeling is feeling, saying is saying, receiving is receiving. Each of the three holds its own ground; run them together, and the whole goes blurred.

This chapter lays the three things out, one by one.

### *Kṣama* Needs Someone to Receive It

Begin with *kṣama*. The point most easily missed is this: the weight of *kṣama* falls not on the end of the one repenting, but on the end of the one who receives the repentance.

The *Madhyama Āgama* records one such making of amends. The Buddha, wandering in the Koliyan country, was staying in the siṃsapā grove outside the Northern Village. The local headman, Pāṭaliya by name, came to ask about the way, and in the course of the exchange spoke offense against the Buddha; after hearing Him teach, fear rose in him, and he rose from his seat at once, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, knelt with palms joined, and said:

> May the Gotama receive my repentance: seeing the offense, I disclose it; having repented, I guard against doing it again.

And the Buddha confirmed it:

> Whoever repents, seeing the offense and disclosing it, and guards against doing it again — such a one nourishes the noble Dhamma without loss.

Mark the word *receive* in the first line — *may the Gotama* **receive** *my repentance*. *Kṣama* is *kṣama* precisely in that it must be received by another party. However deeply a person feels remorse, if no one receives it, the repentance hangs in midair. *Kṣama* is not a monologue; it is a thing someone must come and catch.

Now look at the three beats the Buddha confirmed: seeing the offense, disclosing it, guarding against doing it again — to see the fault as a fault, to say it out, to guard thereafter against repeating it. Repentance that walks all three beats He calls the *growth* of the noble Dhamma. Taste that wording slowly: not the wiping out of an old account, but a step grown forward. The one who can see the fault, say it, and guard against it — his practice does not shrink; it grows. In the Buddha's Dhamma, repentance has been, from the first day, a forward-moving thing.

### Disclosure Is Saying, Not Feeling

Next, disclosure. That it is a different thing altogether from remorse — the cleanest evidence is a passage of the venerable Ānanda's in the Vinaya.

After the Buddha's final passing, the assembly of monks gathered to compile the Dhamma, and in that session an elder charged Ānanda with a minor offense. In his own mind Ānanda did not believe he was truly at fault, yet he said before them all: I do not see this offense; yet out of faith in the elders, I disclose it. — The same words, five times over.

See what this cuts apart. Ānanda had no remorse in him — he said outright that he did not *see* the offense — and he completed the disclosure all the same. So *feeling* is one matter, and *saying* is another. Disclosure is an act: to say, before one's fellow practitioners, a fault plainly and specifically named. It does not wait for the feeling of remorse to arrive before setting out.

The core of disclosure the Vinaya states in one short phrase: *not daring to conceal*. Not hiding, not covering, not screening. To lay what was hidden out into the light — that laying-open is the entire substance of disclosure.

### Remorse Is a Double-Edged Blade

Last, remorse. Remorse is an ingredient of the mind, and it has an unexpected property: it can be medicine, and it can be poison, depending entirely on where it lands.

Down the poison road: the sūtras take that turning, chewing, never-resting regret, join it to the mind's agitation, and call the pair restlessness-and-remorse, listing it among the five hindrances that veil practice. In sitting meditation, this churning remorse is a thing to be set down, because it disturbs calm and insight. The *Saṃyukta Āgama* records its most poisonous face: at the end of life, when the evils of a lifetime crowd in at once —

> The mind burns, the mind is wracked with remorse; and the mind, having given rise to remorse, gains no wholesome state.

A fire of remorse that burns the mind as it goes; the sūtra gives it a name — the law of burning.

Down the medicine road lie the three beats we have already seen: see the offense, disclose it, guard against doing it again. The same remorse, once willing to walk the road of saying-out and guarding-thereafter, is a force that spurs a person forward; bottled in the heart and chewed day and night, it congeals into a hindrance and burns into a fire.

And this says one thing outright: the feeling of remorse cannot carry repentance on its own. Precisely because remorse goes bad when bottled up, this method needs disclosure to say it out, and needs *kṣama* to let it be received — lest it ferment in the heart into an obstruction. The strength of repentance is to be spent on saying and guarding, not on feeling bad about oneself.

### Saying It Out, to Return to the Ranks of the Pure

What, then, is disclosure in the Saṅgha actually like? The Dharmaguptaka *Vinaya* preserves a standard formula. A bhikkhu who has committed an offense is to say, before the assembly:

> Now before the venerable one I make repentance; I do not dare to conceal it. To repent is ease; not to repent is unease. … May the elder remember me as one of pure precept-body, fit to take part in the pure *uposatha*.

See where the passage comes to rest — *may the elder remember me as pure*. The purpose of disclosure is to return to the ranks of the pure, to the company of those who dwell and practice together in the Saṅgha. Before the fortnightly recitation of the precepts, the Saṅgha asks three times over: whoever has an offense upon him, let him disclose it. One who knows he has an offense and through three askings does not bring it out — the Vinaya rules it deliberate false speech, and the Buddha declared deliberate false speech a path-obstructing dhamma. What hiding obstructs is nothing other than one's own path.

Two further provisions, peculiar at first sight, light up the nature of this whole affair. First: if for a time no one in the Saṅgha is pure — no one fit to receive a repentance — the monks may go elsewhere and seek purity from a visiting bhikkhu: purity, it turns out, can be invited in from outside. Second: a bhikkhu too ill to attend may send word of his purity through another: purity, it turns out, can be conveyed by proxy. If purity were only a state at the bottom of one person's heart, neither provision would make sense. Only one understanding makes sense of them: this purity is held in common by the assembly, and can be passed from one to another; disclosure brings the hidden fault before the assembly, and joins one back to that shared purity.

So this method was never, from the start, a person behind a closed door wrestling with his own guilt; nor is it handing the offense into someone's keeping and begging to be absolved. What it asks is to be *received* — received by one's fellow practitioners, returned to the ranks of the pure, guarding well thereafter. The patriarchs of later ages called this repentance — worked on the plane of deeds, through speech and procedure — phenomenal repentance. The name came later; the thing itself had been practiced in the Vinaya since the Buddha's own day.

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

Phenomenal repentance has its ceiling, and it is best seen clearly at the outset, lest expectation set off in the wrong direction.

King Ajātaśatru of Magadha, goaded by an evil friend, killed his father Bimbisāra — a king who ruled by the Dhamma. On a full-moon fifteenth night, led by the royal physician Jīvaka, he came to see the Buddha in Jīvaka's mango grove outside Rājagaha. After hearing the Buddha teach, he rose from his seat, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, and disclosed his offense:

> May the World-Honored One receive my repentance. I was mad, foolish, benighted, and without understanding … deluded by the five desires, I truly killed my father the king. May the World-Honored One, in pity and compassion, receive my repentance.

The Buddha received his repentance:

> You are foolish and benighted, without understanding … one who can now repent the fault within the noble Dharma thereby benefits himself. Out of pity for you, I receive your repentance.

But once King Ajātaśatru had bowed and gone, the Buddha turned to the bhikkhus seated before Him and said:

> This King Ajātaśatru's offenses are lessened, the grave fault already drawn out. Had King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he would have attained the pure Dharma-eye on this very seat; yet now, repenting the fault himself, his guilt is lessened, the grave fault already drawn out.

Hear the two layers of this. The first is *lessened, the grave fault already drawn out* — repentance is genuinely of use; a karmic burden that heavy is truly lightened, loosened. But the other layer the Buddha states just as plainly: had he never harmed his father, he would, on hearing this one teaching, have attained the pure Dharma-eye on the spot; as it is, he has repented, his guilt is lessened — yet the Dharma-eye of that seat was, in the end, missed.

*Lessened*, in the end, is not *erased*. The father was already slain — and that is the place phenomenal repentance cannot reach.

This limit is exactly where the dignity of phenomenal repentance lies. It does not boast of striking every offense from the books at one stroke; it says, truthfully: say it out, guard it, walk on — the obstruction will lighten, and the road will open. As for the deeper layer, that waits for a deeper Dhamma to shine on it; this book, further along, will walk there of itself.

### Walking On

Gather this chapter into one old line of the Vinaya: to disclose is ease; to conceal obstructs the path.

The one who speaks finds ease; the one who hides obstructs his own path — it sounds like a word of encouragement. But in the Vinaya it is far more than encouragement. The Vinaya has put a plain, marked price on hiding: as many days hidden, so many more days on the road home.

In the next chapter, we go to see how that price is reckoned.
