## 7.2 Vimalakīrti Rebukes Upāli

Two monks broke a precept.

Carrying their shame, not daring to bring the matter before the Buddha, they came to Upāli — and the choice was a reasonable one, for Upāli is "foremost in upholding the Vinaya," the one in the community who knows the precepts and the procedures of clearance better than anyone. They asked him to explain the matter lawfully for them, so that the fault might be extinguished and the doubt and remorse in their hearts set down. And Upāli did exactly that: following the Vinaya, he set it out for them point by point.

Up to here, everything is "lawful." Disclosure, explanation, clearing the offense by procedure — this is the proper path of phenomenal repentance, the stable mechanism by which the Saṅgha has handled fault for more than two thousand years. The whole foundation of phenomenal repentance laid earlier in this book — from disclosure and probation to the *uposatha* and the invitation — has, as its guardian, precisely a keeper of the Vinaya like Upāli.

Then Vimalakīrti came. The first thing he said to Upāli was not a supplement, not a word of praise, but a flat rebuke:

> Upāli, do not compound the transgression of these two monks; extinguish it directly, and do not disturb their minds.

The force of the sentence lies exactly in whom it strikes. What Vimalakīrti rebukes is not the two offending monks, but the one who acted by the Vinaya without a single fault — the foremost in upholding it.

How can a Vinaya-master who lawfully explains to others how an offense is to be extinguished be charged, of all things, with "compounding the transgression"?

### What Compounds the Transgression Is Fixing Its Mark, Not the Breach

To read "do not compound the transgression of these two monks," the first point — and the one most easily lost — is this: the sentence does not say "these two monks have no transgression." Vimalakīrti does not deny that they broke the rule; his word is "compound" — do not add a further layer onto the fault already there.

What, then, is that superfluous layer that gets added?

Kumārajīva's comment names it in a single stroke: the trouble lies in "fixing the mark of his transgression and pressing it further upon him." One who has broken the rule is already trembling at heart; if the transgression is now fixed into a settled, immovable real mark, and the offender reproached over and over against this reified mark, the only result is that "the mind is disturbed and the transgression compounded."

In other words, what compounds the transgression is not the deed, but the act of "fixing the mark of transgression" — and the repeated reproach that follows from it.

This unties Vimalakīrti's two seemingly mild but in fact very heavy instructions: "extinguish it directly; do not disturb their minds." "Extinguish it directly" means: finish it cleanly, do not breed it into an object that lodges in the heart. "Do not disturb their minds" points still more sharply at the wound — a "lawful explanation" that traces the mark of transgression deeper with every stroke is exactly what disturbs the mind. There is nothing wrong with the master's explanation itself; what is wrong is that it has been turned into an instrument for fixing the mark of transgression.

Hidden here is a loop that is easy to miss. After a breach, the mind already hangs and panics. An exposition that fixes the mark one detail at a time, reproaching it again and again, does not settle that dread; it gives the dread an ever-clearer object to fasten upon. The clearer the object, the more the dread finds purchase; the deeper the purchase, the more one wants to make the mark sharper still, in hope of "settling it for good" — and so fixing, reproach, and dread feed one another, winding into an ever-tightening coil.

Vimalakīrti's rebuke severs this coil from the outside in a single stroke: not consoling you from within the coil, but pulling out the axis on which it turns — the reified mark of transgression. Once the mind has no mark to fasten upon, the dread loses its purchase. Kumārajīva's "the burden of transgression dissolves of itself" names exactly this loosening, after the purchase is gone.

This is the deepest line between principial and phenomenal repentance: phenomenal repentance extinguishes the transgression lawfully; principial repentance sees that the transgression so extinguished never had a mark that could be fixed in the first place.

### Transgression Is Not Within, Without, or in Between: Searched Out, It Is Wholly Empty

On what ground, then, can one say the mark of transgression cannot be fixed? Sengzhao's comment takes up Vimalakīrti's line — the nature of transgression is "neither within, nor without, nor in between" — and gives it a dependent-origination argument.

Is that transgression "within"? If it subsisted of itself in the offender's mind, it would have to be there even before an object was met and a thought arose — but that is not so. Is it "without"? If it subsisted of itself in the outer object, the object is itself without awareness; what has it to do with me? Is it "in between"? With neither inner nor outer established, there is no further independent "between" in which to lodge it. Searched through all three places and not found, the conclusion follows: transgression has no "real nature" subsisting apart from the concourse of conditions. It is an event-mark arisen from the meeting of body, speech, mind, object, and condition — present-seeming when conditions gather, vanished without trace when they disperse. Sengzhao's four words: "searched out, it is wholly empty."

This must be read together with Vimalakīrti's own similes. He says all dharmas are "like a dream, like a flame, like the moon in water, like an image in a mirror." The moon in the water is not the absence of a reflection; the image in the mirror is not sheer nothing; they do arise, yet they have no self-substance that could be pointed to and seized. So with transgression — it does arise (the breach is a fact), yet it has no own-being that could be fixed and reproached over and over. "Searched out, it is wholly empty" speaks of exactly this "appearing yet without own-being" — not of "it never happened at all."

### Transgression Binds Only the One Willing to Hold It Real

Daosheng's comment drives this line to its most thoroughgoing sentence: the mark of transgression is firm only because a person has, with his own hands, "sealed up" that confusion, held it fast as a really-existent object, and then used it to bind himself. The key that unties the knot, then, was never elsewhere — "no longer binds himself with transgression." Once one sees that transgression has, from the first, no own-being, the self-tied knot loosens; "to one who has left the realm of transgression, can transgression lay hold?" — when a person has walked out of the realm of transgression that deluded thought built, whom is transgression left to seize?

This stroke is of the first importance, because it returns responsibility and freedom to the person in one motion. Transgression is not some stain imposed from outside, needing an outside force to wash it away; it is a knot the person constructs, seals, and binds for himself. The one who can bind is oneself; the one who can release is oneself.

But a very fine, very crucial distinction must be drawn here. "The one who can release is oneself" sounds as if it asks a person to summon a greater resolve and wrench free — yet Daosheng says exactly the opposite. The "sealing" is itself an act of effort: it cost the person effort to seal that confusion firmly into a really-existent mark of transgression, and further effort to keep clutching it. Release, then, is not adding a still greater force against it, but seeing that this whole act of sealing was superfluous, that what was sealed had no own-being to begin with — whereupon the hand that has been straining all along opens of itself.

The knot is not wrenched open by a force pulling the other way; in the seeing that "the axis never existed," it loses, whole, the ground on which it turned.

### "Called a Keeper of the Vinaya": The True Keeper Is the One Who Knows Transgression's Emptiness

Having broken the false grasp, Vimalakīrti does not stop at breaking; he lays down a positive verdict: "One who knows this is called a keeper of the Vinaya."

The weight of the sentence lies in its redefinition of "keeping the Vinaya" — of upholding the discipline.

On the surface, Upāli, foremost in upholding the Vinaya, is the very exemplar of keeping it; but Vimalakīrti says the true keeping belongs to the one who "knows this" — who knows that transgression has no own-being, who does not fix its mark, who does not bind himself to it. And so a precise paradox appears: at the letter of the Vinaya, Upāli is beyond reproach; but at the Vinaya's ultimate, the foremost upholder — of whom it is said "even the foremost upholder of the Vinaya could not have said it" — is overtaken by the "supreme wisdom" the two precept-breaking monks now praise.

Hearing these words, the two monks' doubt and remorse dissolved on the spot, and they exclaimed: "What supreme wisdom! This is beyond Upāli; even the foremost upholder of the Vinaya could not have said it."

And this is the strongest possible answer to the suspicion that "if transgression is empty, then anything goes" — an answer that comes from the sūtra itself, not as an external defense. One might worry: does teaching "transgression has no own-being" not open a back door onto "breaking the rule doesn't matter"? The scene's reply runs flatly in the opposite direction: the one who knows transgression's emptiness is not the one who fails to keep the Vinaya, but the one who truly keeps it. For he keeps it not out of dread of the mark of transgression, nor does he operate his keeping as an instrument for reproaching himself or others again and again; he keeps it cleanly, keeps it with nothing for the mind to snag on, and his observance is, if anything, the steadier for it.

The core of truly keeping the Vinaya is prajñā, not fear.

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

The most dangerous and most common misreading of this chapter must be nailed down head-on: "do not compound the transgression of these two monks" is by no means "these two monks have no transgression," and by no means "breaking the rule doesn't matter."

Vimalakīrti's word is "compound," not "none." From beginning to end he does not deny the fact that the two monks broke the rule, nor does he sweep karmic fruit away. What he rebukes is the added act of "fixing the mark of transgression and reproaching it again and again," not the accomplished fact of the breach. "Transgression has no own-being" says that transgression has no fixable own-being (searched out, it is wholly empty) — not that "it never happened," not that "it need not be faced."

Holding to the measure this book has set down all along: seeing emptiness never cancels conditioned cause and effect; to say transgression cannot be apprehended does not conflict with bearing the karma one has made — the former loosens the knot of "my grasping at the mark of transgression"; the latter faces the conditioned event itself. In reading this chapter, let "emptiness" slip into "annihilation," and the whole vehicle overturns.

One more word must be added: Vimalakīrti's rebuke is a teaching that the wisdom of prajñā delivers to a Vinaya-master at the fitting moment, aimed at disclosing the ultimate sense of the Vinaya; it is not, and should not be rewritten into, an operable set of "contemplate-emptiness-to-extinguish-transgression" steps. To take this scene as a technical sequence of "first fix the transgression, then dissolve it by contemplating emptiness" is to fall straight back into the very "fixing of the mark of transgression" it breaks. Principle and phenomenon must stand apart: the disclosure-procedure of phenomenal repentance keeps its own place, and the seeing of principial repentance neither replaces it nor is reduced to it.

### Walking On

Upāli: from the volume's opening as the founder who compiled the Vinaya and recited the precepts for the Saṅgha, to this scene where Vimalakīrti rebukes him full in the face — one and the same person. By his hand phenomenal repentance was founded; on his figure principial repentance summits. Head and tail, just so, are joined.

And the two monks, their doubt and remorse gone at the hearing, did not rest at "I am free" — in that moment they aroused the awakening-mind, vowing "may all beings attain this discernment." The mark of transgression once emptied, what rose in the space it cleared was, of all things, a vow turned toward beings.

This thread, turning from "emptiness" toward "vow," is held down for now. Before us, the truth that "transgression has no own-being" must land one layer further — onto the six sense-gates of this body of ours.
