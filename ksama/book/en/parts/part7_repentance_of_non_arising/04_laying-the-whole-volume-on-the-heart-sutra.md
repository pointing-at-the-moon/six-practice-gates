## 7.4 Laying the Whole Volume on the Heart Sūtra

The gate of repentance has walked a long way, and its material has changed many times: the Vinaya's disclosure, probation, and *uposatha*; the seeing of an auspicious sign in sign-based repentance; the death-and-rebirth bridge; Vimalakīrti's head-on rebuke; the contemplation in which the six faculties dissolve, one by one, into the real mark. In this final chapter the material changes to a sūtra of barely two hundred characters — the *Heart of the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra*.

What this chapter offers is not one more case but a map: the whole arc of the gate of repentance laid over the Heart Sūtra, phrase by phrase, to see how the two line up, sentence by sentence.

To say it another way: the gate's entire movement from disclosure to non-arising is nothing other than the Heart Sūtra's own movement.

(One discipline must be restated first: the Heart Sūtra contains, from beginning to end, not a single word "repentance." That "repentance is the Heart Sūtra's emptiness," that "the Six Practice Gates answer to the Heart Sūtra's five verbs and one emptiness" — this is this book's own reading, not the sūtra's explicit wording, and not the consensus of the historical commentaries. The phrase-by-phrase convergence below lays this book's reading open for inspection; it does not claim the repentance method was written into the sūtra all along.)

### The Eye That Sees, and the Bridge That Crosses Over

The Heart Sūtra opens:

> When Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva was practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā, he clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty, and crossed beyond all suffering and distress.

This one line already touches two of the Six Practice Gates' neighboring gates.

Take "clearly saw" first. The one word "contemplate" is the border of the mindfulness gate — the fixing of mind and the contemplation that gate cultivates are here pushed to the limit of "practicing the profound prajñā." For repentance, this seeing eye is exactly the eye of principial repentance: phenomenal repentance discloses before the assembly and clears the fault by formal act, and belongs to the realm of marks; principial repentance turns back and sees that the transgression repented has, in the end, no own-being at all. What the sūtra's opening "clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty" supplies is the very eye on which principial repentance rests — not the washing-away of a transgression, but the clear seeing that the five aggregates the transgression rests upon are originally all empty.

Then "crossed beyond all suffering and distress." The one word "cross" is the border of the giving gate. The bridge by which the gate of repentance receives the gate of giving was laid earlier: giving carried to its extreme is "the loss of self," and only the self once lost can a fault be disclosed lawfully. The Heart Sūtra's "crossed beyond all suffering and distress" points out that bridge's far end — not that a self first exists and then has its transgression cleared, but the seeing that there was never a self standing there to be charged with transgression and held onto. The step of "the loss of self" in the gate of repentance is, just here, the synonym of "clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty."

### Form Itself Is Emptiness — Landed on Transgression, It Is the Repentance of Non-Arising

The core of the Heart Sūtra falls in this section:

> Form is not different from emptiness, emptiness is not different from form; form itself is emptiness, emptiness itself is form. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise so. All dharmas are marked with emptiness; they neither arise nor cease, are neither defiled nor pure, neither increase nor decrease.

In the six gates' mapping onto the Heart Sūtra's "five verbs and one emptiness," the "emptiness" to which repentance answers falls exactly on this section. Why?

Because what the gate's deepest form — the repentance of non-arising — sees is exactly this line, "all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing," landed upon transgression.

Transgression is one dharma among all dharmas. If all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing, then transgression too is marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing. And if transgression never truly arose, there is no really-existent transgression waiting to be extinguished; what is called "repentance," then, is not the wiping-away of a really-existent stain, but the seeing that the stain never once arose in the manner of an own-being. This is the literal sense of the three words "repentance of non-arising": what one repents is transgression's "non-arising" — and non-arising is exactly "neither arising nor ceasing."

Seen by position, the correspondence is finer still. This section of the sūtra falls exactly on a seam: above, it carries forward from "form itself is emptiness" back through the "cross" of "crossed beyond all suffering and distress" (the giving side); below, it opens onto "therefore in emptiness there is no form" and all the "no"s that follow (the precepts side). The move of "emptiness" is wedged exactly between "crossing" and "no."

And the place of repentance among the Six Practice Gates is exactly this: it follows the giving gate (giving carried to the loss of self, after which disclosure becomes possible) and opens onto the precept gate (the karma cleared, one is fit to receive the precept-substance). Repentance is the one and only subtractive gate precisely because it stands at this turning — from the marked "crossing" into the markless "no." The position lines up, and so the meaning lines up.

And this line, "neither arising nor ceasing," appears in three texts at once, forming the spine of the convergence. The first is the Heart Sūtra's line itself: all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing. The second is the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra*'s mind-contemplation passage:

> Such dharma-marks neither arise nor cease. What is transgression? What is merit? My own mind is empty of itself; transgression and merit have no owner… this is named repentance without the mark of transgression.

The third is Master Zhiyi's transgression-contemplation passage in the *Shi chan boluomi*:

> Neither apprehending transgression nor non-transgression, contemplating that transgression does not arise, breaking all transgression — because the root-nature of all transgressions is empty and ever pure.

Three sentences, three layers of one and the same thing: the Heart Sūtra gives the general principle of "neither arising nor ceasing"; the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* trains it on transgression and merit — "my own mind is empty of itself; transgression and merit have no owner" — and so earns the name "the repentance free of the mark of transgression"; Master Zhiyi names that training, aimed at transgression and merit, "contemplating that transgression does not arise."

And so the convergence stands: the repentance of non-arising is the name of the Heart Sūtra's "emptiness," landed on the face of transgression.

### No Knowledge, and No Attainment: Subtracted to the End

Past the core section, the Heart Sūtra strings "no" upon "no":

> Therefore in emptiness there is no form, no feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness; no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind; no form, sound, scent, taste, touch, or dharma… no suffering, origin, cessation, path; no knowledge, and no attainment.

This line — "no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind" — is exactly the prajñā floor beneath the last chapter's six-faculty repentance. The last chapter walked six gates, each faculty disclosing in turn, each dissolving into the real mark; here the Heart Sūtra gathers those six gates into a single line — "in emptiness, no six faculties." Not that the six faculties do not exist, but that they have no own-being to be grasped — and it is just this that leaves room for the faculties to dissolve into the real mark.

And that line "no knowledge, and no attainment" drives the "no" to both ends, the knower and the known.

This is the repentance of non-arising's ultimate step. The earlier lines saw that the transgression repented has no own-being (transgression does not arise); this line shines empty, together, the wisdom that repents and the fruit attained — the wisdom that repents is unapprehendable, the transgression repented is unapprehendable, and even "I have repented myself clean," held as an attainment, is unapprehendable. By this point even "repentance," the act itself, is no longer set up as a thing with an attainment to its name. This is the subtractive gate subtracted to the very end: grasping subtracted away, until knower and known are spent together.

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

It is exactly this run of "no"s that most invites a nihilist reading — does "no suffering, origin, cessation, path" not sweep away the Four Noble Truths? Does "no knowledge, and no attainment" not abolish all practice and realization? This is the highest risk this chapter faces — the highest risk faced by any piece of writing on the Heart Sūtra.

See this clearly first: what the string of "no"s negates is "own-being"; it does not negate by positing an entity called "emptiness." "No knowledge, and no attainment" negates "an existent wisdom to be relied on, an existent attainment to be grasped"; it does not say there is no wisdom, no practice, no realization. To seize "emptiness" as a really-existent thing to be grasped is to reify emptiness — exactly the inversion the Heart Sūtra itself most wants to break.

More to the point, the Heart Sūtra does not abandon the reader inside the "no"; it sets its own guard then and there:

> Because there is nothing to attain… ultimate nirvāṇa. The buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi… able to remove all suffering, real, not false.

Follow the line: because there is nothing to attain, the bodhisattva's mind is without obstruction and reaches ultimate nirvāṇa; precisely because they rely on prajñā, the buddhas of the three times attain unsurpassed bodhi; and this sūtra, the most "no"-dense of texts, closes on "able to remove all suffering, real, not false." The "no" is not annihilation; the "no" is the very condition under which "removing all suffering" becomes possible at all — and the efficacy of that removal is "real, not false."

Landed on repentance, the logic is the same: that the repentance of non-arising sees transgression as having no own-being does not mean transgression may be ignored, but the opposite — precisely because transgression has no own-being, it can truly be seen through; had transgression an own-being, solid and immovable, there would never come a day of its being repented clean. The sun of wisdom can dissolve the frost and dew precisely because the frost and dew were never really existent to begin with.

### The Final Meeting, and Summiting Does Not Dismantle the Stairs

Gather the phrase-by-phrase journey into one, and the thread is a single line: clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty (principial repentance opens its eye) → all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing (transgression has no own-being — the repentance of non-arising) → no knowledge, and no attainment (the repenting and the repented alike unapprehendable).

Repentance pressed deep, all the way to where knower and known are spent together, just is prajñā's seeing made present; and prajñā, the moment it falls back on the object of transgression, shows itself as the repentance of non-arising. The two are one thing said two ways: from the side of prajñā, the clear seeing that all dharmas are marked with emptiness; from the side of repentance, the contemplating that transgression does not arise. They meet, at last, on the single line "neither arising nor ceasing" — and this is "the final meeting of repentance and prajñā."

Last, a discipline must be kept — the one most easily lost at the summit: summiting does not dismantle the stairs.

Walking the phrases to "no knowledge, and no attainment," an illusion arises all too easily — if, in the end, knower and known are alike empty, were not the first six parts, all those formed and phenomenal repentances, mere expedients after all, fit in the end to be discarded?

It is not so. What the repentance of non-arising "empties" is the grasping at the prior five gates, not the five gates themselves. To see that transgression has no own-being is not to be excused from disclosure, from the formal act, from the *uposatha* — just as to see that the five aggregates are all empty is not to stop eating and dressing from then on. Phenomenon and principle are two co-standing faces: principial repentance sees through to emptiness exactly so that phenomenal repentance does not stiffen into a regime of self-managed scoring; and phenomenal repentance keeps its concrete form exactly so that principial repentance has stairs to land on and does not drift off into empty talk.

To climb the summit of prajñā is to tread exactly the stairs of phenomenal repentance laid step by step below; and looking back from the top, the stairs, far from dismantled, each at last find their place.

### Walking On

A Heart Sūtra of barely two hundred characters, from "clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty" to "real, not false," turns out, from first to last, to be the whole arc of the gate of repentance, told another way.

This chapter is the doctrinal summit of the whole volume.

But the summit is not yet the sealing of the pen. However high, however transparent this teaching stands — the seeing that transgression has no own-being — it must still be set back into daily use and tested there: when people today co-opt repentance as a tool of psychotherapy, degrade disclosure into a confession-style guilt-management, misuse "non-arising" as a pretext for dodging responsibility — how does this teaching answer?

That is for the last part of the book. From the doctrine's highest point, we go down the mountain — back into daily use.
