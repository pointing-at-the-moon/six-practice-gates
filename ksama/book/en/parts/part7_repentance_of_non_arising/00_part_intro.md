# Part Seven · The Repentance of Non-Arising: Transgression Has No Own-Being · Was the Self That Erred Ever Real

We have come to the deepest part of the whole book.

In the six parts before this, we have seen the whole landscape of repentance — its acts, its institutions, its psychological engine, its limits, the summit of worship, the death-and-rebirth bridge, the ladder of the three repentances, the two poles of the Chinese repentance liturgies. Yet however high the road climbed, every repentance along the way asked one and the same question: "how is this transgression to be cleared?" It granted that the transgression was apparently there, really existent, and then sought ways to clear it, lessen it, turn it.

This part asks that no longer.

It turns back and asks a more fundamental, and more startling, question: that transgression, that "I" who erred — were they themselves ever real?

At several crossroads along the way we set up signposts, their arrows pointing at this door. With King Ajātaśatru — that Dharma-eye, won on the very seat, from which there is no going back: can the root of karmic transgression be purified to the very end? At the top of the ladder of the three repentances — the highest rung, "the repentance of non-arising," was to see into exactly this question. Before the door of the tenth stage of the Lotus Samādhi Repentance — Master Zhiyi said that to enter that deepest single thought, "one must then set aside what was practiced before." All these signposts point at one and the same door. Now, we push it open.

Behind the door is the highest of the three repentances, the one Master Zhiyi calls "most honored and most sublime" — the repentance of non-arising. It answers to that one word of the Heart Sūtra: "emptiness." Through the first six parts we have kept that word pressed down, unspoken; here, the time has come.

Four chapters. The first uncovers the great matter head-on: at repentance's deepest point, what happens is not the erasing of a really-existent transgression, but the seeing that "the self that erred" and "the transgression I committed" cannot, from the first, be apprehended — this is the repentance of non-arising, the emptiness of the Heart Sūtra appearing in the gate of repentance. The second walks into the fullest classical scene of this teaching — Vimalakīrti rebukes Upāli — to watch the paradox that "the true keeper of the Vinaya is precisely the one who knows that transgression has no own-being." The third sets this "emptiness," one by one, into the six sense-gates of eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, to watch how "transgression has no own-being" lands as a principial repentance of the body. The fourth lays the whole volume across the two hundred and some characters of the Heart Sūtra, phrase by phrase, to watch how repentance and prajñā finally meet.

Three boundary-stones must first be set firm at the door, because this part is the deepest — and the most easily misread.

First: to see that transgression has no own-being is by no means to say "transgression is a fiction, so there is no need to repent, and one may do as one pleases." What emptiness negates is transgression's own-being, not karma's fruit. Transgression's nature is empty; karmic fruit is not empty — the law that one reaps what one sows is not moved by a hair. To let "transgression cannot be apprehended" slide into the sweeping-away of cause and effect is the most dangerous wrong turn in this whole part. Emptiness is a seeing, not annihilation.

Second: what is "emptied" here is the grasping that takes transgression as really existent, lodges it in the heart, and reproaches it over and over — together with the "I" who repents; not the fact that karma was once made. "Free of the mark of transgression" is not the same as "free of transgression" — it is repentance carried to the point of seeing that transgression has no own-mark, not a claim that nothing was ever committed.

Third: to climb this highest rung does not dismantle the stairs below. The repentance of non-arising is the highest, hardest rung of the three; it stands upon the two beneath it and lends them support — it does not replace them. One who has not yet repented concretely, in the phenomenal, has no standing to say "transgression is originally empty." The whole long road of the first six parts was walked exactly so that a person might earn the standing to stand before this door.

One more word on method. "Repentance answers to the Heart Sūtra's emptiness," "the Six Practice Gates answer to the Heart Sūtra's five verbs and one emptiness" — this is this book's own reading, not the sūtra's explicit wording, and not the consensus of the historical commentaries. Wherever the convergence is spoken of below, the book's reading is being laid open for inspection — not a claim that the sūtra wrote it so all along.

The boundary-stones are set. We push open the door, to look at the deepest question of all: was transgression itself ever real?
