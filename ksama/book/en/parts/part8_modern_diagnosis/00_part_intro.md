# Part Eight · The Modern Diagnosis, and Daily Use · Down the Mountain, Back into the Days

We have come down the mountain from the highest point of the teaching.

A person who has climbed to the summit cannot simply live on the summit. However high, however clear the seeing that the nature of transgression is empty, it must be set back into the days, one day after another, before it counts. This last part is the descent — taking this whole arc of teaching, from the Pāli Vinaya to the repentance of non-arising, and setting it back into today's daily use.

And at the foot of the mountain, what meets us head-on are the misunderstandings of repentance peculiar to our own age. They are not the ancients' problem; they are our generation's — and for just that reason, they are what this book cannot close without answering.

Two chapters.

The first chapter takes these modern tinted spectacles off and looks at them clearly. People today slip all too easily into reading repentance as three things it is not: as emotion-management (performing a repentance to "feel a little better"); as disclosure to an authority in exchange for absolution (like confession — asking someone to wipe the slate at a stroke); and as self-debasing shame (where owning a fault means admitting "I am a bad person," and so collapsing inward, flogging oneself over and over). This chapter must say it plainly: repentance is not guilt-management; to receive is not to be absolved; clarity is not self-flagellation.

The second chapter is the final chapter of this book. With the spectacles off, it answers, head-on, the plainest question of all: how, exactly, is repentance to land in the life of one day after another? We will see that the shape repentance takes in the scriptures was never a point — a "handle it once you have erred" — but a line: the six periods of day and night, thought after thought in unbroken continuity. At the end, the whole book gathers into that single, consistent posture — laying what was hidden out into the light — and hands this continuing line on to the gates that follow.

A word must come first: neither "the modern diagnosis" nor "daily use" is a ready-made rubric in the scriptures. This is a map this book has drawn up for today's reader. What it sets out to do is to tell you first what repentance is not, so that you can see clearly what repentance is.

We go down the mountain, and take the three pairs of spectacles off — one pair at a time.
