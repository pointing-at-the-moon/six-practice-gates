## 8.2 Returning to Daily Use (Final Chapter)

The three pairs of spectacles are off, and the last question stands before us: how, exactly, is repentance to land in the life of one day after another?

By this point in the volume, repentance has already been carried to a summit. The repentance of non-arising in Part Seven pressed repentance to the furthest reach of the direct contemplation of emptiness — contemplating that the nature of transgression is empty, knowing that transgression has no mark of its own, no substance to be found. That is the highest place repentance can reach in doctrine.

But the summit is not the terminus. A natural question surfaces: having climbed the empty summit, where does repentance go? Having already seen that the nature of transgression is empty, has repentance now done its work and retired, with nothing left to do?

This chapter's answer is: repentance does not halt on the empty summit — it returns to daily use.

### Repentance Is a Line, Not a Point

This answer responds head-on to the first misreading the last chapter diagnosed.

The modern reader most readily folds repentance into a vocabulary of emotion-processing: he takes it for a one-time release after wrongdoing, a self-soothing in a moment of crisis, finished once the episode has passed. This is to read repentance as an event — a point with a beginning and an end, zeroed out once handled.

But the shape repentance takes in the scriptures is not a point but a line: it is a rhythm of practice that runs through the six periods of day and night, thought after thought in unbroken continuity — a continuity to be practiced each day, practiced each half-month, practiced to the very end of time.

To gather repentance from a point back into a line is precisely the work of this chapter — and of the whole volume's gathering-in.

This gathering does not demote repentance into a piece of mechanical routine. A daily office treated as a chore to be clocked in and out of, and a rhythm of practice continuing to the end of time, may look alike on the outside but are worlds apart within: the former grows more burdensome the more it is done; the latter grows more unforced the longer it is walked. The divide lies not in frequency but in where this line is headed and on what strength it continues — which is just what the rest of this chapter will set out, piece by piece. To read repentance as a line rather than a point is therefore not to make it more cumbersome, but to set it back into the time that was always its own: a time nested layer upon layer — by the day, by the half-month, by the year — continuous with the path.

(One word first: "daily use" is not a ready-made rubric in the scriptures; it is this book's gathering of repentance's lived-out side. Its scriptural spine is threefold: the personal six-period repentance; the saṅgha's half-monthly *uposatha* and yearly invitation (*pavāraṇā*); and, running through them, "thought after thought in unbroken continuity, without weariness." We raise these three pillars first.)

### The First Pillar: The Six-Period Repentance

Repentance as a daily office has an ancient and explicit scriptural spine.

Nāgārjuna's *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* (the *Treatise on the Ten Stages*), discussing the bodhisattva's practice, first speaks of being mindful of the Buddhas, calling their names, and paying them homage, and then says plainly: this is not yet enough —

> Not merely being mindful, naming, and paying homage; one should further, before all the buddhas, repent, entreat, rejoice, and dedicate.

One should further, before the Buddhas, repent, entreat, rejoice, and dedicate. Then he sets these acts into an explicit structure of time:

> How are repentance, entreaty, rejoicing, and dedication to be performed? How many times within day and night? Answer: … day and night together make six periods, single-mindedly mindful of the buddhas, as if present before one.

Three periods by day, three by night, six periods in all — six times within one day, performed face to face with the Buddhas. This is one of the sources of what later ages call the "six-period repentance."

What deserves a pause is what this structure is itself saying. It does not ask "does one repent only upon a fault?" but "how many times within a day and a night?" — rewriting repentance from a conditional clause, "arising when something happens," into a timetable, "performed by the hour." Repentance here is not an occasional event waiting on a transgression but a timed practice woven into the daily office: even on a day with no great fault to disclose, the six-period office is still kept. For what it treats is not only the faults already committed, but the habit-energies and obstructions that continue day after day, quietly growing without waiting for any great event. To bind repentance to the hour, and not to the act of erring — this is the decisive step by which it turns from a point into a line.

The *Awakening of Faith* (attributed to Aśvaghoṣa) states the same structure more densely still:

> One should, vigorously and diligently, at the six periods day and night pay homage to the buddhas, sincerely repent, entreat, rejoice, and dedicate toward bodhi, never ceasing — thereby freed of obstructions and growing the roots of good.

Two things deserve notice here.

The first is the wording itself. The *Awakening of Faith* here reads "never ceasing" — unbroken, never let to lapse — not "resting." That one word's difference falls exactly on the lifeline of this chapter: "never ceasing" speaks of nothing other than continuity, of what makes this line a line and not a point.

The second is the structure itself. In both Nāgārjuna and the *Awakening of Faith*, repentance is never a solitary item; it stands in the one list — repent, entreat, rejoice, dedicate; and the phrase "dedicate toward bodhi," above all, has already bound repentance and vow into the breathing of a single daily office. This matters greatly — mark it now: within the six periods, repentance and dedication are the out-breath and in-breath of one and the same breath — where something is regretted, something is turned toward; having disclosed the past, one dedicates toward what is to come. The "hand-off to vow" at this chapter's end was in fact laid down long ago, here, in these very texts.

### The Second Pillar: The Institutional Rhythm

The six-period repentance is the daily office at the personal level; but the continuity of repentance does not stop with the individual. Pull the lens back to the saṅgha, and continuity is institutionalized into two larger cycles.

The first is the *uposatha*, held once each half-month. Every half-month the assembly gathers for the recitation of the precepts, and purifies itself before reciting — those with a fault disclose it and repent by procedure; those without abide silent and pure. The point of the *uposatha* lies not in any single recitation but in the cycle, "every half-month": it locks purity away from being a one-time resolve and into a point in time that returns again and again, so that every half-month becomes a fresh occasion to re-align with the precept-substance.

The second is the invitation (*pavāraṇā*), held once a year. The rains retreat over, the monks invite one another's correction: if you have seen, heard, or suspected any fault of mine, tell me in compassion — taking the year as its cycle, it hands the faults a person cannot easily see in himself over to the assembly's eyes to be illumined. The invitation lays one thing bare above all: repentance is not wholly one's own affair; some faults come to light only in another's gaze, and are settled cleanly only under the assembly's witness.

These two institutions, together with the personal six-period office, compose a nested picture of continuity: the six periods (a day), the *uposatha* (a half-month), the invitation (a year). The time-structure of repentance is therefore not a single thread but a rhythm of several cycles interlocked — the short cycles bearing up the long, the personal office nested within the saṅgha's half-month and year.

More important still: institutionalization means repentance was never a private emotional event of one person — it is public, practiced in common by the assembly, set to fixed hours and fixed cycles. A person alone may let himself off in private; a saṅgha with its *uposatha* and its invitation will not let purity stop at private self-consolation. It is exactly this public, periodic structure that keeps repentance from degenerating into emotional self-management behind a closed door.

### The Third Pillar: Continuity — What Keeps It from Wearying

Continuity, then, is both daily office and institution. But on what strength is continuity itself possible? A line meant to run to the end of time — on what strength does it stay unbroken, never lapse, and yet not sink into fatigue and despair?

The question is no contrivance. A practice that asks for continuity through the six periods of day and night, through half-months, through years, and on to the end of time — if all it rests on is the practitioner's resolve and grit, it is almost bound to collapse some day: the human will tires; and remorse, if driven again and again by guilt, will sooner or later push a person into the downward spiral of "the more I repent, the wearier; the wearier, the more I blame myself." A line meant to walk to the end of time cannot run on fuel like that.

When we spoke of Samantabhadra's vows of practice, we already touched the core of this question. Samantabhadra's vow is:

> Moment after moment it continues, without interruption; in the karmic acts of body, speech, and mind there is no weariness.

And this "no weariness" is possible because it rests on "the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice," not on the gritted-teeth straining of the practitioner's will.

This bears on the daily use of repentance above all: the strength of continuity lies not in the resolve "I must hold on," but in the power of the practice relied upon itself. When repentance is set within a vow-power and a contemplative practice like that, continuity is no longer dragged forward by the will; it is carried by a greater momentum. This is the hinge on which "never ceasing" can stand without collapsing into self-flagellation: one never ceases not because the teeth are clenched, but because this line is held, from the first, by a power that does not weary.

From here, the last chapter's negative diagnosis can be cashed out in the positive.

The first misreading the last chapter diagnosed was the therapeutic one — reading repentance as a long-term emotion-management. The continuity of the six-period repentance is precisely not emotion-management: it does not chew the same guilt over and over, easing it again and again; it is the exercise of practice, a daily returning and dedicating. We said earlier that wholesome remorse, once duly received, is concluded and does not recur; and the repentance free of the mark of transgression is, further still, a turn at the level of prajñā-cognition, not an easing of emotional tension.

Continuity, then, is a continuous seeing and a continuous turning toward the path, not a continuous anxiety. The same act repeated daily is rumination in emotion-management, but tempering in the exercise of practice — the difference lies not in the outward form but in what drives it and where it is headed. And it is for just this reason that the daily continuity of repentance does not push a person ever deeper into the trap of himself: emotion-management draws the attention back, again and again, to "how do I feel," while the six-period repentance dedicates every disclosure outward, toward the Buddhas and the path. The one is an inward circling; the other an outward, upward unfolding. A continuity that dedicates and a continuity that only ruminates, walked far enough, are two utterly different roads.

### The Seal: A Twofold Hand-Off, to Wisdom and to Vow

The three pillars are raised; repentance as the continuous rhythm of daily use has taken shape.

This continuing phenomenal repentance hands off, in the end, in two directions.

First, to wisdom.

Repentance's personal office and the saṅgha's institutions seem alike to fall on the phenomenal side; but the whole volume has shown that repentance has another pole — the repentance of non-arising. The empty summit of Part Seven carried repentance to the direct contemplation of emptiness: contemplating that the nature of transgression is empty. It is at this pole that the repentance gate meets *prajñā* — the line by which repentance opens onto the study of wisdom. The daily six-period phenomenal repentance looks like nothing but a repetition of homage, disclosure, dedication; yet its floor opens onto a road that runs upward: every disclosure made in accord with the Dharma loosens the reified grasping at "I" and at "transgression," and at the end of that loosening stands what the repentance of non-arising sees — that transgression has no own-being. The continuity of phenomenal repentance must, in the end, have its floor lit up within the emptiness of principial repentance.

But here the most critical line of the whole chapter must be set: emptiness is not annihilation.

To read "the nature of transgression is empty" as "since transgression is empty, then there is no karma, no cause and effect, and repentance too may be dispensed with" — this is the category error that must be walled shut. To contemplate that the nature of transgression is empty is to see through the reified grasping at "I" and at "transgression": there is no substantial "transgression" to be seized, and no substantial "I" that is its permanent owner; but this in no way says there is no karma, no recompense, no causal continuity. Karmic fruit is not empty. What was said before — "to receive is not to be absolved" — rises, at this pole, into a sterner sentence: emptiness is not absolution. Emptiness is a seeing, not a remission; it is the seeing-through of "transgression" and "I" as both without mark of their own, not a declaration that the chain of cause and effect does not exist. One who has seen that the nature of transgression is empty cannot therefore disregard cause and effect and act as he pleases — quite the reverse: precisely because he has seen emptiness, he bears the continuity of cause and effect all the more, and without contention.

Second, to vow.

The seam toward vow was in fact laid down already, back when we spoke of the six-period repentance. In its texts, repentance never stands apart from dedication: repent, entreat, rejoice, dedicate — dedicate toward bodhi, never ceasing. Repenting the karmic obstacle and making the vow of dedication are a pair practiced together, not two stages set one after the other: where something is regretted, something is turned toward; having disclosed the obstructions of the past, one dedicates this thought of purity toward the path ahead. The "fivefold repentance" structure of the Chinese repentance liturgies gathers this pair into one complete liturgy: it opens with repentance and seals with vow — repentance and vow being, from the first, the head and tail of one and the same line of practice. The "thought after thought in unbroken continuity, without weariness" spoken of above is at once the strength of continuity and one of vow's own stakes: that unwearying continuity must, in the end, continue toward a vow.

And so repentance, at the last, passes the baton to vow.

As for what comes after this step — how the unsurpassed awakening is consummated within dedication and the making of vows — that is the vow-gate's own province, the business of the very last volume of this series. Here the repentance gate is responsible for one thing only: passing the baton cleanly — carrying this continuing line all the way to the door of the vow-gate, and pointing out the direction of that door. To push it open is another volume's affair. In other words, repentance has by now done its part: it has carried a person from a single disclosure, through the continuity of daily use, through the seeing of emptiness, and set him at last before the vow-gate — from here on, it is for vow to speak.

### Gathering In the Whole Volume: Summiting Does Not Dismantle the Stairs

Last, one easily mistaken point must be gathered back in: now that the repentance of non-arising has climbed the empty summit, has it done away with all this daily phenomenal repentance below?

No. Summiting does not dismantle the stairs.

The repentance of non-arising, seeing that the nature of transgression is empty, does not cancel the six-period repentance, does not cancel the *uposatha* and the invitation, does not cancel the daily use of thought after thought in unbroken continuity. Principial repentance does not replace phenomenal repentance — this is the red thread running through the whole volume: the two faces stand distinct, and the seeing of principle does not dismantle the doing of phenomena. Above the summit, the practitioner still returns, day after day, into the phenomenal repentance of the six periods of day and night; and it is because he has seen emptiness that the continuity of daily use no longer sinks into self-flagellation, but becomes instead an unforced line, running to the end of time. The stairs are not torn down because the summit was reached; they remain, there to be walked daily — and it is precisely the one who has summited who walks them most steadily, with the least grasping.

And so the volume can be gathered in.

Look back over the road this volume has walked.

It rose from the foundation of phenomenal repentance — the parsing of the three terms; concealment and rehabilitation; probation, *mānatta*, the *uposatha*, the invitation; that whole discipline of disclosure and procedure in the Vinaya by which purity is restored. Then to remorse in the Āgamas — that double-edged blade, medicine or poison, and the floor it sounded beneath the heaviest karma. Then to the seven limbs and Samantabhadra — repentance lifted into worship, raised into an inexhaustible vow before the Buddhas of the ten directions, unbroken thought after thought. Then to the death-and-rebirth bridge — giving puts the self to death; repentance clears the karma after that death; the precepts greet the new birth. Then to the ladder of the three repentances — climbing rung by rung from procedural repentance and sign-based repentance up to the repentance of non-arising. Then to the Chinese repentance liturgies — the Lotus Samādhi, the Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance — setting repentance down into a whole course of practice turned inward and outward. Then to the empty summit of the repentance of non-arising — contemplating that the nature of transgression is empty, pressing repentance to the furthest reach of prajñā. And then to this final part — first taking off the three modern pairs of spectacles, then gathering repentance back into the continuous rhythm of daily use.

This road was never meant for dwelling long on any one rung; it was meant to see repentance whole, one rung at a time.

Repentance is at once a single disclosure within the Vinaya, a single seeing within emptiness, and a line running to the end of time, thought after thought in unbroken continuity. The eight parts each light up one face of it, and only together do they make its whole body — leave out any one face and repentance is read awry: look only at the Vinaya's disclosure, and it becomes a piece of saṅgha business; look only at emptiness's seeing, and it starts to sound like a metaphysics that can wipe out cause and effect at a stroke. Only when the foundation of phenomenal repentance, the empty summit of principial repentance, and the continuity of daily use are gathered in together does the form of repentance stand up whole.

And when all is said, this whole volume has spoken of nothing but one plainest of postures — from that first timid disclosure in the Vinaya, to the moment of seeing that transgression is from the first non-arising — what was being walked was, all along, one and the same thing:

Laying what was hidden out into the light.

First a fault is laid open — laid open to the saṅgha, laid open to the Buddhas; at the last, what is laid open is the "I" that had been hiding the fault all along, and had all along been bound by it — and only once it is laid open does one find that there, from the first, nothing arose; from the first, nothing was bound.

The sun of wisdom shines once, and the frost and dew are gone of themselves. It is not that someone absolved your transgression. It is that you have, at last, seen: the self that erred, and the transgression it committed, were from the very first non-arising.

Repentance does not halt on the empty summit — it walks back, along the rhythm of the six periods of day and night, into daily use; and within daily use it hands off to wisdom, and to vow. That single disclosure in the Vinaya has by now become a line running to the end of time, thought after thought in unbroken continuity, without weariness.

The repentance gate closes here.

And the vow-gate awaits opening.
