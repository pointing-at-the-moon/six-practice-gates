## 5.1 Step Back and See the Whole Ladder

The word "repentance," in everyday feeling, has been pressed almost to a point — own up to a fault, say sorry, feel bad for a moment, and the thing counts as past. As though repentance came in one kind only, the same in depth throughout, the only difference being how sincere you are.

But truly open the scriptures and treatises, and you find the ancients drew distinctions about "repentance" far finer than this.

All three extinguish offense: to settle a fault through a set procedure of the Saṅgha, and to see with your own eyes, within stilled concentration, the signs of the offense's extinction, and to probe straight to the offense's root-source so that it can never stand again — these three things are simply not on the same plane. They are three gradients of one and the same road.

Zhiyi states these three gradients most clearly.

### Three Rungs, Each Buttressing the Next

In the *Stages of the Perfection of Dhyāna*, taking repentance "at its essentials," Zhiyi sorts it into three: procedural repentance, sign-contemplating repentance, and the repentance that contemplates non-arising.

One thing must be held first: these are not three options ranged side by side, like dishes to be ordered at will from a menu; they are three rungs of a stair, each buttressing the one above on the way up. In the very sentence that lists the three, Zhiyi himself fits each rung with the one word "buttress" — procedural repentance "buttresses morality," sign-contemplating repentance "buttresses concentration," non-arising repentance "buttresses wisdom."

Morality, concentration, and wisdom are the three oldest axes of Buddhist cultivation. Zhiyi maps the three repentances one to one onto these three axes, and this one step is the main beam of the whole ladder. It tells us: the three grades of repentance are not sorted by some idle hand; they are ranged along the ascending line of morality-concentration-wisdom that was there already. The lowest rung buttresses the most phenomenal, most concrete layer, the moral discipline; the middle rung buttresses the concentration that gathers body and mind; the highest buttresses the wisdom that beholds the real mark. From morality to concentration to wisdom is itself the sequence from coarse into fine, from the phenomenal into the principial — and the three grades of repentance lie stacked upon exactly that sequence.

So the word "ladder" — that axis was set up by Zhiyi himself; it is not a frame later hands forced on.

### The First Rung: Procedural Repentance, Closest to the Ground

Look first at the lowest rung, the one nearest the ground — procedural repentance.

The definition Zhiyi gives it is plain: by performing a "good deed" (a lawful procedure), one reverses and offsets the prior "evil deed" (the fault committed) — and this is named repentance. The weight falls on the word "procedure" — the loan-word *karman* he renders into Chinese on the spot: "*karman* is here translated 'procedure.'" That is, a formal procedure laid down by the Saṅgha according to the discipline: a sufficient number of monks, following the set steps, settling a precept-breaker's fault according to the rule. This is the whole apparatus of the Vinaya's disclosure, probation, and rehabilitation spoken of earlier; here we borrow only its outline and do not walk the detail again.

What matters is to see its texture clearly. Zhiyi frames it clean with two negations: this grade "neither treats of seeing various signs and appearances, nor treats of contemplating emptiness through wisdom."

The first negation: procedural repentance does not ask you to see in the mind any auspicious omen of the offense's extinction, any light — that is the business of the rung above. The second: still less does procedural repentance ask you to behold that the offense was empty from the first — that is the business of the highest rung. Procedural repentance asks one thing only: was the procedure lawfully completed? Lawfully completed, the offense is named extinguished.

And just because it sits closest to the phenomenal, it is also the steadiest, the least liable to error. There is no subjective reckoning of "did I really see a sign?", no still harder self-examination of "have I truly understood emptiness?" The standard is external, held in common sight. This is how the first rung of a ladder ought to look: feet on the ground, the most concrete layer of offense settled first. What it buttresses is the axis of "morality" — it is the tool the moral discipline carries with it for its own repair: where there is a breach, mend it according to the rule.

### The Second Rung: Sign-Contemplating Repentance, Drawn One Layer Inward

One rung up from procedural repentance is sign-contemplating repentance. The gradient plainly turns here.

Zhiyi's definition: the practitioner, following the methods set out in the scriptures, "with focused mind and applied intent, within a stilled mind sees various signs." The keyword leaps out — "stilled mind." The prior rung was completed in the external setting of the Saṅgha; this one turns into the individual's concentrated mind. What it asks is no longer a public procedure, but that the practitioner gather body and mind, enter a settled state of concentration, and then, within that concentrated mind, see the signs of the offense's extinction.

What kind of signs? Zhiyi takes the bodhisattva precepts for his example: to repent a grave offense such as breaking one of the ten grave precepts, one must see a Buddha come to stroke the crown, see light and flowers and other such auspicious signs, before the offense counts as extinguished. And he adds a heavy line: "without a sign seen, repentance, though made, brings no avail."

This line is easily read awry, as though it said "repentance is useless." It does not. What it says is: for a grave offense, "no sign seen" means the verification of the offense's extinction has not yet appeared — not that the act of repentance is itself void.

But Zhiyi at once applies a most important brake. He says: māra too can conjure these signs. Light and auspicious flowers, Māra can make just as well — "the false and the true are hard to tell apart and cannot be fixed upon." So when a sign is seen, there must be a good teacher to discern it face to face; one must keep close to a good and wise friend able to tell the false from the true, and have it checked. The practitioner cannot certify it himself on sight.

This brake is the deepest layer of sign-contemplating repentance's wisdom. The sign, here, is not the goal; not an attainment of which more is better and brighter is higher. The sign is only a verification — a signal of whether the offense has been extinguished — and that signal may itself be misread, may be counterfeited, and must be handed to an experienced teacher to check. Once seeing a sign is taken for a peak to be grasped at, paraded, self-certified, the direction has reversed. Zhiyi says it bluntly: to grasp at signs invites the work of māra.

What this rung "buttresses" is "concentration" — it must be done within the concentrated mind and depends on the abiding of dhyāna. Against the first rung, which halts at an external procedure, it has already drawn one layer inward, into the practitioner's own state of concentration. The ladder, here, has risen one rung.

(A word on names is owed: Zhiyi's own text uses "sign-contemplating repentance" throughout; the "sign-based repentance" we have used before is the later common name — same referent, different name, one and the same rung. When the next chapter crouches down to look at this rung closely, it will follow the later common usage and say "sign-based repentance.")

### On What Ground Is It a "Ladder"

There is one thing to see clearly here: to call these three grades a "ladder," each rung higher than the last, is not an order laid out by a careless hand, still less a high-and-low ranked by someone's preference. In his own text, Zhiyi uses at least three distinct sets of language, each independently ranging these three repentances from shallow to deep. The three sets stacked together — that is the hard ground for the word "ladder." Not a later arrangement: the layering the text itself gives, again and again.

The first set is the one already told — "buttresses morality, buttresses concentration, buttresses wisdom." Morality-concentration-wisdom is itself a sequence from coarse into fine, and the three repentances, stacked upon it, sort themselves high and low.

The second set is "the offense broken, from shallow to deep." Zhiyi says offense has three grades: first, the offense that arises from violating the precept-code and obstructs the path; second, "the offense of intrinsic nature" (killing, stealing — acts evil in themselves); third, "the root offense of ignorance-and-affliction." Then the three repentances map one to one: procedural repentance breaks the first grade, sign-contemplating repentance breaks the second, non-arising repentance breaks the third — and of this highest grade he says it "ultimately removes the very root-source of offense." Watch where this line runs — from the twigs, gathering all the way in to the root: the first rung handles the offense of violating the precept-code; the second digs down to the intrinsic nature of the offense; the third probes all the way to the offense's root cause (ignorance) and removes even the source. What is broken goes deeper and deeper — and that is shallow and deep.

A hidden thread joins here in passing. Treating sign-contemplating repentance's breaking of the offense of intrinsic nature, Zhiyi cites a line: a monk has broken the precept against killing, and though he repents, "the precept-substance is made pure and the path-obstructing offense extinguished, yet the karmic retribution for the killing is not extinguished." Repentance can make the precept-substance pure again, can extinguish the layer of offense that obstructs the path; but the retribution owed for the one killed is not thereby wiped clean in a stroke. This echoes exactly the salt-simile of the earlier part on karmic fruit — repentance can lighten, can transmute, but does not equal erasing, in one stroke, a fruit already made. The ladder of the three repentances and the earlier teaching on karmic fruit are one and the same doctrine; they do not fight.

The third set is the most vivid figure of all — three medicines. Zhiyi says: procedural repentance is like a person who fears the cold taking ginger and cinnamon — the cold malady is cured, and one returns to one's original health; this is "restoration" (returning to the original). Sign-contemplating repentance is like taking a stronger medicine — not only is the illness cured, but the body grows stronger than before the illness, and it can even give rise to concentration; this is "surpassing the original" (going beyond the original). Non-arising repentance is like taking an elixir of immortality — not only is the illness cured, one goes so far as to accomplish the way; this is "further surpassing the original" (surpassing yet more). Three medicines, each stronger in potency than the last; restore, surpass, surpass yet more — these three words are themselves a clear ascending line.

Three sets of tier-language, each independent, all pointing one way. That is the entire ground for the word "ladder."

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

And yet this figure of the "ladder" has one place where it is most easily read backwards, and it must be pinned down on the spot.

To climb a higher rung is not to tear down the rungs below.

There is a reading both handy and wrong: since non-arising repentance is highest and ultimately removes the root-source of offense, are not procedural and sign-contemplating repentance then the lower, the outdated, the superseded? Once one has cultivated to the height, may the first two rungs simply be dropped?

No. The reason lies in that one word "buttress": to buttress is to aid, to reinforce — not to replace. Procedural repentance has its own portion of duty in extinguishing its own grade of offense; the Vinaya "uses this method throughout to extinguish offense" — and that portion does not vanish because you have learned the contemplation of non-arising. In the second set, "surpassing the original" and "further surpassing the original" speak of going beyond, and further beyond, an existing base — they presuppose that the prior rung still stands; they are stacked upon it, not a levelling of the old to build anew. The medicine simile is the same: the elixir is "further" beyond the original precisely because it stands on top of the first two medicines and adds above them, not because it declares them void.

So the right image of this ladder is: layer containing layer — climbing the rungs, not dismantling them. The higher rung contains the lower, extinguishing one more layer of offense at a greater depth, rather than negating or abolishing the lower. Hold this one line, and nothing said later about "the summit" can go astray.

### Walking On

The shape of the whole ladder now stands: three rungs, from the phenomenal into the principial, each buttressing the next, layer containing layer.

In the next chapter we no longer stand back to survey; we crouch down and look hard at the middle rung — sign-based repentance. It is the easiest to pass over in a phrase, yet it is the very rung at which repentance first needs "concentration," the rung where it is drawn in from outside the Saṅgha into the individual's stilled mind. It is the vertebra where phenomenal repentance grows into principial repentance.
