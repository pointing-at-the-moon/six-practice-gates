## Transition ② · From Remorse in the Āgamas to the Sevenfold Worship

Part Two has walked down to the floor of phenomenal repentance, and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath.

With Part Two done, the hand holds something Part One did not give. Part One gave the institution — speaking it out, being received, passing the bar before the assembly, that whole set of rules. Part Two gave the emotion behind the institution, the surge that drives a person, and its two roads out: with an exit, remorse is a gate; without one, a cell. And pressed to the very end, Part Two yielded a finding honest almost to coldness — even before the Buddha, even the most complete repentance of all can only lessen and redirect; it cannot erase. The salt is still in the water.

The two parts together have laid out, in the main, the whole face of phenomenal repentance. Its act (disclosure), its institutions (probation, the invitation, the *uposatha*), its psychological engine (remorse), its limit (it can lessen and redirect; it cannot erase).

Here, one thing is worth stopping to look at.

In the first two parts, the scene of remorse was, from beginning to end, a plain one. A monk speaks his offense before the Saṅgha; a king discloses his remorse before the Buddha; an elder follows the Buddha down a stretch of road only to own a fault to His face. The one addressed is a person — a fellow practitioner, the Saṅgha, the Buddha abiding in the world. Disclosure is one person handing what was hidden over to others.

But after the Buddha's nirvāṇa — what then?

This the language of the first two parts never touched. While the Buddha abode in the world, you could go in person before His seat, as Ajātaśatru did, and ask face to face that your repentance be received. With the Buddha gone, to whom is this act of handing over one's remorse to be handed? The one addressed is no more — does disclosure itself, then, fall through?

What happened was the very opposite.

After the Buddha's passing, the Mahāyāna tradition, far from letting disclosure fall through, raised its station. The act of handing over one's remorse — an act originally made toward persons — it enlarged into a solemn rite made toward all the Buddhas of the ten directions. You no longer disclose to one Buddha abiding in the world, to one concrete Saṅgha; in contemplation, facing the measureless Buddhas who fill all of space and pervade the dharma-realm, you disclose the faults of beginningless time, all at once.

And this disclosure, raised in station, no longer appears alone. It is woven into a fixed sequence of practice — first homage to the Buddhas, then praise of the Tathāgatas, then the vast cultivation of offering; then, and only then, the repentance of karmic obstacles; then rejoicing, requesting the Dharma, requesting the Buddhas to remain in the world, and dedication. Within this sequence, disclosure has a seat of its own, called the confession limb. Seven links in a row, each interlocked with the next — later ages called it "the seven limbs."

And so the weight of remorse changed.

In the Āgamas, remorse is an emotion in one person's heart that needs an exit; disclosure is the act of handing that emotion over. By the sevenfold worship, disclosure has been set inside a great offering made before the Buddhas — no longer only "I speak my fault aloud," but "before the Buddhas of the ten directions, in the most reverent of postures, I disclose the karmic obstacles of beginningless time, all at once, and ask, all at once, that they be repented." The one addressed widens from concrete persons to measureless Buddhas; the scale widens from a single fault to everything since beginningless time; the posture rises from asking to be received into an offering, and a vow.

Handing the remorse over — this old act, walked all the way from the Vinaya, here puts on the solemn splendor of the Avataṃsaka.

What Part Three looks at is this raising of station: the confession limb within the sevenfold worship, and the "repentance of karmic obstacles" among the Ten Great Vows of the bodhisattva Samantabhadra. The same disclosure, set before the Buddhas — what new weight, exactly, is it given?

We turn this page, and begin with that sequence of seven linked limbs.
