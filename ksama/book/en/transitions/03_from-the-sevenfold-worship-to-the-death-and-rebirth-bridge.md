## Transition ③ · From the Sevenfold Worship to the Death-and-Rebirth Bridge

Part Three has walked up to the highest point of phenomenal repentance, and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath.

By now the full breadth of phenomenal repentance has been laid out. Part One looked at its act and its institutions — disclosure, probation, the invitation, the *uposatha*. Part Two looked at its psychological engine and its limit — the double-edged remorse, and that floor which reads "it can lessen and redirect; it cannot erase." Part Three has watched it carried up into worship, onto the summit — one limb among the seven, one vow among Samantabhadra's.

From one fault a monk stammers out before the Saṅgha, to the inexhaustible great vow before the Buddhas of the ten directions, continuing moment after moment without break — the same "speaking out what was hidden" has walked its whole course on the plane of the phenomenal, from the plainest to the most solemn.

The floor has been sounded; the summit has been climbed. The building of phenomenal repentance has been seen whole, foundation to spire.

Then what comes next?

By rights, the next step should be to push straight through the deepest door — to ask the question that has been held down all along: that transgression, that "I" who erred — were they ever real?

But this book is in no hurry to push that door. It stops first at a particular threshold.

The threshold bears a particular name: death-and-rebirth.

What makes it particular? This: it does not belong to the gate of repentance alone — it is a seam between the six gates.

To speak this seam clearly, repentance must first be set back in its place among the six gates. The repentance this book treats is the third of the six. The gate before it is giving — the work of loosening, inch by inch, the hand clenched around "mine." The gate after it is the precepts — to receive the precepts is to take on a new precept-substance, to begin life anew.

Join the three gates in one view, and a moving structure appears.

Giving loosens "mine" — and loosened to the very end, the "I" itself comes loose: something like a death of the self. Then repentance follows on that loss of self: upon the loosened self, the self that no longer grips for dear life, it cleans the old karma away. Then the precepts follow on repentance: only when the cleaning is done can the new precept-substance be received — something like a rebirth.

Death, and then birth. Giving brings the death of the self; repentance clears the old karma after that death; the precepts welcome the new birth. Repentance sits wedged exactly between this death and this birth.

So the threshold has two faces. One face looks backward, joining giving — joining the loosened self on its way to death. One face looks forward, joining the precepts — joining the self about to be reborn, about to receive the new precepts. Repentance stands between, receiving what the gate before it lost, opening what the gate after it will establish.

What Part Four walks is this seam. It watches how repentance receives the loosened self that giving hands across, and how it passes that self, purified, on to the precepts to be set standing anew. In this part, for the first time, repentance is not only about its own business — it shows its place on the whole road of practice, carrying on what came before and opening what comes after.

We turn this page, and look first at the face that looks backward — how repentance catches the dying self that giving hands across.
