## Transition ⑦ · From the Repentance of Non-Arising to the Modern Age, and Daily Use

Part Seven has walked to the doctrinal summit of the whole volume, and stopped. The page is about to turn. This middle page is a breath — a breath let out from the top of the mountain.

We stand at the highest point and look back. From the first sentence a monk stammers out in the Vinaya — "I, so-and-so, have committed such-and-such an offense" — all the way to the *Heart Sūtra*'s "no knowledge, and no attainment," where knower and known are spent together: the long road from the phenomenal into the principial is, at last, walked through. Repentance has turned from an act spoken aloud into a seeing: seeing that the self that erred, and the transgression it committed, were from the very first non-arising.

This is the summit. But the summit is not the end. One who has climbed to the mountaintop cannot live on the mountaintop. He must come down, back into the days at the foot of the mountain — back to firewood and rice, oil and salt; back to the knocks between person and person; back to that surge of remorse that still rises to mind in the dead of night. However high the doctrine is raised, however clear through, it must be set back into daily use before it counts.

And at the foot of the mountain, what meets us head-on are several misreadings of repentance peculiar to this age of ours. They are not the ancients' problems; they are this generation's — and for just that reason, they are what this book, at its end, cannot leave unanswered.

The first misreading conscripts repentance as an instrument of psychotherapy. In the vocabulary of wellness, repentance is rewritten as "emotion-management" — managing guilt, lowering shame, soothing oneself. One performs a repentance in order to "feel a little better." But this book has held one line since Part One: repentance is not emotion-management; repentance is the clearing of obstruction. "Feeling better" is a by-product of the clearing, not the target it aims at. Make emotional relief the purpose, and a subtler self-grasping is set right back at the center — it becomes "I," by means of repentance, managing the good mood of "me." The sun of wisdom's seeing that transgression is at root non-arising is a turn of cognition, not an easing of feeling; degrade it into a tonic for one's own comfort, and one misses precisely the self it was meant to shine through.

The second misreading degrades disclosure into a confessional guilt-management — repentance staged as a performance, before an imagined audience, of "how remorseful I am": the more self-reproach, the more devout it looks, and in the repeated indicting one sinks deeper and deeper. But this book has told of Vimalakīrti's rebuke: what truly disturbs you is rarely the deed itself, but your act of fixing it as real and indicting yourself against it again and again. "Extinguish it directly; do not disturb the mind." — finish it, but do not feed it. Repeated self-flagellation is not repentance; it is taking the whip into your own hand and laying it on yourself. That is not laying what was hidden out into the light; it is enshrining a reified mark of transgression in the heart and burning incense to it day after day.

The third misreading is the most dangerous, and it is committed with this book's own highest part — misusing "non-arising," "transgression has no own-being," as a pretext for fleeing responsibility: "the transgression is empty anyway; why take it seriously?"

But Part Seven spent the strength of an entire part stopping just this mouth: what emptiness negates is the own-being of transgression, not the fruition of karma. Transgression has no own-being; karmic fruit is not empty. "Not apprehending transgression" and "not apprehending non-transgression" are a double negation — neither grasping transgression as real, nor falling into the annihilationist view of "no transgression." One who pleads "transgression is empty at root" to let himself off proves, precisely, that he has understood neither emptiness nor repentance. Vimalakīrti says it plainly: "one who knows this is called a keeper of the Vinaya" — one who truly knows that the nature of transgression is empty keeps the precepts, if anything, the more steadily.

All three misreadings share one root: each would skirt the long road from the phenomenal into the principial and cut itself a shortcut — repentance to manage one's moods, repentance to perform one's devotion, emptiness to dodge one's load. And the seven parts of this book have been saying exactly this: there is no shortcut. To see that the nature of transgression is empty, one must first have repented, solidly, in the phenomenal; to say "transgression cannot be apprehended," one must first have the standing to say it. The view from the summit belongs only to the one who climbed it rung by rung; whoever would ride a cable car straight up never reaches it — only an illusion shaped like it.

There is one more layer to think through on the way down. The first seven parts were set, for the most part, in the saṅgha, in the place of practice, in the scene of sūtra and treatise — where there are precept-masters, fellow practitioners, an altar, the rite of the six periods of day and night. But most who read this book today are not in such a setting: a layperson, with a household and a job, caught up in the give-and-take of human affairs, with no formal act to lean on and perhaps no place of practice to go to. What, then, of this whole discipline from disclosure to non-arising remains, and how can it still be used, when it comes down into one person's ordinary days? This is not to dilute a profound teaching into comfort-broth, but the reverse: if the teaching is true, it must hold up under the plainest daily use — usable still by an ordinary person at bedtime, in the moment guilt rises.

Part Eight closes the volume. Its work is to set this whole body of teaching — from the Pāli Vinaya to the repentance of non-arising — back into the daily use of today: to answer these modern misreadings, and to answer the plainest question of all: a practitioner of today, a layperson, no specialist — how, in the end, is such a person to repent?

Two chapters. And at the last, the whole book gathers into the one posture it has kept throughout: laying what was hidden out into the light. From that first timid disclosure in the Vinaya, to the moment of seeing that transgression never arose — what was walked was, all along, one and the same thing.

We come down the mountain, and return to daily use.
