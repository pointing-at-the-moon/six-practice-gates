# The Death-and-Rebirth Bridge · The Precept-Side Seam

**Then receive the bodhisattva precepts — the exit-seam between repentance and the precept**

*中文主題：死而後生橋·戒面接縫——然後受菩薩戒*

**Author:** Shi Huijing

## Abstract

The second joint of the death-and-rebirth bridge falls between repentance and the precept. The preceding paper crossed the entry-seam — giving loosens "what-is-mine" and so brings about the loss of self, and repentance, standing on that loss, discloses lawfully — and halted at the bridge's midpoint. This paper picks up at that midpoint and turns the lens forward: once repentance has cleared the karma, what is the "then"? The grammatical hinge is a single line of the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Samantabhadra* — "repent all offences, declare one's own faults. **Then**…" — whose "then" welds the repentance side (cleared karma, the offences worn away) to the precept side (a new precept-substance received, the fivefold dharma-body). With that "then" as its spine, the paper argues along three layers. First, that repentance precedes the precept is a grammatical necessity of reception, not a contingency of ritual sequence: the Vinaya's "obstruction → opening" and the lay-side formula "this is called pure precept-keeping" both attest the order. Second, seeing an auspicious sign is the verification of cleared karma and so the switch by which the precept-substance may be received — the *Brahmā's Net Sūtra*'s "without an auspicious sign, repentance, though made, brings no avail" raises to view the invisible threshold between "repentance done" and "fit to receive," while the *Sūtra on Divining* supplies an internal fraud-check against a merely hallucinated sign. Third, the new precept-substance received is the "reborn body" of death-and-rebirth — the fivefold dharma-body is the sūtra's own naming of that reborn body. Three honest declarations are owed: what is "born" is not a substantial self that takes rebirth, but the fivefold dharma-body posited and held upon no-self; receiving the new is not absolution; and the precept-substance is no annihilated-then-reborn soul. The threefold doctrinal analysis of the precept-substance's own ontology is reserved for the *Śīla* volume; the principial-repentance face that sees the intrinsic emptiness of offence is reserved for this volume's end. This paper walks to the bridge's exit, joining the entering and exiting halves into one bridge.

**Keywords:** death-and-rebirth, precept-side seam, then receive the bodhisattva precepts, precept-substance, fivefold dharma-body, auspicious sign, sign-based repentance, liminality, second nature, no-self (*anātman*), *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Samantabhadra*, *Brahmā's Net Sūtra*

## I. Exiting the Bridge: After Repentance Clears Karma, Rebirth

The preceding paper, *The Giving-Side Seam*, treated the entry-bridge. It let the mark-of-self drop away through giving, and disclose lawfully through repentance — the concealing self dies in disclosure, and pure repentance can then open out. That paper ran to the bridge's midpoint and halted there, saying plainly that the bridge had crossed only the entering half; the face of the received-and-abiding precept-substance, and how the "then" of "then receive the bodhisattva precepts" accomplishes rebirth, was the bridge's exit, to be treated here. This paper is that exit, taken up from the midpoint.

The entry-bridge asked after repentance's *antecedent* — it pulled the lens back, asking where repentance arises from. The exit-bridge must push the lens forward and ask after repentance's *sequel*: the karma having been lawfully disclosed and so progressively cleared, what is the next step? Receiving the new and abiding — what does it need a completed repentance to have given it?

The answer is hidden in a line of the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Samantabhadra*. The sūtra says that one who wishes to fulfil the bodhisattva precepts should join the palms, everywhere pay homage to the Buddhas of the ten directions, and

> 懺悔諸罪，自說己過。然後靜處白十方佛而作是言……[^1]

*repent all offences, and declare one's own faults. Then, in a quiet place, address the Buddhas of the ten directions and say…*

— calling Śākyamuni to be one's upādhyāya and Mañjuśrī one's ācārya, and so on up to receiving the bodhisattva precepts.[^1] Before the word "then" is pure repentance — disclosure, the declaring of one's own faults; after it is the rite of reception — addressing the Buddhas, requesting the teachers, taking up the precept-substance. A single "then" joins the two banks of the bridge in one place.

The second joint of the death-and-rebirth bridge sits exactly on this "then." The death of the entry-bridge is the concealing self dying in disclosure; the birth of the exit-bridge is the new precept-substance arising after the karma is cleared. "Then" is no idle word; it is the hinge of this seam: first repentance sets down the old-karma side, and only *then* does reception raise up the new-substance side. Here the bridge is no longer at its midpoint, but at its exit.

## II. "Then": The Grammar by Which Repentance Precedes the Precept

Why can the "then" not be reversed? Why is it repentance first and reception after, and not the other way round? This is no contingent arrangement of ritual order; it is a grammar of the doctrine.

Look first at the spine-line. "Repent all offences, declare one's own faults" is a completed act of repentance; "then, in a quiet place, address the Buddhas of the ten directions" is the starting point of another act. The sūtra's own syntax sets the two on either side of "then": repentance must be completed before the frame of reception opens. What reception takes up is a body already cleared by repentance — and if the karma is not cleared, this body does not yet so much as qualify to receive.

The Vinaya states this grammar more bluntly still. The *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya*, treating false speech as a path-obstructing factor, enumerates what it obstructs: it obstructs the first dhyāna through the fourth, and the fruit of stream-entry through arhatship; whereas "with repentance comes ease" — only after repentance do these obstructed things open one by one.[^2] The same Vinaya says that if, in any matter, one does not disclose and repent, then "the offence is not removed."[^3] This is a clear "obstruction → opening" structure: while the offence remains, the path is blocked; when repentance is done, the path opens. The very path that reception means to take up must first be cleared by repentance. The Vinaya further says that after a monk has cleared an offence lawfully, "I declare this monk pure and without transgression, the offence cleared"[^4] — clearing the offence lawfully comes first, purity-without-transgression after; this too is a "then." The concealing self dies in disclosure; the body purified and fit to receive is born of the lawful clearing of the offence.

On the lay side, the *Upāsaka-śīla Sūtra* gives the same grammar through another incision. It says: "wholeheartedly repent; the repentance once done, the mind gives rise to gladness, and guarding it carefully one dares not offend again — this is called pure precept-keeping."[^5] Pure precept-keeping is named pure not by some name conjured up in the instant of reception, but by the careful guarding-and-keeping that comes *after* "the repentance once done." The sūtra also poses a question framed as a ladder: "if I cannot first receive the lesser restraint, how could I receive the greater?"[^6] — purified to this rung, one is then fit to receive the next. Reception is not a leap had in a single bound, but purity layered rung by rung, the precept-substance made fit to receive rung by rung.

So the grammar of "then" stands up: not receive the precept first and then go to repent, but first repent and clear the karma, be purified and so fit to receive, and then receive. This is the first stone of the exit-bridge — that repentance-clears-the-karma is the antecedent of reception, a doctrinal necessity rather than a dispensable prelude.

## III. Seeing an Auspicious Sign: The Verification of Cleared Karma, the Switch by Which the Precept-Substance May Be Received

But here there is an invisible threshold. To what degree must repentance be carried before it counts as "the karma cleared"? In that instant of "then," on what ground is this body judged fit to receive? The traditional answer is: seeing an auspicious sign.

The *Brahmā's Net Sūtra* states it most severely. One who has transgressed the ten grave precepts should be taught to repent: before images of the Buddhas and bodhisattvas, through the six periods of day and night, to recite the ten grave and forty-eight light precepts and, with bitter earnestness, pay homage to the thousand Buddhas of the three times, "until an auspicious sign is seen." What is an auspicious sign? The sūtra glosses it itself:

> 佛來摩頂，見光見華，種種異相，便得滅罪。若無好相，雖懺無益，是人現身亦不得戒。[^7]

*A Buddha comes and strokes the crown of one's head; one sees light, sees flowers, various extraordinary signs — and the offence is thereby extinguished. Without an auspicious sign, repentance, though made, brings no avail; such a one, in this very body, does not obtain the precept.*

For a transgression of the forty-eight light precepts, by contrast, "the offence is extinguished by repentance before another"; no auspicious sign is required.[^7] Here is a distinction of grave and light: repentance for a grave precept must see an auspicious sign before the offence counts as extinguished; repentance for a light one is settled by disclosure before another. The auspicious sign is exactly the threshold lying across "repentance done" and "fit to receive" — it is the verification of cleared karma, and so the switch by which the precept-substance may be received.

One line must first be corrected, for it is easily misread. "Without an auspicious sign, repentance, though made, brings no avail" does not say that repentance is useless in itself. What it says is: where no auspicious sign has been seen, this particular round of repentance has not yet completed its work of extinguishing the grave offence; the auspicious sign is the verification of extinction, and where the verification is unseen, the grave offence is not yet extinguished, and the door of the precept-substance has not yet opened. To read the line as "repentance is useless" is to invert the whole sentence.

The *Brahmā's Net Sūtra* opens a further provision, bearing especially on the final age. After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, when one of will and good heart wishes to receive the bodhisattva precepts, one may, before images of the Buddhas and bodhisattvas, receive the precepts by self-vow; one should repent before the Buddhas for seven days, and "obtaining the sight of an auspicious sign, one obtains the precept"; if not, one should continue for two weeks, three weeks, up to a year, until an auspicious sign is obtained and the precept then received. Where within a thousand *li* there is no master able to confer the precepts, one may likewise receive by self-vow before images of the Buddhas and bodhisattvas — but an auspicious sign must be seen.[^8] This provision for reception by self-vow pushes the weight of the auspicious sign to its extreme: in a setting where no master is to be had, the sign nearly carries by itself the verdict of "the karma cleared, the precept receivable."

Might the sign, then, be no more than a private flare of one's own hallucination? The *Sūtra on Divining the Requital of Good and Evil Actions* has already set a fraud-check. It relates how a practitioner cultivates repentance until good signs appear — even seeing, in a dream, a Buddha's bodily form come to bear witness, stroking the crown of the head and declaring, "Excellent! You are now pure; I have come to verify you." But it follows at once with a sharp turn:

> 若人未得三業善相，但先見聞如此諸事者，則為虛妄誑惑詐偽，非善相也。[^9]

*If a person has not yet attained the good signs of the three modes of action, yet first sees and hears such things, this is false, deluding, and counterfeit — it is not a true good sign.*

A true sign must rest on good signs of the three modes of action — body, speech, and mind; one who merely sees light and flowers while the three modes remain unpurified is judged a counterfeit. The tradition itself set this fraud-check in place; no later patch was needed. The sūtra further says that one who wishes to receive the pure and excellent precepts but cannot, having first committed a grave offence, "should likewise cultivate the method of repentance as above, bringing forth a wholehearted mind; having obtained the good signs of body, speech, and mind, one should then be permitted to receive"[^10] — the good sign once obtained, one should then be permitted to receive. This is precisely the "cleared karma → fit to receive" switch written into express words.

The Vinaya tradition gives corroboration. Yuanzhao's *Zichi ji* says: "if one cultivates phenomenal repentance, then either by not contravening the method of practice, or by obtaining an auspicious sign, the grave offence is extinguished." It further identifies what the *Sūtra on Divining* opens as "what the northern teaching calls sign-based repentance (取相懺)."[^11] Phenomenal repentance obtaining an auspicious sign so that the grave offence is extinguished — this lineage is named sign-based repentance. In sum, seeing an auspicious sign is no dispensable ornament of the rite of reception, but the gate-valve on this side of the bridge: it translates "repentance is done" into "the precept may be received," the one visible switch on this seam. How the other repentances beyond sign-based repentance are to be classified, and how one ascends from the phenomenal into the principial, is the matter of another paper; this one halts at this valve.

## IV. Receiving the New and Abiding: The Precept-Substance Is the Reborn Body

The door once open, what is it that is taken up? The answer is the precept-substance — and the "birth" of death-and-rebirth falls exactly on this precept-substance.

The *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Samantabhadra* names this reborn body itself. It says that a practitioner who contemplates the meaning of the Vaipulya teachings, for one day up to three weeks, whether monastic or lay, "needing no upādhyāya, requiring no teachers, and without the formal act of the saṅgha," by the power of receiving, holding, reading, and reciting the Mahāyāna scriptures and through the exhortation of the bodhisattva Samantabhadra,

> 自然成就五分法身：戒、定、慧、解脫、解脫知見。諸佛如來從此法生。[^12]

*spontaneously accomplishes the fivefold dharma-body: morality, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and the knowledge-and-vision of liberation. All the Buddhas and Tathāgatas are born from this dharma.*

What is accomplished is the fivefold dharma-body, and morality (the precept) stands first among the five. This is not to say that after receiving the precept one more rule has been added; it is to say that a new moral body is here brought to birth, of which the precept is the first member. The sūtra's own word "are born" in "all the Buddhas and Tathāgatas are born from this dharma" identifies this body as a body that takes birth. The "birth" of death-and-rebirth is thus no slippage into metaphor, but a vocabulary the sūtra's own wording grants.

This received substance has a directional feature. It is "accomplished spontaneously, without the formal act of the saṅgha" — taken up not by the saṅgha's horizontal conferral through the formal act, but vertically, by the witness of the Buddhas of the ten directions and the exhortation of Samantabhadra. The horizontal institution of precept-reception has its own complete machinery, and that is the matter of the *Śīla* volume; this paper takes only one point: along this vertical line of reception, the karma cleared by repentance is its sole antecedent, and the substance taken up is its present fruit.

What kind of existent the precept-substance is in itself — whether it is form, or mind, or seed, and how it continues and abides — is the ontological dispute of precept-substance theory, with three schools differing, and has its own dedicated treatment; this paper does not enter that debate.[^13] Here the precept-substance and the fivefold dharma-body are only identified as the reborn body of death-and-rebirth, and unfolded no further.

Two points must be drawn clear, lest this "birth" be read awry.

First, the "receiving" of a new precept-substance must not be confounded with the Vinaya's "restoration" in clearing an offence and returning to purity. What the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Samantabhadra* receives is the Mahāyāna bodhisattva precept, healed by receiving a new precept-substance; what the Vinaya restores in clearing an offence is the śrāvaka discipline's restored standing, established by returning one to one's original position within the saṅgha. The two are contexts under one and the same logic of "repent → purify → receive/restore," the one receiving a new substance, the other restoring an old standing; they must not be kneaded into a single institution of reception. This side of the bridge speaks of the former: receiving a newly born precept-substance, not merely restoring a pre-existing qualification.

Second, in the series' positional alignment with the *Heart Sūtra*, repentance was placed between "crossing beyond all suffering" and the word "not," and the precept side tied to the phrase "neither arising nor ceasing." An honest declaration is required: this alignment is the series' architectural reading, not the *Heart Sūtra*'s explicit wording. In particular, that "neither arising nor ceasing" should be an ontological attribute of the precept-substance is a genuine claim of precept-substance theory, whose unfolding awaits a dedicated treatment; this paper offers only the alignment as a signpost, and does not, on the strength of the *Heart Sūtra*'s plain text, establish a doctrine of a precept-substance that neither arises nor ceases.[^14]

## V. On the Death-and-Rebirth Bridge, Who Receives the New Substance?

What follows sets three Western accounts as a mirror of comparison. A prior declaration is required: this paper claims that no one of these thinkers ever applied his account to Buddhist precept-reception; they are cited only to throw into relief, against a mirror, the shape of this seam and its difference. Two red lines enter with the mirror: what is "born" is no substantial soul (no-self, carried from the preceding paper), and receiving the new is not absolution (reception ≠ pardon, carried from the earlier papers).

The first mirror is rites of passage and liminality. Arnold van Gennep, in *Les rites de passage*, observed that cross-cultural rites of passage share three phases: separation (departing the old status), liminality (the transitional state in between), and reincorporation (rejoining the group under a new status); Victor Turner, in *The Ritual Process*, deepened that liminal state, holding its subject to die symbolically out of an old status and be born into a new one.[^15] Death-and-rebirth is the mother tongue of ritual studies, not a private metaphor this paper has borrowed from outside. This mirror maps the bridge's three beats precisely: repentance is like liminality, the transition in which the old karmic status dissolves; receiving the bodhisattva precepts is like reincorporation, taking birth under the new status of a precept-holder, the fivefold dharma-body. It licenses this paper to speak of "the death-and-rebirth bridge" as a recognized cross-cultural ritual form, not a solitary illumination. Yet the difference lies just here: what ritual studies describe is a transfer of *social status*, and reincorporation in fact re-secures a more stable social self; whereas this seam is a transformation of *moral substance*, the precept-substance born here being a newly arisen moral nature, and this "new status" stands upon no-self — there is no re-secured social self. Its efficacy, too, lies not in the saṅgha's recognition, but in the karma cleared by phenomenal repentance and in the event of reception itself.

The second mirror is moral conversion and second nature. Aristotle, in the *Nicomachean Ethics*, holds virtue to be a settled disposition (*hexis*), built up by repeated right action into a stable state, until right action becomes a "second nature," effortless and constitutive of what the agent is; MacIntyre, in *After Virtue*, continues this, holding virtue to be a real quality acquired within practices and traditions, reshaping character and not merely regulating behaviour.[^16] This mirror most strongly supports the reading "precept-substance = a real new moral nature received" — the precept-substance is neither a momentary resolve nor an externally added rule, but the disposition that grounds all subsequent action; the fivefold dharma-body names a real moral reality established. Yet the difference lies in the mechanism: second nature is gradually habituated, and what it strengthens is a *continuous agent*, the self thickening by accretion; the precept-substance, by contrast, is taken up once at a threshold, and that threshold stands upon the loss of self and cleared karma — it is discontinuity, not accretion; reception upon no-self, not a circling round a persisting agent. The mirror of "second nature" judges rightly that the precept-substance is a real moral reality, and judges awry the mechanism of its taking birth.

The third mirror must be held with care, and set down once its showing is done. Paul's image in *Romans* 6 — the believer dies with Christ and rises to newness of life; on a comparative-religious reading (Eliade, *The Sacred and the Profane*), baptism is one instance of the universal form of initiation, "a symbolic death preceding spiritual rebirth."[^17] Its silhouette is exactly death-and-rebirth: the old self dies, the new self is born, with the rite as the hinge. But the likeness is only of form, not of meaning. Baptismal regeneration removes sin by grace and divine agency — and that is precisely pardon, an absolution conferred by another, violating the series' line of "reception ≠ pardon"; and it presupposes a persisting soul washed clean and reborn, violating the line of no-self. The same outline, a metaphysics of another kind. A likeness of form cannot pass itself off as an identity of meaning.

Among the three mirrors, liminality frames the structure of the seam, second nature shows what it is that is received, and baptism marks the sharpest of the borders. All three are borrowed to show the form, and all three stop at showing the form.

## VI. Middle-Way Corrections

On the exit side there are boundary-stones, and there are signposts. The boundary-stones are the one line that must not be crossed; the signposts are planted, not walked, pointing toward the matter of another volume. Items marked ❌ are transgressions to be guarded against; items marked ⚠️ are edges to be approached with care.

❌ **Receiving the new is not absolution.** The Buddhas of the ten directions in precept-reception are the realized state the receiver verifies, the object of refuge, not a judge who pardons offences. The obtaining of the precept-substance comes not from a forgiveness handed down from above, but from the karma cleared by phenomenal repentance and the taking-up of reception. To read "then receive the bodhisattva precepts" as "then the offences are pardoned" is to mistake reception for pardon; this line must not be crossed.

❌ **The precept-substance is not a soul that, after an annihilated self, takes rebirth.** What is "born" is the fivefold dharma-body, a moral reality posited, held, and abiding upon no-self — not the death of one self and the birth of another. The "death" of death-and-rebirth is the dropping away of the concealing mark-of-self; its "birth" is a moral body held and established upon no-self — not a swapped-out soul.

⚠️ **"Then" is a doctrinal sequence, not a hard calendar order.** To say repentance precedes the precept is to say that what reception takes up must be an already-cleared karmic body — a doctrinal before-and-after; it is not to say that repentance must be carried to some cut-off date before, in that instant, reception is permitted. The sequence giving → repentance → precept is doctrinal, not a timetable.

⚠️ **Death-and-rebirth is a structural metaphor, not a literal intermediate state.** The "death" and "birth" of the bridge are positional terms describing the structure of a moral transformation, a metaphor that aids understanding; they must not be reified into the literal death and literal rebirth of an intermediate being (*antarābhava*).

One signpost: the principial-repentance face that beholds the offence in its very substance and sees the intrinsic emptiness of offence is a deeper layer of repentance, to be treated at this volume's end — here only a signpost is planted, not walked into.

## VII. Conclusion: The Bridge's Exit · The Whole Bridge Joined

The preceding paper walked the entry-bridge — giving puts the self to death, repentance utters faithfully, running to the bridge's midpoint; this paper walks the exit-bridge — repentance clears the karma, "then" the new precept-substance is received, the fivefold dharma-body taking birth after the karma is cleared. The death of the entry and the birth of the exit are here joined into one bridge: giving → repentance → precept, the whole course of death-and-rebirth, walked through upon this single bridge.

At the close, three honest declarations are owed.

First, "repentance clears the karma → then the new precept-substance is received = rebirth" is a well-supported synthesis, not the verbatim wording of any single sutra. It stands by reading several passages together — the grammar of "then," the fivefold dharma-body's self-naming, the verification of the auspicious sign, the Vinaya's obstruction-and-opening, the lay precept's "this is called pure precept-keeping"; no single sutra says, word for word, "by repentance one obtains the rebirth of the precept-substance." "The precept-substance is the reborn body of death-and-rebirth" is an articulation the author shoulders, not the proof of a single sentence.[^18]

Second, the series' positional alignment with the *Heart Sūtra* (repentance tied to emptiness, the precept tied to neither-arising-nor-ceasing) is the series' architectural reading, not the *Heart Sūtra*'s explicit wording; in particular the ontological attribute "neither arising nor ceasing" of the precept-substance awaits a dedicated treatment, and this paper offers only the alignment as a signpost.

Third, the Vinaya tradition's corroborating words are drawn from Yuanzhao's *Zichi ji* (Song dynasty), not from a precept-commentary by Daoxuan himself; should one need Daoxuan's own argument that purity is the antecedent of reception, his *Xingshi chao* must be taken separately.[^19] This precision of attribution must not be left vague.

The bridge now joined, the road ahead is barely opened, not entered: beyond sign-based repentance, repentance has a whole apparatus of classification and a ladder ascending from the phenomenal into the principial, awaiting the paper of the three repentances; and the principial-repentance face that sees the intrinsic emptiness of offence awaits this volume's end. Here only their names are marked, their meanings not unfolded. This paper halts at the bridge's exit.
