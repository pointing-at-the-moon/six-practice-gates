# The Death-and-Rebirth Bridge · The Giving-Side Seam

**Only after the loss of self is disclosure lawful — the entry-seam between giving and repentance**

## Abstract

The preceding papers worked at the summit of phenomenal repentance; this one turns back to cross a new threshold — the bridge of death-and-rebirth. The series reads giving (*dāna*), repentance (*kṣamā*), and the precept (*śīla*) as a single bridge: giving loosens "what-is-mine" and so brings about *the loss of self* (a death of the self); repentance, standing upon that loss, discloses lawfully; the precept receives the new and abides (rebirth). This paper treats only the bridge's first joint — the entry-seam between giving and repentance — and argues one directional thesis: only after the loss of self is disclosure (*deśanā*) a lawful declaration of what was done, rather than an atonement-performance staged by the "I" who claims it. The carrying template is the *Diamond Sūtra*'s: a bodhisattva liberates all beings, yet "no being is in reality liberated"; giving at its limit is the giver's own self-effacement, the dropping away of the notion-of-self. Transposed onto repentance, the same logic yields that a confession bearing the notion-of-self — an "I" who sinned and now comes to atone — is *not* lawful disclosure. The paper then argues that the root of concealment (*chādana*) is the self-grasping (*ātma-grāha*) that guards a self-image; the loss of self dissolves that root, and disclosure turns from dread to gladness. The conscience-and-shame (*hiri-ottappa*) that drives non-concealment proves to be one current with the stream that washes out self-view (*Saṃyukta-āgama* 346: from conscience-and-shame … to not-clinging-to-self-view). A closing comparison with James, Parfit, and Sartre answers the standing objection: "if there is no self, who, on this bridge, confesses and who bears the fruit?" Throughout, one load-bearing distinction is held without slippage: what is emptied here is the *confessing self* (the loss of self), not the *offence confessed* — the offence stays vivid at the level of phenomenal repentance; the intrinsic emptiness of offence, the business of principial repentance, is reserved for this volume's end.

**Keywords:** death-and-rebirth, giving-side seam, loss of self, lawful disclosure, no-self (*anātman*), conscience-and-shame, concealment, self-grasping, *Diamond Sūtra*, *Saṃyukta-āgama*, *Sūtra on the Contemplation of Samantabhadra*, phenomenal repentance

## I. Entering the Bridge: Giving's Loss of Self

The earlier papers in this volume all pressed inward on repentance itself — how a single limb rises into a vow, how one point of disclosure is drawn out into a line extending to the end of future time. This paper pulls the lens back one step and asks after repentance's *antecedent*: before repentance, what has already had to happen so that this act of disclosure can be lawful at all? The answer begins from a larger structure.

The series reads giving, repentance, and the precept as a single bridge of "death-and-rebirth": giving loosens what-is-mine and is the self's "death"; repentance, upon that already-dead self, discloses what is so, and is the crossing; the precept receives the new and abides, and is the "birth." An honest declaration must come first: this three-beat counterpoint is the present author's architectural reading, not the explicit wording of any scripture. Its skeleton is taken from three phrases set consecutively in the *Heart Sūtra* — "crossing beyond all suffering," "form is itself emptiness," and "all dharmas are marked with emptiness; they neither arise nor cease."[^1] The series reads these as a linear progression of *crossing → emptiness → not-*, then aligns giving with "crossing" (the first of the perfections that ferry one across is giving), repentance with "emptiness" (repentance is the one gate among the three that points toward diminution, the one gate standing at the position of "emptiness"), and the precept with "not-" (the new body that neither arises nor ceases). But the *Heart Sūtra* speaks of the emptiness of all dharmas; it never says "giving corresponds to crossing, the precept corresponds to non-arising." So wherever this alignment appears, it carries the force of "the series aligns it thus" or "one may read it thus," and must never be read as "the *Heart Sūtra* says giving corresponds to crossing." The phrase "crossing beyond all suffering" also follows Xuanzang's recension; another recension reads "departing from all suffering," and the alignment set here is stated with respect to Xuanzang's text.[^1] Likewise, the "death" and "birth" of "death-and-rebirth" are a serviceable structural metaphor — isomorphic with the form "death → intermediate state → rebirth," and borrowed for that reason as a bridge-image; but this paper builds no literal ontology of an intermediate state (*antarābhava*) upon it, and the *Heart Sūtra*'s line "no aging-and-death, and no end of aging-and-death" precisely refutes the substantial reality of birth-and-death. What is taken is only the positional sequence, not the body of any intermediate being.

Return now to the entry side. Why is giving "the loss of self"? The point lies not in "how much wealth I gave away" but in this: when giving is done to the utmost, the "I who gives" also disappears along with the gift. The *Diamond Sūtra* states it most transparently:

> 菩薩於法，應無所住行於布施……若菩薩不住相布施，其福德不可思量……滅度一切眾生已，而無有一眾生實滅度者。何以故？須菩提！若菩薩有我相、人相、眾生相、壽者相，即非菩薩。[^2]

*A bodhisattva, with respect to things, should give while abiding in nothing… If a bodhisattva gives without abiding in any mark, the merit is beyond measure… Having liberated all beings, yet no being is in reality liberated. Why? Subhūti, if a bodhisattva holds the notion of self, of person, of being, or of life-span, he is then not a bodhisattva.*

All beings have been liberated, and yet not "one being is really liberated by me" — for the moment the mind sets up the marks "I (the liberator) / being (the liberated)," one is "then not a bodhisattva." Giving at its limit is not "I gave a great deal" but the withdrawal of the giver together with the "self that gives." The *Treatise on the Great Perfection of Wisdom* states the same point as "the emptiness of the three spheres": "by the principle of having nothing to relinquish, the perfection of giving is fulfilled, because giver, receiver, and gift are all unfindable."[^3] Giver, receiver, and the thing given — all three are unfindable. Worth recording in particular is the question the treatise-author then poses to himself: it is the convergence of three things that is called "giving"; now, if the three things are said to be unfindable, how is this instead called "the fulfilment and completion of the perfection of giving"?[^3] The answer is exactly the key this paper means to borrow: the loss of self (the unfindability of the three) does not cancel giving — it *perfects* it. Lose the self that keeps the ledger, and only then does giving turn from a transaction into giving itself.

This can be put more precisely, for here lies the very crux of "self-loss perfects" rather than "self-loss voids." The text says, "giving without abiding in any mark, the merit is beyond measure." Measurable merit is precisely the merit reckoned by that ledger-keeping self: how much I gave, how much I am owed, how much credit this act has secured me. Once one no longer abides in any mark, the self that would convert giving into entries in a merit-passbook withdraws, and giving no longer has any countable boundary — and so "the merit is beyond measure." Plainly, the loss of self performs no subtraction upon giving, makes it neither less nor empty; what it does is untie the rope that bound giving to the "I," letting giving recover its native boundlessness. As with giving, so with repentance — and this is the leverage to be transposed in the next section.

This, then, is the first stone of the entry-bridge. As for the full derivation of "how crossing beyond all suffering loosens what-is-mine, and why the five aggregates are not-self," that was established in the prior Giving volume and is here simply inherited; this paper does not re-walk the proof of no-self aggregate by aggregate. What it sets out to do is to carry this "loss of self," raised at the gate of giving, over to the gate of repentance.[^4]

## II. After the Loss of Self, Disclosure Is the Lawful Utterance

Transpose the *Diamond Sūtra*'s template onto the gate of repentance, and the spine of this paper stands. Giving at its limit is "no being is in reality liberated" — the giver effaced. What, then, does repentance look like when it is done lawfully? By the same logic: a repentance that still sets up "the notion-of-self" — an "I" who sinned, and now this "I" who comes to atone, to perform its remorse — is precisely *not* lawful disclosure. In a confession bearing the notion-of-self, the centre of gravity still rests on the "I" that wants to be seen, to be absolved, to recover its standing; it is a self-management conducted in the form of penitence. Only when the claimant withdraws does disclosure cease to be the "I"'s performance and become instead a faithful utterance of the deeds that were done.

A question must be met head-on at once: if even the "self" is lost, then who is left to repent? Does disclosure not collapse into nothing? The *Diamond Sūtra* itself gives the shape of the answer — the loss of self is not an annihilationist erasure, for if it were, there would indeed be no one to disclose:

> 諸菩薩摩訶薩應如是生清淨心，不應住色生心，不應住聲、香、味、觸、法生心，應無所住而生其心。[^2]

*The great bodhisattvas should give rise to a pure mind thus: they should not give rise to a mind abiding in form, nor in sound, smell, taste, touch, or mental objects; they should give rise to a mind that abides in nothing.*

"Abiding in nothing" is the loss-of-self side — not setting up, upon form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or mental objects, an "I" that abides clinging to them; "give rise to a mind" is the disclosure side — precisely upon this non-abiding, mind arises, disclosure arises. The loss of self does not stall the mind; it draws out the abiding self that would skim a profit inside the act of disclosure, so that the faithful utterance can instead arise. Thus loss-of-self and disclosure are not two things that supplant one another in sequence — not "the self is gone, therefore I cannot repent," but "precisely because nothing is abided in, disclosure can arise, and arise truly."

Here a boundary-stone, the most crucial in this paper, must be set, lest a single phrase's slip carry the argument out of bounds: what is "lost" here, what is "emptied," is that *confessing* "self" — the loosening of self-grasping; it is *not* the offence confessed. The offence stays vivid at the level of phenomenal repentance — the cause, mark, and fruit of the deed are each distinctly there, and it is precisely this vivid offence that disclosure faces. The loss of self does not empty the offence along with the self; it only unties the grasping self that would deny the offence, or be crushed under it. The moment "disclosing upon the loss of self" slides into "the self being lost, the offence too is empty," the subject has been switched out — from "self" onto "offence" — and that is no longer the phenomenal repentance of this paper but a further level, principial repentance, reserved for this volume's end. Wherever this paper says "empty," it means the emptiness *of self* (the loss of self); wherever it says offence, it holds the offence as "vivid, not lost, faithfully so," and never says "the offence is intrinsically empty, the offence is unfindable."

## III. The Root of Concealment Is Self-Grasping

If the loss of self remains only doctrine, it still hangs in the air. Its real leverage is that it dismantles, with precision, repentance's greatest enemy — concealment. An earlier paper, treating the confession of faults, already showed concealment to be the obverse of disclosure: hide the offence, refuse to disclose, and repentance cannot even begin. What this paper presses for is one layer deeper: why does a person conceal at all? Where is the root of concealment sunk?

In the Āgamas, concealment is mostly discussed in terms of the monastic institution; the text that states its *psychological* root most transparently is the *Upāsaka-śīla Sūtra*. It depicts the inner monologue of one who conceals:

> 我今云何而自欺誑？……有得清淨天耳、天眼及他心智，我若作惡，是人必當見、聞、知我；若見、聞、知我，當云何不生慚愧而作惡耶？[^5]

*How is it that I now deceive myself?… There are those who have attained the pure divine ear, divine eye, and knowledge of others' minds; if I do evil, such a one will surely see, hear, and know me. And if they see, hear, and know me, how could I do evil without giving rise to conscience-and-shame?*

Look at what the concealer truly fears. Not the offence itself, but being "seen, heard, known" — fearing that the pure image of self will break before an imagined witness. Concealment is, in essence, image-management before an imagined audience; what it labours to guard is exactly the image "I am pure." The text's three words "deceive myself" cut to the bone: concealment is not only deceiving others but deceiving oneself — knowing full well there is an offence, yet, before that witness (and before oneself), maintaining a self that is without offence.

So the leverage of self-loss opens out plainly: if the root of concealment is the self-grasping that "guards the image of self," then once that to-be-guarded image "I-am-pure" comes loose, ceases to be the thing that must at all costs be defended, concealment loses its whole motive force. With no self that must remain respectable to preserve, there is nothing that must at all costs be hidden. The loss of self does not make a person numb to the offence — quite the reverse: what it dismantles is the layer of self-cosmetic standing between offence and faithfulness, so that the offence can be set out exactly as it is. The same sūtra then gives the resolving arc of disclosure, which is precisely the scene after this dismantling:

> 是人常觀犯輕如重，觀已生悔及慚愧心，怖畏愁惱，心不樂之，至心懺悔，既懺悔已，心生歡喜，慎護受持更不敢犯。[^5]

*Such a person regards a light offence as grave; having so regarded it, remorse and conscience-and-shame arise — dread, sorrow, a mind that takes no pleasure in it; with wholehearted repentance, and the repentance once done, the mind gives rise to gladness, and guarding it carefully, he dares not offend again.*

From dread and sorrow, to wholehearted repentance, to — the repentance once done — "the mind gives rise to gladness": the terminus of this arc is release, not an unending self-flagellation. Disclosure here shows its native character: it is the setting down of what was hidden, and so the shedding of weight; it is not the taking-up of the whip to beat oneself. What the loss of self perfects is exactly this light, generative disclosure. (A note on the boundary is required: this sūtra also touches the institutional face of receiving and purifying precepts; this paper takes only its psychological arc of conscience-and-shame → repentance → gladness, and its root-of-concealment as self-deception, while the precept-institution belongs to another volume and is not treated here.)

## IV. Conscience-and-Shame and the Loss of Self Are One Current

The previous section said that the loss of self dissolves the root of concealment. But to set the loss of self against "conscience-and-shame" — as though self-loss were cool detachment and conscience-and-shame were cutting self-reproach, the two running opposite ways — would be to mistake it. This section means to show the very reverse: the conscience-and-shame that drives disclosure, and the stream that washes out self-view, are one and the same current. This is the paper's strongest single scriptural proof. *Saṃyukta-āgama*, fascicle 14, sūtra 346, sets out a long causal chain:

> 以慚愧故不放逸，不放逸故恭敬順語、為善知識……精進故不掉、住律儀、學戒，學戒故不失念、正知、住不亂心，不亂心故正思惟、習近正道、心不懈怠，心不懈怠故不著身見、不著戒取、度疑惑……[^6]

*By conscience-and-shame, non-heedlessness; by non-heedlessness, reverence, agreeable speech, and good companionship… by diligence, no agitation, abiding in restraint, training in the precepts; by training in the precepts, no loss of mindfulness, clear comprehension, a mind abiding undisturbed; by an undisturbed mind, right intention, drawing near the right path, a mind not slack; by a mind not slack, not clinging to self-view, not clinging to precept-grasping, crossing beyond doubt…*

This chain begins with "conscience-and-shame" and runs, by way of non-heedlessness, training in the precepts, right mindfulness and clear comprehension, and right intention, finally into "not clinging to self-view." Self-view (*sakkāya-diṭṭhi*) is exactly the core of the self-grasping that holds "the five aggregates are self, the self possesses the aggregates"; not clinging to self-view is the loss of self. The text does not say outright "because there is no self, one can disclose," but it plainly places "conscience-and-shame" — that virtue of disclosure — at the head of a causal chain ending in "not clinging to self-view." Conscience-and-shame is not the opponent of self-loss; it sits in the same river, the headwater stretch of the very current that flows toward self-loss. So "disclosure after the loss of self" is not coldness; its warmth comes precisely from conscience-and-shame. And the reason conscience-and-shame does not crush a person, does not harden into the rock of self-judgement, is exactly that it flows toward the loosening of self-view, not the fortifying of the self.

Sūtra 347 of the same fascicle (the Susīma sūtra) adds a further stroke, showing that the bent of disclosure is generative, not punitive:

> 凡人有罪，自見、自知而悔過者，於當來世律儀成就，功德增長，終不退減。[^7]

*Whoever, having an offence, sees it for himself, knows it for himself, and repents, in a future life will accomplish restraint, and his merit will grow and in the end never decline.*

To see one's offence for oneself, to know it for oneself, and then to repent — the result of this disclosure is "merit will grow and never decline." Disclosure is something that raises a person up, not a mill that grinds him ever thinner. It requires first that one "see and know" the offence for oneself (the offence is vivid, hence must be seen, must be known), and then, upon that seeing-and-knowing, disclose; and what the loss of self perfects is exactly this "seeing and knowing for oneself" that need not first apply cosmetic, need not fear the breaking of an image.

An honest accounting of this paper's thesis is owed here. The directional thesis "only after the loss of self can disclosure be lawful" is not a conclusion stated verbatim by any single Āgama sūtra — no one text writes out "because there is no self, one can disclose" or "the root of concealment is self-grasping." It is a synthesis raised by reading several scriptural witnesses together: the five aggregates' not being self dissolves the ledger-keeping, controlling self (established in the prior volume); the chain of conscience-and-shame ends in not clinging to self-view (sūtra 346); seeing one's offence for oneself and repenting makes merit grow (sūtra 347); the root of concealment is self-deception before an imagined witness (*Upāsaka-śīla Sūtra*). Set these passages side by side, and the direction "loss of self → lawful disclosure" surfaces clearly. But it remains, in the end, a reading that stitches several witnesses together — a reading the author shoulders, an articulation, not the proof of a single sentence. This paper marks the matter honestly here, and does not pass it off as ready-made scriptural wording.[^8]

## V. After the Loss of Self, Who Repents?

Having come this far, the objection that has loitered at the door throughout must be invited in and answered to its face: after the loss of self, who, on this bridge, actually repents, bears the karma, receives the fruit of rebirth? If not even one substantial self remains, do repentance and karmic fruit not both lose their owner? This paper sets three Western accounts as a mirror of comparison, to throw the answer into relief. A prior declaration is required: each of the following is used only for comparison; this paper does not claim that any Western thinker ever applied his account to Buddhist disclosure — only that the two may be set side by side.

Take first William James, *The Varieties of Religious Experience*, on the "twice-born" type. He sees truly: there is a kind of person whose real transformation must pass through an inner death, and the decisive move is not to try harder but precisely to *let go* — he calls it "self-surrender" and regards it as the pivotal turn of the religious life. This face resonates with giving's loss of self and with the volume's repeated insistence on "not redoubling the self-reproach," and may be received. But the distinction is just as clear: James hands the self that is set down to a "higher Other," and after the surrender ultimately reaffirms the existence of a substantial self; the loss of self hands the self to no one — it sees through the self as having no substance from the start (*anātman*). The one is a transfer of ownership; the other is the dropping away of the ownership-fiction itself. They are alike in form and unlike in substance.[^9]

At the centre, taking the objection head-on, is Derek Parfit's Reductionism in *Reasons and Persons*. Parfit argues that a person's existence "just consists in" the continuity of bodily and mental events; there is no self beyond this continuity, no further fact. Personal identity, therefore, is not what truly matters; what matters is psychological continuity and connectedness. Buddhist Reductionists (such as Mark Siderits) press this further: the person is a conventional designation, a name imputed upon the causally continuous aggregates, with no real self at the ultimate level — yet this conventional designation already suffices to carry ethics, agency, and karmic fruit. No substantial self need be smuggled in; the continuity itself can bear the weight of "who receives the fruit." One may candidly grant that Parfit, by pure argument, independently arrives at the heart of the no-self structure (there is no further-fact self), and that dependent continuity suffices to bear responsibility without a substantial self — a striking convergence with what the Buddhist tradition sees. But two cuts must still be made, lest convergence be inflated into identity. First, this paper must carry the thesis from metaphysics into soteriology: in the Buddhist tradition, the loss of self is not a consoling theoretical conclusion but a realization meant to change the act of confession itself — to know there is no self, and to truly disclose upon no-self, are two different things. Second, the loss of self is not merely *compatible* with lawful disclosure; it is its *condition*: precisely because there is no "I-am-pure" image left to guard, disclosure can cease to be performance. (A caution must be added: one cannot say "the Buddha agreed with Parfit" — this is convergence-with-a-difference, not unity; and Parfit's "identity is not what matters" is by no means "nothing matters" — he explicitly rejects nihilism.)

Take last Jean-Paul Sartre on "bad faith" (*mauvaise foi*, self-deception), to seal off a wrong turn. Bad faith, for Sartre, is the person playing a role for himself, deceiving himself into being what he is not — and this is exactly the concealer of the third section: the self-management conducted in the form of penitence is, in essence, a performance of bad faith, an "I" acting out for an imagined audience "the penitent I." Sartre sees this with great accuracy. Yet the cure he prescribes still leaves a self that must shoulder its freedom and achieve its authenticity; the loss of self goes one move deeper — it does not teach the claimant to shoulder things more sincerely, but sees through the claimant as having no own-nature, removing at the root the very subject that would stage bad faith. So Sartre's account seals off exactly the wrong turn into which repentance most often slides: turning disclosure into a more refined performance about "how remorseful I am." The disclosure that the loss of self perfects has no one to perform it, and so nothing through which bad faith could enter.

The three mirrors held up, the answer to the objection is now plain: the loss of self is not the destruction of the self but the setting down of the "self-as-owner" fiction; and precisely for that reason, disclosure is a lawful utterance rather than the claimant's atonement-performance. The bridge is not unpeopled in the crossing — what crosses is the dependently arisen continuity of body and mind; there is simply no longer a real self hidden behind it, keeping the ledger.

## VI. Middle-Way Corrections

This section gathers the boundaries scattered through the foregoing into several plain corrections. Items marked ❌ are transgressions to be guarded against; items marked ⚠️ are edges to be approached with care.

❌ **The loss of self must not slide into the intrinsic emptiness of offence.** This is the most crucial cut in the paper. What is emptied here is, throughout, the "confessing self" (the loosening of self-grasping, the loss of self); the offence confessed stays vivid at the level of phenomenal repentance — its cause, mark, and fruit are each pointable, and disclosure faces exactly this vivid offence. The moment "disclosing upon the loss of self" becomes "the self being lost, the offence too is empty," the predicate has been switched from "self" onto "offence," and that crosses into principial repentance (the business of this volume's end). Wherever this paper says "empty," it means self-empty; wherever it says "offence," it keeps the offence vivid. The face that sees the intrinsic emptiness of offence is reserved for the volume's end; here only a signpost is set, with none of its content unfolded.

❌ **The loss of self is not annihilationism.** No-self is not "there was a permanent self, now destroyed," still less "no one acts, no one bears fruit." The *Saṃyukta-āgama*'s Yamaka sūtra rebukes as an evil wrong view the claim that "an arahant whose outflows are exhausted, his body broken and life ended, is utterly nothing," and Śāriputra corrects it by analysis: form is "neither the Tathāgata, nor other than the Tathāgata, nor mutually located."[^10] What is established is "the self is unfindable," not "the self is nothing." The Kaccānagotta sūtra states it more directly still: "not grasping, not abiding, not reckoning a self; suffering, arising, arises; suffering, ceasing, ceases… this is called teaching the Middle Way apart from the two extremes."[^10] Dependent arising avoids both eternalism and annihilationism; "not reckoning a self" and "the vivid arising-and-ceasing of suffering" coexist without contradiction. The loss of self sits upon this Middle Way — neither a permanent self nor annihilation.

❌ **No-self does not exempt one from repentance.** Precisely because karmic marks stay vivid under dependent arising, disclosure remains required; the loss of self is the *condition* of faithful disclosure (untying the self that guards the lie), not a passport that exempts one from repentance. One cannot slide from "the self is unfindable" to the antinomian view that "since there is no self, there is no offence to repent." What the loss of self dissolves is the way guilt paralyses a person and turns into performance, not the obligation of disclosure itself. Receiving repentance is likewise not absolution — the Buddhas of the ten directions stand within disclosure as the realized state, the object of refuge, not as judges who pass sentence and grant pardon, in keeping with the distinction drawn in this volume's earlier papers.

⚠️ **"Death-and-rebirth" is a structural metaphor, not a literal intermediate state.** The three-beat of giving's death, repentance's crossing, the precept's birth is isomorphic with "death → intermediate state → rebirth" and is borrowed as a bridge-image; but this paper builds no ontology of an intermediate state upon it, nor uses the *Heart Sūtra* to argue for or against the existence of an intermediate state — the two are not in the same domain. What is taken is only the emptiness of the positional sequence, not the body of any intermediate being.

⚠️ **Giving → repentance → precept is a doctrinal sequence, not a hard temporal order.** The arrangement of these three gates is the doctrinal architecture of "death-and-rebirth" and the series' skeleton, not a calendar order; it is not that giving must be perfected before repentance is permitted. A practitioner need not finish the gate of giving before disclosure is possible; to speak of "entering the bridge" is to speak of a doctrinal joining, not a hard sequence in practice.

## VII. Conclusion

**Restated in brief.** The first joint of the death-and-rebirth bridge falls between giving and repentance. Giving at its limit is the giver's self-effacement, the dropping away of the notion-of-self — the loss of self; repentance receives exactly this loss and discloses faithfully upon no-self. The crux: only after the loss of self is disclosure a lawful utterance of the deeds that were done, rather than an atonement-performance staged by a claimant. The root of concealment is the self-grasping that guards the image "I-am-pure"; the loss of self dissolves that root, and disclosure turns from dread to gladness, from performance to release. The conscience-and-shame that drives disclosure is one current with the stream that washes out self-view, so the repentance of self-loss is not cold and does not harden into the rock of self-judgement. The whole paper works only at the level of phenomenal repentance: the offence stays vivid, disclosure is made to the Buddhas, and what is emptied is only the confessing self, not the offence confessed.

**The bridge has its scriptural home.** The sequence of "death-and-rebirth" is not wholly the series author's architectural arrangement — the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of the Practice of Samantabhadra* writes it into the sūtra's own grammar by a forward-moving ritual arc. The sūtra says that one who wishes to fulfil the bodhisattva precepts should "everywhere pay homage to the Buddhas of the ten directions, repent all offences, and declare one's own faults. Then…" receive the bodhisattva precepts, and so "spontaneously accomplish the fivefold dharma-body: precept, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and the knowledge-and-vision of liberation."[^11] That word "then" is the grammatical hinge of repentance preceding the precept — repentance to clear the karma first, the precept to receive the new body after; this is exactly the sequence of "death-and-rebirth," and the name "fivefold dharma-body" is the sūtra's own naming of that reborn body. The same sūtra further sets "recollection of giving" and "recollection of the precept" side by side at the very threshold of "disclosing prior offences, repenting wholeheartedly"[^11] — the sūtra itself ties the gate of giving and the gate of the precept to the ground of disclosure. The giving-side entry-seam treated in this paper finds here its scriptural anchor.

**Signpost and transition.** This paper holds strictly to the phenomenal-repentance level of the loss of self and lawful disclosure. As for the face of principial repentance that sees the intrinsic emptiness of offence — written equally in this sūtra, interwoven with the text of disclosure and precept-reception — this paper takes not a word of it, deferring it to the volume's end (the paper of principial repentance); here only a signpost is set, with nothing unfolded. And this bridge has crossed only the entering half: after the loss of self, the karma having been lawfully disclosed and so cleared, the face of the received-and-abiding precept-body — how the "then" of "then receive the bodhisattva precepts" accomplishes rebirth — is the bridge's exit, to be treated in the next paper. This paper halts at the bridge's midpoint: giving has put the self to death; repentance has uttered faithfully upon no-self; what is born from this is a matter for later.
