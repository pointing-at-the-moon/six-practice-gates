# Transgression Has No Own-Being

**Vimalakīrti Rebukes Upāli, and the Paradox of Truly Keeping the Vinaya**

*中文主題：罪性本空·維摩詰呵優波離，與真持律的弔詭*

## Abstract

Upāli, foremost among the Buddha's disciples in upholding the monastic discipline (the Vinaya), explains the rule lawfully to two monks who, having broken a precept and sunk in shame, have come to him — by every measure, the most reliable machinery of phenomenal repentance. Yet a single sentence from the layman Vimalakīrti — "do not compound the transgression of these two monks" — judges that lawful explanation itself to be *compounding the offense*. Taking the full narrative of the Upāli episode in the Disciples chapter of the *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa* as its stage, and drawing on the earliest surviving commentary, the *Commentary on the Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa*, with its glosses from three hands — Kumārajīva, Sengzhao, and Daosheng — this paper unfolds, layer by layer, the mechanism of that rebuke. What compounds the offense is not the breach itself but the *act of fixing the mark of transgression*, the reifying grasp (Kumārajīva); transgression is neither within, nor without, nor in between, because it is formed by causes and conditions and, searched out, is wholly empty (Sengzhao); transgression binds only the one who consents to hold it real and so binds himself (Daosheng). From this comes the chapter's great positive verdict — "one who knows this is called a keeper of the Vinaya": the true keeper of the rule is precisely the one who has seen that transgression has no own-being and does not bind himself to its mark. The paper also draws the single boundary this chapter is most often misread across: "do not compound the transgression of these two monks" is by no means "these two monks have no transgression." Vimalakīrti neither denies that the breach occurred nor sweeps karmic fruit away; he rebukes only the *manner* of compounding. The character-arc closes where the foremost upholder of the rule is himself rebuked on the keeping of the rule; and the paper notes the two monks' final arousing of the awakening-mind, their vow turned toward all beings, as a thread held in reserve for the gate of vow to come.

**Keywords:** transgression has no own-being; Vimalakīrti; Upāli; fixing the mark of transgression; dependent origination and emptiness; sealing delusion and self-binding; "one who knows this is called a keeper of the Vinaya"; truly keeping the Vinaya; principial repentance; the Prajñā-school's rebuke-as-teaching; emptiness is not annihilation; *Commentary on the Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa*

## I. The Problem: Why a Lawful Explanation Was Judged to Compound the Offense

Two monks have broken a precept. Carrying their shame, not daring to bring the matter before the Buddha, they come instead to Upāli — and the choice is a reasonable one, for Upāli is foremost in upholding the Vinaya, the one in the community who knows the precepts and the procedures of clearance better than anyone. They ask him to explain the matter lawfully, so that the fault may be cleared and the doubt and remorse in their hearts set down. And Upāli does exactly that: following the rule, he sets it out for them point by point.

Up to here, everything is "lawful" (如法). Disclosure, explanation, clearance by procedure — this is the proper path of phenomenal repentance (事懺, *shìchàn*), the stable mechanism by which the monastic community has handled fault for two and a half millennia. The whole foundation of phenomenal repentance laid out in the earlier parts of this volume — from disclosure to probation to the fortnightly confession rite — has, as its guardian, precisely a keeper of the Vinaya like Upāli.

Then Vimalakīrti arrives. His first words to Upāli are not a supplement, not an endorsement, but a flat rebuke:

> Upāli, do not compound the transgression of these two monks; extinguish it directly, and do not disturb their minds.[^1]

The force of the sentence lies exactly in its target. What Vimalakīrti rebukes is not the two offending monks but the impeccable rule-master, the one who has done everything by the book. How can a Vinaya-master who lawfully explains to others how an offense is to be cleared be charged, of all things, with *compounding the offense*? Having broken a precept, should one not own it truthfully and clear it by the procedure? And what entitles "extinguish it directly" and "do not disturb their minds" to displace a meticulous, by-the-letter exposition of the rule?

This tension is hard to release because it touches an intuition we are deeply unwilling to let go of: that the deeper the confession, the heavier the remorse, the more sharply the fault is held in view — the more devout we seem. We instinctively distrust someone who has broken a precept and yet refuses to take the offense seriously. Upāli is acting straight along this intuition — he neither indulges nor blurs; he sets out the mark of the offense, its gravity, and the method of its clearance, one by one. If even this counts as "compounding the offense," then what, exactly, is clearing it? What Vimalakīrti means to unsettle is precisely this intuition that *the more clearly the fault is held, the better*. And to see why he does so, one must first separate two things: owning a fault truthfully, and fixing it into a haunting, re-indictable *real mark* that lodges in the heart and can be belabored again and again. These are not the same act.

This paper answers that tension. Vimalakīrti does not mean to abolish the Vinaya in the master's hands, nor does he say the two monks did not in fact break the precept. What he means to disclose is the ultimate sense of the discipline: when "clearing an offense" is operated as *fixing a real mark of transgression and reproaching the offender over and over against it*, that act itself disturbs the mind and tightens the knot more deeply than the breach ever did. This chapter is the fullest classical scene of "transgression has no own-being" (罪性本空) in the Chinese repentance tradition. It takes the Upāli episode that the previous paper only named, and unfolds it into a story of being-rebuked — then hands it to three later commentators to draw out the mechanism the root text leaves implicit.

## II. The Textual Basis: The Upāli Episode and the Three Commentators

The root text of this chapter is the Disciples chapter of the *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa* as translated by Kumārajīva. Immediately after the rebuke, Vimalakīrti gives the positive teaching — that the nature of transgression is not to be found in three places:

> Why so? The nature of that transgression is neither within, nor without, nor in between… As the mind is, so is the defilement of transgression, so are all dharmas — none departing from suchness.[^1]

He then draws the criterion of "defilement" and "purity" back from outward behavior into the mind's grasping and letting-go:

> Deluded thought is defilement, no deluded thought is purity; distortion is defilement, no distortion is purity; grasping a self is defilement, not grasping a self is purity.[^2]

A step further, through a chain of similes, he discloses the arising-and-ceasing mark of all things:

> All dharmas arise and cease without abiding, like an illusion, like lightning; dharmas do not wait upon one another, not for a single thought-moment do they abide; all dharmas are falsely seen — like a dream, like a flame, like the moon in water, like an image in a mirror, born of deluded thought.[^3]

Most decisive is the verdict that follows this teaching — the line that turns the whole chapter from breaking to building:

> One who knows this is called a keeper of the Vinaya; one who knows this is called one who truly understands.[^4]

Hearing this teaching, the two monks' doubt and remorse dissolve on the spot, and they exclaim:

> What supreme wisdom! This is beyond Upāli; even the foremost upholder of the Vinaya could not have said it.[^5]

The root text gives the conclusion — transgression is not in the three places; this is named keeping the Vinaya — but it does not spell out the *why*. The why is supplied by the earliest surviving commentary on the *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa*: the *Commentary on the Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa* (*Zhu Weimojie jing*), a compilation that gathers the comments of Kumārajīva's school, edited by Sengzhao. Three hands gloss this passage, and their positions differ; each must be read under its own name.

Kumārajīva (Kumārajīva comments, 什曰) enters by way of the *psychology* of compounding:

> One who has broken a precept is constantly in dread; if one fixes the mark of his transgression and presses it further upon him, this may be called disturbing the mind and compounding the transgression. But hearing the real mark, the mind, profound, has nowhere to lodge, and the burden of transgression dissolves of itself.[^6]

Sengzhao (Sengzhao comments, 肇曰) gives the *dependent-origination* argument for "transgression is not in the three places":

> The arising of the burden of transgression is formed by causes and conditions; sought, its real nature is not in the three places… arisen from a concourse of conditions, searched out it is wholly empty.[^7]

Daosheng (Daosheng comments, 生曰) names the crux of *self-binding* — whether transgression exists at all turns on whether a person consents to hold it real:

> Sealing delusion is the person's own doing — how should that transgression have any being? One who can realize this no longer binds himself with transgression… to one who has left the realm of transgression, can transgression lay hold?[^8]

Taken together, the three commentators are exactly the three layers of this one rebuke: why fixing the mark compounds the offense (Kumārajīva), why transgression is not in the three places (Sengzhao), and how transgression binds only one who consents to hold it real (Daosheng). The three sections that follow unfold these layers in turn.

## III. The Core Argument: The Mechanism of Compounding, the Non-Reality of Transgression, and True Vinaya-Keeping

### 3.1 What Compounds the Offense Is Fixing the Mark of Transgression, Not the Transgression Itself

To read "do not compound the transgression of these two monks," the first point — and the one most easily lost — is this: the sentence does not say "these two monks have no transgression." Vimalakīrti does not deny that they broke the precept. His word is *compound* (重增) — do not add a further layer onto the fault already there. So what, exactly, is the superfluous layer that gets added?

Kumārajīva's gloss names it in a phrase: the trouble is "fixing the mark of his transgression and pressing it further." One who has broken a precept is already in dread; if one now fixes that breach into a settled, immovable real mark, and then reproaches the offender over and over against this reified mark, the only result is that "the mind is disturbed and the transgression compounded." In other words, what compounds the offense is not the deed but the *cognitive act* of fixing the mark — and the repeated reproach that follows from it.

This explains Vimalakīrti's two seemingly mild but in fact very heavy instructions: "extinguish it directly; do not disturb their minds." "Extinguish it directly" means: finish it cleanly, do not breed it into an object that lodges in the heart. "Do not disturb their minds" points still more sharply at the wound — an exposition that deepens the mark of transgression with every stroke, however "lawful," *is* the disturbing of the mind. There is nothing wrong with the master's explanation in itself; what is wrong is that it has been turned into an instrument for fixing the mark.

It is worth noticing that the remedy Kumārajīva offers is *not* "tell the offender he is fine," but: "hearing the real mark, the mind, profound, has nowhere to lodge, and the burden of transgression dissolves of itself." The burden dissolves not because it has been pardoned, but because the mind no longer has a "mark of transgression" to fasten upon and fix.

Hidden here is a self-feeding loop that is easy to miss. Kumārajīva says the precept-breaker's mind is "constantly in dread" — that is the starting point: after the breach, the mind already hangs and panics. An exposition that fixes the mark one detail at a time, reproaching it again and again, does not settle that dread; it gives the dread an ever-clearer object to fasten upon. The clearer the object, the more the dread finds purchase; the deeper the purchase, the more one wants to sharpen the mark still further in the hope of "settling it for good" — and so fixing-the-mark, reproach, and dread feed one another, winding into an ever-tighter coil. Vimalakīrti's rebuke severs this coil from the outside in a single stroke: not by consoling from within the coil, but by removing the axis on which it turns — the reified mark of transgression. Once the mind has no mark to fasten upon, the dread loses its purchase; "the burden of transgression dissolves of itself" names exactly this loosening after the purchase is gone. This is the deepest line between principial and phenomenal repentance: phenomenal repentance clears the transgression lawfully; principial repentance (理懺, *lǐchàn*) sees that the transgression so cleared was never susceptible of a fixed mark to begin with.

### 3.2 Neither Within, Without, Nor in Between: Arisen from Conditions, Sought It Is Found Empty

On what ground, then, can one say the mark of transgression cannot be fixed? Sengzhao's gloss takes up Vimalakīrti's line — "the nature of that transgression is neither within, nor without, nor in between" — and supplies the argument from dependent origination: "the arising of the burden of transgression is formed by causes and conditions; sought, its real nature is not in the three places… arisen from a concourse of conditions, searched out it is wholly empty."

This is rigorous reasoning that dismantles a fixed grasp, not rhetoric. Ask: is that transgression *within*? If the transgression subsisted of itself within the offender's mind, it would have to be there even before an object was met and a thought arose — but this is not so. Is it *without*? If it subsisted of itself in the outer object, the object is itself without awareness; what has it to do with me? Is it *in between*? With neither inner nor outer established, there is no further independent "between" in which to lodge it. Searched throughout all three places and not to be found, the conclusion follows: transgression has no "real nature" subsisting apart from the concourse of conditions. It is an event-mark arisen from the meeting of body, speech, mind, object, and condition — present-seeming when conditions gather, vanished without trace when conditions disperse. "Searched out, it is wholly empty."

This must be read together with Vimalakīrti's own similes: all dharmas are "like an illusion, like lightning," "like a dream, like a flame, like the moon in water, like an image in a mirror, born of deluded thought." The moon in water is not the absence of any moon-reflection; the image in the mirror is not sheer nothing. They do arise; yet they have no graspable self-substance one could point to and seize. So too with the mark of transgression — it does arise (the breach is a fact), yet it has no own-being that could be fixed and reproached over and over. Sengzhao's "searched out, it is wholly empty" speaks of exactly this "appears yet has no own-being," not of "never happened."

And so the criterion — "deluded thought is defilement, no deluded thought is purity; grasping a self is defilement, not grasping a self is purity" — becomes lucid. The true line between defilement and purity does not run along whether the deed was done, but along whether the mind fixes it with deluded thought, whether it fastens upon it with the self-grasping "I committed a transgression." The weight of the mark of transgression is what deluded thought and self-grasping add to it.

### 3.3 Sealing Delusion, Binding Oneself: Transgression Binds Only One Who Holds It Real

Daosheng's gloss drives this line to its most thoroughgoing single sentence: "Sealing delusion is the person's own doing — how should that transgression have any being? One who can realize this no longer binds himself with transgression… to one who has left the realm of transgression, can transgression lay hold?"

"Sealing delusion" (封惑) — to seal up and hold fast a confusion — is, Daosheng says, something the person does to himself. The mark of transgression is firm only because the person has, with his own hands, sealed it up into a really-existent object, and then used it to bind himself. The key that releases the knot is therefore never elsewhere: "no longer binds himself with transgression." Once one sees that transgression has, from the first, no own-being, the self-tied knot loosens; "to one who has left the realm of transgression, can transgression lay hold?" — when a person has walked out of the realm of transgression that deluded thought built, what is left for transgression to seize?

This stroke of Daosheng's is of the first importance, because it returns responsibility and freedom to the person in one motion. Transgression is not some stain imposed on the person from outside that needs an outside force to wash away; it is a knot the person constructs, seals, and binds for himself. The one who can bind is oneself, and the one who can release is oneself — but "release" comes not by the will's counter-effort, but by seeing emptiness. This loops back to Kumārajīva's "the mind has nowhere to lodge": once the mind has nothing to fasten upon, the sealed-up confusion has nowhere left to be sealed, and the knot loosens of itself.

A very fine but crucial distinction is needed here. "The one who can release is oneself" sounds as if it asks the person to summon a *greater* resolve to break free — but Daosheng says precisely the opposite. "Sealing delusion" is an act of *effort*: it cost the person effort to seal that confusion firmly into a real mark of transgression, and further effort to keep clutching it. Release, then, is not adding a still greater counter-force; it is seeing that this whole sealing operation was superfluous, that what was sealed had no own-being to begin with — whereupon the hand that has been clenching all along opens of itself. "To one who has left the realm of transgression, can transgression lay hold?" — once one sees that there was never a real realm of transgression to imprison anyone, "what is left for transgression to seize?" becomes a rhetorical question: the thing that could seize never existed. This answers exactly to the ever-tightening coil of the earlier section: the coil is not wrenched open by a force pulling the other way; in the seeing that "the axis never existed," it simply loses all ground on which to turn.

The three commentators thus compose one continuous chain of reasoning: if the mind fixes the mark of transgression and reproaches it again and again, the transgression is compounded (Kumārajīva); yet transgression is itself arisen from a concourse of conditions, unfindable in the three places, with no own-being to be fixed (Sengzhao); from which it is clear that the "sealing delusion" of clutching and self-binding is wholly the person's own making, dissolved in the very seeing (Daosheng). From the psychological mechanism, to the dependently-arisen ontology, to self-binding and release — link locks into link.

### 3.4 "This Is Named Keeping the Vinaya": The True Keeper Is the One Who Knows Transgression's Emptiness

Here the chapter's great reversal arrives. Having broken the false grasp, Vimalakīrti does not stop at breaking; he lays down a positive verdict: "one who knows this is called a keeper of the Vinaya; one who knows this is called one who truly understands."

The weight of this sentence lies in its redefinition of "keeping the Vinaya" — of upholding the discipline. On the surface, Upāli, foremost in upholding the Vinaya, is the exemplary keeper; but Vimalakīrti says that the true keeper is the one who "knows this," who knows that transgression has no own-being, who does not fix its mark, who does not bind himself to it. And so a precise *paradox* emerges: at the letter of the discipline, Upāli is beyond reproach; but at the ultimate of the discipline, the foremost upholder "who could not have said it" is overtaken by the "supreme wisdom" the two precept-breaking monks have just praised.

This is the strongest possible answer to the worry about antinomianism — and the answer comes from the sūtra itself, not as an external defense. One might fear: does teaching that "transgression has no own-being" not open a back door onto "breaking the precepts doesn't matter"? The Disciples chapter's reply runs flatly in the opposite direction: the one who knows transgression's emptiness is not the one who fails to keep the discipline, but the one who truly keeps it. For he keeps the precepts not out of dread of the mark of transgression, nor does he operate his keeping as an instrument for reproaching himself or others again and again; he keeps them cleanly, keeps them with nowhere for the mind to lodge, and his observance is, if anything, the steadier for it. The core of truly keeping the Vinaya is prajñā — discerning wisdom — not fear. The letter's protective function has its own real value (the foundation of phenomenal repentance is not abolished); but to reify the mark of the rule and bind oneself with the mark of transgression is precisely to betray the liberation the Vinaya exists to serve.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Resonance: Letter and Ultimate, Refutation and Bad Faith

The scene of Vimalakīrti rebuking Upāli can be set beside several scenes in the history of Western thought, and in each the likeness comes paired with a difference that cannot be crossed. Three mirrors, near to far.

The first, and the most apt, is the tension in legal and ethical philosophy between the *letter* of a rule and the *spirit* or purpose it serves. One who strictly observes the letter of the law has not thereby realized the value the law was meant to protect; at times mechanical adherence to the wording defeats the very intent behind it. Vimalakīrti rebuking the foremost Vinaya-keeper is, structurally, just this judgment of "spirit above letter": to hold the statute of the discipline is not to hold its ultimate. **The likeness** lies here: both distinguish "literal rule-following" from "the end the rule means to accomplish," and both grant that the former may fall short of the latter. **The root difference** lies here: the law's "spirit" is still a contestable normative content requiring interpretation — it addresses the problem of how a norm is correctly applied; whereas Vimalakīrti's "ultimate" is not another, higher norm but the seeing that the *transgression the rule addresses has no own-being*. He does not replace the literal rule with a better rule; he unmasks the reified mark of transgression — the very thing the master had fixed — as unfixable in the first place. **Non-reduction:** the parallel holds only along the single structural axis of "letter and ultimate"; it is a heuristic analogy, not a theoretical equivalence. To equate the Buddhist "transgression has no own-being" with legal purposivism would lose the former's ontological claim — that transgression has no own-being — and that claim is the whole weight of the chapter.

The second mirror is the elenchus of Socrates (Socrates, 蘇格拉底). Socrates offers neither consolation nor answers; he questions relentlessly, forcing the interlocutor's supposed certainties to show that they cannot stand. Vimalakīrti, too, neither consoles nor proceeds along the master's procedure, but rebukes head-on, dismantling the presupposed premise that "the offense is certain." **The likeness:** both work by dismantling mistaken self-knowledge, both rely not on comfort but on exposure. **The root difference:** the Socratic elenchus often rests at the impasse of "knowing that one does not know" (aporia) — to bring the interlocutor to recognize his own ignorance is itself the yield; whereas Vimalakīrti's rebuke does not stop at cognitive correction but drives toward a direct seeing of the real mark, toward the lived realization of "the burden of transgression dissolves of itself," "the mind has nowhere to lodge." One halts at "you do not really know"; the other presses on to "see that it was, from the first, thus."

The third mirror, looser, is what Sartre (Sartre, 沙特) called bad faith (mauvaise foi). Daosheng's "sealing delusion, binding oneself" stands at a distance opposite the existentialist self-built cage: a person often raises, with his own hands, the prison that binds him, and then declares himself helpless within it. **The likeness:** both name binding as self-constructed, the responsibility one's own. **The root difference:** Sartre's exit is the return to authentic free choice, a taking-up of oneself anew within freedom; the Buddhist release rests not on the counter-effort of choice but on seeing emptiness — the knot is loosened not by a more resolute will but in the seeing that there was no real "realm of transgression" to begin with. To read "no longer binds himself with transgression" as an act of the will's self-liberation is to miss the point where prajñā's single cut lands.

The three mirrors together point to one thing: the singularity of Vimalakīrti's rebuke is not "spirit above letter" (this is held in common), nor "dismantling mistaken cognition" or "binding is self-made" (these too are held in common), but its gathering of all of this under a single ontological claim — transgression has, at root, no own-being; to see it is release. Without that claim, each of these mirrors is left showing only half its face.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

**❌ "Do not compound the transgression of these two monks" = "these two monks have no transgression" / breaking precepts doesn't matter.** This is the chapter's most dangerous and most common misreading. Vimalakīrti's word is *compound* (重增), not *none* (無); from beginning to end he does not deny the fact that the two monks broke the precept, nor does he sweep karmic fruit away. What he rebukes is the *added* act of "fixing the mark of transgression and pressing it further," not the *accomplished* fact of the breach. "Transgression has no own-being" says that transgression has no fixable own-being (searched out, it is wholly empty), not that it "never happened" or "need not be faced." Holding to the measure already set down across this volume: seeing emptiness never cancels conditioned cause and effect; to say transgression is unapprehendable does not conflict with bearing the karma one has made — the former loosens the knot of "my grasping at the mark of transgression," the latter faces the conditioned event itself. To let "emptiness" slip into "annihilation" (斷滅) here is to overturn the whole vehicle.

**⚠️ The Disciples chapter is the Prajñā-school's rebuke addressed to the occasion, not a liturgical procedure.** Vimalakīrti's rebuke is a teaching that the wisdom of prajñā delivers to a Vinaya-master at the fitting moment, aimed at disclosing the ultimate sense of the discipline; it is not, and should not be rewritten into, an operable "contemplate-emptiness-to-erase-transgression" procedure. To take this scene as a technical sequence of "first fix the transgression, then dissolve it by contemplating emptiness" is to fall back into the very "fixing of the mark of transgression" the scene breaks. This paper describes the mechanism at the doctrinal level only and offers no practice-level stages of method: principle and phenomenon must stand apart; the disclosure-procedure of phenomenal repentance keeps its own place, and the seeing of principial repentance neither replaces it nor is reduced to it.

**⚠️ The three commentators, kept distinct by name, must not be merged into one voice.** Kumārajīva (什曰) bears on the psychological mechanism of "fix the mark and the transgression is compounded"; Sengzhao (肇曰) on the dependent-origination argument of "formed by causes and conditions, searched out wholly empty"; Daosheng (生曰) on the liberative face of "sealing delusion and self-binding, dissolved in the seeing." The three positions are complementary but distinct, and in citation each must be returned to its own author. That the commentary is titled as compiled by Sengzhao is no warrant for gathering all three into a single man's word.

## VI. Conclusion

**The thesis, restated.** The classical scene of "transgression has no own-being" is Vimalakīrti's rebuke of Upāli, foremost in upholding the Vinaya. One sentence — "do not compound the transgression of these two monks" — discloses that what compounds the offense is not the breach itself but the reifying grasp that "fixes the mark of transgression" (Kumārajīva); that transgression is neither within, without, nor in between, because it is arisen from a concourse of conditions and, searched out, is wholly empty (Sengzhao); and that it binds only the one who consents to seal it up as real and bind himself within it (Daosheng). From this comes the chapter's positive verdict — "one who knows this is called a keeper of the Vinaya": the true keeper of the discipline is precisely the one who knows transgression's emptiness and does not bind himself to its mark.

**The closing of the character-arc.** The drama of this scene is completed by who stands in it. Upāli is foremost in upholding the Vinaya — the one who would later, at the council, recite the discipline for the whole community; the foundation of phenomenal repentance with which this volume began was laid upon a keeper of the rule like him. And here, the guardian of that foundation is himself rebuked, on the very keeping of the rule, by Vimalakīrti — the two monks' "even the foremost upholder of the Vinaya could not have said it" is the stroke that joins head to tail: by his hand phenomenal repentance was founded, and on his figure principial repentance reaches its summit. This is no disparagement of keeping the discipline; it is letting the keeping of the discipline see its own ultimate.

**A pointer for practice.** For anyone who has reproached himself over a fault again and again, breeding one error into a real mark that lodges and will not leave the heart, this chapter's reminder is: what truly disturbs you is rarely the deed itself, but your act of fixing it as real and re-indicting yourself against it. "Extinguish it directly; do not disturb the mind" — finish it, but do not feed it. This is not licence; it is the very opposite — it is letting a person out of the self-tied knot, so that he may bear what he has done cleanly and keep the precepts cleanly anew. One who can do this keeps the discipline, if anything, the more steadily: for he is not driven along by dread of the mark of transgression, but moves from the clarity of seeing. This is what "this is named keeping the Vinaya" looks like in daily use — not indifference to fault, but freedom from being bound by fault's mark.

**An open question.** Having heard the teaching, the two monks' doubt and remorse vanish at once, and they "arouse the mind of *anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi* — unsurpassed perfect awakening — making this vow: may all beings attain this discernment."[^9] They do not rest at "I am liberated"; they turn, in the same moment, to share this seeing as a vow with all beings. When the mark of transgression empties, what rises in the space it opens is, of all things, a vow turned toward beings. How, then, after the seeing that transgression has no own-being, do repentance and vow join? This thread, turning from "emptiness" toward "vow," is reserved for a later paper. Carrying the previous paper's closing words: the sun of wisdom shines once and the frost and dew are gone of themselves — and the cleared sky is there for the vow-mind to open in, unobstructed.
