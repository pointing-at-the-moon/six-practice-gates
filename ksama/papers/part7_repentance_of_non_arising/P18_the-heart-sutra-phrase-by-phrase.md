# The Heart Sūtra, Phrase by Phrase

**Where Repentance and Prajñā Finally Meet**

*中文主題：心經逐句會通——懺與般若的終會*

## Abstract

This paper reads the *Heart of the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* against the gate of repentance, phrase by phrase, and argues a single claim: from the most concrete end of the gate — the fortnightly disclosure of faults in the Vinaya — to its deepest end — the repentance of non-arising, which sees that transgression has no own-being — the whole arc of the gate turns out to be nothing other than the movement of the Heart Sūtra itself. "Clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty" is the eye of principial repentance; "all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing" is the repentance of non-arising, transgression seen to have no own-nature; "no knowledge, and no attainment" is the point at which the wisdom that repents and the transgression repented are alike unapprehendable. Repentance driven to its furthest depth is just prajñā's direct seeing; prajñā fallen back upon transgression is just the repentance of non-arising. The two meet here — and this meeting is the cashing-out, across the phrases of a single sūtra, of the one subtraction-gate among the five additions of the Six Practice Gates. The paper also takes the sūtra's own line — "because there is nothing to attain… able to remove all suffering, real, not false" — as the in-text answer to the most dangerous misreading of all: that emptiness means annihilation. Throughout, it must be stated plainly that the Heart Sūtra nowhere uses the word "repentance"; the convergence drawn here is this series' own reading, laid open for inspection, not a claim about what the sūtra says.

**Keywords:** Heart Sūtra; prajñāpāramitā; the repentance of non-arising; all dharmas marked with emptiness; neither arising nor ceasing; transgression and merit have no owner; contemplating that transgression does not arise; emptiness is not annihilation; apophatic theology; the *via negativa*; prasajya negation; the meeting of repentance and prajñā; the Six Practice Gates

## I. Laying the Whole Volume Across Two Hundred Characters

The previous paper of this part walked the six sense faculties, one by one, to the very end, and left a signpost standing: that the line "all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing" — and with it the meeting of repentance and prajñā — was reserved for the closing paper of the part; the previous paper set the marker and did not dig.[^1] This is that paper.

The gate of repentance has travelled a long road, beginning from its most concrete end. Twice each month the community gathers for the *poṣadha*, and a monk discloses before the assembly the faults he has committed: this is repentance in the Vinaya's most tangible, most countable sense. From there the road runs back through sign-taking repentance, through the death-and-rebirth seam where one precept-life is laid down and another taken up, through the blow Vimalakīrti deals in the single line "transgression has no own-being," through the contemplation of the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of the Practice of Samantabhadra* in which the six faculties dissolve, faculty by faculty, into the real mark. By now the material has changed: it is a sūtra of barely two hundred and sixty characters — the *Heart of the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra*.

What this paper sets out is not one more case-study but a map. It lays the gate's whole arc across the Heart Sūtra, phrase by phrase, to see how the two line up — to show, in other words, that the gate's entire movement from disclosure to non-arising is not something other than the sūtra's own movement, but the very same one. "Clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty" is the eye that principial repentance opens; "all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing" is the face on which transgression has no own-being to speak of; "no knowledge, and no attainment" is the step at which the wisdom that repents and the transgression repented fall empty together. Driven to its furthest depth, repentance just *is* prajñā's seeing made present; and prajñā, once it falls upon the concrete object of transgression, becomes the repentance of non-arising. This is the meeting of repentance and prajñā — and it is the one subtraction-gate, set apart from the five additions of recollection, giving, the precepts, concentration, and vow, cashed out across the phrases of a single sūtra.[^2]

One discipline must be said first. The Heart Sūtra, from beginning to end, contains not a single instance of the word "repentance." That "repentance is the Heart Sūtra's emptiness," and that the six gates map onto the sūtra's five verbs and one emptiness, are this series' own interpretive frame — a constructive reading. They are not the wording of the sūtra, and they are not the consensus of the traditional commentaries.[^3] To read the sūtra phrase by phrase here is to lay this series' reading open for inspection, not to claim that the gate was written into the sūtra all along. The opening paper of this part already announced the thesis head-on — "repentance is the Heart Sūtra's emptiness" — as its grand reveal;[^4] this paper does not re-announce it, but, as the foregoing papers walked it through one case (Vimalakīrti) and through the six faculties, walks it here phrase by phrase, and adds the argument for *how* repentance and prajñā meet.

Let the verse that is the spine of the whole essay stand first:

> All dharmas are marked with emptiness; they neither arise nor cease, are neither defiled nor pure, neither increase nor decrease.[^5]

Once "all dharmas are marked with emptiness" is landed onto the one dharma called "transgression," it becomes exactly what the repentance of non-arising sees: transgression itself neither arises nor ceases, is neither defiled nor pure. The whole phrase-by-phrase journey of this paper exists to drive that single line home.

## II. The First Half, Phrase by Phrase: The Eye That Sees, the Bridge That Crosses Over

The Heart Sūtra opens at once on seeing and on crossing over:

> When Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva was practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā, he clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty, and crossed beyond all suffering and distress.[^6]

This single opening line already touches two of the neighbouring gates.

Take "clearly saw" first. The verb "to see," to contemplate, borders on the gate of recollection: the fixing of mind and the contemplation that recollection cultivates is here pushed to its limit — "practicing the profound prajñā" — so that it is no longer a mind dwelling on some object but prajñā itself seeing the five aggregates to be empty. For repentance, this seeing eye is precisely the eye of principial repentance. Phenomenal repentance discloses before the assembly and clears the fault by formal act; it belongs to the realm of marks. Principial repentance turns back and sees that the transgression disclosed has, in the end, no own-being at all. What the sūtra's opening "clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty" supplies is exactly the eye on which principial repentance rests: not the washing-away of a transgression, but the seeing-through of the very aggregates the transgression rests upon. Only one sentence of the recollection-gate is taken here, as a neighbour; the gate is not opened in full.[^7]

Then "crossed beyond all suffering and distress." The verb "to cross over" borders on the gate of giving. Giving is letting-go, the passage from the near shore to the far; and the bridge by which the gate of repentance receives that passage was already laid in the foregoing part — giving carried to its extreme is "losing the self," and only the self once lost can a fault be disclosed lawfully, and only after lawful disclosure is the precept-essence received in purity.[^8] The sūtra's "crossed beyond all suffering and distress" names that bridge's far end: the crossing is not another ferrying you across, but that, once the five aggregates are clearly seen to be empty, suffering finds nowhere to land. The "losing the self" of the gate of repentance is, just here, the synonym of "clearly saw the five aggregates are all empty": not that a self first exists and then has its transgression cleared, but the seeing that there was never a self standing to be charged with transgression in the first place. Here too only a single sentence of the gate of giving is taken as a neighbour.

With the first half walked this far, the gate's entrance is matched: principial repentance opens its eye (clearly saw), and the self is lost in the crossing-over (crossed beyond all suffering). What follows is the heart of the paper.

## III. The Deep Dive: "Form Is Emptiness" and the Three-Source Convergence on "Neither Arising nor Ceasing"

The core of the Heart Sūtra falls between these two lines:

> Form is not different from emptiness, emptiness is not different from form; form itself is emptiness, emptiness itself is form. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise so.[^9]

> All dharmas are marked with emptiness; they neither arise nor cease, are neither defiled nor pure, neither increase nor decrease.[^10]

The Six Practice Gates each answer to one of the sūtra's five verbs and one emptiness: seeing to recollection, crossing to giving, **emptiness to repentance**, the "no" to the precepts, the ultimate to concentration, attainment to vow.[^11] Among these six, the "emptiness" to which the gate of repentance answers falls exactly upon this section — "form itself is emptiness… all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing" — which sits, precisely, on the seam between "crossing" (the gate of giving) and the "no" that the text turns to next (the gate of the precepts). The deep dive stops here, and here only; the other five gates each take a single neighbouring sentence and no more. This is not the place for a synopsis of all six gates — that is the work of the master plan and the general preface, not of this paper.

Why does the gate of repentance answer to the verb "emptiness"? Because what the gate's deepest form — the repentance of non-arising — sees is precisely "all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing," landed upon transgression. Transgression is one dharma among all dharmas. If all dharmas neither arise nor cease, then transgression neither arises nor ceases; and if it never truly arose, there is no real stain standing in wait to be removed. To "repent," then, is not to scrub away an existent blot, but to see that the blot never once arose in the manner of an own-being. That is the literal sense of the *repentance of non-arising*: what one repents is transgression's non-arising — and non-arising is just "neither arising nor ceasing."

The aptness of the correspondence shows in its position, too. This section of the sūtra falls exactly on a seam: above, it carries forward from "form itself is emptiness" through to the "crossing" of "crossed beyond all suffering and distress" (the giving side); below, it opens onto "therefore in emptiness there is no form…" and all the "no"s that follow (the side of the precepts). The verb "emptiness" is wedged between "crossing" and "no." And the place of repentance among the six gates is exactly this — it follows giving (giving carried to the loss of the self, after which disclosure becomes possible) and opens onto the precepts (the karma cleared, one is fit to receive the precept-essence). Repentance is the one and only subtraction-gate precisely because it stands at the turning where one passes from the crossing-over that still bears a mark into the markless "no": crossing is passing-over, not yet the release of grasping, and repentance is exactly that step of "having passed over, now letting the grasping go." The sūtra writes this step as the passage from "form itself is emptiness" to "all dharmas are marked with emptiness"; the gate of repentance walks it as the passage from phenomenal to principial repentance, from disclosure to non-arising. The position lines up, and so the meaning lines up.

This line, "neither arising nor ceasing," appears in three texts at once, and they form the vertebra of this paper's convergence.

First, the Heart Sūtra's own line: all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing.

Second, the contemplation-of-mind passage of the *Sūtra on the Contemplation of the Practice of Samantabhadra*:

> Such dharma-marks neither arise nor cease. What is transgression? What is merit? My own mind is empty of itself; transgression and merit have no owner… this is named repentance without the mark of transgression.[^12]

Third, the passage on contemplating transgression in Zhiyi's *Stages of the Perfection of Dhyāna*:

> Neither apprehending transgression nor non-transgression, contemplating that transgression does not arise, breaking all transgression — because the root-nature of all transgressions is empty and ever pure.[^13]

Read together, the thread is plain. The Heart Sūtra states the general principle: "all dharmas" are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing. The *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* aims that principle directly at transgression-and-merit — "what is transgression? what is merit? my own mind is empty of itself; transgression and merit have no owner" — and so earns the name "repentance without the mark of transgression." The *Stages of the Perfection of Dhyāna* names that aiming as a contemplation — "contemplating that transgression does not arise" — on the ground that "the root-nature of all transgressions is empty." Three sentences, three layers of one and the same thing: the Heart Sūtra gives the general principle of non-arising; the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* trains it on transgression and merit ("no owner," hence "without the mark"); the *Stages of the Perfection of Dhyāna* names that training a contemplation.

So the convergence stands: the repentance of non-arising is the *name* of the Heart Sūtra's "emptiness" landed upon transgression. The opening paper of this part only announced the thesis; what this paper adds is the aligning of the texts, phrase for phrase — the Heart Sūtra's "neither arising nor ceasing," the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra*'s "transgression and merit have no owner," and the *Stages of the Perfection of Dhyāna*'s "contemplating that transgression does not arise," three sources pointing at one thing. That alignment is what is genuinely new here.[^14]

One discipline must be tightened again. "Contemplating that transgression does not arise" and "transgression and merit have no owner" sound as though they cancel transgression with a stroke of the pen, but none of the three texts means anything of the kind. The line in the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* is called "repentance without the mark of transgression" only on the premise of "my own mind is empty of itself" — one first sees that the very mind that could raise a transgression is itself empty, and only then says transgression has no owner; it is not a license to commit transgression with impunity. The *Stages of the Perfection of Dhyāna* is more explicit still: "neither apprehending transgression nor non-transgression." That transgression is unapprehendable is true; but "non-transgression" — the conceit of being already innocent, of having nothing to repent — is equally unapprehendable. This is the Middle Way's double negation: it sets up neither a reified transgression nor a reified "innocence" to lean upon. What the repentance of non-arising sees is that transgression has no own-nature — not that transgression has no causation.[^15]

## IV. The Second Half, Phrase by Phrase: No Six Faculties in Emptiness, and "No Knowledge, No Attainment"

Past the core section, the Heart Sūtra strings together a run of "no"s:

> Therefore in emptiness there is no form, no feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness; no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind; no form, sound, scent, taste, touch, or dharma.[^16]

This line — "no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind" — is the prajñā floor of the previous paper's six-faculty repentance. That paper walked six gates: the eye, the ear, and the rest, each faculty disclosing in turn, each dissolving into "contemplating the real mark of all dharmas." Here the Heart Sūtra gathers those six gates into a single line — in emptiness there are no six faculties. The previous paper walked faculty by faculty; this paper draws them shut in one line. The six-faculty repentance can stand only because one has clearly seen that "in emptiness there is no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind" — not that the faculties do not exist, but that they have no own-nature to be grasped, and it is just this that leaves room for the faculties to dissolve into the real mark.[^17]

The next line drives the "no" to both ends, to the knower and to the gained:

> No suffering, origin, cessation, path; no knowledge, and no attainment.[^18]

"No knowledge, and no attainment" is the furthest step of the repentance of non-arising. The earlier lines saw that the transgression repented has no own-being (transgression does not arise); this line empties both the wisdom that repents and the fruit attained — the wisdom that repents is unapprehendable, the transgression repented is unapprehendable, and even "I have repented myself clean," held as a thing gained, is unapprehendable. Here the subtraction-gate is subtracted to its limit: grasping subtracted away until knower and known are spent together. At this point even "repentance," the act itself, is no longer set up as something with an attainment to its name.

And yet it is exactly this run of "no"s that most invites a nihilist reading. Does "no suffering, origin, cessation, path" not strike out the Four Noble Truths? Does "no knowledge, no attainment" not abolish all practice and realization? This is the highest risk this paper runs — and the highest risk run by any essay on the Heart Sūtra. The literary-historical correction must come first: this string of "no"s is the Middle Way's *prasajya* negation. What it negates is own-nature; it does not posit a thing called "emptiness." To seize "emptiness" as an existent thing to be grasped is to reify emptiness — the very inversion the sūtra most wants to break.[^19]

More to the point, the sūtra does not abandon the reader inside the "no." It sets its own guard, then and there:

> Because there is nothing to attain… ultimate nirvāṇa. The buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi… able to remove all suffering, real, not false.[^20]

Follow the line: precisely *because* there is nothing to attain, the bodhisattva's mind is without obstruction and reaches ultimate nirvāṇa; precisely because they rely on prajñā, the buddhas of the three times attain supreme awakening; and this sūtra — the most "no"-dense text in the whole canon — closes on "able to remove all suffering, real, not false." The "no" is not annihilation. The "no" is the very condition under which "removing all suffering" becomes possible at all, and the efficacy of that removal is "real, not false." This is the hardest piece of in-text support for the whole volume's firewall — that emptiness is not annihilation. The same logic lands on repentance: that the repentance of non-arising sees transgression as having no own-nature does not mean transgression may be ignored, but the opposite — *because* transgression has no own-nature, it can truly be seen through. Had transgression a fixed own-nature, solid and immovable, there would be no day of its clearing. The sun of wisdom can melt the frost and dew precisely because the frost and dew were never solid to begin with.[^21]

## V. Three Western Mirrors

To walk the Heart Sūtra phrase by phrase is, inevitably, to brush against several paths in Western thought that lie near "negation" and "the unsayable." This paper holds up three such mirrors for the sake of conceptual comparison — to bring out likeness and difference, not to praise or to blame any tradition.[^22]

The first is apophatic theology, the *via negativa*. The line of Pseudo-Dionysius and Meister Eckhart holds that the divine can be approached only by negation: God is not this, not that; whatever can be said is not it. With the Heart Sūtra's chain of "no" there is a real resonance — both walk the path of negation, both work to dismantle the mind's conceptual grip. But at the end of the negation the two part ways. Apophatic negation remains, in the last analysis, *directional*: every negation is in service of drawing near a transcendent divine ground beyond being, a God beyond being, and the more thorough the negation, the loftier the ground it indicates. The Heart Sūtra's "nothing to attain" empties even that indicated ground. It says not only "transgression cannot be apprehended" but "non-transgression cannot be apprehended either" — both ends negated, no higher reality left as a home. On repentance this difference is decisive: the repentance of non-arising does not hand transgression over to a deeper being to dissolve or to absolve; it sees that there is no substantial transgression for anyone to dissolve in the first place.

The second is the limit of language and the unsayable. Wittgenstein's *Tractatus* closes on its final proposition: of what cannot be spoken, one must remain silent — touching, likewise, the edge of what words can do. Both reach the boundary of predication; both know the real exceeds the proposition. But the Heart Sūtra does not close in silence. It ends with a mantra — *gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā* — and the four words "real, not false." Wittgenstein's silence is an epistemic withdrawal: the line once drawn, one stops at it. The sūtra's mantra is not withdrawal but prajñā's affirmative return — after the profound practice, not a stalling in wordlessness but a turning back to the actual business of "removing all suffering." After emptiness, a return to practice; and repentance is the name of that practice landed upon the concrete site of transgression. To have seen emptiness is not to have nothing left to do, but to turn back to lawful disclosure and to the ferrying of beings.

The third mirror may be set aside as the least load-bearing: mystical union, *unio mystica*. Plotinus speaks of union with the One — the soul ascends by stages and is finally merged into the One that lies beyond all. On the surface this lies near prajñā's "seeing emptiness"; in fact it runs in the opposite direction. Mystical union is union *with a higher being*; prajñā's seeing unites with no being whatever — neither increase nor decrease, nothing to attain, no greater Self into which to merge. The "losing the self" of the repentance of non-arising is therefore not the small self dissolving into a great Self, but the seeing that there was never a self there to be merged at all. (Vimalakīrti's "neither within, nor without, nor in between," treated in an earlier paper of this volume, may be cross-referenced here.)[^23]

Held up together, the three mirrors make the singularity of the Heart Sūtra — and of repentance — the clearer: its negation points to no higher reality, its closure is not silence but a return to practice, and its loss of self is not union but the seeing that there is no self to unite. Each mirror, taken alone, catches only half.

## VI. The Meeting, the Summit, and Two Heights Handed Forward

Walked to its end, the Heart Sūtra divides into two heights:

> …the mind without obstruction… ultimate nirvāṇa. The buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi…[^24]

The first height — "the bodhisattva… ultimate nirvāṇa" — borders on the gate of concentration: the quiescence that "ultimate nirvāṇa" establishes belongs to the doctrine of the concentration volume, and this paper sets only a signpost there, without digging. The second height — "the buddhas of the three times… attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi" — borders on the gate of vow, and the gate of vow is this series' sealing volume; the phrase-by-phrase placement of that great matter, "attaining supreme awakening," is left for that volume to gather, and here, too, the paper only points the direction and does not draw the material forward.[^25]

Return now to the paper's theme: the meeting of repentance and prajñā. Read the phrase-walk as a single line: clearly saw the five aggregates are all empty (the eye of principial repentance) → all dharmas are marked with emptiness, neither arising nor ceasing (transgression has no own-nature — the repentance of non-arising) → no knowledge, and no attainment (the knower and the known alike unapprehendable). Repentance pressed all the way down, to where knower and known are spent together, just *is* prajñā's seeing made present; and prajñā, the moment it falls back upon the object of transgression, shows itself as the repentance of non-arising. The two are one thing said two ways: from the side of prajñā, it is the clear seeing of all dharmas as marked with emptiness; from the side of repentance, it is the contemplating that transgression does not arise. They meet, at last, on the single line "neither arising nor ceasing." This is the meeting of repentance and prajñā — and it is the whole course by which the one subtraction-gate, among the five additions of the Six Practice Gates, is cashed out across the phrases of a single sūtra.

A last discipline must be kept — the one most easily lost at the summit: reaching the summit does not discard the ladder. Having walked the phrases to "no knowledge, and no attainment," it is all too easy for a certain illusion to arise — that if, in the end, knower and known are alike empty, then the foregoing six parts, all those formed and phenomenal repentances, were mere expedients now superseded and fit to be thrown away. It is not so. What the repentance of non-arising empties is the *grasping* at the prior five gates, not the five gates themselves.[^26] To see that transgression has no own-nature is not to be excused from disclosure, from the formal act, from the *poṣadha* — just as to see that the five aggregates are empty is not to stop eating and dressing. Principle and phenomenon stand as two faces, not a before-superseded-by-after. Principial repentance sees through to emptiness precisely so that phenomenal repentance does not harden into a regime of self-scoring; phenomenal repentance keeps its concrete form precisely so that principial repentance has ground to stand on and does not float off into mere talk. The summit of prajñā is climbed on the very rungs of phenomenal repentance set below; and from the top, looking back, the ladder is not discarded — rather, each rung at last finds its place.

As for how this whole line of reasoning answers the present age — when repentance is co-opted as a tool of psychotherapy, when disclosure degrades into a confession-style management of guilt, when "non-arising" is misused as a pretext for dodging responsibility — that is the business of this volume's next part, *Modern Diagnosis and Daily Use*. This paper closes on the prajñā face and leaves those questions to what follows; it does not draw that material forward here.[^27] This is the doctrinal summit of the whole volume — but not its sealing. Beyond the summit there is still a road to walk: the setting of this doctrine back down into daily use, to be tested there.

The map, phrase by phrase, is now laid down. A *Heart Sūtra* of barely two hundred and sixty characters, from "clearly saw that the five aggregates are all empty" to "real, not false," turns out to have been, from first to last, one more telling of the whole arc of the gate of repentance.

[^15]: This is one of the Middle-Way corrections: the repentance of non-arising sees that transgression has no *own-nature*, not that transgression has no *causation*. "Neither apprehending transgression nor non-transgression" is a double negation — setting up neither a reified transgression nor a reified "innocence" to lean upon. This continues the distinctions drawn in the earlier papers of this volume ("lessening is not erasure," "the aggravation of grave transgression is not innocence").

[^18]: *Heart Sūtra*, Taishō vol. 8, No. 251, p. 848b (T08n0251_p0848c13).

[^19]: *Prasajya* negation is a Madhyamaka term for pure negation — the negating of own-nature without positing, beyond the negation, some further affirmative entity. The "no" of "no knowledge, and no attainment" negates "an existent wisdom to be relied on, an existent attainment to be grasped"; it does not say that practice and realization are abolished.

[^21]: The image "the sun of wisdom melts the frost and dew" is from the real-mark repentance verse of the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra*: "sit upright and contemplate the real mark; the host of transgressions are like frost and dew; the sun of wisdom can dissolve them" (Taishō vol. 9, No. 277, p. 393b, T09n0277_p0393b10), a principal citation of the opening paper of this part; only one line is echoed here, not the full verse. Frost and dew were never solid, and so the sun can melt them — exactly the figure for transgression having no own-nature, and so being able to be seen through.

[^22]: The three mirrors of §V are confined to comparison of conceptual structure, with no praise or blame of any tradition. Apophatic theology's negation finally points toward a transcendent divine ground beyond being (God beyond being); the *Heart Sūtra*'s "nothing to attain" empties even that ground ("neither apprehending transgression nor non-transgression," a double negation), and is not a negation in service of some higher reality.

[^23]: This mirror may be set aside. *Unio mystica* is union with a higher being; prajñā's seeing of emptiness is union with no being at all (neither increase nor decrease, nothing to attain). The "losing the self" of the repentance of non-arising is not a merging into a great Self but the seeing that there was never a self to merge. Vimalakīrti's double negation — transgression "neither within, nor without, nor in between" — may be cross-referenced here (see the paper on Vimalakīrti in this volume).

This paper sets only forward-looking signposts at these two heights, digging at neither and drawing neither forward.

[^26]: This is the Middle-Way correction most needing to be kept at the summit: what the repentance of non-arising empties is the *grasping* at the prior five gates, not the five gates themselves. This continues the volume's earlier distinction, "climbing the ladder is not dismantling the ladder."

This paper closes on the prajñā face and hands off to what follows, without drawing that material forward.
