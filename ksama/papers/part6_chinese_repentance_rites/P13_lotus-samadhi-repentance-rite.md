# The Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite

**The Phenomenal Anatomy of a Model Contemplative Rite**

## Abstract

The previous paper stood at the door of the genre of "repentance liturgy" (*chanfa* 懺法) and made out its shape — the standard template, the skeleton of the fivefold repentance (*wuhui* 五悔), and the divide between the contemplative repentance rite and the ceremonial repentance rite. This paper walks into the first arena that paper pointed at: the *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite* (*Fahua sanmei chanyi* 法華三昧懺儀), composed by Zhiyi. Its argument is that this rite is a ten-stage scaffold of phenomenal practice (*shifa* 事法) that Zhiyi built by hand for "novice bodhisattvas who cannot yet enter deep *samādhi*": consecrating the ritual arena, purifying the body, making offerings, inviting and praising the Three Jewels, and paying homage subdue the scattered mind through phenomenal acts; at the seventh stage, the confession of the six faculties one by one — carrying the Vinaya spine of "I dare not conceal it" — together with the fivefold repentance forms the beating heart of the whole rite; circumambulation and recitation then sustain the gathered mind; and only at the end does the sequence point toward the door of seated contemplation of the real mark. That door, Zhiyi himself says, requires that one "discard the foregoing practice" — and it is kept shut until Part VII of this series. The whole value of the rite lies in the path it lays for the beginner, a path of flesh and blood that one can actually walk: phenomenal practice subdues the mind, breaks the grave sins that obstruct the Way, and so body and mind become pure and taste the savor of Dharma-joy.

**Keywords:** Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite; Zhiyi; contemplative repentance rite; the ten stages; consecrating the ritual arena; confession of the six faculties; disclosure (*facu*); "dare not conceal"; the fivefold repentance (*wuhui*); phenomenal repentance; the signs of attainment of the three grades; precepts-concentration-wisdom (the three trainings); novice bodhisattva; the model rite

## I. Walking Into the First Ritual Arena

The previous paper ended with us standing outside a door. The chapters to come, it said, would walk into the individual rites within these arenas — the Lotus Samādhi, the Golden Light, the Liang Emperor's, the Great Compassion Repentance — to look at the particulars and the depths of each rite in turn; and the door that opens onto the deepest contemplation would not be pushed open until Part VII. This paper picks up exactly there. It walks into the first arena.

Choosing the *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite* as the first stop is not arbitrary. It is the earliest and fullest living instance of what the previous paper named the "model contemplative repentance rite" — the standard template of this type, the first witness to it in black and white. It is also the concrete flesh of "phenomenal repentance grown to its high-water mark in the Chinese tradition." If a person trained in the Vinaya wishes to know what became, on Chinese soil, of those Indic bones — disclosure (*facu* 發露), concealment (*fucang* 覆藏), emergence from offense — how they grew into something one could recite day after day, this rite is the answer.

The rite marks out for itself a definite frame of space and time. The practitioner binds the boundary and sets up the altar in a quiet, secluded place; the period is thrice-seven days (twenty-one days); the practice runs through the six periods of day and night, single-mindedly and diligently.[^1] Before the formal repentance there is a stretch of "preliminary expedients" (*qian fangbian* 前方便) — Zhiyi says that all rites of repentance must first make their preliminary expedients, and that in the one-seven days before the main repentance one should first subdue the mind and still all conditioning affairs.[^2] In other words, before the repentance proper there is a period of "drawing the mind back in." One does not repent the instant the door opens; one first sets the clutter of affairs down, and only then walks in.

It is worth noting how wide the rite throws its welcome. Zhiyi states specifically that even one who has in this very body committed the five heinous acts or the four *pārājika* offenses and so lost the monk's status — if he wishes to be purified and to regain the *śramaṇa*'s discipline — should likewise practice the Lotus Samādhi single-mindedly through the thrice-seven days.[^3] This exhortation opens the door very wide: even the gravest karma is not barred at the threshold. As for the seam at which "one who has broken the precepts may, through repentance, receive the precepts anew and regain the precept-essence" — this series has laid out that ground elsewhere; here I only set a signpost and do not unfold it.

A word in passing, lest there be misunderstanding on a textual-critical point. The text we read today is the one collated and transmitted by Zunshi of the Song; in his *Preface to the Collated Original Text* (*Kanding yuanben xu* 勘定元本序), Zunshi himself plainly distinguishes a passage on the method of seated contemplation as a later "recent addition" interpolated into the main text.[^4] So, to be honest, what we hold is not the Waguan-temple autograph arriving intact, but a text that has passed through collation and transmission. Setting aside the textual-critical question, this does not affect what this paper looks at — what we look at is the skeleton of the ten stages, and the skeleton is stable.

## II. The Ten Stages as a Skeleton

Once inside the arena, the rite arranges ten things for the practitioner. Zhiyi sets them out in full: on first entering the arena one must be furnished with ten methods — first, consecrating the arena; second, purifying the body; third, the threefold-act offering; fourth, inviting the Three Jewels; fifth, praising the Three Jewels; sixth, paying homage to the Buddhas; seventh, repentance; eighth, circumambulation; ninth, reciting the *Lotus Sūtra*; tenth, contemplating the one true reality.[^5] These ten are not ten independent ritual units set side by side, but a single sequenced path. The first six stages furnish the place, the body, the offerings, the assembly of sages, the praises of the mouth, the homage of the body, one by one; the seventh stage is the heart of the repentance; the eighth and ninth — circumambulation and recitation — sustain the strength of the mind; and the tenth, "contemplating the one true reality," stands guard at the deepest place. Through the six periods of day and night, the practitioner walks this path again and again.

This skeleton carries a self-description that is easily passed over. Zhiyi notes, in his own voice, that among these ten methods some merely state the procedure to be performed, some teach how to dispose the mind in thought, and some are phrases to be recited aloud; the practitioner should take the sense well and use it, and need not recite every text.[^6] This sentence is crucial. It tells us that, from the author's own vantage, the rite was never a how-to manual of "do such-and-such at this minute," but a model whose sense may be taken, whose parts may be added to or trimmed, and into which generation after generation may walk afresh. We therefore read it as a specimen of the genre, not as a step-by-step procedure to be performed by rote — and this is precisely the weight of the word "model" that the previous paper assigned.

A word in passing as well: phenomenon and principle, in these ten stages, are not divided so that the first nine manage the phenomenal and only the tenth manages the principial. Zhiyi says plainly that in each of the six periods, in each single period, there must be no lack of either phenomenon or principle — only this is called single-minded diligence through the thrice-seven days.[^7] This is a structural fact: contemplation of principle is woven through every stage, not bolted on at the end. This paper takes only this "woven-through" structure; it does not touch the question of what, exactly, the contemplation contemplates — that is another, deeper mountain, and it belongs to Part VII.

## III. The Scaffold of Phenomenal Acts (Stages 1–6)

The first six stages are the pillars of the whole scaffold, and each is built of phenomenal act.

The first stage, consecrating the arena, shows the most deliberate care. On the high seat is placed only a copy of the *Lotus Sūtra*, with no other images mixed in; the practitioner is to make offering with his own resources to the utmost of his ability. Here Zhiyi says a hard thing: if one cannot give up one's own resources to make offering to the Great Vehicle, then in the end one will never summon the worthies and move the sages; the grave sins will not be extinguished, and how then could the *samādhi* arise?[^8] This places "giving up" (*she* 捨) squarely on the causal chain of expiation and of arousing concentration — offering is not decoration at the gate; it is the first labor of phenomenal repentance. The second stage, purifying the body: bathing, changing one's robes, keeping clean on entering and leaving. The third stage, the threefold-act offering: the body's homage, the mouth's praise, the mind's contemplation, all three set in motion together. The fourth stage, inviting the Three Jewels: Śākyamuni, Prabhūtaratna, the Buddhas of the ten directions who are his emanation-bodies, and the assembly of sages in the lineage of the *Lotus*, are invited one by one into the arena. The fifth stage, praising the Three Jewels: praise voiced aloud in verse. The sixth stage, paying homage: prostrating the five parts of the body to the ground in full reverence.

Stacked up, these six stages make a place that gathers inward layer by layer, from outer to inner: first the ground is purified, then the body, and then, by offerings, by the assembly of sages, by the mouth's praise and the body's homage, the whole body-and-mind is settled into a posture of "facing the Buddhas and the sages." By the time the practitioner opens his mouth to repent at the seventh stage, he is no longer a scattered person freshly pulled out of worldly affairs, but one whom the phenomenal acts have step by step subdued and set in place.

There is a point here that cannot be slid past. The Lotus Samādhi has an explicitly designated "lord of repentance" (*chanhui zhu* 懺悔主) — the bodhisattva Samantabhadra. The interlinear note in the liturgy says it clearly: the practitioner reverences Samantabhadra Bodhisattva with one mind; this bodhisattva is the lord of repentance of the Lotus; the practitioner should set his own mind, kneel facing this bodhisattva to confess his sins and repent and make his vows, while the other Buddhas and bodhisattvas all bear witness.[^9] What matters is this: Samantabhadra is the one revered, the one confided in, the one who witnesses — not an authority who pronounces a pardon. The extinguishing of wrongdoing turns on the practitioner's own truthful disclosure in accord with the Dharma, together with the sustaining power of the Buddhas; no sage here brings down the stamp of "absolved." The line this series has held all along — that being received is not the same as being absolved — still stands upright in this arena.

## IV. The Heart of the Rite: Confession of the Six Faculties and the Fivefold Repentance (Stage 7)

The seventh stage is where this rite truly beats. It opens by pinning down a single sentence: though the nature of karma is empty, its fruits are not lost; one who knows emptiness will not even do good, still less commit sin… therefore, precisely because he knows emptiness, the practitioner gives rise to great remorse, burns incense, scatters flowers, and discloses and repents.[^10]

This sentence is worth pausing on. It is often misread as a climax of "emptiness" — as if Zhiyi here meant to dissolve sin into emptiness. The truth is exactly the opposite. This is a brake that Zhiyi presses down with his own hand at the very heart of the rite: emptiness is not grounds for skipping repentance; emptiness is, on the contrary, grounds for deeper repentance. "Though the nature of karma is empty, its fruits are not lost" belongs to phenomenal repentance's understanding of itself — it answers the question "given all this, why repent at all once things are empty?", and the answer is: because the fruits are not lost, because remorse is the more keen. It runs in exactly the opposite direction from the no-arising repentance of Part VII, which lets repentance melt into emptiness. Here, phenomenal repentance is still the load-bearing wall.

The main body of the repentance is the confession of the six faculties one by one. Eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind — each faculty is confessed in turn. Take the eye-faculty as the specimen, to see its texture: with utmost mind I repent; the monk so-and-so, together with all sentient beings of the dharma-realm, has from countless lifetimes, through the conditioning of the eye-faculty, coveted forms… I confess this sin of the eye and dare not conceal it; may the Buddhas and bodhisattvas wash it away with the dharma-water of the wisdom-eye, that all the grave sins of the eye-faculty be ultimately purified.[^11] The confession of each faculty closes on the same formula: "I confess this particular sin and dare not conceal it."

Those four words — "dare not conceal it" (*bugan fucang* 不敢覆藏) — are the keystone finding of this paper. They are not a new coinage. They are the very flesh into which the Indic Vinaya's paired concepts of "disclosure" (*facu*) and "concealment" (*fucang*) have grown, here in the arena. The Vinaya's procedure — a monk disclosing before another, not concealing his own offense — has here been turned into liturgy: a confession that can be recited through the six periods, that can sweep through all six faculties. The same spine, in a changed space and on a changed scale: from disclosure before the saṅgha by a single person, it has grown into a long recitation made before Samantabhadra and the Buddhas of the ten directions, sweeping faculty by faculty. The disclosure and concealment discussed at the very opening of this series here meet their fullest single appearance in the Chinese repentance rite.

Beyond the confession of the six faculties, the seventh stage also seats the remaining slots of the fivefold repentance: after confession (the six-faculty confession itself) come entreaty, rejoicing, dedication, and vow. This is precisely the standard configuration of the "fivefold-repentance skeleton" named in the previous paper, the same set of components found later in Zhili's Great Compassion Repentance. Within it, the vow carries a Pure-Land dedication — the vow that, by this merit, one may be reborn in Sukhāvatī and face Amitābha.[^12] This is a signpost of the gate of vows embedded within the Lotus rite; the gate of vows itself is reserved for the volume that closes this series, and here I only note that it is present.

## V. Circumambulation, Recitation, and the Door Kept Outside

The mind of repentance accomplished, the eighth stage of circumambulation and the ninth of reciting the *Lotus Sūtra* follow on. The practitioner rises to circle and chants the sūtra aloud, sustaining and extending — through the body's circling and the mouth's recitation — the strength of mind just gathered in the repentance. Phenomenal repentance, even here, is still the affair of body, speech, and mind set in motion together.

In its section on the "signs of attainment," the rite describes the signs of the three grades: the practitioner of the lowest grade sees the sign of the purity of the moral faculty, the middle grade the sign of the purity of the meditative faculty, the highest grade the sign of the purity of the wisdom faculty. Precepts, concentration, wisdom — the ladder of attainment internal to this very rite is the sequence of the three trainings. In its treatment of the three repentances, this series pointed out that the repentances of ritual procedure, of sign-taking, and of no-arising aid, respectively, the precepts, concentration, and wisdom; the three-grade signs of the Lotus Samādhi are homologous with that line and may be brought into mutual confirmation with it. But one must add a qualification at once: homologous is not identical. The Lotus Samādhi is one type of contemplative repentance rite, and it has its own internal ladder of precepts-concentration-wisdom; this does not amount to saying that "the Lotus Samādhi *is* the three repentances." One takes the structural correspondence and stops there; the two cannot be set equal.

As for the tenth stage — contemplating the one true reality, the seated contemplation of the real mark in correct vision — this paper sets down only the name of the stage. The reason is not evasion but that Zhiyi himself draws the line here. He says: those who practice through the six periods do so to teach novice bodhisattvas who cannot yet enter deep *samādhi*; for these, one first uses phenomenal practices to subdue the mind and break the grave obstructive sins, so that body and mind become pure and taste the savor of Dharma-joy; but one who would single-mindedly abide in stillness and enter deep *samādhi* must then discard the foregoing practice and rely directly on the Course of Ease and Bliss, constantly delighting in seated meditation.[^13]

This passage is the spine of the whole rite, and it is the cleanest line between this paper and Part VII — a line that Zhiyi himself drew. The ten stages of phenomenal practice are a scaffold built for the novice who "cannot yet climb higher": it has flesh and blood, it can be walked, it breaks the grave obstructive sins, it lets one taste the savor of Dharma-joy. But from its very design it concedes that it will be surpassed — to enter that deepest door, this whole apparatus of phenomenal practice "must be discarded." What that door opens onto is a state in which repentance at last dissolves, where there is no more sign of sin left to repent. This paper does not push that door. It keeps watch outside, leaves the name here, and waits for Part VII to push it open.

## VI. Three Western Mirrors, and a Handing-On

Set this arena before three Western mirrors, and they reflect several of its facets — and also the places they cannot reach.

The first mirror is Richard Schechner's performance studies and his notion of "restored behavior," also called "twice-behaved behavior." In *Between Theater and Anthropology*, Schechner argues that ritual and performance share a core of strips of action coded to be re-performed — actions done, as he puts it, never for the first time. The ten stages are exactly such a string of coded, re-performable behavior: Zhili re-performs them centuries later, and later arenas walk them again. This confirms the shape of the "model" the previous paper named — a string of behavior one can walk into and walk again, generation after generation. But the mirror has a face it cannot show. Zhiyi says the practice is designed to be transcended — at deep *samādhi* one "must discard the foregoing practice." A rite with a built-in self-abolition is something a purely performative frame cannot reflect.

The second mirror, and the strongest, is the account given by Talal Asad and Saba Mahmood of how a disciplined body cultivates an interior. In *Genealogies of Religion*, Asad argues that in medieval monastic discipline, repeated bodily practice *forms* virtues and inner dispositions — in the sense of *habitus* — rather than expressing a belief already in place; Mahmood, in *Politics of Piety*, argues that ritual practice *cultivates* the pious interior rather than externalizing a prior inner state. This is almost a line-by-line match with Zhiyi: use phenomenal practices to subdue the mind, mobilize body and speech to aid the mental act. Remorse is not a finished thing the practitioner carries into the arena; it is grown, step by step, by the acts of consecration, offering, and prostration. This mirror lights up the very thesis of the paper: the phenomenal acts are formative, not expressive.

The third mirror is Ignatius of Loyola's *Spiritual Exercises* — a time-bounded enclosed retreat, graded by weeks, with a daily timed sequence, a director to guide, and a daily examen. It is a structural cousin of the thrice-seven days, the six periods, the ten stages, and the three-grade signs. But here two redlines must be drawn. First, the Exercises' "composition of place" — the imaginative construction of a scene in meditation — is a kind of imaginative meditation, and it is not the same thing as Lotus contemplation; the two are not to be conflated, nor may one stand in for the other. Second, the structure of absolution differs at the root: the Exercises operate within the framework of confession and absolution, whereas the Lotus Samādhi's Samantabhadra is the lord of repentance, an object of avowal and witness, not an absolving authority. The comparison takes only the institutional shape; it does not extend into soteriology.

All three mirrors are illuminating analogies, not theoretical equivalences. To read Zhiyi's phenomenal subduing as bodily discipline, the ten stages as restored behavior, the thrice-seven days as a timed enclosed retreat — each reflects a real facet. But Zhiyi's framework holds something none of them has: a scaffold built in order to be surpassed, one that will in the end be torn down by "discarding the foregoing practice." An analogy reflects a facet; it cannot replace the thing itself.

A word to close. The central finding of this paper is that the *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite* is a ten-stage scaffold of phenomenal practice built for the novice, with the six-faculty disclosure of "I dare not conceal it" as its heart — the fullest single piece of liturgical flesh that the Vinaya's disclosure ever grew into in a Chinese arena; and its far end is fixed by Zhiyi himself on the line that reads "discard the foregoing practice." The bearing on practice lies just here: phenomenal repentance is not the opposite of contemplation, nor an appendage to it. It is the only stretch of road actually walkable under the feet of one who cannot yet climb higher — first the mind is subdued, then remorse can rise, and only then is there a door to push. As for what lies behind that door, this paper deliberately does not answer.

Walking out of the first arena, there are several more waiting behind it: the Golden Light Repentance, the Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance, the Great Compassion Repentance, each with its own particulars and depths, left to the chapters to come. And the door kept outside the tenth stage — the one Zhiyi says one enters only by "discarding the foregoing practice," the no-arising repentance in which repentance at last dissolves into emptiness — will not be pushed open until Part VII.
