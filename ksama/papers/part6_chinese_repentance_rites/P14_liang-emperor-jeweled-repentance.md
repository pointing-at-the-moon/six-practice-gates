# The Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance

**The Outward Losing of the Self — The Ceremonial Repentance Rite and the Far Edge of Delivering the Dead**

*中文主題：梁皇寶懺·向外的失我——儀軌懺與度亡之極*

## Abstract

The Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance (its proper name is the *Repentance Rite of the Compassionate Ritual Arena*, *Cibei daochang chanfa* 慈悲道場懺法) takes repentance from a single inward moment of disclosure and grows it into a rite thrown wide open to the outside: it repents one's own sins, lays out the karmic fruits of the six paths, unties enmity and loosens the knot, and pays homage to the Buddhas on behalf of the six paths while making a great vow and dedicating the merit. This paper takes "the outward losing of the self" as its spine. It argues that this rite dissolves the division of foe and kin that springs from "the I-and-mine mind," and that it forms, together with the inward contemplative rite of the *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite* — which presses toward the real mark (see the previous paper) — the two poles of the Chinese repentance tradition, two poles that meet in a single event: the losing of the self. It holds the line of "received, not absolved," distinguishing the deliverance of the dead as an occasion of merit's sustaining grace rather than a transactional buying-back of guilt. This paper closes Part VI, looks back at the "bridge of dying and being reborn" laid earlier in the series, and leaves a distant signpost toward the repentance of non-arising not yet unfolded.

**Keywords:** Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance; *Repentance Rite of the Compassionate Ritual Arena*; ceremonial repentance rite; untying enmity and loosening the knot; deliverance of the dead; dedication of merit; the losing of the self; received not absolved; purgatory; contemplative repentance rite; the homage of the six paths; Chinese repentance liturgy

## I. A Serpent's Disclosure: A Modern Reader's Doorway

For a modern reader with no prior acquaintance with Buddhist repentance, the least strenuous door into the Chinese tradition of contrition may well be a story that has been told for more than a thousand years.

The story goes that the empress of Emperor Wu of Liang, Consort Chi, after her death became a great serpent, and that one night she took form and laid her plight before the emperor:

> In life I was jealous of the women of the harem… for this sin I was condemned to the body of a serpent… and so, lodged in this ugly shape, I lay myself bare before His Majesty, begging one work of merit by which I might be delivered.[^1]

The emperor consulted the eminent monk Zhigong, whose answer was: nothing will serve but to pay homage to the Buddhas and to wash it clean by sincere repentance. So the emperor gathered the words of the Buddhas, struck out the idle phrases, and "made it into a rite of repentance" — composing this repentance text; and the serpent, "by the merit His Majesty had made, was reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven."[^2]

This *Account* presses nearly the whole doctrinal weight of the rite onto the body of a single serpent. What deserves a modern reader's notice is not whether it is sober history — its authenticity is taken up below — but its grammar. No one struck the serpent's sin off with a stroke of the pen; she had first to "lay herself bare," to speak out, before the emperor's face, the very thought of "jealousy of the harem" she had carried in life. That phrase, "laying bare" (*chenlu* 陳露), is exactly the "disclosure" (*facu* 發露) parsed at the opening of this series: bringing the concealed out into the open, carrying what was in the dark into the light. In other words, even this most myth-like of stories runs, at its bones, on the same plainest grammar the Vinaya laid down — first disclose, then be cleansed.

And Consort Chi's sin was jealousy. Jealousy is a mind that sorts people into "my side" and "her side"; it is precisely the "knot" that the later chapter "Untying Enmity and Loosening the Knot" sets out to untie. So the narrative and the rite turn out to be two tellings of one thing: an empress who fell into a serpent's body through the discriminating mind, and a ten-fascicle repentance text whose whole labor, from first to last, is to dissolve that line of discrimination inch by inch. What this paper traces is the direction in which that line is dissolved — not inward and gathered, but outward and opened.

Chinese repentance has by now reached a high-water mark. The earlier paper of this part set out the two forms of the "repentance liturgy" as a genre; the previous paper went deep into one of them, the inward pole — the *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite*, which takes seated correct contemplation as its summit and draws the "self" back into the real mark. This paper walks the other pole: the Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance lets the "self" out, out into the suffering of the beings of the six paths. One gathers in, one lets out; the two look like opposite roads, yet they meet at a single point.

## II. Repentance as Untying: From a Single Moment of Disclosure to a Rite to Be Bowed and Chanted

Before entering its outward structure, one must see how this text defines "repentance." A sentence in its third chapter, "On Repentance," can almost serve as the heading of the whole book:

> In the ordinary person it is called bondage; in the sage it is called release. Bondage is the evil arisen from the three kinds of action; release is the good that is without obstruction.[^3]

The definition is worth a pause. It does not cast repentance as "confessing to someone, begging someone's pardon," but as the untying of bondage — the loosening of the knots tied by the three actions of body, speech, and mind. Here sin is not a debt that must be paid off but a knot that binds a person and leaves them "not without obstruction"; repentance is the act of untying. This step moves repentance out of the grammar of "buying back" and into the grammar of "loosening" — and that is the key to the whole apparatus of deliverance and dedication to come. Read the deliverance of the dead as the paying-off of a debt and the whole thing goes wrong; it is to be read as the untying of beings' knots.

Here an honest word about the text's provenance is needed. The current ten-fascicle version bears the attribution "compiled by the great dharma-masters of Liang," and is traditionally said to have been assembled by Emperor Wu for Consort Chi; but that tradition is itself legend. The *Account* admits it stands "several hundred years" after the Liang, and the present ten-fascicle scale has been expanded by later hands.[^4] So whenever one speaks of "the Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance," it is best to carry a qualifier — "traditionally," "said to be" — and not to assert outright that Emperor Wu composed its present form. "Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance" is in any case a colloquial name; its proper title is the *Repentance Rite of the Compassionate Ritual Arena* — where "ritual arena" means the altar of repentance.

Setting aside the matter of provenance, this text does one structurally decisive thing: it takes the one-time, spoken "disclosure" of the Vinaya and grows it into a piece of writing that can be bowed to and chanted aloud. Zhigong's gathering of the Buddhas' words and striking-out of idle phrases "to make it into a rite of repentance"[^2] is exactly what makes a "ceremonial rite" ceremonial — disclosure is no longer just a sentence one person said to one witness at one time, but a set of observances any person may carry out, over and over, at any time. The contemplative rite of the previous paper gathers repentance back into one person's seated meditation; the ceremonial rite of this paper spreads repentance out into homage that a whole assembly may perform together. Both are descendants of disclosure — one walking inward into the state of concentration, one walking outward toward the ritual arena.

## III. The Outward Losing of the Self: Showing the Fruits, Untying the Knot, and the "Also for Myself" Dedication

The spine of this paper is a "losing of the self" running in the opposite direction from the previous one. There, the losing of the self was inward: the "self" was dissolved into the real mark contemplated in seated meditation. Here, the losing of the self is outward: the "self" is dissolved into the suffering and the rescue of the beings of the six paths. To see this outward road clearly, one follows the rite's own order in three steps.

**First step: showing the fruits — stretching the field of vision from "the self" out to the six paths.** The text first spends a great deal of space laying out the causes, effects, and forms of suffering across the six paths.[^5] This is not intimidation but an expansion of view: the practitioner first sees the sins of this one body of his, and is then led to see the measureless beings of the whole round of rebirth suffering alongside him. This step is the cosmological floor beneath every later movement "for others" — if the eye holds nothing but one body's gains and losses, no mind to repent for the six paths can arise.

**Second step: untying enmity and loosening the knot — dissolving the foe-and-kin that springs from "the I-and-mine mind."** This is the pivot of the whole paper. The text names the root of enmity:

> Because of inequality, the I-and-mine mind arises; thoughts of foe and kin are born; and so enmity fills the six paths.[^6]

Note the causal chain in that sentence: first a view of "inequality," then the rising of "the I-and-mine mind," then the birth of the division "foe" and "kin," and only then can enmity fill the six paths. Foe and kin stand up at all only because there is a "self" planted at the center, taking itself as the ruler, measuring out who is near and who is far, who lovable and who hateful. To untie enmity, then, is not to "forgive some particular enemy" but to take apart that measuring-rod itself. The remedy the text gives falls exactly here:

> With a heart of compassion, holding no thought of foe or kin, equal to the heart of all Buddhas, one with the vow of all Buddhas.[^7]

"Holding no thought of foe or kin" is not re-filing everyone under the single heading of "kin" — that would still be discrimination, merely switched to the other side; it is to set down the whole pairing of "foe / kin" at once, and return to the non-discrimination of "equal to the heart of all Buddhas." What is lost in that instant is precisely the "self" that stood in the center taking the measure. So this paper calls it a losing of the self, and an outward one: the previous paper loses the self by drawing it into the real mark; this one loses the self by spreading it into beings. One tends toward emptiness, one toward universal deliverance; the landing places differ, but the thing lost is one and the same.

**Third step: dedication — and putting oneself last.** After the self is lost, the most moving stroke of this rite is the order of its dedication. In the "Homage of the Six Paths," the practitioner makes plea and homage and repentance for all beings of the six paths who have already suffered and who have not yet suffered, taking them as parents, teachers, and kin, and only at the very end does his own turn come:

> Universally, for those of the six paths who have already suffered and those who have not yet suffered, we make plea, homage, and repentance… and also for ourselves, we make plea, homage, and repentance.[^8]

The single word "also" in "also for ourselves" is the key to the whole economy of dedication: the self is not excluded but pushed behind the six paths, the parents, the kin. This is the exact reverse of a prayer-structure that begins from "first let me be saved" — it is an "outward-first" that places the self at the end of the sequence. The whole paper then closes on the making of the great vow:

> By the merit of repentance and the arousing of the mind made now, we vow universally that throughout the ten directions, to the ends of empty space…[^9]

The merit of repentance is not kept in reserve for oneself but dedicated wholesale to all things to the ends of empty space. Even the power of the vow that unfolds faculty by faculty as the vow is made "reaches widely to all beings of the four modes of birth and the six paths in the ten directions."[^10] At this point a single moment's repentance has grown into a rite of universal deliverance: it begins by repenting one's own sins and ends by vowing for all beings. This is the full reach of the ceremonial rite opening outward — not polishing the self up, but spending the self out.

## IV. Three Western Mirrors: Purgatory, Forgiveness, and Universal Deliverance

This structure of "repenting for the dead, for the six paths" is not wholly foreign to a Western reader. Three mirrors can throw back both its nearness and its distance.

**The first mirror: works of merit on the dead's behalf, and purgatory.** The French historian Jacques Le Goff, in *The Birth of Purgatory*, describes how medieval Christianity developed a notion of a "treasury of merit" and of *suffrages* — works on the dead's behalf, whereby the prayers, masses, and good deeds of the living could benefit the dead still being purged in purgatory. This is structurally a cousin of the Liang Emperor's dedicatory deliverance, in which "the living perform repentance and the dead are benefited": both hold that this world's merit can cross the boundary of life and death and reach those on the far side. But the admission stops there. What must be pushed back is this: in the Liang Emperor's scene there is no judge holding the power of pardon, no "juridical remission." That serpent's rebirth in the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven rested on two conditions, neither dispensable — her own "laying bare," and the sustaining grace of the merit the emperor made on her behalf; the merit is a supporting condition, not a ransom paid clean on her behalf. In other words, here it is always a matter of being *received*, never of being *absolved* (carrying forward the line laid down earlier in the series): no one can, on another's behalf, make a single thought of jealousy never to have happened. Repentance made on another's behalf can lend sustaining grace; it cannot strike the thing off. This is the sharpest line to hold between this paper and the imagination of purgatory.

**The second mirror: "forgiveness frees the one who forgives."** Modern psychology has a fairly influential account: forgiveness benefits, first of all, the forgiver — letting go of resentment is a way of walking oneself out of resentment's cage. The observation is not without merit; it does point to the harm resentment works on the one who holds it. But the Liang Emperor's "holding no thought of foe or kin" is alike in form and unlike in substance. "Holding no thought of foe or kin" is not a feel-better technique of emotional management but the direct contemplation of "equal to the heart of all Buddhas" — what it sets out to take apart is the very "self" that would give rise to the foe-and-kin division, not to ease that "self's" feelings. The strongest counter-evidence is the "also for ourselves" of the previous section: if the aim of untying enmity were self-interest, why would the practitioner place himself behind the six paths and all beings? A structure that puts the self at the tail of the sequence is itself the proof that this is no scheme of self-care.

**The third mirror: "we," and the vector of universal deliverance.** A prayer that leans toward individual salvation often keeps "the saving of my one soul" at its center. The Liang Emperor's homage is, from the very start, plural and outward-facing: "universally for… all beings of the four births and six paths," and only then a single clause, "also for ourselves." Its dedication does not start from the self and reach out to others; it starts from all beings and circles back to the self. This difference of vector is the plainest expression of the ceremonial rite's outwardness — the arrow of deliverance shoots outward, and the self is only the last one the light happens to catch on its way.

(The comparison here is confined to doctrinal structure and carries no verdict of higher or lower against Christianity. Purgatory and intercessory prayer have their own complete theological context; this paper takes their structural kinship as a mirror, and what the mirror shows is the Liang Emperor's rite, not theirs.)

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

The ceremonial rite and the deliverance of the dead are this paper's home ground, and for just that reason the places where one most easily slips must be marked plainly.

❌ **"Merit = an indulgence-slip / automatic deliverance."** This is the highest tension of the paper. A reader will all too easily read deliverance as a transaction: burn one rite of repentance, make one quantum of merit, and the dead are delivered automatically. But the Liang Emperor's text does not permit that reading. The serpent's rebirth in the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven was the joint fruit of two conditions — "her own laying bare" and "the sustaining grace of the emperor's merit"; lacking the serpent's own disclosure, the merit had nothing to take hold of. Deliverance is an occasion of sustaining grace, not a tradeable warrant of pardon. Every time the writing touches dedication and deliverance, it must ask itself: does this sentence imply a transactional absolution?

⚠️ **"Repenting on behalf of the dead" is not merely a funeral service.** The Liang Emperor's homage runs through both the living and the dead — it is for the dead "who have already suffered," for the living "who have not yet suffered," and "also for ourselves." To narrow it to a rite for ferrying the dead across is to miss its full dimension of being "for all beings of the six paths."

⚠️ **"Holding no thought of foe or kin" is not "forgiveness to make oneself feel better."** As the previous section distinguished, this is direct contemplation, not emotional management; the writing must not slide into a therapeutic vocabulary that turns the untying of enmity into a technique of self-consolation. This carries forward the series' standing red line against "therapeuticization, the management of guilt."

⚠️ **Be honest about provenance.** The writing must not pretend to quote Emperor Wu's original form directly; "traditionally assembled by Emperor Wu for Consort Chi" is legend, and the present ten-fascicle version has been expanded by later hands — a point that must be plainly disclosed somewhere in the text, as the second section of this paper does.

As for the one place where this paper is unusually relaxed: the Liang Emperor's rite contains nothing of "contemplating emptiness, the real mark, non-arising" anywhere in its ten fascicles — it is, by nature, the pole that does *not* press toward emptiness. The one point to hold is to keep the reader from carrying this "outward losing of the self" straight on into "losing the self is realizing emptiness." That is another road, left for what comes later.

## VI. The Two Poles Meet: Closing Part VI

**The gist.** The Liang Emperor's Jeweled Repentance takes repentance from a single inward moment of disclosure and grows it into a rite thrown wide open to the outside: first it shows the karmic fruits of the six paths to stretch the field of vision, then it unties enmity and loosens the knot to dissolve the foe-and-kin that springs from "the I-and-mine mind," and at last, with the "also for ourselves" dedication, it pushes the whole of the merit out to all beings. Together with the inward contemplative rite of the *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite*, which presses toward the real mark, it forms the two poles of the Chinese repentance tradition. One inward, one outward, they look like opposite-facing roads, yet they meet at the single point of "losing the self": one draws the self into the real mark, one spreads the self into beings; one tends toward emptiness, one toward universal deliverance — and the self that is lost is one and the same.

**A pointer for practice (not an operating manual).** What this paper takes from the Liang Emperor's rite is its structure and its vector, not its ceremonial procedure. For a practitioner, the deepest hint of this rite may lie in that single word "also" — that at repentance's depth, the work is not to scrub oneself cleaner but to put oneself at the end of the line. When the mind that pleads for others outweighs the mind that would clear oneself of guilt, the knot tied by the discriminating mind has already begun to loosen. This answers, too, the "bridge of dying and being reborn" laid down in Part IV of this series: giving loosens the grip of "what is mine" and so the self is lost, and repentance cleanses karma upon that no-self ground — the Liang Emperor's outward losing of the self is a window on that bridge, opening outward.

**An open question.** What, then, of the inward pole? The previous paper led the practitioner up to an inward door, a door which, once pushed open, requires that one "discard the foregoing practice," and which opens onto the real mark in seated correct contemplation. The contemplative rite and the ceremonial rite, one inward and one outward, look like opposite-facing roads, yet meet at the losing of the self; but what lies behind that inward door once it is pushed open, this paper does not answer — it is left for Part VII, for the deepest and hardest-to-speak of all forms of repentance.

Part VI closes here.
