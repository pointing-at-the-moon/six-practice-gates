# What *Repentance* Actually Means
***kṣama*, *deśanā*, *kaukṛtya*, and the Ground Floor of Phenomenal Repentance**

*中文主題：三詞辨義——懺、發露、悔——事懺之地基：從律藏與阿含重認「懺悔」*

**Abstract**

The English word *repentance* — and the cluster around it, *confession*, *contrition*, *penance* — arrives in any discussion of Buddhist practice already loaded. Each of these words carries Christian theological freight: sin as an offense against God, guilt at the center, declaration to an ordained intermediary, absolution granted from above. The Chinese compound 懺悔, conventionally translated by those words, is itself a stitched-together term that presses three distinct functions into a single lump: *kṣama* (Pāli *khama*) — the *receiving*, *allowing*, *bearing* of a relational axis; *deśanā* — the *declaration of a named offense* on a procedural axis; and *kaukṛtya* (Pāli *kukkucca*) — the *feeling of remorse* on a psychological axis. Drawing on the Dharmaguptaka *Vinaya* (T1428) and *Bhikṣu Prātimokṣa* (T1429), the four Chinese Āgamas (T0001 / T0026 / T0099 / T0125), and the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* (T0277), this paper restores each of the three slots to its own place and shows that the Vinaya's *deśanā* is not confession to an authority or a deity but a refusal to conceal (*not daring to cover over*) performed before the Saṅgha — a purification mechanism whose purpose is the recovery of communal eligibility, communal and transferable rather than the private processing of guilt. Three consequences follow. First, remorse is double-edged — medicine or poison depending on the practice-context — and cannot carry "repentance" on its own. Second, phenomenal repentance (*shì-chàn* 事懺) has an honest ceiling: a grave offense can be *reduced* but not *erased*. Third, Christian confession can be set beside 懺 as a mirror but never reduced to an equals sign. Phenomenal repentance is a gate, not a summit; the face on which it meets *prajñā* is reserved for a later volume.

**Keywords:** *kṣama* (懺), *deśanā* (發露), *kukkucca* / *kaukṛtya* (悔), *vippaṭisāra* (remorse), *uddhacca-kukkucca* (restlessness-and-worry), concealment (覆藏), *uposatha* (布薩), *pavāraṇā* (自恣), *pāṭidesanīya* (波羅提提舍尼), *saṅghādisesa* (僧伽婆尸沙), phenomenal repentance (事懺), purification (清淨), removal of obstruction (消障), Saṅgha (僧團)

## I. A Stitched-Together Word

You did something you regret. The feeling sits in your chest. You reach for a word for what comes next, and the word that arrives — in English, *repentance*; in the Chinese the tradition handed down, 懺悔 — sounds like a single, self-evident thing: *to feel sorry for what one has done and to seek to be forgiven*. It reads as one concept. It is, in fact, at least three.

The Chinese 懺悔 is a graft. 懺 (*chàn*) is an abbreviated transliteration of a Sanskrit word — *kṣama*, Pāli *khama*, fully written 懺摩 — whose root sense is close to *allow, bear, beg leave to be borne with*. 悔 (*huǐ*) is a sense-translation of an entirely different word — Sanskrit *kaukṛtya*, Pāli *kukkucca* — naming the inner churn of remorse and unease. Two words of separate ancestry were sewn into one compound in the course of Chinese sutra translation, and ever since, readers of 懺悔 have tended to take it as a single undifferentiated mood — *being sorry and asking pardon* — flattening in one breath three functions that originally each held their own ground.

More importantly, the compound conceals a third word entirely. In the Vinaya and the Āgamas, what actually carries the whole procedure of making good on a fault is not the feeling of remorse but *deśanā* — *disclosure*. Disclosure is not an emotion; it is an act: to *say*, before one's fellow practitioners, the specific and named offense one has committed. A person may feel no remorse whatsoever and still disclose; a person may be sleepless with remorse and never bring himself to say the thing aloud. *Feeling* is one matter, *saying* is another, and *being received* is a third.

So the work of this opening paper is an analysis that the habitual compound has hidden. We take 懺悔 for one concept; it is at least three —

- **懺 (*kṣama*)**: on the *relational* axis, *receiving, allowing, bearing with*;
- **發露 (*deśanā*)**: on the *procedural* axis, *declaring the committed fault*;
- **悔 (*kukkucca*)**: on the *psychological* axis, *the feeling of remorse*.

This three-slot division is not a later reader's imposition; it is in the texts themselves — grammatically and functionally, each term occupies its own register, as the sections below will show by citation. Reduced to its plainest formula: **remorse is *feeling*, disclosure is *saying*, *kṣama* is *receiving*.**

For a reader trained in Western languages there is one further layer of difficulty, and it is worth naming the English words directly, because the words are the problem. *Repentance*, *confession*, *contrition*, *penance* — each drags in the apparatus of the Augustinian-and-Reformation inheritance: sin construed as an offense against God, guilt at the center, declaration to an ordained intermediary, and final pardon dispensed by God. That architecture does not seat cleanly onto the Buddhist 懺; forced onto it, it distorts. The matter is large, and §IV takes it up in full.

One boundary must be drawn before beginning. 懺 has two faces. One is *phenomenal* (事) — procedural, psychological, operable. The other is *noumenal* (理) — the deepest reach of contemplative insight, where 懺 meets *prajñā*. **This paper treats only the first** — the layer the tradition would later call *shì-chàn* (事懺, phenomenal repentance) — holding to the procedural and psychological face. The second face (what later doctrine named 理懺, *lǐ-chàn*, and 無生懺, *wúshēng-chàn*, the repentance of non-arising) is the business of a dedicated chapter in a later volume; here it is only pointed to at the close, not unfolded.

## II. The Three Terms: Feeling, Speaking, Receiving

### 2.1 *Kṣama*: Receiving, on the Relational Axis

Begin with 懺. The point most easily missed is that the weight of 懺 does not fall on the *feeling* of the one repenting; it falls on the *receiving* of the one who takes the repentance. 懺 is, in other words, a relational act — it requires an addressee, a *field* to catch it. What it seeks is to be borne with, to be allowed.

The Vinaya's account of the assassin's repentance states this most clearly. The man confesses *I was foolish, I was deluded, I was unskillful*, and asks to be borne with — *may you receive this, so that in future I may guard myself*; and the one who receives the repentance answers that, since the man can see the transgression as a transgression and make amends in accordance with the Dhamma, he is received. The text then names the whole process *growth in the discipline of the noble ones*: to see the transgression as a transgression, to make amends according to the Dhamma, to undertake restraint for the future — these three beats are precisely where the "growth" lies.[^1]

The Chinese prototype of this passage is matched, with exact symmetry, in the *Pāṭaliya*-parallel of the *Madhyama Āgama*:

> 唯願瞿曇受我悔過，見罪發露，我悔過已，護不更作。……若有悔過，見罪發露，護不更作者，則長養聖法而無有失。[^2]

*May the Gotama receive my repentance: seeing the offense, I disclose it; having repented, I guard against doing it again. … Whoever repents, seeing the offense and disclosing it, and guards against doing it again — such a one nourishes the noble Dhamma without loss.*

The sequence *see the offense and disclose it → guard against repetition → nourish the noble Dhamma* is exactly the three beats above. What rewards attention is the verb 受, *receive* — *may the Gotama* **receive** *my repentance*. 懺 is 懺 precisely in that it must be *received* by another party. The root sense of *kṣama* is *can, bear, allow*, and what the receiving party performs is just this *allowing*. The Āgamas elsewhere devote a section, in the Śāriputra cluster, to the *receiving* side,[^3] confirming the same point: 懺 is not a monologue but a relation that needs someone to catch it.

### 2.2 *Deśanā*: Speaking, on the Procedural Axis

Next, 發露 — disclosure. The cleanest proof that disclosure is categorically distinct from remorse is a passage in which Ānanda speaks. The assembly suspects Ānanda of a *dukkaṭa* (a minor offense). Ānanda does not think he has in fact committed it, yet he discloses it before all of them. The Pāli is to be preserved exactly:

> *Nāhaṁ taṁ dukkaṭaṁ passāmi, api cāyasmantānaṁ saddhāya desemi taṁ dukkaṭan ti*[^4]

*I do not see this* dukkaṭa*; yet out of faith in the venerable ones, I disclose it as a* dukkaṭa.

He says it five times over. Note the structure: Ānanda has **no remorse** in his mind — he says outright that he does *not see* the offense — and yet he completes the disclosure all the same. This cuts disclosure cleanly away from remorse. Disclosure is a procedural act of speech: *to say, before one's fellow practitioners, a specific and named fault*; it does not depend on whether the feeling of remorse is present.

The operational core of disclosure is *not daring to conceal* (不敢覆藏) — not hiding, not covering over. To bring what was hidden into the open: that act of *bringing-into-the-open* is the entire substance of *deśanā*. The next section shows that the Vinaya treats this very act as the hinge on which the recovery of purity turns.

### 2.3 *Kukkucca*: Feeling, on the Psychological Axis

Last, 悔 — remorse. *Kukkucca* is a mental factor (*cetasika*), and it is morally ambiguous — wholesome or unwholesome according to context. The most common error is to take remorse as uniformly good, or uniformly bad; in fact the one word runs both directions.

In its harmful direction, remorse hardens into *uddhacca-kukkucca* (restlessness-and-worry) — the second half of the fourth of the five hindrances. In the meditative regimen of the *Satipaṭṭhāna* discourses, this *kukkucca* is an object to be *abandoned*, because it disturbs *samatha* and *vipassanā*.[^5] The Chinese welds 掉 (agitation) and 悔 (worry) into the single hindrance 掉悔 — structurally identical to the Pāli compound, which is no coincidence: excessive, ruminative remorse is classed, from the start, as a path-obstructing hindrance.

This is decisive, and it props up one of this paper's philosophical conclusions: **remorse cannot carry "repentance" on its own.** Because in the meditative setting the teaching tells you to *abandon* it, the system of 懺 needs *deśanā* (to externalize it, to say it out) and *receiving / allowing* (to dissolve it), lest the remorse fester inwardly into a hindrance. Where the watershed between remorse-as-wholesome and remorse-as-unwholesome lies, and when it is medicine and when poison, is taken up in detail in §V.

***

The three terms now separated, they collapse into a single contrast: **remorse is *feeling* (the inward churn), disclosure is *speaking* (saying the fault aloud before the assembly), *kṣama* is *receiving* (being borne with, being allowed by another party).** Three slots, three functions; lose any one, or run them together, and the whole is lost.

## III. Vinaya Disclosure: The Saṅgha's Shared Purification Machinery

With the three terms distinguished, this section concentrates on how *deśanā* actually operates in the Vinaya — and aims to establish one thing: **the Vinaya's disclosure is the Saṅgha's shared purification machinery, not the inner digestion of private guilt.**

### 3.1 The Standard Procedure of Disclosure

The disclosure formula of the Dharmaguptaka *Vinaya* writes the purpose of the mechanism plainly:

> 大德憶念！我某甲比丘，犯某甲罪。今向大德懺悔，不敢覆藏，懺悔則安樂，不懺悔不安樂。憶念犯發露，知而不覆藏，願長老憶我清淨戒身具足清淨布薩。如是三說。[^6]

*Venerable one, attend to me: I, the bhikkhu so-and-so, have committed such-and-such an offense. Now before the venerable one I make repentance; I do not dare to conceal it. To repent is ease; not to repent is unease. Mindful of the offense, I disclose it; knowing, I do not cover it over. May the elder remember me as one of pure precept-body, fit to take part in the pure* uposatha. *(Said three times thus.)*

Note where the formula lands: *may the elder remember me as fit to take part in the pure* uposatha. The purpose of disclosure is to recover *eligibility to participate in the* uposatha — that is, to return to the ranks of communal dwelling and communal practice (what may be called the *recovery of purity*). And its operational core is again that line — *I do not dare to conceal it; knowing, I do not cover it over* — the bringing-into-the-open of what was hidden. The ease of disclosure rests entirely on this *non-concealment*.

### 3.2 A Graded Ladder

Phenomenal repentance is not a single undifferentiated block; it is a graded ladder, its procedure varying with the gravity of the offense:

- **Pāṭidesanīya** (波羅提提舍尼): this entire class of offense is, by its very definition, *to be cleared by saying it out (disclosing it) before a single bhikkhu* — hence the name, "that which is to be disclosed."[^7]
- **Saṅghādisesa** (僧伽婆尸沙): graver; it requires first a *parivāsa*-and-*mānatta* probation of six nights' discipline, and is then lifted by a Saṅgha of twenty bhikkhus performing the formal *abbhāna* (rehabilitation) act.[^7]
- **Pārājika** (波羅夷): gravest; it lies beyond the upper bound of this ladder altogether — it admits of no repentance, only expulsion.

The "procedural ground floor of phenomenal repentance" *is* this ladder: from *one person speaking out before another* all the way up to *a Saṅgha of twenty rehabilitating the offender*. The gravity of the disclosed offense and the depth of the Saṅgha's involvement correspond one-to-one.

### 3.3 *Uposatha* and *Pavāraṇā*: Compulsory Periodic Disclosure

The Saṅgha does not leave disclosure to individual conscience; it builds in periodic checkpoints. The fortnightly *uposatha* recitation of the precepts opens with a round of purity-questioning:

> 若自知有犯者即應自懺悔，不犯者默然；默然者，知諸大德清淨。……乃至三問，憶念有罪而不懺悔者，得故妄語罪；故妄語者佛說障道法。若彼比丘憶念有罪，欲求清淨者，應懺悔；懺悔得安樂。[^8]

*Whoever knows himself to have an offense should repent it; one without offense remains silent. By the silence it is known that the venerable ones are pure. … Up to a third asking: one who, recalling an offense, does not repent it incurs the offense of deliberate false speech; and deliberate false speech the Buddha declared a path-obstructing dhamma. If that bhikkhu, recalling an offense, wishes for purity, he should repent; to repent is ease.*

Three rounds of questioning, and to *fail to disclose* constitutes *deliberate false speech*, which the Buddha ruled a *path-obstructing dhamma*. The Vinaya then glosses itself: an undisclosed offense obstructs the attainments from the first to the fourth *jhāna*, and from stream-entry up to *arahant*-ship; to disclose and gain ease is to gain those very fruits.[^8] This says the function of disclosure outright: **disclosure is the removal of obstruction** (the clearing of karmic obstacle). What concealment obstructs is nothing other than one's own path.

The annual *pavāraṇā* is the inverse form of the same mechanism: rather than *saying it oneself*, one *opens oneself to correction*, inviting fellow practitioners to raise what they have *seen, heard, or suspected*. The Vinaya states plainly that *pavāraṇā is itself the recitation of the precepts*.[^9] *Uposatha* is self-declaration, *pavāraṇā* is mutual correction — *yin* and *yang* of one machine, both maintaining the purity of the Saṅgha as a whole.

### 3.4 Purity Is Transferable and Lendable

The strongest proof that the purity of 懺 is *not* an inner mental state lies in two seemingly peculiar provisions. First: when the entire Saṅgha has committed the same offense, so that no pure bhikkhu remains within to receive the repentance, the monks must go elsewhere and seek purity from a *visiting bhikkhu* (客比丘) — purity can be imported from outside. Second: a monk who, through illness or the like, cannot attend the *uposatha* in person may *send word* of his "consent and purity" by proxy (the procedure called 說欲及清淨) — purity can be conveyed through another.[^10]

If purity were a purely inner state of mind, neither of these would make sense. They make sense only if purity is understood as a **relational, transferable** eligibility. This proves decisively: the 懺 of the Vinaya is the Saṅgha's *shared* mechanism — lendable from outside, conveyable by proxy — and not a private settling of guilt accomplished silently in the heart.

The Āgamas leave isomorphic evidence. The *Saṃyukta Āgama* preserves a recurring fixed formula:

> 汝今自知、自見而悔過，於未來世得具足戒……令汝善法增長，終不退滅。[^11]

*Now you, knowing for yourself and seeing for yourself, repent the offense, and in a future life will attain the full precepts … causing your wholesome states to grow and never to fall away.*

*Knowing for oneself, seeing for oneself → repenting (disclosing) → the restraint-precepts arising in a future life*: this formula recurs across different fascicles, almost as a fixed ritual unit. The repetition is itself a finding — the compilers treated *seeing the offense → disclosing → future attainment of the precepts* as a settled, reusable unit, and this is the earliest formative text of phenomenal repentance.

To close the section: disclosure is a public act that brings a hidden fault before the *field of purity*; and here, purity is the Saṅgha's shared, transferable mechanism — not a person handling his own guilt behind a closed door.

## IV. Dialogue with the West: Comparable, Not Reducible

With the three slots of 懺 and the Vinaya's mechanism clarified, the problem of translation and comparison can no longer be avoided.

The English words used to render 懺悔 — *repentance*, *confession*, *contrition*, *penance* — are nearly all steeped in the Christian framework (the Augustinian tradition plus the Reformation): sin as an offense against God, guilt at the center, declaration to an ordained intermediary, and final pardon dispensed by God. Using these words to translate 懺 drags that whole apparatus in with them and distorts 懺. What follows treats three classes of reading in turn, taking the strength of each and supplying its lack, and then draws one redline.

**First, the comparative-religion (institutional-ritual) reading** points out that the Vinaya's disclosure of offenses, the *uposatha* recitation, the *pavāraṇā*, and the *parivāsa* probation are structurally highly parallel to the Christian institution of *confession* — both have an enumerated list of clauses, both have grades of severity, both have an oral declaration, both have the restoration of standing as their outcome; the very term *pāṭidesanīya* means, literally, "that which is to be disclosed." **This deserves to be conceded:** the institutional-level parallel is real, and the vocabulary of "disclosure of offenses" is the Vinaya's own, not imported from outside; conceding the parallel, far from naïveté, shows that the comparison is not a starry-eyed claim of Buddhist uniqueness. **But the parallel holds only at the level of form, and breaks at three points.** The *addressee* differs: disclosure is made to the Saṅgha as witness, in order to be readmitted to communal dwelling — not to a priest acting *in persona Christi*, nor to a pardoning deity. The *mechanism* differs: purity is recovered through *saying-out + restraint + being-acknowledged* — not through grace cancelling a debt. The *telos* differs: what is restored is the *forward* capacity to practice — not a retrospective erasure of the past.

**Second, the Buddhist-modernist critique** charges that the Western lineage has stripped 懺 of its ritual, communal, and devotional dimensions and repackaged it as a private, psychological self-improvement — keeping the introspection and dropping the repentance-procedure (*chàn-fǎ* 懺法). **At the descriptive level this is quite accurate:** Western lay practitioners do largely meet 懺 as a private inner affair, and the traditional repentance-rites have all but vanished from view; nor is internalization entirely a loss (the *noumenal* face of 懺 need not depend on communal ritual at all). **But a clause must be added:** the phenomenal/noumenal (事/理) distinction is *tradition-internal* — it issues from a later doctrinal classification, and predates modernity — so the inward turn is not a modern foreign transplant; and the real corrective is not to *re-ritualize everything*, but to recover the whole ladder (from phenomenal to noumenal) as a single thing.

**Third, the therapeutic-culture critique** says that, in the idiom of wellness, 懺 has been rewritten as emotion-regulation — managing guilt, lowering shame, soothing oneself. **Its pastoral concern is legitimate**, and it happens to align with this volume's deliberate exclusion of self-flagellation: the tradition has never prized wallowing self-reproach, and excessive remorse (*uddhacca-kukkucca*) is itself classed as a path-obstructing hindrance. Granting this lets us cut 懺 and 悔 apart more cleanly still — *remorse* is a symptom-word, *repentance* is a practice-word. **But it must still be pushed back:** 懺 is not emotion-management but the removal of obstruction; *feeling better* is a by-product of clearing the obstruction, not the target it aims at. Once emotional relief becomes the goal, a subtler self-grasping is quietly reinstalled at the center.

**The redline:** comparable, not reducible. Confession may be held up as a mirror, to see in it the likeness and the difference of the two; it may not be used as an equals sign. 懺 is not "the Buddhist version of confession."

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section sets three guards, lest the foregoing be pushed too far.

**First, remorse is double-edged, medicine or poison by context.** Remorse must not be written off as uniformly negative, nor written up as uniformly positive. Its poisonous direction the Āgamas describe in stark terms: at the moment of death, when evils crowd in, *the mind then turns to remorse … the mind burns, the mind is wracked with regret; and the mind, having given rise to remorse, gains no wholesome state* — and even after death continues on with an unwholesome mind. This is the "law of burning."[^12] Its medicinal direction is the one already seen: *see the offense → disclose → guard against doing it again → nourish the noble Dhamma*. One and the same word, turning entirely on the practice-context in which it lands: bound to *samatha-vipassanā*, congealed into restlessness, it is a hindrance; walking the road of *seeing-the-offense, disclosing, guarding*, it is medicine.

**Second, "phenomenal repentance" and "noumenal repentance" are later doctrinal labels, not the wording of the texts.** It must be said specially: the term *shì-chàn* (事懺) used in this paper, together with the threefold division into 作法 (procedural), 取相 (sign-taking), and 無生 (non-arising) repentance, is not the native vocabulary of the Āgamas, the Vinaya, or the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* — that sūtra calls what it teaches the method of *repenting the six sense-faculties* and does not use the words 事懺 / 理懺 at all. To divide 懺 into three kinds and rank them is a later classificatory framework (a *Tiantai*-lineage schematization, whose textual placement awaits a dedicated chapter in a later volume). So wherever this paper uses "phenomenal repentance," the reader must keep in mind that this is a later classification, not a phrase of the texts themselves.

**Third, phenomenal repentance has an honest ceiling.** What phenomenal repentance can clear is obstruction at the *behavioral* level, not an *ontological* erasure. The frankest example is the repentance of King Ajātaśatru after the patricide (a deed of immediate retribution, *ānantarya*): the sūtra says that after his repentance *his transgressions were reduced, the grave fault drawn out*, yet states equally plainly that *had King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he would, on this very seat, have attained the pure Dharma-eye* — the *ānantarya* deed, though repented, still obstructs his attainment of the fruit in that seat.[^13]

> 過罪損減，已拔重咎……若阿闍世王不殺父者，即當於此座上得法眼淨。[^13]

*His transgressions reduced, the grave fault drawn out … had King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he would, on this very seat, have attained the pure Dharma-eye.*

*Reduced* and *the grave fault drawn out* are, in the end, not the same as *erased*. The *Ekottarika Āgama* has a verse — *though one commits a grave offense, repenting and not offending again … one can then draw out the very root of the offense*[^14] — and what this *drawing out of the root of the offense* draws out is the *root of recurrence* (the behavioral level), not the cancellation of the offense at the level of being. Phenomenal repentance has, faithfully, its limit — and this honesty about the limit is exactly where its dignity lies, and where it leaves a door open to a later volume.

## VI. Conclusion: Repentance Is a Gate, Not a Summit

**First, the essentials restated.** 懺悔 is not one word but three: remorse is the inward *feeling*, disclosure is the public *saying*, 懺 is the *receiving* of being borne with. And the Vinaya's disclosure is the Saṅgha's shared, transferable machinery for the removal of obstruction — this machinery is the ground floor of phenomenal repentance.

**Second, what it is worth to practice.** The distinction is intensely practical for a practitioner: do not mistake the surge of *remorse* for 懺 itself. Bottle up a charge of remorse without disclosing it, and remorse readily congeals into a hindrance that disturbs *samatha-vipassanā*; conversely, to say the fault out plainly (disclosure) and undertake restraint thereafter is far more useful than ruminative self-reproach. The force of 懺 is to be spent on *saying-out* and *guarding* — not on *feeling bad*.

**Third, an open question left standing.** Mahāyāna phenomenal repentance pushes the principle to a moving height. The *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* says:

> 向諸世尊，口自發露；既發露已，尋時即得諸佛現前三昧。[^15]

*Facing all the World-Honored Ones, with one's own mouth one discloses; and the disclosure once made, one straightway attains the samādhi of the presence of all the Buddhas.*

The fruit of disclosure turns out to be the *samādhi of beholding the Buddhas* — phenomenal repentance, then, has real depth and breadth, but it remains a **gate**, not a **summit**. For at its deepest reach, what 懺 asks is no longer *how to clear away this one offense*, but: *was the "I" who committed the error ever real?* — and this turning-point is precisely where what later tradition calls 理懺 and 無生懺 meets *prajñā*. That is the road of a dedicated chapter in a later volume; this paper stops at the gate.

To end, a line of the Vinaya for the paper's signboard: **to disclose is ease; to conceal obstructs the path** (發露則安樂，覆藏則障道). The practice of 懺 begins from this one sentence.

[^1]: The assassin's repentance and the three-beat receiving: *Theravāda Vinaya, Cūḷavagga* VII (the assassin episode), Pali Text Society edition Vin ii 180. The Pāli three beats read *accayaṁ accayato disvā / yathādhammaṁ paṭikaroti / āyatiṁ saṁvaraṁ āpajjati = vuddhi ariyassa vinaye* ("having seen the transgression as a transgression, he makes amends according to the Dhamma and undertakes restraint for the future = growth in the discipline of the noble ones"). The English rendering follows the sense, not a word-for-word translation.

[^2]: *Madhyama Āgama* (《中阿含經》), fascicle 4, *Pāṭaliya-sūtra* (Karma-correspondence chapter), Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 1, no. 0026. This three-beat — *seeing the offense and disclosing it, guarding against doing it again, nourishing the noble Dhamma* — is isomorphic with the Pāli three-beat of footnote [^1] and may be taken as its Chinese prototype.

[^3]: The *receiving* side: *Madhyama Āgama* (《中阿含經》), fascicle 5 (Śāriputra-correspondence chapter), Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 1, no. 0026.

[^4]: Ānanda's fivefold disclosure: *Theravāda Vinaya, Cūḷavagga* XI (the council of five hundred), Pali Text Society edition Vin ii 288. The wording is *Nāhaṁ taṁ dukkaṭaṁ passāmi, api cāyasmantānaṁ saddhāya desemi taṁ dukkaṭan ti* ("I do not see this *dukkaṭa*; yet out of faith in the venerable ones, I disclose it"), said five times. English rendering by sense.

[^5]: *Uddhacca-kukkucca* as the fourth hindrance: the *Satipaṭṭhāna* family (Pāli MN 10 / DN 22), where *uddhacca-kukkucca* is listed among the five hindrances as an object to be *abandoned* in *samatha-vipassanā*. Chinese parallels appear in the awakening-factor and hindrance discourses of the *Saṃyukta Āgama* (《雜阿含經》), Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 2, no. 0099, fascicle 26.

[^6]: The disclosure formula: *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* (《四分律》), fascicle 36 (Precept-recitation section), Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 22, no. 1428.

[^7]: The purity-ladder: the "cleared by disclosure" status of *pāṭidesanīya* appears in the *Dharmaguptaka Bhikṣu Prātimokṣa* (《四分僧戒本》), fascicle 1, Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 22, no. 1429; the six-night *parivāsa* of *saṅghādisesa* and the twenty-monk *abbhāna* act appear in the *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* (《四分律》), fascicle 45, Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 22, no. 1428.

[^8]: The *uposatha* purity-checkpoint and the Vinaya's self-gloss: *Dharmaguptaka Bhikṣu Prātimokṣa* (《四分僧戒本》), fascicle 1, no. 1429 (= *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* fascicle 35, no. 1428). The Vinaya's self-gloss holds that an unrepented offense obstructs the first through fourth *jhāna*, the three *samādhi*-gates (emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness), and the fruits from stream-entry to *arahant*-ship; this "emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness" refers to the three *samādhis* as fruits of purity, within the phenomenal-repentance context, and is *not* the "emptiness" of noumenal repentance — the reader should not conflate them.

[^9]: *Pavāraṇā*: the mutual correction by the three things *seen, heard, suspected*, and the statement that *pavāraṇā is itself the recitation of the precepts*, appear in the *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* (《四分律》), fascicles 37 and 38, Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 22, no. 1428.

[^10]: The lending and proxy-conveyance of purity: the whole-Saṅgha-offended case of seeking purity from a *visiting bhikkhu* appears in the *Dharmaguptaka Vinaya* (《四分律》), fascicle 36; the proxy-conveyance of "consent and purity" (說欲及清淨) appears in the *Dharmaguptaka Bhikṣu Prātimokṣa* (《四分僧戒本》), fascicle 1 (no. 1429).

[^11]: The "knowing for oneself, seeing for oneself, repenting → future arising of the restraint-precepts" fixed formula, three witnesses: *Saṃyukta Āgama* (《雜阿含經》), fascicles 21, 30, and 41, Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 2, no. 0099.

[^12]: The law of burning (the poisonous direction of remorse): *Saṃyukta Āgama* (《雜阿含經》), fascicle 47, Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 2, no. 0099.

[^13]: King Ajātaśatru's repentance — "transgressions reduced, the grave fault drawn out," while the *ānantarya* deed still obstructs: the *Sāmaññaphala* family; for the *Dīrgha Āgama* version see Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 1, no. 0001, fascicle 17.

[^14]: "Drawing out the very root of the offense": *Ekottarika Āgama* (《增一阿含經》), fascicle 5, Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 2, no. 0125. This "drawing out the root of the offense" draws out the *root of recurrence* (the behavioral level), not an ontological cancellation of the offense.

[^15]: Disclosing with one's own mouth, and the samādhi of presence: *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* (《觀普賢菩薩行法經》), fascicle 1, Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 9, no. 0277.
