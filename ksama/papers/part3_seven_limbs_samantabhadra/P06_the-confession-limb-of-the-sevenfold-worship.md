# The Confession Limb of the Sevenfold Worship
***The Same Disclosure, a Different Witness — Where Confession Sits in the Liturgy of Worship***

*中文主題：七支供之懺悔支——同一發露，異其證人*

**Abstract**

The sevenfold worship (*saptāṅga-pūjā*) lists confession as one *limb*, set after homage and offering and before rejoicing, requesting, and dedication. The question this paper asks is not *why repent* but a finer one: why is confession a *limb* in a liturgy of worship rather than a free-standing act of atonement — and where, and why, does it sit? The paper first dissects the order of the seven limbs and shows that confession is the sole *subtractive* limb among them, lodged at the hinge of a *purify-then-increase* logic. It then traces three historical strata — the Vinaya's disclosure (*deśanā*), the "three events" / *triskandhaka*, and the full sevenfold worship — to argue that the confession limb is no Mahāyāna invention but the *same* act the Vinaya already operated, re-contextualized within worship: all that changes is the *witness*, exchanged from the purified saṅgha for the present Buddhas of the ten directions (the bridge text being the "three events" of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, fascicle 7). Drawing on the *Śikṣāsamuccaya* for the doctrinal necessity of *why confession precedes practice*, and on the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession as the limb's lived liturgical instance, the paper concludes: at the sevenfold worship, phenomenal repentance reaches its devotional high-water mark, yet it remains phenomenal repentance — the deed stands as a deed, disclosure is made *to* the Buddhas, removal is sought. The emptiness-side — the principle-repentance that beholds the fault as unarisen — is reserved for this volume's Part VII; here the paper plants only a signpost.

**Keywords:** sevenfold worship (七支供), the confession limb (懺悔支), disclosure (*deśanā* / 發露), *triskandhaka* (三聚), the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession (三十五佛懺), purify-then-increase (淨而後增), the path of accumulation (資糧道), purification of obstacles (淨障), phenomenal repentance (事懺), *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (大智度論), comparative liturgics, ritualization, the earnest mind (至心), receive ≠ absolve (受 ≠ 赦)

## I. Why Confession Is a Limb, Not an Act

When the Catholic Mass opens, the congregation does not pass straight to the readings and the Eucharist. It first recites, in one voice, a confession of fault — the *Confiteor* — acknowledging before almighty God, and before the brothers and sisters present, that it has sinned in thought, in word, and in deed. This confession is not a free-standing sacrament. It is one movement within the whole act of worship: it has a fixed *place* (at the very opening), a fixed *form* (corporate, recited, formulaic), and a fixed *function* (to purify the heart before drawing near to the Liturgy of the Word and the Eucharist). A person at home in liturgy does not ask "why confess" — that is too obvious. He asks the finer question: why is confession arranged *here*, and not elsewhere?

Carry that finer question into the Buddhist tradition of worship and one arrives at this paper's subject. The Mahāyāna possesses a highly fixed structure of worship that the Chinese tradition calls the *sevenfold worship* (*saptāṅga-pūjā*, 七支供): homage, (praise,) offering, **confession**, rejoicing, requesting (which includes the request that the Buddhas remain in the world), and dedication. Here too confession is not an isolated business of atonement but one *limb* of seven. Like the *Confiteor*, it has place, form, and function. So the same fine question stands: within the seven limbs, where exactly does confession sit, and why there? What is its relation to the limbs that flank it?

This paper's answer proceeds in four steps. First, it dissects the order of the seven limbs and fixes the structural position of the confession limb (§II). Second, it traces the three-stratum history that runs from the Vinaya's disclosure to the sevenfold worship, arguing that the confession limb is the re-contextualization of an old Vinaya act rather than a Mahāyāna addition (§III). Third, it gives the doctrinal necessity of *why confession precedes practice* (§IV). Fourth, it offers the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession as the limb made into actual liturgy (§V). Finally it sets the matter beside Western liturgics and ritual studies, and closes on a boundary: phenomenal repentance reaches its summit here, and remains phenomenal repentance (§VI).

⚠️ The paper's scope must be stated at the outset. "The confession limb of the sevenfold worship" is a *structure-first* title. What the paper erects is the framework of the seven limbs; what it locates is the *position of confession within the sequence* — not the inner psychology of remorse (treated earlier in this volume), nor the deep reading of the fourth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, "repentance of karmic obstacles" (reserved for the next paper). The paper takes the *limb* (one of seven) and its place, not the vow-force structure of a single vow.

## II. The Anatomy of the Seven Limbs: Where Confession Sits

The clearest single witness to the seven limbs is the *Samantabhadra-caryā-praṇidhāna-rāja* (普賢菩薩行願讚), in the Tang rendering of Amoghavajra. The core verse of its confession limb reads:

> 我曾所作眾罪業，皆由貪欲瞋恚癡，
> 由身口意亦如是，我皆陳說於一切。
>
> *Whatever karmic faults I have committed, all arising from greed, hatred, and delusion, / born alike of body, speech, and mind — these I now declare, every one.*[^1]

The same verse, in Prajña's Tang rendering of the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* (the forty-fascicle *Gaṇḍavyūha*, "Chapter of Samantabhadra's Conduct and Vows," fascicle 40), runs:

> 我昔所造諸惡業，皆由無始貪恚癡，
> 從身語意之所生，一切我今皆懺悔。
>
> *Whatever evil deeds I have done in the past, all arising from beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion, / born of body, speech, and mind — all of them I now confess.*[^2]

Read the two renderings together and one telling correspondence emerges. The verb that closes the verse is, in Amoghavajra, "declare" (陳說); in Prajña, "confess" (懺悔) — with two extra words, "beginningless" (無始), deepening the temporal reach. "Declare" carries the sense of *deśanā* — to state, to disclose, to bring into the open; "confess" is its common idiomatic rendering. That "declare" and "confess" stand interchangeable in the same verse-slot is itself the disclosure: the core act of the confession limb is to *say the fault aloud* — declaration, disclosure — not the excavation of interior feeling. This point becomes the spine of §III.

The position of the confession limb among the seven is attested by a summary verse near the close of the same text:

> 禮拜供養及陳罪，隨喜功德及勸請，
> 我所積集諸功德，悉皆迴向於菩提。
>
> *Homage, offering, and confession of fault; rejoicing in merit, and requesting; / whatever merit I have gathered — all of it I dedicate to awakening.*[^3]

"Homage, offering, and confession of fault; rejoicing in merit, and requesting … dedicated to awakening": the order stands plain — homage and offering before, confession of fault in the middle, rejoicing, requesting, and dedication after. ⟨Whether one numbers confession third by the standard count of seven limbs, or finds slight variance in this verse's own count, the *relative* position is stable: confession follows immediately upon offering and precedes rejoicing.⟩

This position is no accidental ordering of a ritual manual; it carries structural meaning. Consider the *direction* of each limb. Homage, praise, and offering are all outward-tending, augmenting — body, speech, and mind turn toward the Buddhas and gather provisions. Rejoicing augments others' merit; requesting and the petition-to-remain pray for the Dharma's endurance; dedication turns merit toward awakening. Every one of these limbs *adds*. Confession alone *subtracts* — it adjoins nothing; it removes, it cancels, the obstacle of fault already incurred. **The confession limb is the sole subtractive limb among the seven.** And it is arranged *after* the outward gathering of merit (homage, offering) and *before* the augmentation that follows (rejoicing and the rest) — precisely at the pivot where "increase" turns to "decrease" and then back again to "increase."

Why must subtraction be inserted in the middle of addition? Because to heap up merit while the obstacle of fault remains unremoved is to pour water into a leaking vessel: the vessel being unclean and unsealed, what it holds will in the end drain away. The wisdom of the seven limbs is to draw near to the Buddhas first by homage, praise, and offering, gathering an initial store; then at once to clear the obstacle by confession, emptying the leaking vessel; and only when the vessel is clean to fill it through rejoicing, requesting, and dedication. This is the formula the paper will use throughout: **purify, then increase** (empty the vessel first, then fill it). The position of the confession limb *is* its meaning: it is the hinge-gate of "purify-then-increase" among the seven limbs.

⚠️ Two firewalls are needed here. First, the offering limb is cited only by its opening line, to mark its position; the paper does not develop the doctrine of "vast cultivation of offering" or "the offering of Dharma as supreme" — that belongs to the volume on giving (DĀNA), not here. Second, Samantabhadra's *Ten Great Vows* form a separate series whose fourth vow is precisely "repentance of karmic obstacles"; but the vow-force structure of the Ten Vows (the similes of space-exhausted, beings-exhausted, thought succeeding thought without interruption) is not the same order of thing as the *confession limb* of the sevenfold worship. The former is the deep reading of a single vow-text; the latter is one limb in a sequence of worship. The paper keeps to the *limb*; the vow-text is reserved for the next paper.

## III. The Same Disclosure, a Different Witness

§II established that the core act of the confession limb is "declaration, disclosure" (*deśanā* — to say the fault aloud). This act is no invention of the Mahāyāna tradition of worship. Peel back its provenance, layer by layer, and it proves to be one of the oldest and most load-bearing acts in the Vinaya.

Begin with the Vinaya. In the śrāvaka discipline, the monk who knows he has a fault and seeks purity does so by *disclosure* (發露) — saying the fault aloud to the saṅgha, or to a single pure monk, and not concealing it. Kumārajīva's rendering of the *Daśabhāṇavāra* prātimokṣa puts it most bluntly:

> 比丘於此中欲求清淨，憶有罪應發露，發露則安隱，不發露罪益深。
>
> *A monk who would here seek purity, recalling that he has a fault, should disclose it; disclosing it, he is at ease; not disclosing it, the fault grows deeper.*[^4]

"Disclosing it, he is at ease; not disclosing it, the fault grows deeper" — disclosure is itself the gate of purity, and concealment makes the fault grow. (The Dharmaguptaka tradition's prātimokṣa carries an almost word-for-word parallel, marking this as a cross-school commonplace.) For the concrete form of disclosure, Yijing's rendering of the *Mūlasarvāstivāda-vinaya-kṣudraka-vastu* preserves the face-to-face formula:

> 「阿離移迦存念！我苾芻某甲犯某罪，我今對阿離移迦發露說罪不覆藏，由發露故得安樂住。」……佛言：「苾芻……宜於清淨苾芻見解同者發露說罪。」
>
> *"Venerable, attend! I, the monk so-and-so, have committed such-and-such a fault; I now, before the venerable, disclose and declare the fault without concealing it, and by this disclosure abide at ease." … The Buddha said: "A monk … should disclose and declare the fault before a pure monk of like view."*[^5]

Note the structure of this formula. It calls the *witness* ("Venerable, attend" — the venerable being the pure monk who receives the confession), then self-identifies ("I, the monk so-and-so"), then states the fault ("have committed such-and-such a fault"), then discloses without concealment ("disclose and declare the fault without concealing it"). There is, within it, a *witness slot*: the Buddha is explicit that one "should disclose and declare the fault before a pure monk of like view" — the disclosure requires a pure object, of concordant view, to bear witness.

Now set this formula beside the Mahāyāna scene of worship. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (大智度論), attributed to Nāgārjuna and rendered by Kumārajīva, describes in fascicle 7 how the bodhisattva, at the six times of day and night, "constantly performs three events," the first of which is disclosure before the Buddhas of the ten directions:

> 復次，菩薩法，晝三時、夜三時常行三事：一者、清旦偏袒右肩，合掌禮十方佛，言：「我某甲若今世、若過世無量劫，身口意惡業罪，於十方現在佛前懺悔，願令滅除，不復更作。」……二者、念十方三世諸佛所行功德……隨喜勸助。三者、勸請現在十方諸佛初轉法輪，及請諸佛久住世間。
>
> *Again, the bodhisattva's practice: thrice by day and thrice by night he constantly performs three events. The first: at dawn, baring the right shoulder, palms joined, he pays homage to the Buddhas of the ten directions and says, "I, so-and-so, whatever evil karmic faults of body, speech, and mind I have committed in this life or in past lives over measureless eons — these I confess before the present Buddhas of the ten directions, praying that they be extinguished, and I shall not do them again." … The second: recalling the merits of the Buddhas of the ten directions and the three times … he rejoices and exhorts. The third: he requests the present Buddhas of the ten directions to turn the wheel of the Dharma, and asks the Buddhas to remain long in the world.*[^6]

Read the first of these "three events" slot-for-slot against the Vinaya's face-to-face disclosure, and it is almost the same mould:

- Calling the witness: Vinaya's "Venerable, attend" ↔ the *Śāstra*'s "palms joined, he pays homage to the Buddhas of the ten directions";
- Self-identification: Vinaya's "I, the monk so-and-so" ↔ the *Śāstra*'s "I, so-and-so";
- Stating the fault: Vinaya's "have committed such-and-such a fault" ↔ the *Śāstra*'s "evil karmic faults of body, speech, and mind";
- Disclosing-and-seeking-purity: Vinaya's "disclose and declare the fault without concealing it" ↔ the *Śāstra*'s "confess before the present Buddhas of the ten directions, praying that they be extinguished";
- The terminus: the *Śāstra* adds "and I shall not do them again" — precisely the self-terminating terminus of "guarding against recurrence" established earlier in this volume.

A slot-for-slot mirror. **All that changes is one thing: the witness.** The Vinaya's witness is a pure monk present before one, a present saṅgha; the *Śāstra*'s witness is "the present Buddhas of the ten directions." The same act of disclosure (*deśanā*) merely exchanges the object that bears witness — from the pure saṅgha to the Buddhas of the ten directions. This is what the paper means by *re-contextualization*: the act is unchanged; the scene has changed; the witness has changed.

From this one can draw a three-stratum history:

1. **The Vinaya disclosure stratum**: witness = the saṅgha / a pure monk (the *Daśabhāṇavāra* prātimokṣa, the Dharmaguptaka prātimokṣa, the *Kṣudraka-vastu*).
2. **The three-events / *triskandhaka* stratum**: witness = the present Buddhas of the ten directions. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 7 names this "the three events" (confession / rejoicing-and-exhorting / requesting), confession standing first. Śāntideva's *Śikṣāsamuccaya* names the same family of gatherings the "three heaps" (*triskandhaka*).
3. **The sevenfold worship stratum**: around the confession-nucleus of the three events, two prefixes (homage, offering) and two suffixes (the petition that the Buddhas remain, dedication) are added, unfolding into the full seven (the *Samantabhadra-caryā-praṇidhāna-rāja*). Confession here stands as the third limb.

Across the three strata, **confession is the stable nucleus that runs through all of them.** The three events have only three items, the seven limbs have seven, but both contain confession, and in both confession stands near the front. In other words, the sevenfold worship is nothing other than "the confession-nucleus of the three events, plus two layers of liturgical scaffolding." And the crucial shift — the witness exchanged from saṅgha to the Buddhas of the ten directions — does *not* occur when the sevenfold worship takes shape. It was already in place at the "three events / *prajñā*" stratum (the *Śāstra*'s three events are all performed "before the present Buddhas of the ten directions").

This bears out a single judgment: **the confession limb is the oldest and most load-bearing of the seven.** It is not a step newly added by the Mahāyāna to adorn its worship; it is the carrying of the plainest act of the Vinaya chapter-house — *say aloud what was hidden* — up into worship before the Buddhas of the ten directions. Continuing this volume: the *deśanā* (disclosure) line distinguished earlier is here extended, and the "guard against recurrence" terminus established earlier here recurs.

## IV. Why Confession Precedes Practice: The Doctrinal Necessity of the Limb

§II said that the confession limb stands at the gate of "purify-then-increase"; that was an argument from sequence-position. This section asks for the doctrinal ground: why must confession come *first*, and practice after? This necessity is given most completely by Śāntideva's *Śikṣāsamuccaya* (大乘集菩薩學論, in the Northern Song rendering by Dānapāla and others).

First, "confess first" is a literal command. The treatise, quoting the *Upāliparipṛcchā*, states plainly:

> 應先志誠懺十惡罪及五種愆，亦當懺悔……菩薩爾時如犯彼罪，應向三十五如來前於晝夜中一懺悔云：「我某甲，歸依佛、歸依法、歸依僧。」
>
> *One should first, with earnest sincerity, confess the ten evil deeds and the five transgressions, and likewise confess … When a bodhisattva has incurred such faults, he should, before the Thirty-Five Tathāgatas, confess once by day and night, saying, "I, so-and-so, take refuge in the Buddha, take refuge in the Dharma, take refuge in the Saṅgha."*[^7]

"One should *first*, with earnest sincerity, confess" — confession is the prefatory act, the thing to be done *first*.

Second, the purification of fault is the precondition of seeing the Buddhas and of attaining concentration. The treatise continues:

> 由罪清淨故，此諸佛世尊當現其前。……若有菩薩於此諸佛如來名號而常持念，晝夜三時轉正法行，出離彼罪得三摩地，說此是名悔過及對治行。
>
> *Because the fault is purified, these Buddha-Bhagavāns will appear before him. … If a bodhisattva constantly holds in mind the names of these Buddha-Tathāgatas, and at the three times of day and night turns the practice of the true Dharma, departing from those faults he attains samādhi; this is called the practice of repentance and of the counteracting force.*[^8]

"Because the fault is purified, the Buddhas appear"; "departing from those faults he attains samādhi" — the order is clear: purify the fault first, and the Buddhas appear; depart from the fault first, and then samādhi is attained. ⟨The content of "attaining samādhi" belongs to the volume on concentration (SAMĀDHI); the paper takes only the one line establishing the causal relation "purify-fault → attain-concentration," and does not develop the science of concentration itself.⟩ This makes the reason for confession's *primacy* fully plain: without purity there is no seeing the Buddhas, no gathering of provisions, no attaining of concentration. Confession is not the polite overture of worship; it is the causal ground of everything that follows.

Third, concealment turns roots-of-good into fault. The treatise carries a further admonition:

> ……若不發露於佛覆藏，而翻彼善說為罪性；於彼罪惱行懺除者，由此無罪而獲福喜。
>
> *… If one does not disclose but conceals before the Buddhas, it turns even what was well-spoken into the nature of fault; whereas one who, regarding that fault and affliction, practices confession-and-removal, by this becomes faultless and gains the joy of merit.*[^9]

Concealment is not merely "not-yet-purified"; it is *corrosive* — it can "turn even what was well-spoken into the nature of fault," converting what might have become good into fault. Conversely, one who discloses and removes by confession "becomes faultless and gains the joy of merit." This confirms, from the negative side, the "leaking vessel" of §II: if the vessel is unclean (concealment within), the merit poured in not only drains away but turns back into fault. So confession must clear the ground *before* the gathering of merit.

Two corrections by way of appendix, lest the reader read the material awry.

First, the "three heaps" (*triskandhaka*) have an explicit definition in the *Śikṣāsamuccaya*, to be taken from the text itself. In the "Chapter on Increasing the Force of Power" the treatise says:

> 頭面接足禮敬諸佛，則能懺除一切罪垢。三聚者，謂懺悔、勸請、隨喜福等。
>
> *Bowing head and face to the feet of the Buddhas in homage, one can confess away all defilement of fault. The three heaps are: confession, requesting, and rejoicing in merit, and the like.*[^10]

⚠️ Note: the three heaps = **confession / requesting / rejoicing**, not the often-misremembered "confession / rejoicing / dedication." Offering and dedication are listed separately by the *Śikṣāsamuccaya*, as "the modes of vast offering and dedication," *after* the three heaps; they are not within the definition of the three heaps. One must not force dedication into the three heaps when citing — that is a conflation foreign to the text. Further, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 7 uses "three *events*" (confession / rejoicing-and-exhorting / requesting); "three *heaps*" (*triskandhaka*) is the *Śikṣāsamuccaya*'s doctrinal-category term. The two belong to different strata and are best used distinctly.

Second, the line "homage → confess-away" ("Bowing head and face to the feet of the Buddhas in homage, one can confess away all defilement of fault") is the microscopic internal proof of §III's three-stratum sequence: homage first, confession hard upon it — the seam "homage → offering → confession" of the sevenfold worship is already present in miniature in this single line of the *Śikṣāsamuccaya*.

Confession before practice now has three grounds: the literal command (one should confess first), the causal precondition (purify-fault → see-the-Buddhas → attain-concentration), and the corrosion from the negative side (concealment turns good into fault). Add the treatise's two forces — the counteracting force (*pratipakṣa*, which destroys fault already incurred) and the restraining force (which guards against fault not yet incurred) — and the necessity of the confession limb's *primacy* is complete: it is the first link in the causal chain "without purity there is no gathering of merit, no seeing the Buddhas, no attaining concentration."

## V. The Limb Made Liturgy: The Thirty-Five-Buddha Confession

The first three sections argue at the level of framework; this section descends to the concrete and asks what the confession limb looks like when it becomes an actual liturgy — recitable, named, with attested fruit. The most exemplary instance is the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession. Its textual witness survives in two parallels: the *Vinaya-viniścaya-upāliparipṛcchā* (佛說決定毘尼經, Western Jin, catalogue-ascribed to "the Dunhuang Tripiṭaka-master") and the "Upāli Assembly" of the *Mahāratnakūṭa* (大寶積經·優波離會).

⚠️ A firewall first. The *Vinaya-viniścaya* and the "Upāli Assembly" share a common source, and most of their text addresses the permissions, prohibitions, observances, and transgressions of the bodhisattva precepts versus the śrāvaka precepts — the *pārājika* and *saṅghāvaśeṣa* categories, the procedures for emergence from fault, and so on. *That* face belongs to the volume on moral discipline (SĪLA), which shares the same Vinaya corpus. The paper takes only its "confession — disclosure / purification" face; the precept-mechanics of permission and prohibition are detailed in the SĪLA volume. Further, the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession is treated here as the Indian canonical source; its later Chinese ritualization (the *Lotus Samādhi* repentance rite, the *Emperor Liang* repentance, the *Water Repentance*, and the like) belongs to the Chinese liturgical manuals taken up later in this volume, and is not engaged here.

The structure of the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession may be seen in three movements: the names, the disclosure, and the return-to-purity.

First, the names — the Thirty-Five Buddhas as witnesses. The text sets the scene:

> 若有菩薩成就五無間罪……菩薩應當三十五佛邊，所犯重罪晝夜獨處至心懺悔。
>
> *If a bodhisattva has incurred the five *ānantarika* faults … the bodhisattva should, before the Thirty-Five Buddhas, in solitude by day and night, with earnest mind confess the grave faults he has committed.*[^11]

The confessant, before the names of the Thirty-Five Buddhas, in solitude by day and night, with earnest mind confesses. These Thirty-Five Buddhas are the concretization of §III's "witness exchanged from saṅgha to the Buddhas of the ten directions" — here, thirty-five named Buddhas, each named and revered one by one, bearing witness to the disclosure. ⟨The roster of the Thirty-Five amounts, by enumeration, to thirty-five Buddhas (the text itself does not number them); the *Vinaya-viniścaya* styles the last eight as "Tathāgata," the "Upāli Assembly" as "Buddha," a divergence between the two renderings, not a doctrinal difference. The widely circulated "Confession-Text of the Thirty-Five Buddhas" is a later compilation whose canonical seed is precisely these two scriptures; when citing the roster, one should note that the division follows a continuous reading of the CBETA text.⟩

Second, the disclosure — the body of confession. The text's disclosure reads:

> ……從無始生死已來，所作眾罪，若自作、若教他作、見作隨喜。若塔、若僧、若四方僧物……五無間罪……十不善道……所作罪障，或有覆藏、或不覆藏……如是等處所作罪障、今皆懺悔。諸佛世尊當證知我，當憶念我。
>
> *… Whatever faults I have committed throughout beginningless birth-and-death — whether done by myself, or caused to be done by others, or rejoiced in upon seeing them done; whether against a stūpa, against the saṅgha, against the property of the saṅgha of the four directions … the five *ānantarika* faults … the ten unwholesome courses … whatever obstacles of fault I have made, whether concealed or not concealed … all such obstacles of fault, I now confess. May the Buddha-Bhagavāns bear witness to me, may they hold me in mind.*[^12]

This passage unfolds the "disclosure" of §III to the full: the *range* of fault (the three modes — done oneself, caused to be done, rejoiced in upon seeing; stūpa-property, saṅgha-property, four-directions saṅgha-property; the five *ānantarika*, the ten unwholesome courses), and the *state* of fault ("whether concealed or not concealed"), all laid open — "all of them I now confess." Note especially the phrase "whether concealed or not concealed": it ties directly back to the *concealment* thread argued earlier in this volume — even what was hidden, even what one is not oneself sure whether one has hidden, is disclosed along with the rest. The closing line, "May the Buddha-Bhagavāns bear witness to me, may they hold me in mind," fills the witness slot: the Buddhas here are *those who bear witness*, not those who pass sentence.

Third, the return-to-purity — the fruit of disclosure. The text gives its fruit:

> 菩薩若能淨此罪已，爾時諸佛為其現身……
> 菩薩若能稱彼諸佛所有名號，常於晝夜行三事者，得離犯罪及諸憂悔并得三昧。
>
> *If the bodhisattva can purify these faults, then the Buddhas will manifest their bodies to him … / If the bodhisattva can recite all the names of those Buddhas, constantly performing the three events by day and night, he gains release from transgression and from all remorse, and attains samādhi.*[^13]

"Having purified these faults, the Buddhas manifest their bodies" — exactly corresponding to §IV's *Śikṣāsamuccaya* line, "because the fault is purified, the Buddhas appear." "Gains release from transgression and from all remorse" — the word "remorse" here (*kukkucca*, the unsettled mind of after-regret) ties back to the "remorse" distinguished earlier in this volume. (The "attains samādhi" belongs to the volume on concentration and is not developed here.)

More striking still: the *Vinaya-viniścaya* appends to this confession-rite a verse that compresses the whole sevenfold worship into a single quatrain:

> 眾罪皆懺悔，諸福盡隨喜，及請佛功德，願成無上智。
> 去來現在佛，於眾生最勝，無量功德海，歸依合掌禮。
>
> *All faults I confess; all merits I rejoice in; / and requesting the Buddhas' merit, I vow to perfect supreme wisdom. / Buddhas of past, future, and present, supreme among beings, / ocean of measureless merit — I take refuge and, palms joined, pay homage.*[^14]

"I take refuge and, palms joined, pay homage" (homage) — "all faults I confess" (confession) — "all merits I rejoice in" (rejoicing) — "and requesting the Buddhas' merit" (requesting) — "I vow to perfect supreme wisdom" (dedication to awakening): five limbs — homage, confession, rejoicing, requesting, dedication — all within this one short verse. The Thirty-Five-Buddha confession is therefore not merely a rite of atonement; it is itself a miniature sevenfold worship — and the confession limb stands at its core.

Two honest weaknesses in this section's materials (and the paper's) must be stated plainly.

First, the reader may ask: since the sevenfold sequence is so clear, why not cite some single scripture's "chapter of the seven stages" as proof? The truth is that the sevenfold sequence is *not* a single passage of any one scripture's "seven-stage chapter," but a cross-textual reconstruction: the fullest seven appears in the *Samantabhadra-caryā-praṇidhāna-rāja*; the smaller, older "three heaps" (confession / requesting / rejoicing) in the *Śikṣāsamuccaya* and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*; and "confession-precedes-practice" is reconstructed from the *Śikṣāsamuccaya*'s housing of the confession material under the chapter named *pāpaśodhana* (清淨品, "purification of faults"), together with scattered passages. The paper takes the *Caryā-praṇidhāna-rāja* as the backbone of the seven, the three events / three heaps as its older stratum, and pieces them together layer by layer — the honest course for a structure-first subject.

Second, it must be stated plainly that the Northern Song rendering of the *Bodhicaryāvatāra* (菩提行經, by Devaśāntika) is a *structurally weak* witness. (Its frontispiece falsely ascribes the verse-collection to "the noble Nāgārjuna," whereas the work is in fact Śāntideva's *Bodhicaryāvatāra*; the provenance of the ascription is tangled and warrants a separate note.) The Sanskrit *Bodhicaryāvatāra* has ten chapters, its "Confession" being the second, carrying the seven limbs / three heaps; but the Chinese *Bodhicaryāvatāra* has only eight chapters and *no* standalone confession chapter — confession is dispersed across the chapters on offering, on guarding the precepts, and on forbearance. In other words, **the seven-limb / three-heap sequence cannot be read off the internal structure of the Chinese *Bodhicaryāvatāra***. It cannot be used to prove the sequence; it can supply only two things. One is a maxim pointing to confession's *function*:

> 懺悔於業因，彼初為先導，是故於忍果，如是而得生。
>
> *Confession, with respect to the cause in karma, is the fore-leader at the outset; and so the fruit of forbearance is thereby born.*[^15]

— "the fore-leader at the outset": confession is the fore-leader of all practices, confirming §IV's "one should confess first." The other is the cadence of sixfold-time confession:

> 於一晝一夜，分之各三時，行道普懺悔，住佛菩提心。
>
> *In one day and one night, dividing each into three times, walking the path one confesses universally, abiding in the Buddha's mind of awakening.*[^16]

— sixfold-time universal confession by day and night, matching the "thrice by day, thrice by night" cadence of the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s three events. The idiom and function are taken from this text; the sequence is not.

## VI. Dialogue with the West, and a Close

*Descriptively*, the confession limb has many resemblances to confession in the Western traditions of worship; the resemblances are instructive, but each must carry the rider "yet the *telos* differs." This section sets two angles beside it — **set beside, not equated; this is not to say that any Western scholar has ever treated the sevenfold worship.** Each spoke of his own tradition; the paper merely places the two side by side to see their likeness and their difference.

### 6.1 Where Confession Sits: The Angle of Comparative Liturgics

§I's *Confiteor* at the Mass already opened this. Comparative liturgics is precisely the study of *how worship is structured*. The liturgist Gregory Dix, in *The Shape of the Liturgy* (1945), had a famous intuition: liturgy has a fixed *shape* — the sequence of acts itself bears meaning, and is not ornament to be reshuffled at will. ⟨Dix spoke of the *shape* of the Eucharist as a whole; he did not treat the penitential sub-rite, still less Buddhism. Only his methodological intuition — *sequence is meaning* — is borrowed here.⟩ This is a Western precedent for §II's claim that "the position of the confession limb is its meaning." Robert Taft's comparative liturgics carries the *ordo* method further, reading a liturgical unit by its *position and function* — which is also the method by which this paper reads the confession limb.

**What may be conceded.** The Catholic penitential act (*actus paenitentialis*) is indeed *one limb* within the flow of worship rather than a free-standing sacrament; it is recited, corporate, formulaic, and placed *before* drawing near to the Eucharist — "purify, then approach." This maps quite closely onto the confession limb's position after homage and offering, before rejoicing and dedication. Sequence does carry meaning (Dix's shape-intuition).

**Yet three points must be pressed back.**

First, ⚠️ **no priestly mediation, no decree of absolution.** The Catholic penitential act, though it does not itself absolve, sits within a sacramental economy that *includes* eventual priestly absolution. The confession limb has no absolver whatever — the confessant discloses *to* the Buddhas of the ten directions, who serve as witness and as the object of refuge, but who pass no verdict and pronounce no absolution. (This continues the "receive ≠ absolve" established earlier in this volume: the Buddhas can *receive* a confessant's disclosure, yet the purifying efficacy of confession does not come from an external decree of pardon.)

Second, **the *telos* differs.** The penitential rite tends toward *reconciliation* and *worthiness to receive the Eucharist*; the confession limb tends toward the **purification of obstacles on the path of accumulation** — clearing the obstacle of fault so that the provisions of merit and wisdom may increase, as the precursor to the "increasing" limbs. The one tends toward reconciliation with God and Church; the other toward a clear-then-increase ledger on the path of accumulation.

Third, **the frame is that of a witness, not of a tribunal.** The Buddhas of the ten directions are, in the confession limb, the *witness* of purification and the object of refuge, not judges who try and sentence. The purifying efficacy runs within the continuum of the confessant's own mind and along the trajectory of karma (continuing the "lessening / purification-of-obstacle" model established in this volume), not by a pardon handed down from without.

### 6.2 How Recited Confession Takes Effect: The Angle of Ritual Studies

The second angle puts a sharper question: a recited confession with fixed text, fixed order, and repeatability — like the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession — does it take effect by **the earnest mind** (the inner sincerity), or by **correct performance** (recited in the right position, by the fixed formula)?

Ritual studies offers three concepts. Catherine Bell's *ritualization*: ritual is an action that distinguishes itself from everyday action through strategies of formalization, repetition, fixed sequence, and invariance. Stanley Tambiah's *performative approach to ritual*: a ritual "accomplishes" something because it is *correctly performed* (his account drawing on Austin). J. L. Austin's *performative utterance*: some words "in saying are doing" (as in "I hereby declare …"), their effect resting on a set of *felicity conditions*. By these three, the Thirty-Five-Buddha confession — fixed text, fixed order, repeatable — is virtually a textbook case of ritualization and performative efficacy.

**What may be conceded.** The sevenfold worship is, descriptively, a paradigm of ritualization (formalized, invariant, fixed in sequence, recited, repeatable), and the confession limb is an act of formulaic recitation (the thirty-five Buddha-names). Its working lies *in part* in the *form* — repetition, fixed text, fixed position all do work. To deny any performative dimension is to misdescribe the practice. Bell and Tambiah voice a real truth: a recited confession is not empty simply because it is recited.

**Yet three points must be pressed back, and together they are the Buddhist middle way.**

First, **form alone is insufficient — the earnest mind is necessary.** Phenomenal repentance requires the earnest mind; recitation without it does not accomplish the purification of obstacles. So the purely performative description — "correctly recited, hence effective" — *under-describes* the Buddhist case: it is not *opus operatum* (effective by the mere doing).

Second, **nor is it pure interiority.** The *form* of recitation does real work: fixed text, the witness of the Buddhas, the position in sequence all do work. So the Buddhist position is the **middle way of form + earnest mind**, refusing both "mere ritual" and "mere mood."

Third, ⚠️ **efficacy without an absolver.** The felicitous performatives of Austin and Tambiah typically presuppose an *institutional authority* (one who can say "I hereby declare …" and make it so). In the circuit of the confession limb there is no such absolving authority — its efficacy is internal to the continuum of one's own mind, along the dimension of karma (continuing "lessening / purification-of-obstacle"), not conferred by the standing of a presiding officiant.

⚠️ The redline this section must hold above all: **the vocabulary of ritual studies must not be allowed to dissolve "the earnest mind" into "it is all performance."** Bell and Tambiah described only the formal side; their tools cannot account for the side of the earnest mind. The paper borrows their description of form, while holding, by the middle way, the earnest mind as that which may not be conceded.

### 6.3 The Middle-Way Correction

In writing this paper, the following boundaries must be held:

❌ **No equation.** The two juxtapositions above are *structural* juxtapositions, not equations (to set beside ≠ to equate). No Western scholar has ever applied his theory to the sevenfold worship; the paper uses "may be set beside," not "so-and-so treated the sevenfold worship."

❌ **Receive ≠ absolve.** The Buddhas of the ten directions are witness and object of refuge; they pass no verdict and pronounce no absolution (continuing this volume's earlier finding). In the circuit of the confession limb there is no absolver.

⚠️ **The *telos* differs.** What the confession limb seeks is the purification of obstacles on the path of accumulation — not reconciliation with God, not the remission of sin, and still less psychological closure.

❌ **No slide into the therapeutic register.** Rejoicing especially must not be read as the regulation of mood; the confession limb is not the consolation of "learning to forgive oneself," "making peace with the past," or "letting go." The paper holds to scholarly contrast.

❌ **No drift into emptiness.** Throughout, the paper holds to the level of the phenomenal and the structural. The deeper face of confession — the principle-repentance (the fault as empty by nature, the repentance of non-arising) — is reserved for the very end of this volume (Part VII); here a signpost is planted only, its meaning not unfolded.

### Closing

At the sevenfold worship, confession has passed from a single disclosure in the Vinaya chapter-house into a liturgy of worship before the Buddhas of the ten directions, performed six times by day and night — more solemn, more colored by the devotion of worship. Yet it remains the same act: *to say aloud what was hidden*. Within the seven limbs it abides at the gate of "purify-then-increase," emptying the leaking vessel first and filling it after. Earlier in this volume the floor of phenomenal repentance was sounded upon the gravest karma — it can lessen, but cannot wholly cleanse; here, at the summit of worship, the *ceiling* of phenomenal repentance comes into view: even at the solemn high-water mark of the sevenfold worship, what it beholds is still, every time, a real karmic fault, a real self that seeks repentance, disclosing to the Buddhas, seeking removal.

It is still phenomenal repentance.

As for the face of principle-repentance — the fault as empty by nature, the repentance of non-arising — that is reserved for the end of this volume: a matter of another order, here marked by a signpost only.
