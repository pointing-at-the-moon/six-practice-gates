# Samantabhadra's Confession of Karmic Obstacles
***From a Limb to a Vow — The Aspirational Structure of the Fourth Vow***

*中文主題：普賢懺悔業障——從「一支」到「一願」：第四願的願力結構*

**Abstract**

At the fourth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, confession reaches the high-water mark of its aspirational form: it rises from a *limb* of the sevenfold worship (*saptāṅga-pūjā*, 七支供) — a single, completed liturgical act — to a *vow* (*praṇidhāna*, 願) among the Ten, loaded onto the vow-form chassis of *fourfold inexhaustibility* (四喻無盡) plus *moment-to-moment continuity without interruption* (念念相續無有間斷). Reading the long-prose of the fourth vow in the *Bhadracarī* ("Chapter of Samantabhadra's Conduct and Vows," forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, fascicle 40), the paper turns on a structural asymmetry between the saptāṅga source-verses and the long-prose vow: the *verses* halt at a completed act and carry no inexhaustibility clause, whereas only the *long-prose* vow carries the four exhaustion-similes. Once confession is posited as a *vow*, it inherits the vow-form's inexhaustible chassis; so phenomenal repentance (事懺) turns from "a single point of disclosure" into "a line stretching to the end of future time, unbroken, without weariness." The paper then argues: the four exhaustion-similes are a *logical construction* (they set a termination-condition that can never obtain); moment-to-moment continuity is a continuity *established by Samantabhadra's contemplative practice* (觀行), not the grinding of willpower; the object of the karmic obstacle is a cause→manifestation→effect generative chain ("from greed, hatred, and delusion arising in body, speech, and mind"), not a substance; and the mechanism of confession is a *redirect* ("henceforth not committing it again, abiding ever in pure precepts"), not an erasure. Setting the matter beside Kant, Levinas, the myth of Sisyphus, and religious scrupulosity, the paper shows that the inexhaustibility of this vow rests on *the boundlessness of the object aspired to*, not on "I am never enough"; the text's "without weariness" (無有疲厭) positively refutes the futility-and-anxiety reading. Throughout, the paper keeps strictly to phenomenal repentance; the principial side — beholding the very nature of sin as empty — is reserved, on Zongmi's own statement that the fourth vow "omits the contemplation of sin's emptiness" because emptiness is its presupposed ground, for the end of this volume.

**Keywords:** the *Bhadracarī* (普賢行願), confession of karmic obstacles (懺悔業障), the fourth vow (第四願), the four exhaustion-similes (四喻無盡), moment-to-moment continuity (念念相續), aspirational structure (願力結構), phenomenal repentance (事懺), the karmic obstacle (業障), redirect not erasure (轉向非抹除), without weariness (無有疲厭), Chengguan (澄觀), Zongmi (宗密)

## I. From a Limb to a Vow

The previous paper read confession as one *limb* of the sevenfold worship — not a free-standing rite, but a position within the worship-sequence of homage, praise, offering, confession, and rejoicing, ending at a completed act. This paper asks after the other face of that same confession: the *same* confession — why is it also a *vow*?

Set the two faces side by side and an asymmetry appears, and it is precisely this asymmetry that is the paper's problem. The same body of *Bhadracarī* material gives rise to two structures. One is verse. Whether in Amoghavajra's *Samantabhadra-caryā-praṇidhāna-rāja*, 「我曾所作眾罪業，皆由貪欲瞋恚癡，由身口意亦如是，我皆陳說於一切」[^1] ("Whatever karmic faults I have committed, all from greed, hatred, and delusion, born alike of body, speech, and mind — these I declare, every one"), or in the closing reprise-gāthā of Prajña's rendering, 「我昔所造諸惡業，皆由無始貪恚癡，從身語意之所生，一切我今皆懺悔」[^2] ("Whatever evil deeds I have done in the past, all from beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion, born of body, speech, and mind — all of them I now confess"), confession falls within the saptāṅga sequence as an act said and done — 「我皆陳說」 ("I declare, every one"), 「我今皆懺悔」 ("all of them I now confess") — perfective, finished, ending there. The other is long prose. In fascicle 40 of the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, "Chapter of Samantabhadra's Conduct and Vows," confession is the fourth of the Ten Great Vows, and the close of its vow-text carries a sentence the verses do not have at all:

> 我此懺悔無有窮盡，念念相續，無有間斷，身、語、意業無有疲厭。[^3]
>
> *This confession of mine has no exhaustion; moment after moment it continues, without interruption; in the karmic acts of body, speech, and mind there is no weariness.*

The load-bearing evidence is exactly here: **the verses carry no inexhaustibility clause; only the long-prose vow does.** The same confession, in the form of a *limb*, halts at completion; in the form of a *vow*, is pushed toward "no exhaustion." The difference is not that confession has changed its content, but that it has been set into a different *structure* — once it passes from a limb among the seven to a vow among the Ten, it inherits the chassis of the vow-form. This is the exact sense of "from a limb to a vow": not that confession has grown larger, but that confession has been cast into the *temporal structure of a vow*.

This confession, posited as "never completed," at once invites a suspicion. Does a confession that claims to be "moment after moment, without interruption" not look like Sisyphus's boulder that never reaches the summit? Or like the religious scrupulant's compulsion that "can never be washed clean" — done and done again, with no day of finishing? The suspicion deserves to be taken seriously, for it strikes at exactly the point where "inexhaustibility" is most easily misread. The paper's answer unfolds in three steps: first, what the fourth vow's text actually confesses (what the karmic obstacle is) and how it confesses (redirect, not erasure); next, how the two vow-force components — "the four exhaustion-similes" and "moment-to-moment continuity" — turn confession *from a point into a line*; finally, a return to the suspicion, to show that the inexhaustibility of this vow rests on *the boundlessness of the object aspired to*, and not on "this person of mine is never enough" — a distinction that decides whether it is futility, or a bodhi-vow that generates.

## II. The Text of the Fourth Vow: What the Karmic Obstacle Is

Read the fourth vow's text once through in full, for every component of the argument is inside it:

> 復次，善男子！言懺除業障者：菩薩自念我於過去無始劫中，由貪、瞋、癡發身、口、意，作諸惡業無量無邊。若此惡業有體相者，盡虛空界不能容受。我今悉以清淨三業，遍於法界極微塵剎一切諸佛菩薩眾前，誠心懺悔，後不復造，恒住淨戒一切功德。如是虛空界盡、眾生界盡、眾生業盡、眾生煩惱盡，我懺乃盡。而虛空界乃至眾生煩惱不可盡故，我此懺悔無有窮盡，念念相續，無有間斷，身、語、意業無有疲厭。[^3]
>
> *Again, good son! As for confessing-and-removing the karmic obstacle: the bodhisattva reflects, "Throughout the past, beginningless eons, by greed, hatred, and delusion arising in body, speech, and mind, I have done evil deeds measureless and boundless. Had these evil deeds a bodily form, all of empty space could not contain them. I now, with the three karmas made pure, before all the Buddhas and bodhisattvas in every dust-mote land throughout the dharma-realm, with earnest mind confess, henceforth not committing them again, abiding ever in the pure precepts and all their merit." Thus: were the realm of space exhausted, the realm of beings exhausted, the karma of beings exhausted, the afflictions of beings exhausted — then my confession would be exhausted. But because the realm of space, and so on down to the afflictions of beings, cannot be exhausted, this confession of mine has no exhaustion; moment after moment it continues, without interruption; in the karmic acts of body, speech, and mind there is no weariness.*

A difference of *name and substance* deserves to be noted first. The heading of the Ten Great Vows reads 「四者、懺**悔**業障」 ("the fourth: confessing-*and-repenting* the karmic obstacle"), while the opening of the long prose here reads 「言懺**除**業障者」 ("as for confessing-*and-removing* the karmic obstacle") — 悔 ("repent") and 除 ("remove") both appearing in the same fascicle, the same vow, the same Prajña rendering, and not a scribal error.[^4] The heading marks, by 悔, the *heart's turning*; the long prose marks, by 除, the *obstacle's dissolution*; the two are not synonymous variants but two sides of one vow, and so each citation must record the character as it stands and not be normalized to a single spelling. Where below the heading is cited it reads "confess-and-repent," where the long prose is cited it reads "confess-and-remove" — for just this reason.

The text first defines *what the karmic obstacle is*. It does not treat the obstacle as a ready-made, substantial thing to be hauled away; it describes a *generative chain*: 「由貪、瞋、癡」 (from the three poisons, *rāga-dveṣa-moha*) is the *cause*; 「發身、口、意」 (the arising of the three karmas of body, speech, and mind) is the *manifestation*; 「作諸惡業無量無邊」 (deeds measureless and boundless) is the *effect*. The karmic obstacle is therefore not a stone lodged in the way, but a process continually produced, from cause to manifestation to effect.[^5] The line that follows — 「若此惡業有體相者，盡虛空界不能容受」 ("had these evil deeds a bodily form, all of empty space could not contain them") — is the most easily misread and needs special calibration: it is a sentence in the *counterfactual* mood — 「若……有體相」 ("had they a bodily form"), the premise being that karma has no bodily form; it states the *sheer quantity* (so vast that space itself could not hold it), a hyperbole proper to phenomenal repentance for the gravity of fault, and *not* an ontological assertion that "karma has self-nature," still less, reversed, that "karma is without self-nature and therefore empty." In other words, this line belongs to the hyperbole of *quantity*, not to a verdict on *nature*.

Only with the object understood can the remedy be understood. The way the text counters the karmic obstacle is not to declare it cancelled at a stroke, but two turnings that arise together. The first is 「悉以清淨三業……誠心懺悔」 ("with the three karmas made pure … with earnest mind confess"): defilement arose by the three karmas of body, speech, and mind, and purification too enters in reverse through this same gate of the three karmas — by pure body, speech, and mind, disclosing the deeds formerly done by defiled body, speech, and mind. The second, and the more decisive, is what immediately follows: 「後不復造，恒住淨戒」 ("henceforth not committing it again, abiding ever in the pure precepts"): the true landing-point of confession is not that the past deed is wiped away, but that "henceforth it is not committed" and that one "abides ever in pure precepts."[^5] This reconstitutes confession as a *redirect*, not an *erasure* — it does not promise that the debt incurred is struck off the books; it promises the turning of one's heading.

Here a boundary must be drawn, lest the paper overrun itself. The phrase 「恒住淨戒」 ("abiding ever in pure precepts") is taken only in its one bearing — the *seam between confession and pure precepts* — that one is first purified and then able to abide in precepts, confession being the prior condition of abiding; as for the precept-essence, the taking of precepts, and the permissions, prohibitions, observances, and transgressions, those belong to another volume and are not unfolded here. Likewise, the Buddhas and bodhisattvas appear here in the *"before-whom"* slot (眾前): the confessant confesses "before all the Buddhas and bodhisattvas in every dust-mote land throughout the dharma-realm." This "before-whom" is the witnessing field, the object to which one entrusts oneself — not a judge who pronounces sentence and grants pardon. There is, in the text, no pronouncement that "your sins are forgiven"; the mechanism that can make the obstacle dissolve is, throughout, the confessant's own pure three karmas and his not committing it again. *To receive a confession is not to absolve* — a distinction inherited from the earlier papers of this volume, and not re-argued here.

## III. The Four Exhaustion-Similes and Moment-to-Moment Continuity: Confession from a Point to a Line

§II read the first half of the vow-text (what is confessed, how it is confessed). This section reads the second half — that "inexhaustibility" the verses do not have, which is precisely the load-bearing seam of "from a limb to a vow."

### 3.1 The Four Exhaustion-Similes Are a Logical Construction

「如是虛空界盡、眾生界盡、眾生業盡、眾生煩惱盡，我懺乃盡」 ("Thus: were the realm of space exhausted, the realm of beings exhausted, the karma of beings exhausted, the afflictions of beings exhausted — then my confession would be exhausted") — these four similes are not a rhetorical flourish but a precise *logical construction*. They set a *termination-condition*: only when all four — the realm of space, the realm of beings, the karma of beings, the afflictions of beings — are "exhausted" does "my confession" become "exhausted." The text then at once supplies: 「而虛空界乃至眾生煩惱不可盡故」 ("but because the realm of space, and so on down to the afflictions of beings, cannot be exhausted") — these four can never be exhausted. So the condition for "then my confession would be exhausted" never obtains; the consequent "would be exhausted" is never triggered. Confession's having no exhaustion is not because the confessant resolves to confess to the end of time, but because its termination-condition is *constructed so that it can never obtain*. It is inexhaustible *because it has been posited as a vow*.

These four similes have a lineage. On Zongmi's account, the four similes of the *Bhadracarī* gather in the "ten inexhaustible realms" that close the great vow of the first ground in the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* — taking the realm of space and the realm of beings as two pillars, then opening, within the pillar of beings, "karma" and "afflictions," yielding the four.[^6] Zongmi also explains why ten are gathered into four, and why karma-and-affliction is opened within beings: 「又以觀行是對治門故，故眾生開出業及煩惱也」 ("and because contemplative practice is the gate of counteraction, beings are opened into karma and affliction").[^6] This reminds us that the four similes are not an arbitrary rhetorical parallelism but a vow-form component with a provenance and a constructive logic.

A discipline must be set here, or the whole thesis is told crooked. This "four exhaustion-similes + moment-to-moment continuity" is *not* a phrasing peculiar to the confession-vow, but the shared closing chassis of *every one* of the Ten Great Vows. The homage-vow says "then my homage would be exhausted"; the offering-vow, "then my offering would be exhausted"; the confession-vow, "then my confession would be exhausted"; the rejoicing-vow, "then my rejoicing would be exhausted" — the verb changes vow by vow, but the inexhaustibility-frame is one and the same. In fact the commentaries of Chengguan and Zongmi both place the full inexhaustibility-sense up front at the first vow (homage), saying out the principle of "the general conclusion of inexhaustibility" at homage, and at confession write only 「例此」 ("the like of this") — 「下之九門，無盡例此」 ("the nine gates below: their inexhaustibility is the like of this").[^7] So the prose must never be made to say "only the confession-vow has inexhaustibility" (that is, on the documents, false); it must be put as: the *verse* (limb-form) lacks the clause; the *long prose* (vow-form) carries it; confession shares *one* inexhaustible chassis with the other nine vows. This may look like it weakens confession's distinctiveness, but it is in fact corroboration of the very thesis: confession is inexhaustible precisely because it has been set into the vow-form that "shares the inexhaustible chassis with nine vows" — which is just what "from a limb to a vow" means. The load-bearing commentary is taken from the general gloss at homage, and the scripture, by "the like of this," brings confession under the same treatment; this thinness is honest, not a gap.

### 3.2 Moment-to-Moment Continuity: A Continuity Established by Contemplative Practice

The four similes push "termination" toward never-obtaining; 「念念相續，無有間斷……無有疲厭」 ("moment after moment it continues, without interruption … there is no weariness") then spreads confession into a line of time stretched across the three karmas of body, speech, and mind. The question is: moment after moment without break — on what strength? On gritted-teeth perseverance? Zongmi's commentary gives an answer counter to the intuition, and it is the richest footnote to "from a limb to a vow":

> 念念相續無有間斷，身語意業無有疲厭……若不以普賢觀行之力，如何得念念不斷？願智者審思此文，無以生滅之心、取相之禮而為禮，例下九門，亦無以生滅取相之讚歎、供養乃至迴向等而為普賢行願也。[^8]
>
> *Moment after moment it continues without interruption, and in the karmic acts of body, speech, and mind there is no weariness … If not by the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice, how could it be unbroken moment after moment? Let the wise ponder this text well: do not, with a mind of arising-and-ceasing, with a homage that grasps at marks, make homage; and as for the nine gates below, neither with arising-and-ceasing, mark-grasping praise, offering, and so on down to dedication, perform Samantabhadra's conduct and vows.*

Zongmi says it plainly: what moment-to-moment continuity rests on is "the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice," not the grinding of willpower. What cannot hold out is "a mind of arising-and-ceasing, a homage that grasps at marks" — perform it with a mind that rises and falls, clinging to outward marks, and of course the moments will break, will tire, will weary. What truly keeps it unbroken is the power of a contemplative practice that neither arises-and-ceases nor grasps at marks. And with one phrase — 「例下九門」 ("as for the nine gates below") — Zongmi extends this "continuity established by contemplative practice" plainly to the nine gates, confession among them. The moment-to-moment continuity of the confession-vow is precisely the inheritance of this freedom from fatigue and from aversion that contemplative practice establishes.

This is just where the weight of the text's four words 「無有疲厭」 ("there is no weariness") lies. An inexhaustible task, received in the key of a burden, must lead to weariness and anxiety; yet the vow-text places, right after "inexhaustible," the words "there is no weariness" — it is not describing a still-unreached, exhausting goal, but stating that this very aspirational structure carries no key of weariness. "There is no weariness" thus becomes the anchor against every "futility" and "anxiety" reading, to which the next section returns. (A word of boundary is needed: Zongmi's term "contemplative practice" here already nears the ground of principial repentance; the paper takes only its one bearing — *the source of continuity* — and does not unfold doctrines of the order of "contemplating the emptiness of sin's nature.")

After confession turns from a point to a line, there is one more proof to match the inexhaustibility. Zongmi once weighed the merit of a single moment of confession against the karma made through measureless eons, calling the two equal in measure, and cited the dark-room simile of the *Mahāvibhāṣā*:

> 此一念善有形色者，大千世界不能容受……故以一念懺悔功德，便與無量劫所造之業分量齊等。如一暗室中，須臾燈光之明所遍分量，便與此室千年之暗分量齊等。[^9]
>
> *Had this single moment of goodness a bodily form, the great-thousand world could not contain it … Therefore the merit of a single moment of confession is equal in measure to the karma made through measureless eons. As in a dark room, the measure that a moment's lamplight pervades is equal in measure to that room's thousand years of dark.*

A thousand years of dark, and the lamp pervades it in a moment — the finite single moment has an infinite range. This pairs exactly with the four exhaustion-similes: an inexhaustible vow need not carry, in every moment, the weight of measureless eons to be repaid; the confession of one moment has, in itself, a range of merit commensurate with the karma of measureless eons. Inexhaustibility is therefore not "a debt that can never be paid off," but a vow that is "sufficient in each moment, and unbroken in its stretch." (A word is needed: this simile is Zongmi citing and restating the *Mahāvibhāṣā*, not the scripture itself; the citation should mark this chain of attribution.)

## IV. The Inexhaustible Vow and Sisyphus: A Cross-Traditional Juxtaposition and Its Discrimination

Turn the lens outside the Buddhist canon. A confession constructed as "never completed" is not without echo in Western thought — the perpetuity of repentance, the infinite bearing of responsibility, labor that never ends, have all been turned over again and again. This section sets three Western families beside the fourth vow, not to prove that they speak of the *Bhadracarī*, but to use them to drive out, one by one, the three misreadings into which "the inexhaustible vow" most easily slides, and then to discriminate each one clearly. The through-line across the three families is this: the inexhaustible vow is *generative*; its inexhaustibility rests on *the boundlessness of the object aspired to* (the object is boundless), not on "this person of mine is never enough" (I fall short); and the text's "there is no weariness" positively disproves the anxiety-and-futility reading.

The first family is the *perpetuity* of repentance versus its *punctuality*. The Orthodox tradition understands repentance (*metanoia*, an unceasing turning) as a lifelong, moment-by-moment turning, not a once-settled event; Luther's *Ninety-five Theses* opens by declaring that the believer's whole life is to be one of repentance.[^10] This stands in contrast with the Latin tradition's treatment of confession and penance as a "one-time event." On the surface, "unceasing turning" and "moment-to-moment continuity" are almost interchangeable. But pressed to the end, the discrimination emerges: Christian perpetual repentance still finally points toward an *absolver* and an eschatological *reckoning*, whereas the fourth vow's inexhaustible confession has neither a final pronouncement of absolution nor a last-day audit — its inexhaustibility is founded on "the four boundless realms," the structure of the object aspired to, not on the anxiety that "a lifespan is finite, the atonement never finished." (This family overlaps much with the next and has weaker discriminative force; it is placed first as a lead-in.)

The second family is *infinite commitment* in philosophy. Levinas, on responsibility for the other, holds that the more it is met the deeper it grows and is never discharged; Kierkegaard, on "repetition," sees it as an effort never completed in time; Kant, with the immortality of the soul as a postulate, lets the will progress endlessly and asymptotically toward the highest good, in tension with the demand that "ought implies can."[^11] What the three share is a task *constructed as "never completed."* But the discrimination is just as clear: the fourth vow's "there is no weariness" *refuses the key of guilt and burden* — it is not an infinite debt pressing on the shoulders, growing heavier the more it is met; its *telos* is the purification of obstacles and the provisions of bodhi, not moral self-perfection or the endless repayment of a deficit. More decisively, its inexhaustibility is founded on "*the object is boundless*" (the object aspired to being boundless, the vow is inexhaustible), not on "*I fall short*" (oneself never reaching it, so the task is inexhaustible). To read the former as the latter is this family's most common slip. (To be stated: all the above are mirrors of contrast; the paper does *not* claim that any Western thinker ever applied his account to the *Bhadracarī*, only that the two may be set side by side.)

The third family is the skepticism to be broken head-on — *Sisyphus and religious scrupulosity*. In Camus's *Myth of Sisyphus*, Sisyphus, sentenced by the gods to roll a stone up a hill forever, the stone falling back at the summit, is the archetype of "futility"; *religious scrupulosity* (the religious compulsion) is the obsession of "never feeling washed clean," done and done again, with no end. Does a confession posited as "inexhaustible, moment after moment" not look exactly like these two? Here four cuts must fall together, to open the surface resemblance. *First cut: the sentenced is not the vowed.* Sisyphus is *punished*; his labor is a penalty imposed on him. The fourth vow's confessant *vows*; the inexhaustible confession is an oath he himself takes — one passively sentenced, one actively resolved, the key opposite from the start. *Second cut: "there is no weariness" directly disproves the futility-and-anxiety reading.* The suffering of futility lies in fatigue and aversion, yet the vow-text expressly marks out "there is no weariness" — not an optimistic consolation, but a constructive property of this aspirational structure. (Camus must still have us "imagine Sisyphus happy"; the fourth vow simply does not read the road as hard labor in the first place.) *Third cut: inexhaustible is not a finite task "stuck incomplete."* Sisyphus's stone and the scrupulant's washing are *finite* tasks that could in principle be finished but are jammed and cannot be; the fourth vow's inexhaustibility is a vow *co-extensive with the realms* — its "unfinished" is not being jammed, but that the object aspired to (beings, afflictions) is boundless from the start. *Fourth cut: its telos is generative, not punitive.* It points toward the accumulation of provisions and the purification of the obstacle, not a treadmill that punishes without end. After the four cuts, the resemblance collapses and the discrimination stands: the inexhaustible vow is neither futility nor scrupulosity; its stretch is the stretch of aspiration, not the stretch of penalty or compulsion.

## V. The Middle-Path Calibration

This section gathers the boundaries scattered through the paper into a few explicit calibrations. Items marked ❌ are the over-steppings to be guarded against; items marked ⚠️ are the close-borderings to be handled with care.

❌ **The "had it a bodily form" hyperbole must not be read as the contemplation of emptiness.** 「若此惡業有體相者，盡虛空界不能容受」 ("had these evil deeds a bodily form, all of empty space could not contain them") is a counterfactual hyperbole of *quantity*, phenomenal repentance's way of voicing the multiplied gravity of fault; it is neither an ontological assertion that "karma has self-nature," nor to be reversed into "karma is without self-nature and therefore empty." To read this line as the contemplation of emptiness is to mistake phenomenal repentance's *rhetoric* for principial repentance's *doctrine*. The same holds for Zongmi's simile, 「一念善有形色……大千不能容受」 ("had a single moment of goodness a bodily form … the great-thousand could not contain it") — also a hyperbole of quantity, not a verdict on nature.

❌ **Inexhaustibility must not be told as peculiar to the confession-vow.** The four exhaustion-similes and moment-to-moment continuity are the shared closing chassis of all Ten Great Vows, and the commentaries place the full inexhaustibility-sense up front at the homage-vow. Wherever inexhaustibility is discussed, it must carry the qualifier "the limb-form lacks the clause / the vow-form carries it / confession shares one chassis with the nine vows," and must never be written as "only the confession-vow has inexhaustibility" — that, on the documents, is a false proposition.

❌ **To receive a confession is not to absolve.** The Buddhas and bodhisattvas stand in the "before-whom" slot, the witnessing field, the object of entrustment — not a judge who grants pardon; the vow-text carries no pronouncement of absolution, and what makes the obstacle dissolve is the confessant's own pure three karmas and his not committing it again. This inherits the discrimination of the earlier papers of this volume.

⚠️ **"Abiding ever in pure precepts" is not unfolded into precept-essence.** Take only the one line of the causal seam "confession → pure precepts" — first purified, then able to abide in precepts; precept-essence, the taking of precepts, and the marks of precepts all belong to another volume and are not argued here.

⚠️ **"Contemplative practice" borders the ground of principial repentance; take only the one bearing, the source of continuity.** Zongmi's phrase "the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice" nears the principial; the paper takes only its one bearing — *the source on which moment-to-moment continuity rests, not the grinding of willpower* — and does not, riding the slope, gloss "contemplating the emptiness of sin's nature."

❌ **Do not slide into the self-help key.** "The inexhaustible confession" is most to be guarded from being read as "the never-enough therapy of self-blame." The paper's juxtaposition with the Western accounts is a scholarly contrast; its *telos* (purification of obstacles, the provisions of bodhi) is not the same thing as psychological "closure," "reconciliation," or "healing"; "there is no weariness" is the anchor against anxiety, not a word of comfort.

## VI. Conclusion

**The essential restatement.** At the fourth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, confession reaches the high-water mark of its aspirational form. It rises from a *limb* of the sevenfold worship (a verse ending at a completed act) to a *vow* among the Ten (long prose carrying the four exhaustion-similes); the verses lack the inexhaustibility clause and only the long prose carries it, and this structural asymmetry is the exact evidence of "from a limb to a vow." Posited as a vow, confession inherits the inexhaustible chassis the vow-form shares with the nine vows: the four similes construct the termination-condition so that it can never obtain, and moment-to-moment continuity — by the power of Samantabhadra's contemplative practice (not the grinding of willpower) — spreads confession into a line of time without weariness. Phenomenal repentance thus turns from "a single point of disclosure" into "a line stretching to the end of future time, unbroken." And the karmic obstacle it confesses is a cause→manifestation→effect generative chain — greed, hatred, and delusion arising in body, speech, and mind — not a substance; and the way it confesses is the *redirect* of "henceforth not committing it again, abiding ever in pure precepts," not an erasure.

**What this means for understanding and practice.** The one decisive discrimination of the inexhaustible confession is that its inexhaustibility rests on "*the object is boundless*," not on "*I fall short*." Read as the anxiety of "I can never finish atoning," it falls into the trap of Sisyphus and scrupulosity; read as "a bodhi-vow co-extensive with the realms," it is a vow that generates. The four words "there is no weariness" are the touchstone of this discrimination — a rightly received inexhaustible confession is unwearied, unaverse, unanxious, because what it stretches is aspiration, not penalty. This also means that confession here is still, through and through, *phenomenal* repentance: the marks of fault stand vivid (the cause, manifestation, and effect of the karmic obstacle can be counted one by one), disclosure is made before the Buddhas (earnest-minded confession before the assembly), and it is redirect, not erasure (henceforth not committing it again, abiding ever in pure precepts). It does not — and need not, at this level — claim that fault is, at root, unarisen.

**The question left to what follows.** Where, then, has the other side gone — the side that beholds the very nature of sin as empty, principial repentance? The fourth vow's text nowhere foregrounds it. This is not an oversight. Zongmi says it plainly: the fourth vow "omits the contemplation of sin's emptiness" because 「性空是緣起之體，行願必是無性之緣起故，故此經文不更別標」 ("emptiness-of-nature is the substance of dependent arising, and conduct-and-vows must be a dependent arising without self-nature; therefore this scripture does not separately signpost it").[^13] Emptiness is taken as the *silent ground*; conduct-and-vows are the surface enacted upon that ground, and so the scripture does not separately mark it out. In other words, it is precisely this doctrinal arrangement — "taking emptiness-of-nature as the presupposed ground" — that licenses the paper to handle only the phenomenal, enacted level of the aspirational structure, and to reserve the principial side — sin's nature as empty, the repentance of non-arising — for separate treatment at the end of this volume; here only a signpost is planted, its meaning not unfolded. This volume has climbed from the *floor* of phenomenal repentance (it can lessen but cannot wholly cleanse) to its *summit* here (the inexhaustible, aspirational confession), both pointing toward the still-unopened principial repentance. One step further, and from this summit of phenomenal repentance one turns toward the other threshold — the "die-and-then-live" passage — but that is for later.
