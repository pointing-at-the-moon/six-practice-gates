# Modern Diagnosis, Three Misreadings

**The Three Things the Modern Reader Takes Repentance For, Walking Down from the Summit of Non-Arising**

*中文主題：現代診斷·三種誤讀——走出無生懺的空頂之後*

## Abstract

This volume began at the most concrete end of the gate of repentance — the disclosure of faults in the Vinaya — and climbed, phrase by phrase through the *Heart Sūtra*, to the summit of the repentance of non-arising. Walking down from that summit, the modern reader does not step into repentance with a blank mind. He arrives carrying three pre-understandings: he reads repentance as emotion-management (the therapeutic misreading), as avowal to an authority in exchange for absolution (the equivalence with confession), and as self-debasing shame. This paper argues that the whole arc the volume has already walked — disclosure in the Vinaya, the repentance of non-arising, the vow — has, in fact, answered these three misreadings layer by layer; the answers were simply scattered across the separate papers. This paper digs up no new scripture. It gathers the answers already given into a single diagnostic map: repentance is not guilt-management; to receive is not to be absolved; clarity is not self-flagellation. Each axis takes a chapter, and only as far as the map requires; the exhaustive daily use, and the full seam that stitches the repentance of obstruction to the making of the vow, are left to the paper that closes the volume.

**Keywords:** modern diagnosis; three misreadings; the therapeutic misreading; equivalence with confession; shame; guilt-management; to receive is not to be absolved; emptiness is not absolution; transgression has no own-being; karmic cause-and-effect is not empty; the repentance of non-arising; diagnostic map; summiting does not dismantle the stairs; deeds without weariness

## I. Walking Down from the Summit: Three Pre-Understandings and a Bridge

The previous paper of this volume read the *Heart Sūtra* phrase by phrase, and repentance reached its summit on the side of non-arising. At its close it handed forward a baton in plain words: the work of resisting the therapeutic reduction, of resisting confession-style management of guilt, of resisting the use of "non-arising" to evade responsibility — all of this belongs to the latter part of the volume; that paper closed on the side of prajñā and passed the rest forward.[^1] This is the paper that takes up the baton.

One thing must be said before anything else. The scriptures contain no rubric called "modern diagnosis." The "three modern misreadings" are not the analytic divisions of any sūtra; they are a diagnostic map drawn by this series for the situation of the contemporary reader — a problematization of the reader's pre-understandings, not a transcription of explicit scripture. The declaration is made here precisely so that a constructive frame is not disguised as settled doctrine.

Why these three, and not some others? Here it is worth borrowing a Western mirror to look first at the problem itself. Michel Foucault, in the first volume of his *History of Sexuality*, offers a famous judgment: the West has made of man a confessing animal — for several centuries the dominant technique for handling wrongdoing has been to disclose one's inner self to a present authority, who then judges and settles it.[^6] This technique has sedimented so deep that when the modern person so much as thinks of "facing his own fault," the script in his head has shrunk to almost two tracks: either hand it to a therapist to be eased, or hand it to an absolver to be remitted; and if he takes neither track, he collapses inward into the shame of "I am a bad person." The therapeutic, confession, and shame — these three pre-understandings are exactly the three pairs of tinted spectacles the modern reader brings to the gate of repentance.

But the first danger off the summit does not, in fact, lie within these three axes. It lies on a bridge. A reader who has just heard "non-arising" and "transgression has no own-being" is most tempted to read it, offhand, as a disclaimer: if transgression has no own-being, then what is there to regret, what is there to change? This is to read emptiness as annihilation. It must be said on the spot: that transgression has no own-being means the emptying of one grasping — the grasping at a transgression that exists from its own side, at a real and bound self — not the emptying of cause and effect. Karmic cause-and-effect is not empty; conditioned arising does not fail; the slightest deed still bears its fruit exactly as it is.[^9] Emptiness is a seeing, not an erasure; to say "originally empty" was never to issue a permit for evasion. This bridge — "evasion by way of non-arising" — is the first danger this paper diagnoses, and it is not this paper's thesis. The earlier paper of the volume already answered it in depth (that transgression has no own-being and that karmic fruit is not empty stand together; see the close of the prior paper on the prajñā side), so here it is treated only as a door one must pass through before entering the three axes, not raised as a fourth axis of its own. Only once this door is passed can the three spectacles be taken off one by one.

## II. First Misreading — The Therapeutic Reduction: Repentance Is Not Guilt-Management

The first pair of spectacles takes repentance for emotion-management. Through this lens, repentance becomes a technique for making a person "feel a little better": speak the remorse aloud, comb out the feelings, dissolve the knot in the chest — for all the world a Buddhist version of cognitive-behavioral therapy. The paper of this volume that handled phenomenal repentance already drew this red line: repentance is not guilt-management; and "writing repentance as a Buddhist cognitive-behavioral therapy is the greatest trap of that section."[^2]

Why a trap? The hinge is the mode of closure. The wholesome contrition of phenomenal repentance is a wholesome blade: once its task is done it sheathes itself — "once disclosed and received, wholesome contrition concludes; it does not recur."[^2] It is not an emotion to be kept and stroked over the long term; it does the thing that had to be done and then withdraws. Emotion-management is the exact reverse: it treats that unease as a standing condition requiring continual care, so that remorse becomes a form of self-maintenance with no terminus. The one self-terminates, the other self-perpetuates — this is the line between the wholesome blade and the morbid blade.

Here a second mirror is worth borrowing. The social theorist Philip Rieff, in *The Triumph of the Therapeutic*, observed that modern Western culture has turned from a "religious man" centered on faith to a "psychological man" centered on well-being; Frank Furedi, in *Therapy Culture*, described how this therapeutic self translates all suffering into "emotion that needs to be processed."[^3][^4] The concession should be made plainly: this mirror reflects accurately. Modern suffering, and the need for relief from it, are real; and repentance does indeed have a side that sets the heart at ease — after disclosure, the heart does grow lighter.

⚠️ And yet the stronger the correspondence, the sharper the line must be drawn. The terminus of the therapeutic self is adjustment — to be refitted to the life one already has, to feel good and recover function, that is the end of the road; it does not ask "where is this ease meant to carry the person," because ease is itself the destination. The terminus of repentance is not ease but purity and the path; the calm is an incidental by-product, not the place to be reached. The one unrolls outward with the comfort of the "I" as its center; the other gathers upward toward the stilling of that "I." Their *telos* is simply not the same. Set repentance into the coordinates of therapy and it retains a single dimension — "making the person feel better" — while the dimension of "and after feeling better, toward what?" is quietly erased. This is the least noticeable loss the therapeutic reduction inflicts.

On the prajñā side, the volume pressed this distinction to its furthest depth: the sign-less repentance, the repentance with no mark of transgression, is not a higher-grade emotional technique but the seeing of the "sun of wisdom" — a noetic turn of cognition, not an affective easing of tension.[^5] The difference is not one of degree but of kind: easing handles the tension of feeling; seeing transforms the manner of seeing. The one lowers the water-level of unease; the other changes the eye that looks at the water. In other words, what repentance turns at its deepest is the seeing, not the feeling; and this is the cashing-out, at the lowest layer, of the very red line by which the volume resists the therapeutic reduction. The therapeutic reduction is a misreading not because it says anything false — its description of modern suffering is often precise — but because it demotes a fundamental change in *how one sees* into a long-running maintenance of *how one feels*.

Exactly how repentance and therapy divide their labor in daily life, when one may lean on therapy and when one must hand the matter back to the path — that is finer work, and is left to the paper that closes the volume. This chapter is responsible only for marking the shape of this axis: repentance has a side that sets the heart at ease, but it does not take ease for its terminus.

## III. Second Misreading — Equivalence with Confession: To Receive Is Not to Be Absolved

The second pair of spectacles takes repentance for confession: disclose a fault to a present authority, who pronounces absolution, and the slate is wiped at a stroke. This lens is not pulled out of thin air — repentance does include a step of disclosure, whose outward form closely resembles confession, and the paper of this volume on institutions conceded as much: the institution, in its form, is open to analogy.[^8]

Borrow Foucault's mirror once more: the confession he dissects is a technology of power — it produces a subject who must avow his truth to authority and submit to its discipline and judgment. That disclosure and confession resemble each other in form is precisely what makes this lens so stubborn. But the resemblance stops there. The line the institutional paper drew was this: the institution may be analogized; the doctrine of absolution may not be reduced. To receive repentance is not to be absolved — a line held all the way to here, and never altered.[^8]

The lifeline of the difference is a single word: *to receive*. At its outset the volume cited the Vinaya: "one who can now repent within the noble Dharma thereby benefits himself; out of compassion for you, I receive your repentance."[^7] The Buddha can *receive*, but he never *absolves*. To receive is to accept this disclosure as it is, to confirm this turning — as a mirror reflects, just as it is, whoever stands before it. To absolve is for one vested with authority to cancel a charge of guilt by his power and declare it not to exist. The two look alike but differ in substance: receiving takes up, absolving wipes out; receiving hands the fault back to its doer to be borne, absolving strikes the fault off the ledger. Hence the volume's repeated insistence: what disclosure yields is a *lessening*, not an *erasure* — it lessens the force of concealment and the momentum of recurrence (what was hidden loosens; the impetus of the deed slows), and it does not remove for you some really existent body of guilt.[^7]

❌ To read *receiving* as absolution is a category error. The error is stubborn because it assumes, all at once, three things that simply do not exist in the scene: an absolver with the power to cancel, a real transgression awaiting cancellation, and an authority that wipes the slate clean. The script of confession needs all three to be staged; in the scene of repentance, not one of them is present. The "receiving" of disclosure produces no subject obliged to avow his truth to authority and then be judged by it; and no one stands across from him raising a hand to pronounce, "your transgression is absolved." It does the very opposite: it returns the burden to the one who bears it — and this is "to benefit oneself," to gain by one's own honest facing-up. To bear is not to be pardoned; the one who gains is the practitioner himself, not someone released by an authority outside him.

On the prajñā side, this line is lifted a whole tier higher. At the level of emptiness, the volume raised "to receive is not to absolve" into "emptiness is not absolution": emptiness is the seeing that all dharmas have no own-being — it is not the remission of deeds already done; there is no absolution in the repentance of non-arising "because no one was ever bound."[^9] This is the most thoroughgoing cut: the whole script of confession — first presuppose a bondage, then seek release, finally be absolved — has even its stage withdrawn here. If there is no real rope that ties a person down, what need for an absolution that unties it? This of course does not mean that fault need not be owned, or that karmic fruit may be ignored — karmic fruit is not empty; the deed still bears fruit exactly as it is. It means rather that the way of owning does not run the road of "petitioning some authority for remission" at all; it runs the road of "seeing that there was never a binding." To seek absolution is to put out a hand for a writ of pardon; to see is to look within and find no chain in the hand at all.

The deeper excavation of this axis — the item-by-item reading of repentance against confession across institutional history, the structure of power, and the doctrine of absolution — was set out in full by the institutional paper of this volume. This chapter takes only its conclusion, and marks the shape of one axis.

## IV. Third Misreading — Shame: Clarity Is Not Self-Flagellation

The third pair of spectacles takes repentance for shame: to own a fault is to admit that "I, this person, am bad," and so to collapse inward into self-debasement, cutting at oneself again and again, sinking deeper the more one cuts. This lens is the subtlest, because it so often wears the dress of "sincere contrition."

The phenomenal-repentance paper of this volume already used psychology to part shame from guilt: shame focuses on the self — "I am a bad person" — and drives a person to withdraw and to hide; guilt focuses on the act — "I did a bad thing" — and drives a person to repair and to amend. Shame makes one retreat; guilt makes one change.[^10] What repentance wants is the latter's force, the one that drives a person to amend, not the former's self-negation, the one that nails a person where he stands. To read repentance as shame is to take an engine meant to push a person forward and wire it backward into a mill that grinds inward.

Borrow a third mirror: the philosopher Bernard Williams, in *Shame and Necessity*, distinguished two moral terrains — the guilt-culture and the shame-culture. The first asks "what did I do," with the act as its focus; the second asks "what am I in the eyes of others," with one's self-image as its focus.[^11] The concession should be made: these two axes each catch a side of repentance, and they can be read almost item by item against the shame-and-guilt distinction the volume itself cited; it is a fairly accurate mirror.

But repentance's final answer does not take sides between these two axes. Guilt fixes on the act, shame fixes on the self; on the surface they look like opposite poles, but at bottom they share a single fulcrum — that "I." The one says "I did a bad thing," the other says "I am a bad person"; whichever it says, it has already presupposed, standing behind the words, a solid and definable "I." Put another way, the very dispute over "is the act to blame, or the self?" is built on the ground of "there is a real self to blame." What the volume does at the level of emptiness is not to choose a side in this dispute but to pull out the ground both sides are standing on: that transgression has no own-being empties precisely the grasping at "a real bad self" — while karmic fruit remains un-empty, the fault still to be owned as it is and changed as it is, the fruit of conditions failing in not the slightest degree.[^9] What is emptied is the "bad self," that object whipped over and over; what is not emptied is the fact that "this deed was once done, and this conduct should now be changed." And so repentance need neither nail itself dead into "I am a bad person" (the withdrawal of shame), nor stall in the self-defense of "I only did one wrong thing"; it steps clear of the very ground the two share, and sees clearly that even that "I" is dependently arisen and without own-being.

Lifted to this point, the line that closed the volume's paper on the Vinaya's final sense fits best: to own a fault as it is, and to fix the fault into a real thing enthroned in the heart to be cut at again and again, are two different things.[^12] The former is clarity: having seen, one owns; having owned, one turns; one does not shrink from changing what is wrong; and having changed, one sets it down. The latter is self-flagellation: having seen, one will not let go, and instead enshrines the fault like a thing in a shrine, lighting incense to it daily, flogging it daily, supposing that the harder the flogging, the more sincere it is. But that is not contrition; that is another form of grasping — a grasping at the self-image of "I am a bad person who deserves to be punished," and it is rooted in the same soil as shame. Clarity is not self-flagellation — this is the red line of the third axis: repentance wants the wholesome blade that turns the moment a fault is owned, not the morbid blade that cuts deeper the more it cuts.

## V. Hand-Off: Wisdom and Vow — The Repentance of Non-Arising as the Ultimate Resolution

Reading this far, one might ask: to break these three misreadings, must a new method, a new technique, be added? Quite the opposite. The volume's answer lies not in adding anything from outside, but in the very arc the six parts of the volume have already walked — disclosure in the Vinaya (phenomenal repentance), the repentance of non-arising (principial repentance, wisdom), the vow — an arc that has already answered the three axes, layer by layer.

Seen from the side of wisdom: of the three misreadings, the therapeutic clings to "my feeling," confession clings to "my guilt-debt," shame clings to "this person I am" — the lenses of all three spectacles are ground from the same pane of glass, the "I." The repentance of non-arising is the ultimate resolution precisely because it sees through that pane at a single stroke: if no one is bound, then no one needs to be healed, to be absolved, or to be debased. But here a word of the first importance must be added: summiting does not dismantle the stairs. That the repentance of non-arising has seen through "the real self" by no means declares phenomenal repentance superfluous — the modern diagnosis loosens the pre-understandings of a path that has already reached the summit; it does not turn back to tear down the stairs by which it climbed. Phenomenal repentance must still disclose, must still be received, must still amend as the facts require; non-arising only keeps all of this from sticking to a fictional self-of-guilt. The map tells you where the summit lies; it does not tell you to burn the stone steps along the way. And the emptying of the "I" never empties cause and effect: karmic fruit is not empty; the deed bears fruit exactly as it is.

Seen from the side of the vow, the answer falls precisely on the therapeutic reduction's deepest misgiving — "if remorse has no terminus, has it not become an endless self-maintenance? Is that not exactly what the therapeutic reduction feared?" The volume's paper on the vow gives this answer: continuity without fatigue. "Thought after thought, in unbroken continuity… deeds of body, speech, and mind without weariness."[^13] The key lies in where the strength behind "without weariness" comes from: it does not lean on the bitter propping-up of the will but rests "on the strength of Samantabhadra's contemplation and practice" — a strength born of contemplative seeing (seeing the real mark), not a strength squeezed out by gritted teeth and self-flogging.[^13] This very distinction dissolves the therapeutic misgiving at its root. The "endlessness" the therapeutic reduction fears is the continuity of the morbid blade — the same unease soothed and relapsing, soothed and relapsing, a maintenance kept up by the will and doomed to tire, to weary, to burn out. The continuity of the vow is of the wholesome blade: each thought is a closure, and each thought is a fresh arising, the closing and the arising being two faces of one thing — and so there is no weariness; not "holding on so as not to let myself tire," but a continuity that simply does not issue from "I am laboring to keep this up," and so has nothing in it to tire, nothing to weary. Continuity is no longer a string pulled ever tighter, but a spring that flows ever more alive.

How the repentance of obstruction and the making of the vow are finally stitched into this one weariless spring is the whole task of the paper that closes the volume; this chapter sets only the signpost and does not borrow its materials in advance. As for where this arc finally dedicates itself — "to the attainment of unsurpassed, perfect awakening" — that is the home to which the closing paper of the vow-gate turns, and it is touched here only lightly, not dug into.

## VI. Conclusion — A Diagnostic Map

Taken together, the three axes are what this paper hands over: a map.

The first axis — **repentance is not guilt-management**. It is not an emotional maintenance that makes a person feel better, but a noetic turn in "how one sees"; the wholesome blade self-terminates, it is not the morbid blade that self-perpetuates.

The second axis — **to receive is not to be absolved**. What disclosure yields is a "receiving" and a "lessening," not the absolver's "absolving" and "erasing"; lifted to the level of emptiness, it becomes "emptiness is not absolution," because no one was ever bound — and even so, karmic fruit is not empty.

The third axis — **clarity is not self-flagellation**. Repentance does not take a side in "guilt vs. shame" but empties the "I" both cling to; to own a fault as it is, is not to cut at oneself again and again.

These three sentences are not added rebuttals but fruit the whole arc of the six-part volume grew on its way; they first tell you what repentance **is not**, so that you can see clearly what repentance **is**. And the part of this map most worth turning over is this: its three answers were not three rebuttals patched together for this paper, but fruit the volume's whole arc grew layer by layer. The phenomenal layer (disclosure in the Vinaya) answered the factual side of axes one and three — wholesome contrition self-terminates, the fault is owned as it is. The institutional layer answered axis two — to receive is not to be absolved. The prajñā layer (the repentance of non-arising) lifted all three at once, emptying the "I" they jointly cling to, so that the therapeutic has no "I" to maintain, confession has no "I" awaiting absolution, and shame has no "I" to debase. The vow-gate then caught up the problem of continuity, making all of this weariless. In other words, the antidotes to the three misreadings were in truth already laid in, layer by layer, all along the road the volume walked; this paper only gathers the answers scattered across the separate papers into one map that can be read at a glance.

The three pairs of spectacles — the therapeutic, confession, shame — were the default equipment of the modern mind. Take them off, and repentance shows its original face: not a technique for managing guilt, not a rite for procuring absolution, not an execution-ground for self-debasement, but a discipline of seeing truthfully, bearing truthfully, and going on in unbroken continuity.

It must be affirmed once more: to spread this map open is not to deny a single rung of the climb. The diagnosis takes the spectacles off one who has already summited; it does not dismantle the stairs for one who has not yet set out. Phenomenal repentance is still phenomenal repentance; disclosure must still be disclosed; karmic fruit remains un-empty. The map marks where the misreadings lie; it does not abolish the practice.

How, then, once the spectacles are off, does repentance land in the life of one ordinary day after another? How do the three axes calibrate one another in daily use, and join the full seam that stitches the repentance of obstruction to the making of the vow? The exhaustive daily use is left to the paper that closes the volume. This paper is responsible only for spreading the map flat — so that the modern reader, walking down from the summit, can at least first see clearly the pair of spectacles in his own hand, and then decide whether to take them off.
