# The Double-Edged Remorse
***kukkucca as Engine and as Hindrance — One Feeling, Two Roads***

*中文主題：阿含的悔之雙刃——如法追悔與掉悔蓋：一種情緒，兩條去路*

**Abstract**

The remorse of the Āgamas — *kukkucca*, 悔 — is a single word that carries two values at once. The very same remorse, turned one way, grows the noble Dharma; turned the other, it becomes the fifth hindrance. Reading the *Madhyama-*, *Saṃyukta-*, and alternate *Saṃyukta-āgama* together, this paper argues that the dividing line lies not in the feeling itself but in whether a *terminal* is attached behind it — disclosure (發露) and release (捨). Remorse done in accordance with the Dharma runs a three-beat rhythm: *see the fault, disclose it, guard against its recurrence*; the teacher receives the remorse, the discloser "departs rejoicing and bows," and the remorse closes itself out. The same feeling, with that terminal stripped away, is restlessness-and-remorse (掉悔, *uddhacca-kukkucca*): fed by memory and rumination, never stilled, it "keeps producing an agitated, remorseful mind" and "becomes a cause of scatter," sinking into an anguish-producing fetter. The Āgamas grant remorse no independent hindrance-slot of its own; it counts as a hindrance only when it is welded to *agitation* (掉) — that is, only as *agitated* remorse, looping. Remorse, then, is a gate of repentance, the psychological engine of phenomenal repentance rather than its summit: with an exit it is a gate; idling, with no exit, it becomes a cell.

**Keywords:** remorse (悔), regret/after-the-deed remorse (追悔/惡作), remorse-in-accord-with-the-Dharma (如法追悔), restlessness-and-remorse (掉悔), the fifth hindrance (掉悔蓋), five hindrances (*pañca nīvaraṇā*), disclosure (發露), concealment (覆藏), guarding-against-recurrence (護不更作), stilling (寂止), cause-of-scatter (散亂因), rumination, guilt, shame, phenomenal repentance (事懺), Āgama, *kukkucca*

## I. The Problem: One Word, Two Faces

For many readers who walk into Buddhism carrying the common sense of modern moral psychology, "remorse" is an unsettlingly ambiguous word. On one side, we are told that a person should answer for their faults — should face them, should be able to own a mistake; the one who cannot own a mistake is the most dangerous of all. On the other side, we are told that excessive self-reproach is an illness, that chewing over old errors only drags a person into the swamp of depression, and "learn to let yourself off the hook" has become almost a health rule of the age. So remorse wears two faces at once: at once the mark of maturity and the symptom of pathology. Are we, in the end, to feel remorse, or not?

This tension is not a modern invention. The Āgamas' handling of "remorse" sets exactly these two faces on the same word, and does not flinch from the strain between them. In the *Rāhulovāda* of the *Madhyama-āgama*, the Buddha admonishes Rāhula and, in a single breath, gives both halves of remorse:

> 「彼已作身業，至心發露，應悔過說，慎莫覆藏，更善持護。」……「羅云！汝當捨彼過去意業。」

> *Having done such a deed of body, disclose it wholeheartedly; you should confess the fault and on no account conceal it, and guard yourself the better hereafter.* … *Rāhula! Let go of that past deed of mind.*[^1]

Notice the structure of these words. They first require one to *disclose* — to speak the fault out, not to cover it — and then require one to *let go of the past* — to set down what has already been disclosed. Disclosure is handing the remorse over; release is not snatching it back again. Only the two together make up the complete course that the Āgamas sanction for remorse.

And the problem lies just here: what happens if you remove either of these two steps? If one does not disclose but instead conceals, the remorse is sealed inside the heart to ferment. If one discloses yet refuses to release — repeatedly seizing the past back and replaying it — the remorse becomes a replay with no endpoint. For this second state the Āgamas have a name of their own: this remorse that turns and turns without stopping they list as the fifth of the five hindrances (*pañca nīvaraṇā*), calling it restlessness-and-remorse (掉悔, *uddhacca-kukkucca*).

So we face a fact that looks, at first, like contradiction: within one and the same body of scripture, remorse is both a true provision for the path and a hindrance that blocks it. What this paper argues is that this is no self-contradiction in the texts but their precise portrait of the feeling. The Chinese Āgamas and the Pāli alike load both the wholesome and the unwholesome remorse onto a single term — Chinese has but one 悔, Pāli but one *kukkucca*. The difference between sound remorse and sick remorse lies not in the wording but in whether, behind that surge of feeling, there is attached a terminal that lets it close out. This is the structure the paper calls the *double-edged remorse*: one feeling, two roads. One leads to a gate; the other to a cell.

This continues the volume's first three papers. P01 distinguished the senses of three words — repentance, disclosure, remorse; P02 set out the Saṅgha's machinery of concealment-and-rehabilitation, its restoration to purity; P03 set out invitation (*pavāraṇā*) and recitation (*uposatha*), that paired institution of *asking the Saṅgha to call out one's own faults*, the anti-concealment apparatus. All three worked at the *institutional* layer of phenomenal repentance: how the Saṅgha uses outward rule to make *not concealing* the norm. This paper turns from institution to the *interior*: in that very feeling of remorse — solicited out into the open, hiding behind the concealment — is the feeling itself a help, or another hindrance? This is the first question of Part II, the Āgama view of remorse.

## II. The Good Edge: Remorse-in-Accord-with-the-Dharma as the Engine of Phenomenal Repentance

Take first the wholesome face of remorse. The Āgamas' description of remorse done in accordance with the Dharma has one remarkably stable fixed formula, which may be called the *three-beat rhythm*: see the fault → disclose it → guard against its recurrence. After the three beats there always follows a clause of growth. The cleanest sample comes from the *Gāmaṇi* discourse of the *Madhyama-āgama*, addressed to a layman, the headman Pāṭaliya:

> 「然汝能悔過，見罪發露，護不更作。如是，伽彌尼！若有悔過，見罪發露，護不更作者，則長養聖法而無有失。」

> *Yet you are able to repent the fault — to see the fault, disclose it, and guard against its recurrence. Just so, Gāmaṇi! Whoever repents the fault, sees the fault, discloses it, and guards against its recurrence, grows the noble Dharma and suffers no loss.*[^2]

This passage is the banner of the good edge for two reasons. First, it sets out all three terminal components together: seeing the fault (recognizing that one has erred), disclosure (speaking it, not hiding it), and guarding against recurrence (holding the future, not offending again). Second, its closing clause is not punishment but growth — *grows the noble Dharma and suffers no loss*. In the Āgamas' assessment, remorse-in-accord-with-the-Dharma is no debt but a true provision for the path; the one who can feel remorse is not the one who is docked points but the one who "gains and does not lose within the noble training."

This deserves a further word to the modern reader. Our intuition tends to read "owning a mistake" as a kind of loss: to admit error is to be diminished a notch. The Āgamas' view is exactly the reverse — to admit, to disclose, to guard the future is, as a whole motion, itself increase. The error, once seen and handled in accordance with the Dharma, does not drag the person down but lifts the person up.

More important still is the way this wholesome remorse *closes*. It is not open-ended; it carries its own endpoint. Fascicle 41 of the *Saṃyukta-āgama* records two monks who quarrelled, then each saw his own fault, came before the Buddha, and repented; the Buddha received their remorse:

> 「今已自知罪，自見罪，知見悔過……我今受汝，憐愍故，令汝善法增長，終不退減……」時，二比丘聞佛所說，歡喜隨喜，作禮而去。

> *"Now you have known your own fault, seen your own fault, and in knowing and seeing have repented… I now receive you, out of compassion, that your wholesome states may grow and never decline…"* And the two monks, hearing what the Buddha said, rejoiced and were gladdened, bowed, and departed.[^3]

Note the tail of this narrative — *rejoiced and were gladdened, bowed, and departed*. Here remorse has a definite point of closure: once the parties have seen the fault, disclosed it, and had the remorse received, the feeling dissolves; the two depart in gladness and carry no further grief. This is precisely the axis on which the good edge contrasts with the sick edge below: once wholesome remorse is disclosed and received, it concludes — *it does not loop*. (The reward-clause of this passage runs "the future restraint of the precepts arises," touching the side of receiving precepts and the precept-essence — a matter proper to the volume on moral discipline; this paper takes only the psychological arc *repent → grow → not decline*, and leaves the precept-essence aside.)

This closure can even be seen at the first stirring of remorse. Fascicle 30 of the *Saṃyukta-āgama* records the venerable Kāśyapa, who, having heard the Dharma:

> 時，尊者迦葉氏……心即生悔……「我今日自知罪悔，自見罪悔，唯願世尊受我悔過」……佛告迦葉氏：「如是悔者，善法增長，終不退減。」

> The venerable Kāśyapa … *at once gave rise to remorse in his mind* … *"Today I myself know the fault and feel remorse, I myself see the fault and feel remorse; may the World-Honored One receive my repentance."* … The Buddha said to Kāśyapa: *"One who feels remorse thus — his wholesome states grow and never decline."*[^4]

The four words *at once gave rise to remorse in his mind* matter. The remorse here is the very same word, the very same surge of feeling, as the remorse in the restlessness-and-remorse hindrance below. In itself it is not a hindrance; it is the *bud* of wholesome remorse — because it immediately drives the person to disclose, to ask that the repentance be received, and so is steered toward the terminal. The same surge of remorse, finding its exit here, becomes a gate.

The Pāli transmission supplies the same backbone. The *Vinaya khandhakas* and the *Sāmaññaphala-sutta* both carry a growth-formula: having seen a transgression as a transgression (*accayaṁ accayato disvā*), one makes amends in accordance with the Dhamma (*yathādhammaṁ paṭikaroti*), and undertakes restraint for the future (*āyatiṁ saṁvaraṁ āpajjati*) — prefaced by the verdict *Vuḍḍhi hesā, āvuso, ariyassa vinaye*, "for this is growth in the training of the noble one."[^5] The two episodes that carry the formula differ (one is the assassin sent by Devadatta; the other is King Ajātasattu), but the formula is identical, and corresponds tightly to the Chinese "see the fault, disclose it → grow the noble Dharma": phenomenal repentance is growth, not punishment.

To sum up the build of the good edge: it is defined by its *terminal*. Disclosure is remorse's outward exit; guarding-against-recurrence is its forward seal; release is its backward letting-go. With all three present, remorse walks the road it ought to walk, concludes itself, and does not linger. This is the shape of remorse as the engine of phenomenal repentance — it drives the person to see, to hand over, to guard, and then it cuts off. When the engine has done its work, it ought to stop.

## III. The Bad Edge: The Hindrance of Restlessness-and-Remorse as Idling Rumination

Now strip away the terminal, and look at the other road of the same surge of remorse.

The Āgamas lodge pathological remorse within the five hindrances, at the fifth — restlessness-and-remorse. Here there is an easily overlooked but crucial detail of construction: the Chinese binds *agitation* (掉, restlessness, scatter) and *remorse* (悔) into one compound hindrance, not two. The Āgamas grant remorse no independent hindrance-slot. A micro-dissection in fascicle 27 of the *Saṃyukta-āgama* (SĀ 713) states this most clearly:

> 「有掉有悔，彼掉彼悔即是蓋，非智非等覺，不轉趣涅槃。」

> *There is agitation, there is remorse; that agitation, that remorse, is just the hindrance — not knowledge, not full awakening, not turning toward Nibbāna.*[^6]

*That agitation, that remorse, is just the hindrance* — what forms the hindrance is not remorse in isolation but the remorse bound to *agitation*, welded to the mind's restlessness and turning round and round. In other words, pathological remorse is by definition *agitated remorse*: the same surge of remorse, when it joins the mind's unrest and begins to spin, becomes a hindrance. The pivot of the double edge lives in just this compound. (Later Abhidhamma analysis separates *agitation* and *remorse* into two distinct mental factors; that is a later analytic layer. On the Āgama surface, restlessness-and-remorse is always bound together as the single fifth hindrance, and this paper does not read it back as two.)

So how does remorse tip from sound to sick? The Āgamas give a highly operational mechanism — the hindrance's *food* and *anti-food*, the condition that nourishes it and the condition that starves it. *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle 27 (SĀ 715):

> 「何等為掉悔蓋食？有四法……謂親屬覺、人眾覺、天覺、本所經娛樂覺。自憶念、他人令憶念而生覺，於彼起不正思惟……」「何等為掉悔蓋不食？彼寂止思惟，未生掉悔蓋不起，已生掉悔蓋令滅。」

> *What is the food of the hindrance of restlessness-and-remorse? There are four things … thoughts of kin, of company, of gods, of pleasures once enjoyed. Recalling them oneself, or being made to recall them by another, gives rise to thought, and one engages in unwise attention to them …* *What is the anti-food of the hindrance of restlessness-and-remorse? Attention that stills: the unarisen hindrance does not arise, and the arisen hindrance is made to cease.*[^7]

This passage lays the tipping mechanism of the double edge bare. The *food* (nourishment) of restlessness-and-remorse is recollection plus unwise attention — turning the matter over and over oneself, or being prompted to turn it over by another, and turning it over in a way that is not wise. The *anti-food* (starvation) of it is attention that stills — the mind coming to rest. Put plainly: when remorse becomes a compulsive recollection of the past, it is fed mouthful by mouthful; the moment stilling arrives, it is starved out. The Pāli is wholly parallel: the food is "the un-stilling of the mind" (*cetaso avūpasamo*), the anti-food "the stilling of the mind" (*cetaso vūpasamo*).[^8] Wholesome remorse concludes through disclosure and release; sick remorse keeps itself alive through repeated recollection — the watershed lies exactly here.

The most explicit single line in the Āgamas on this looping remorse comes from fascicle 8 of the alternate *Saṃyukta-āgama*:

> 「若復有人，多生掉悔，以其常生掉悔心故，於諸法相不能分明。當知掉悔為散亂因，以是因緣，不知出要、對治之法。」

> *If a person gives rise to much restlessness-and-remorse — because they constantly produce an agitated, remorseful mind — they cannot see the marks of things clearly. Know that restlessness-and-remorse is a cause of scatter; on this account they do not know the way out, the means of counteracting.*[^9]

In a single sentence it names three features of the sick edge. First, the *loop* — *constantly produce an agitated, remorseful mind*, the remorse regenerating without cease, refusing to stop. Second, *cognitive harm* — *cannot see the marks of things clearly*; once the remorse turns without stopping, the mind can no longer see straight. Third, the *diagnosis* — *restlessness-and-remorse is a cause of scatter*, definitively classed as a cause of scatter. A surge of remorse that could have been the occasion for seeing a fault, once it loses its terminal and begins to spin, turns instead into a hindrance that leaves the person unable to see clearly or to find the way out.

And this sick remorse has its own emotional signature — it is anguish-producing. Fascicle 4 of the *Madhyama-āgama* lists restlessness-and-remorse among the fetters; where there is a fetter the mind gives rise to anguish, and when the fetter is removed the anguish ceases:

> 「……瞋恚、睡眠、掉悔、疑惑纏者……心生憂苦……除疑惑纏已，憂苦便滅。」

> *… one fettered by ill-will, sloth, restlessness-and-remorse, doubt … the mind gives rise to anguish … once the fetter of doubt is removed, the anguish ceases.*[^10]

This is the dark mirror of wholesome remorse. Wholesome remorse runs to its endpoint as *rejoicing, bowing, and departing*; the result of being bound by sick remorse is, instead, *the mind gives rise to anguish*. The same feeling — one closing in gladness, one trapped in suffering, the whole difference being only whether or not it has found a way out.

For this remorse that turns without stopping, the Āgamas prescribe a consistent medicine — not more analysis, but stilling. *Saṃyukta-āgama* fascicle 27 (SĀ 714), discussing the time when an agitated mind arises, holds that one should not then cultivate the awakening-factors that make the mind spring up further — investigation, energy, and the like (that would be "adding fuel to fire") — but should cultivate the side that lets the mind settle and abide, "able to bring it to inward stillness, one-pointed and composed."[^11] This paper takes only the logic — when restlessness-and-remorse arises, the response is to still, not to stir further — and leaves the fine workings of the awakening-factors to the volume on concentration. The Pāli gives this logic its finest image: the agitated mind is like water stirred by wind,

> *udapatto vāterito calito bhanto ūmijāto* — *a bowl of water stirred by the wind, churning, swirling, breaking into waves* …[^12]

— on a stirred surface even a clear-eyed person cannot make out their own face. Sick remorse is just such a pool of stirred water: the more anxiously you try to see, in it, exactly where you went wrong, the murkier the water and the less the face shows. To see the reflection, you must first let the water grow still.

To sum up the build of the sick edge: it is the same surge of remorse with the terminal stripped away. With no exit of disclosure, no letting-go of release, the remorse is fed over and over by recollection, welds to the mind's agitation and spins, and sinks into a cause of scatter, a fetter that produces suffering. It no longer drives the seeing of faults; it veils the seeing of faults. The engine is still idling, but it no longer carries anything forward — this is the shape of remorse become a hindrance.

## IV. Three Western Interlocutors

The double edge of remorse is, for readers carrying the common sense of Western moral psychology, a subject both familiar and strange. Familiar, because the West has built up fairly fine-grained research on *which self-reproach is useful and which is harmful*, with an almost item-by-item correspondence to the Āgamas' good-edge / sick-edge split. Strange, because the destination of that research and the destination of repentance are not the same. Precisely because the correspondence is so strong, each of the three readings below must add a line of demarcation: the descriptions may run close, but the aims do not meet — Western moral psychology seeks psychological adjustment and the relief of symptoms; repentance seeks purity, liberation, and the path. The drumbeat of this section is one phrase: *comparable, not reducible*.

**First, the guilt / shame distinction.** In the study of moral emotions there is a repeatedly tested divide (one could set the work of Tangney and Dearing here, in the 1990s–2000s): the difference between *guilt* and *shame* lies not in intensity but in the object of focus. Guilt focuses on *behavior* — "I did a bad thing" — the self remaining intact, and so it drives toward repair, approach, talking the thing out; shame focuses on the *self* — "I am a bad person" — the whole self devalued, and so it drives toward withdrawal, hiding, defense. In a phrase: shame makes a person retreat, guilt makes a person change. Read back against the Āgamas, one could map it thus: remorse-in-accord-with-the-Dharma resembles that approach-and-repair guilt (focused on behavior, so disclosure is psychologically bearable rather than annihilating), while the concealment treated in P02 resembles a shame-style withdrawal — for a person can confess a *behavior*, but has no way to confess a *self*. ⚠️ But this is a heuristic mapping, not an equation, and three lines of demarcation must be drawn at once. First, repentance's destination is purity, liberation, and the path — not a "better-functioning" psychological adjustment. Second, the mechanism of wholesome remorse is not the cognitive relabeling of re-attributing shame as guilt, but disclosing-then-releasing, closing itself out; the Āgamas never teach a person to keep guilt warm indefinitely. Third, repentance is not guilt-management.

**Second, rumination and scrupulosity.** This is the strongest correspondence of the three. *Rumination* (in the work associated with Response Styles Theory, e.g. Nolen-Hoeksema, 1990s) means passively focusing, over and over, on one's own distress and thereby magnifying it — it deepens negative thinking, blocks problem-solving, and dams up the action one could take. *Scrupulosity* (the religious-and-moral face of obsessive-compulsive disorder, in the clinical literature, no single author) goes a step further: compulsive confession is driven by the temporary relief of anxiety rather than by a genuine seeking of the good; the same long-since-absolved fault is confessed again and again, the closing ritual never reaching its end. Read back against the Āgamas, the fit is almost seamless: restlessness-and-remorse is precisely the maladaptive rumination the Āgamas had already named — SĀ 715's "recollection feeds it, stilling cuts it off" runs just as the mechanism of Response Styles Theory describes; and compulsive confession is exactly the *form* of remorse without the *substance* of stilling. ⚠️ Yet the stronger the correspondence, the more sharply the line must be drawn. First, the aim of cutting off restlessness-and-remorse is not to bring anxiety down but to cut off the hindrance on the path of liberation (release). Second, restlessness-and-remorse is *released*, not managed, tolerated, or coped with — which differs from the route of the cognitive-behavioral tradition that teaches one to "tolerate, defuse from, live alongside" it. Third, do not invert the order of priority: the Āgamas precede these clinical studies and redefined this surge of remorse within their own frame; the clinical research is a later witness that recognized the phenomenon, not the mother-frame that explains the Āgamas. To write repentance as "the Buddhist version of cognitive-behavioral therapy" is this section's largest trap.

**Third, agent-regret.** (This reading may be passed over lightly; it is set here to round out the three.) In *Moral Luck* (1981), Bernard Williams proposed *agent-regret*: a person feels, over the bad outcome their own action brought about, an irreducible regret, even where they are responsible causally rather than morally (as a lorry driver who, without fault, runs someone down). This regret has no fault to confess, no ritual to close it out — it is morally appropriate, yet structurally open, unsealable. ⚠️ One must concede that Western moral life does contain such regret, which does *not* carry its own terminal. But this throws the feature of the Āgamas' wholesome remorse into relief — wholesome remorse is born to conclude itself (see the fault → make amends in accordance with the Dharma → guard against recurrence → quiet down), and it is fitted with a communal ritual that can genuinely close the loop (the disclosure treated in P02 and P03). Williams's agent-regret is a principled *non-closure*; the double edge of repentance, by contrast, holds that *remorse should have an edge that can close*, and supplies the very track of that closure. To be clear, the contrast here is structural (whether or not there is a ritual of closure), not evaluative (it does not say Buddhism is the higher).

The three readings may be gathered into one line: Western moral psychology is a fine-grained corroborating witness to the double edge of remorse, but its coordinate is health, and repentance's coordinate is the path. Where they correspond, there is no need for concealment; where they cross the line, there can be no reduction.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections: The Pivot of the Double Edge, and the Lines to Hold

Having set the good edge and the sick edge side by side, the paper's core thesis tightens to a single sentence: what makes the edge two-faced is the *presence or absence of a terminal*. Disclosure (not concealing) plus release, and the remorse concludes itself, becoming the good edge; concealment, or refusing to release after disclosing — repeatedly seizing it back — and the remorse spins, becoming the sick edge. There is only one surge of feeling; whether or not the terminal is triggered is the cutting line itself.

This thesis must be held by several corrections, lest it be read awry.

⚠️ **First, the double edge is a structural argument, not the ready-made narrative of any one discourse.** This must be told to the reader honestly: there is no single discourse in the Āgamas that takes one surge of remorse and stages it the whole way from sound to sick, acting out a complete line of descent. The paper's mode of argument is *component-subtraction* — setting the good-edge formula (see the fault → disclose it → guard against recurrence → grow the noble Dharma) beside the corpus of restlessness-and-remorse (food / anti-food, "constantly producing, a cause of scatter," the fetter that produces anguish), and showing that *the only subtracted component — the terminal (disclosure + release) — is precisely where the two edges lie*. The feeling is one and the same (Kāśyapa's "at once gave rise to remorse in his mind" is the bud of wholesome remorse, the same word 悔 as the restlessness-and-remorse among the hindrances); whether the terminal is triggered parts the two roads. This is to assemble, by their inner logic, components scattered across different discourses — not to claim that one discourse tells it so.

⚠️ **Second, "remorse" is one word valenced two ways; sound and sick are not a matter of the wording.** The Chinese Āgamas have no separate words for "wholesome remorse / unwholesome remorse," and the Pāli has but one *kukkucca*. The remorse of "at once gave rise to remorse in his mind" is the bud of wholesome remorse; the remorse of the fifth of the five hindrances is the fetter of sick remorse; the two use the same word. The whole difference between sound and sick is carried by what follows: attach "disclosure + release" and it concludes itself; attach "concealment + re-grasping" and it loops. The reader must not suppose the Āgamas have two names for two kinds of remorse — they do not; what they would have us see is precisely the two fates of one and the same feeling.

❌ **Third, one must not treat "remorse" as an independent hindrance.** The five hindrances always count restlessness-and-remorse as their fifth, with agitation and remorse bound into one; SĀ 713's "there is agitation, there is remorse; that agitation, that remorse, is just the hindrance" is an analytic restatement of this *one* hindrance, not a recount to six. What forms the hindrance is remorse-as-agitation — remorse bound to agitation, turning without stopping; it is not that remorse is by nature a hindrance. This point bears weight doctrinally: to judge remorse itself a hindrance would condemn remorse-in-accord-with-the-Dharma along with it, directly contradicting all the scriptural evidence on the good-edge side. (The later Abhidhamma separation of *uddhacca* and *kukkucca* into two mental factors is post-canonical; it should be labeled so if invoked, and not read back into the Nikāyas.)

❌ **Fourth, one must not reduce wholesome remorse to confession, nor reduce sick remorse to the clinical science of rumination.** The reading against Western confession, healing, and guilt-management stops at heuristic analogy; disclosure and confession may mirror each other in the *form* of their rite, but they do not meet in the *substance* of their destination (repentance seeks purity, liberation, the path; confession seeks to obtain absolution). Likewise, though restlessness-and-remorse and maladaptive rumination are close in mechanism, the Āgamas precede it and redefined it within their own frame; one must not invert the order of priority and read it as "the Buddhist version of cognitive-behavioral therapy."

As for the full-length narrative of wholesome remorse run all the way to its end — how a person of grave fault is benefited by being able to feel remorse — the Āgamas' signal example is King Ajātasattu. In the *Sāmaññaphala-sutta* the Buddha pronounces:

> 「今於賢聖法中能悔過者，即自饒益。」……「此阿闍世王……今自悔過，罪咎損減，已拔重咎。」

> *One who is now able, within the noble Dharma, to repent the fault, thereby benefits himself.* … *This King Ajātasattu … now repents the fault himself; his guilt is lessened, the grave fault drawn out.*[^13]

This is taken only as one instance of "able to feel remorse, therefore benefits himself": even the grave fault of patricide, once repented in accordance with the Dharma, has its guilt lessened. ⚠️ As for the full narrative of Ajātasattu's repentance — his tossing, restless state of mind, the turn at which he did not gain the pure Dhamma-eye — the Chinese *Dīrgha-āgama* treats it very sparingly, and this paper does not dramatize his torment from a single text; the full narrative is reserved for a later paper (P05) to treat as a concrete case. Here it is touched lightly, only to show how deep the good edge can reach.

## VI. Conclusion: Remorse with an Exit Is a Gate, with No Exit a Cell

**Restated in brief.** The remorse of the Āgamas is one and the same surge of feeling, one word valenced two ways. Whether or not it has a *terminal* — disclosure plus release — decides whether it is the engine of phenomenal repentance or the cell of the fifth hindrance. Remorse-in-accord-with-the-Dharma runs the road *see the fault → disclose it → guard against recurrence → grow the noble Dharma*; the teacher receives the remorse, the person rejoices, bows, and departs, and the remorse concludes itself. Restlessness-and-remorse is the same surge with the terminal stripped away: fed by recollection and rumination, never stilled, it keeps producing and scattering, and sinks into a fetter that produces suffering. Remorse as a gate of repentance is the psychological engine of phenomenal repentance, not its summit: when the engine has done its work it ought to cut off; idling, it becomes a hindrance.

**Guidance for practice.** From this it follows that, facing one's own remorse, the question to ask is not "am I remorseful enough" but "does this remorse have an exit." To see the fault, speak it, guard the future, and then set it down — this road is itself increase, not loss; whereas merely replaying the old error in the mind over and over, refusing to hand it over, refusing to set it down, turns that same surge of remorse from a help into a hindrance. In other words, the worth of remorse lies not in its concentration but in its direction: with an exit it is a gate; with no exit, a cell.

**Open question.** This paper lodges remorse at the Āgamas' psychological layer — it is still a private feeling in need of an exit. But that exit, disclosure, did not stop at private psychology in the traditions that followed; it was raised into a devotional limb of practice, one with an object and a rite. In the seven-limb worship it became the *confession-limb* (*pāpadeśanā*); in Samantabhadra's vows it became the great vow to "repent karmic obstructions." When "handing the remorse over" turns from a movement of the mind into an act of offering and aspiration before the Buddhas, what new weight will disclosure be given? This is what Part III takes up next. (This paper argues purely at the psychological layer of phenomenal repentance; it does not touch the side of "the nature of fault is originally empty" or "repentance of non-arising" — that belongs to the principle-repentance of the volume's later reaches — nor does it develop the sense of receiving precepts and the precept-essence, which is proper to the volume on moral discipline.)
