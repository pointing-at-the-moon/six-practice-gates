# The Deepest Remorse
***Ajātaśatru and the Limit of Phenomenal Repentance — It can lessen, it can redirect; it cannot wholly cleanse***

*中文主題：最深的悔——阿闍世王與事懺的極限：能減、能轉，不能全淨*

**Abstract**

The question this paper sets is: when the *good blade* of remorse forged in P04 — remorse-in-accord-with-the-Dharma, the self-terminating engine of phenomenal repentance (事懺) — falls on the heaviest karma a person can carry, on patricide, on an *ānantarika* act, how much of the blade is left? King Ajātaśatru of Magadha is the limit-specimen. He confessed before the living Buddha, with the most complete disclosure, and was received by the Buddha's own mouth ("one who can repent the fault thereby benefits himself … I receive your repentance") — the most ideal conditions phenomenal repentance can attain. Reading the Chinese *Sāmaññaphala-sutta* (DA27) limit line, the *Salt-Simile Discourse*, the determinacy floor of the *Intention Discourse*, and the Āgama's own self-sufficient exhibit of Devadatta ("present-body fall to hell, yet at the last a paccekabuddha"), this paper argues one hard conclusion: even ideal phenomenal repentance can only **lessen and redirect** the trajectory of the karma — it cannot **erase** the ripened *ānantarika*, and it cannot **refund** the very Dharma-eye the patricide structurally blocked ("had he not killed his father, he would have attained the pure Dharma-eye on this very seat"). Phenomenal repentance has a hard floor, and the five 逆 sit below it. The Buddha **receives** (*paṭiggaṇhāti*); he does not **absolve**: to receive a confession is not to remit a sin, and the result is **self-benefit** (自饒益), not **pardon** (蒙赦). The ultimate cleansing awaits principle-repentance (理懺), treated separately in this volume's Part VII; here the paper plants only a signpost and does not step onto it.

**Keywords:** Ajātaśatru (阿闍世王), phenomenal repentance (事懺), *ānantarika*-karma, the five 逆 (五逆), patricide, the salt-simile (鹽喻經), the Dharma-eye (法眼), rootless faith (無根信), receiving the remorse (受悔過), the lessening-and-redirection of karma (業之減轉), Devadatta, *paṭiggaṇhāti*, mitigate ≠ erase, receive ≠ absolve, the middle-way correction (中道校正)

## I. The Problem: The Deepest Remorse

The previous paper, *The Double-Edged Remorse* (P04), forged a blade. Remorse-in-accord-with-the-Dharma is not pathological self-reproach but the psychological engine of phenomenal repentance — *see the fault, disclose it, guard against its recurrence, grow the noble Dharma*. This good edge can sever old habit and turn the continuum, and it parts cleanly from the sick edge that idles, loops, and hardens into a hindrance. But what P04 tested was the working of this blade on *ordinary* faults. This paper drives the same blade to its breaking point and asks a sharper question: when the deed is not an ordinary fault but the heaviest karma there is — patricide, an *ānantarika* act — how much of the blade is left?

The Āgamas and the Vinaya hold a case almost purpose-built for the question: Ajātaśatru, king of Magadha. He killed his father Bimbisāra, who ruled in accord with the Dharma, and so committed the gravest of the five 逆; and afterward, before the living Śākyamuni, he made an unreserved disclosure-confession and was received by the Buddha's own mouth. The setting is extreme past the point of further extremity: the crime stands at the human limit, and so do the conditions of the repentance — the one to whom the fault is disclosed is the Buddha himself, and the one who receives it is the Buddha himself. If phenomenal repentance can anywhere pull a person wholly out of the deepest pit, it is here.

This paper's claim is that it cannot. Ajātaśatru is the **limit-specimen** of phenomenal repentance. Before the living Buddha, with the most complete disclosure, received by the Buddha's own mouth, the good blade **still only lessens the trajectory of the karma and redirects the destination of its fruit** — it secured the king his "rootless faith" and seeded the line of his eventual liberation — yet it **cannot erase the *ānantarika* of patricide, and cannot refund the Dharma-eye available on that very seat**. Here repentance meets its own floor: the Buddha can *receive* (take up his disclosure) but cannot *absolve* (cancel his karma); and the deeper door, the one that can cleanse the root of a deed, lies not in phenomenal repentance but in principle-repentance — Part VII's separate treatment, to which this paper plants a single signpost without stepping past it. ⚠️ This is the one line the whole paper must hold: to mark the limit precisely and stop there, not to let it slide into "the nature of sin is empty anyway, so there is nothing to repent" — a dilution that would betray, at the decisive point, the very hard conclusion the paper exists to set.

## II. The Moral Floor: Who Ajātaśatru Is

To see the weight of this limit-case, one must first see that the protagonist is neither a flattened villain nor the prodigal of some sentimental redemption-drama. The Ajātaśatru of the Āgamas and the Vinaya is a man of fully real moral mass.

His crime does not stop at patricide. The *Ekottarika-āgama* records that he sent five hundred cauldrons of food a day to provision Devadatta — a long-running, institutionalized patronage of the schism-maker, at state scale.[^1] The command that loosed the drunken elephant Nāḷāgiri came from his own mouth: Devadatta went to the king and laid the plan — get the king's elephant drunk on strong liquor and release it to trample the Buddha on his road; the king, hearing the counsel, gave the order through the kingdom.[^2] Weapon and decree alike came from his hand. The Vinaya draws the line into a whole coup-blueprint: the Mahīśāsaka *Vinaya* narrates, in a connected sweep, the "new king, new Buddha" conspiracy, the hired assassins, and the rolling of the rock that drew the Buddha's blood.[^3] Set together, his crime-radius touches three of the five 逆 at once — patricide, abetting the breaking of the harmonious Saṅgha, and drawing the Buddha's blood.

It is precisely because the one whom the Buddha *received* was *him*, and not some minor offender, that the case becomes the strongest demonstration of the good blade still operating *above the moral floor*. To write him soft — to stage him as a prodigal for whom one turn of the heart clears the sky — is to pull out the very weight the paper means to measure.

Here a first honest crack must be marked — the **asymmetry of shared karma**. ⚠️ Across the whole of the Āgamas and the Vinaya, what Ajātaśatru plainly discloses and plainly repents is always only the *one* deed of patricide; nowhere is he shown separately, explicitly repenting his share — the provisioning of Devadatta, the order that loosed the elephant. What the repentance covers, in other words, does not coincide with the full span of the deed. The fruit of the Devadatta line the Buddha handles on a separate track (foretelling the place to which it must fall), not by gathering it into Ajātaśatru's act of disclosure. Repentance is not a net that flattens every entanglement at one pass; it covers, precisely, the one deed the discloser has *seen*, *owned*, and *repented*. This asymmetry is itself already a sign that phenomenal repentance has an edge. (The Devadatta-complicity is taken here only for moral weight; the mechanics of schism and the "five-rules" dispute belong to the volume on moral discipline, and the Mahīśāsaka material is flagged as Vinaya-layer.)

## III. The Remorse That Was Received: The Disclosure-Formula from Ordinary Monk to Patricide

Ajātaśatru's remorse is a standard phenomenal repentance. The *Sāmaññaphala-sutta* records his words:

> 唯願世尊受我悔過，我為狂愚癡冥無識……而我迷惑五欲，實害父王，唯願世尊加哀慈愍，受我悔過。

> *May the World-Honored One receive my repentance. I was mad, foolish, benighted, and without understanding … deluded by the five desires, I truly killed my father the king. May the World-Honored One, in pity and compassion, receive my repentance.*[^4]

The Buddha's response is to *receive*; and its result he names, in a single phrase, as **self-benefit** (自饒益):

> 今於賢聖法中能悔過者，即自饒益。吾愍汝故，受汝悔過。

> *One who can now repent the fault within the noble Dharma thereby benefits himself. Out of pity for you, I receive your repentance.*[^5]

Mark the wording. The Buddha says, *one who can repent thereby benefits himself* — the source of the benefit is the repenter's own lawful disclosure, not a pardon-decree dropped from the Buddha's side. The three characters 自饒益, "benefits himself," are the hinge to which the later argument (§V) returns again and again: it is *gaining for oneself*, not *receiving absolution*. This division, carried from P04's definition of the good blade, still holds on the patricide.

What deserves still more emphasis is the **universality** of this good blade — it does not switch formula because the crime is grave. Set the confession of Ajātaśatru in the Pāli *Sāmaññaphala-sutta* (DN 2) beside the confession of an ordinary monk repenting a quarrel in the *Cullavagga*'s chapter on schism, and the two run nearly word for word: only the clause naming the deed committed is swapped, while the closing growth-clause — *vuddhi hesā, mahārāja, ariyassa vinaye*, "this, great king, is growth in the discipline of the noble ones" — stands unchanged.[^6] The verbatim Pāli of the king's confession and its reception carries this plainly:

> *accayo maṁ, bhante, accagamā … yohaṁ pitaraṁ dhammikaṁ dhammarājānaṁ … jīvitā voropesiṁ. tassa me … bhagavā accayaṁ accayato paṭiggaṇhātu āyatiṁ saṁvarāyā ti.*

> *A transgression overcame me, venerable sir … in that I deprived my father, a righteous king of the Dharma, of life. May the Blessed One receive this transgression of mine as a transgression, for the sake of restraint in the future.*

> *… accayaṁ accayato disvā yathādhammaṁ paṭikarosi, taṁ te mayaṁ paṭiggaṇhāma. vuddhi hesā, mahārāja, ariyassa vinaye …*

> *… since you have seen the transgression as a transgression and made amends in accordance with the Dharma, we receive it from you. For this, great king, is growth in the discipline of the noble ones …*

The ordinary monk repenting a small dispute and the patricide repenting an *ānantarika* walk the one disclosure-formula and are met with the one verdict, *this is growth in the discipline of the noble ones*. The good blade does not lose its edge because the thing it cuts is enormous; the Buddha's "one who can repent thereby benefits himself," spoken to Ajātaśatru, is the same blade running on the same logic he speaks to any monk. And it did turn the king's destination too — securing him the rootless faith, seeding the line of his eventual release (the next section takes this up).

Note, further, that the blade points *forward*. The core of disclosure closes on *restraint for the future* (*āyatiṁ saṁvara*, the guarding against any further doing) — it governs the repenter's mind and conduct *from here on*, and claims no power to reverse what is already done. This, too, is the lead-in to the next section's "what repentance cannot do."

(One matter of textual provenance must be marked honestly: ⚠️ in the narrative frame of the Chinese DA27, Ajātaśatru has no all-night sleeplessness, no body breaking out in sores, no scene of violent torment — there is a stretch of curiosity under a clear moonlit night and a traveler's dread of ambush, but not the classic agony of remorse. The intense torment later readers know comes in part from the weaker "grief, gloom, dread" of the variant *Jìzhìguǒ-jīng*, and more heavily from the "sores over the whole body" and the "moon-love samādhi" of the Mahāyāna *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*. The latter belongs to a Mahāyāna context; this paper takes it only as a light cross-reference, neither centering it nor passing it off as native to the Āgama. The one line in that sūtra that touches the spine of repentance is Jīvaka's: "there are two bright dharmas that can save beings — first shame, second scruple … shame is inward self-reproach; scruple is disclosure to others."[^7] "Scruple discloses to others" runs on the same engine as the Āgama's good blade; this one line is taken as corroboration, and that suffices.)

## IV. What Repentance Cannot Do: The Cost of the Dharma-Eye

This is the spine of the paper. The previous section established that Ajātaśatru's remorse was a standard phenomenal repentance, received by the Buddha, gaining the "self-benefit." So: what did this ideal phenomenal repentance turn away, and what did it leave standing in place?

### 4.1 The Limit Line: The Dharma-Eye Available on That Very Seat

After the king has taken his leave, the *Sāmaññaphala-sutta* records the Buddha's diagnosis to the monks. This is the keystone of the whole paper:

> 此阿闍世王過罪損減，已拔重咎。若阿闍世王不殺父者，即當於此坐上得法眼淨；而阿闍世王今自悔過，罪咎損減，已拔重咎。

> *This King Ajātaśatru's offenses are lessened, the grave fault already drawn out. Had King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he would have attained the pure Dharma-eye on this very seat; yet now, repenting the fault himself, his guilt is lessened, the grave fault already drawn out.*[^8]

Two acts must be held apart. The first — *lessened*, *the grave fault drawn out* — the guilt lightened, the heavy load lifted: this is what phenomenal repentance genuinely does. The second — the counterfactual, *had he not killed his father, he would have attained the pure Dharma-eye on this very seat* — is what phenomenal repentance **did not**, and **cannot**, do. The Buddha names the cost in his own words: the disclosure ran its whole course and was received, and still, on that seat, the fruit of the Dharma-eye could not arise in him. The reason lies not in any insufficiency of his sincerity, nor in any unreality of the Buddha's receiving, but in this: patricide had already, structurally, hollowed out the soil that the fruit of the Dharma-eye requires. ✅ The wording must be kept exact: the text says *lessened*, *the grave fault drawn out* — not *the sin extinguished*, not *the offense removed*. **It lessens; it does not remove.**

The Pāli DN 2 gives, at the same place, a dual-track corroboration. The Buddha says this king is *khata*, *upahata* — ruined, undone — and appends the same counterfactual:

> *khatāyaṁ, bhikkhave, rājā. upahatāyaṁ, bhikkhave, rājā. sacāyaṁ … rājā pitaraṁ dhammikaṁ dhammarājānaṁ jīvitā na voropessatha, imasmiññeva āsane virajaṁ vītamalaṁ dhammacakkhuṁ uppajjissathā ti.*

> *The king is ruined, monks; the king is undone. Had this king not deprived his father, a righteous king of the Dharma, of life, the dust-free, stainless Dharma-eye would have arisen in him on this very seat.*[^9]

The Chinese and the Pāli vouch for one another on this limit line: after the ideal phenomenal repentance, the patricide still blocked the Dharma-eye on that seat.

What, then, was left to Ajātaśatru after the Dharma-eye was blocked? The *Ekottarika-āgama* keeps this account most completely — the counterfactual and the remainder set down in one continuous breath:

> 王去不遠，佛告諸比丘：「今此阿闍世王不取父王害者，今日應得初沙門果證……雖爾，今猶獲大幸，得無根之信。是故，比丘！為罪之人，當求方便，成無根之信。我優婆塞中得無根信者，所謂阿闍世是也。」

> *When the king had gone but a little way, the Buddha said to the monks: "Had this King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he should this very day have won the first fruit of recluseship … Yet even so, he has now gained great good fortune: he has gained rootless faith. Therefore, monks, let a person of crime seek the means to accomplish rootless faith. Among my lay-followers, the one who has gained rootless faith — that is Ajātaśatru."*[^10]

"Rootless faith" (無根之信) is the real but bounded fruit left by this limit-case repentance. ⚠️ A common misreading must be corrected: "rootless faith" is not "groundless faith" — faith with no reason behind it — but "faith *without a root*": the patricide severed the root of realization, and what remains is a conviction that has lost the support of the path-root yet is still genuine resolve. This is already the great good fortune a "person of crime" can reach — the Buddha even urges crime-doers to seek the means to accomplish it — and yet it is not the first-fruit Dharma-eye that was available on that seat. The redirection is real; so is the cost.

### 4.2 The Mechanism: The Salt-Simile — The Salt Is Not Lessened, the Water Is Widened

Why can phenomenal repentance "lessen and redirect" yet not "erase"? For this the Āgama supplies a paradigm-diagram, the *Salt-Simile Discourse*:

> 猶如有人以一兩鹽投少水中，欲令水鹹不可得飲……〔修身、修戒、修心、修慧者〕猶如有人以一兩鹽投恒水中……恒水甚多，一兩鹽少，是故不能令鹹叵飲。

> *As when a man casts an ounce of salt into a little water, making it too salty to drink … [but for one who cultivates body, virtue, mind, and wisdom] as when a man casts an ounce of salt into the Ganges … the Ganges-water being very great and the ounce of salt small, the water cannot be made too salty to drink.*[^11]

Put plainly: the karma is like that ounce of salt — not one gram of it is gone; what changes is not the salt but the water. Cast the same salt into a cupful and it is salty past drinking (the hell-result); cast it into a great river and no saltiness can be tasted (a present, light experiencing). The difference never lies in "the salt was taken away" but in "the water that bears this salt was enlarged — by the cultivation of body, of virtue, of mind, of wisdom." What phenomenal repentance, and the cultivation that follows it, enlarges is exactly that continuum-river bearing the karma; it forever turns the **trajectory** of the karma, never the **deed already done**. The salt is still in the water.

Paired with this diagram is the determinacy floor drawn by the *Intention Discourse*:

> 若有故作業，我說彼必受其報，或現世受，或後世受；若不故作業，我說此不必受報。

> *If one does a deed intentionally, I say one must experience its result — in this life or a later; if one does a deed unintentionally, I say one need not necessarily experience the result.*[^12]

A deed done with intention must be experienced as its result — a baseline not to be diluted. Phenomenal repentance can widen the bearing-river, thin the saltiness to where it can be borne, turn the result into a present, light experiencing — but it cannot make the salt vanish into thin air. ⚠️ It is precisely before this determinacy floor that the paper must halt: to hold "phenomenal repentance cannot erase" as a hard finding, and not to dissolve it with "the nature of sin is empty." The ultimate purification of the root of sin is a different door — principle-repentance — and where that door leads is treated separately in this volume's Part VII; here the paper sets only a signpost and does not take up its meaning.

### 4.3 The Āgama's Self-Sufficient Exhibit: Devadatta

The structure "redirect, not erase" need not lean on commentary to be completed — the Āgama itself holds a complete, self-sufficient exhibit, and one cleaner and sharper than Ajātaśatru: Devadatta.

> 此是提婆達兜最初犯五逆惡。……復由提婆達兜最後命終之時，起和悅心，稱南無故，後作辟支佛，號名曰南無。

> *This was Devadatta's first committing of the five-逆 evils … And because, at the very end, in the last moment of his life, Devadatta raised a mind of gladness and uttered "namo," he afterward became a paccekabuddha, named Namo.*[^13]

Devadatta committed the five 逆 and fell bodily into the Avīci hell, there for a kalpa — not half a measure of the karma was erased; and yet, because at the moment of death he raised one thought of gladness and uttered one "namo," he at last became a paccekabuddha named Namo — his destination was turned. The kalpa in hell (the karma not erased) and the eventual paccekabuddhahood (the destination redirected) stand together in one and the same person: this is the plainest possible diagram of "redirect, not erase." ✅ For this reason, the self-sufficient Āgama proof of "phenomenal repentance redirects but does not erase" is best carried by Devadatta as the **primary** exhibit: his whole arc (Avīci for a kalpa → the paccekabuddha Namo) is the Āgama's own, requiring no commentary to complete it. Ajātaśatru, as the **protagonist of the limit-case**, has an after-death detail (the Sumaṅgalavilāsinī tradition of the Lohakumbha hell → the paccekabuddha Vijitāvī) that ⚠️ must be flagged as belonging to the commentarial layer (*aṭṭhakathā*), not native to the canon, and must not be passed off as the words of a *sutta*.

### 4.4 The Institutional Terminus: The Vinaya's Bar (Seen from the Repentance-Face)

That phenomenal repentance cannot purify the five 逆 left a mark at the institutional layer as well. The Vinaya states it outright:

> 故殺父母人不得出家受戒，若出家受戒應滅擯。

> *One who has intentionally killed father or mother may not go forth and receive the precepts; if he goes forth and receives them, he should be expelled.*[^14]

Even the most formal rite of ordination opens no door to a patricide — and since not even the entering act of the Saṅgha is granted him, ritual repentance (作法懺, the ceremonial repentance carried out by the formal acts of the Saṅgha) can do still less to lift this bar from a patricide. ⚠️ Only the repentance-face of this datum is taken here — "the rite does not lift the bar" — as a side-corroboration; the full picture of the ordination machinery, the complete enumeration of the thirteen-plus-ten disqualifications, and the question of whether the precept-essence (戒體) is constituted at all belong to the volume on moral discipline (SĪLA), and are not developed here.

### 4.5 The Disunity of the Variants: An Honest Crack

Finally, an honest debt must be carried — the Āgama is not, here, of one voice. ⚠️ The Chinese DA27, the *Ekottarika-āgama* fascicle 40, and the Pāli DN i. 86 all read *hard*: the patricide blocked the Dharma-eye on that seat, and only rootless faith remains. But the variant *Jìzhìguǒ-jīng* gives the opposite counterfactual, saying the Dharma-eye did, in fact, arise on the seat:

> 王阿闍世，已得生忍。雖害法王，了除瑕穢，無有諸漏，已住於法，而不動轉，於是坐上，遠塵離垢，諸法眼生。

> *King Ajātaśatru had attained patience-toward-arising. Though he had killed the king of the Dharma, his blemishes were thoroughly cleared, he was without any taints, he abode in the Dharma and was unmoved, and on that very seat, dust-free and free of stain, the Dharma-eyes arose in him.*[^15]

Both readings must be set out side by side: one may neither erase the soft reading to tell only the hard, nor let the soft reading dissolve the whole limit-case. This paper takes DA27 / *Ekottarika* as the principal hard reading of the limit-case and marks the *Jìzhìguǒ-jīng* as the dissenting variant. ⚠️ One word must be corrected again here: the "已得生忍," "had attained patience-toward-arising," of the *Jìzhìguǒ-jīng* is most likely a patience *toward arising* (patience-toward-what-arises), and must not be over-read as the patience of non-arising (*anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti*) — for the moment that latter is fixed in place, it touches principle-repentance and emptiness, the door reserved for Part VII.

And the place where this paper truly stands firm is this: **the conclusion "redirect, not erase" does not depend on the hard reading's winning.** ✅ Whether or not the Dharma-eye arose on that seat, by the mechanism of the salt-simile the *ānantarika* ripening of the patricide (the salt) was never taken away — the salt is still in the water; the Avīci-passage still must be undergone. So the central thesis stands firm on either reading. The disunity of the variants shakes only the surface — *whether the Dharma-eye arose on the seat* — and cannot shake the bedrock: *the karma was not erased*.

(A layer of terms must be kept distinct in passing: the word "five 逆" is already in the Āgama and may be used directly — the *Ekottarika* says "the five-逆 evils," the *Saṃyukta-āgama* gathers it under "the path of evil destinies."[^16] But "the five *ānantarika* deeds" (*pañca ānantarika*), as a closed system-term, is the systematizing achievement of the later Abhidharma — Kośa, Vibhāṣā, Yogācāra — and when invoked must be marked as belonging to the commentators' term-layer, not retrojected as the original wording of the Āgama. The foreground should remain, throughout, the Āgama's phenomenon: the patricide's Dharma-eye blocked, his present-body fall to hell, only rootless faith remaining; "*ānantarika*-karma" is a name added afterward.)

## V. None Can Absolve: Receive ≠ Absolve

The previous section set firm the *fact* of "what repentance cannot do." This section sets that structure beside three imported frames of thought — not to take their measure of the Dharma, but the reverse: to watch how this Āgama case shows its own outline under their reflection. ⚠️ One red line runs through the whole section and must be stated first: the three frames below are **comparable but not reducible** (可比而不可化約). No Western thinker has ever actually applied his frame to Ajātaśatru; this section only sets the frames *alongside* the case to see their likeness and unlikeness. In order of priority it is the Āgama case that comes first — and that, in turn, reframes these questions; the Western frames are summoned as recognizing witnesses, not as the standard of measure. The three share one through-line: **the descriptions converge, the *telos* diverges** — what the West seeks is, variously, forgiveness, repair, or closure; what repentance trends toward is the path, liberation, the lessening-and-redirection of karma.

### 5.1 The Unforgivable

The first frame is the thought of "the unforgivable." Jankélévitch on *l'imprescriptible*, the not-subject-to-prescription, and that line — "forgiveness died in the death camps"; Arendt on "radical evil," the crime we can "neither punish nor forgive"; Derrida's paradox — "forgiveness forgives only the unforgivable." Set these alongside the five 逆 as "not transacted by phenomenal repentance":

**Where they converge** — each marks out a bounded zone, a forbidden region where "the ordinary economy of repair–punishment–closure fails here"; and the five 逆, independently, in another vocabulary, mark out the same forbidden zone.

**Where they diverge (no bare concession)** — *First*, the *ānantarika* (無間) is, before anything else, a **causal-karmic descriptor** ("no interval" means the result follows without a gap, the ripening structurally locked), not a **moral verdict of refusal**; it stands nearer to a law of nature than to a moral judgment. *Second*, the ultimate of the five 逆 is not the terminus "forever unforgivable"; at the phenomenal layer it is a terminus, but at the principle layer there is another door of re-opening (principle-repentance / emptiness, Part VII — a gesture only, not developed here) — the Western "unforgivable" is a final station, whereas the *ānantarika* is only the terminus *of the phenomenal layer*. *Third*, there is here no offended party that "withholds forgiveness having been wronged": the dead father is not the gatekeeper of Ajātaśatru's attainment; no party holds the authority to grant or to refuse pardon. The outcome is **self-benefit**, not **pardon withheld**.

### 5.2 Forgiveness Without a Pardoner (the Axis of This Section)

The second frame is this section's most crucial face of dialogue, and the very place where a reader formed in a Christian idiom most easily steps into a category error: the Catholic model of absolution. In that model, the handling of mortal sin runs "contrition + confession + the priest's absolution"; the priest, *in persona Christi*, pronounces the remission of the sin, the guilt (*culpa*) is forgiven, while the due punishment (*poena*) is reckoned separately. Set this model beside the Buddha's "receiving" (*paṭiggaṇhāti*):

**Where they converge** — both traditions bar the door to no one, not even to the gravest offender, and both hold out a real road ahead. Just for that, the resonance of this pair is nearly perfect — and just for that, it is the most dangerous.

**Where they diverge (the axis of this section: to receive a confession ≠ to absolve a sin)** —

❌ *First, there is no transfer of guilt.* Karma cannot be borne by proxy nor absolved by proxy; one's own deed, one's own experiencing. The Buddha cannot, as one might move a stone, lift the patricide-karma off Ajātaśatru by a decree. What is called "forgiveness without a pardoner" is a real release that wears the shape of forgiveness — yet with **no pardoner who performs an act of absolution**.

*Second, redirection is not cancellation.* The re-ordering of the mind, together with the lessening-and-redirection of the karma (the new continuum softening the old karma), does not amount to the cancellation of the sin; Ajātaśatru still pays the price — the loss of the Dharma-eye on that seat, and the passage through hell the commentary transmits.

❌ *Third*, and the one word above all to hold: *paṭiggaṇhāti* is "to receive / to take up" — what the Buddha takes up is "that transgression *as* a transgression, seen as it is and owned as it is" (transgression-as-transgression: the discloser has already rightly seen and rightly named what he did), and **not a remission of sin** (*remissio*). To translate, or to read, "receiving the repentance" as "to forgive / to absolve" is exactly the core category error this section exists to block. **To receive the repentance is not to absolve the sin.**

### 5.3 Restorative Justice and Irreversibility

The third frame — restorative justice (Zehr) and the "irreparable wound" it collides with in cases of homicide; and the predicament of "agent-regret" (Williams), pushed to its extreme (a strand carried from P04).

**Where they converge** — irreversibility is real, and this case names it without flinching: the loss of the Dharma-eye on that seat is a permanent cost, more unevasive than much of the therapeutic literature.

**Where they diverge (no bare concession)** — *First*, what repentance reaches is the agent's mind and the guarding of the future (*āyatiṁ saṁvara*, no further doing); it is a **forward causation**, not a **retrospective** one, and it never claims to reverse a past already made. *Second*, repentance's *telos* is **the path and liberation** — it is neither the "reintegration" restorative justice seeks, nor the "living-with-the-irreparable" in which agent-regret settles, still less the "psychological closure" of self-forgiveness. ⚠️ A register must be held here: neither this section nor the conclusion may slide into the therapeutic idiom ("learn to forgive yourself / make peace with the past / let it go / heal") — that would quietly swap out a grave judgment about the boundary of karma for a bowl of comfort-soup. Repentance is not a therapy; it is a precise description of *how the agent's mind may lawfully move forward*, together with an unflinching admission that *the past is irreversible*.

## VI. Conclusion: The End of the Floor, the Signpost Pointing Elsewhere

Gathered into a single sentence: Ajātaśatru is the limit-specimen of phenomenal repentance — before the living Buddha, with the most complete disclosure, received by the Buddha's own mouth, the good blade **lessened the trajectory of the karma and turned the destination of the fruit** (securing him his rootless faith, seeding the line of his eventual release), yet **could not erase the *ānantarika* of patricide, and could not refund the Dharma-eye available on that seat**. Here repentance meets its floor: the Buddha can receive, he cannot absolve; to receive the repentance is not to absolve the sin.

**Guidance for practice.** This limit-case is worth reading word by word not to gawk at a doer of the five 逆, but to correct an expectation just as fatal to the ordinary practitioner — the supposition that a remorse sincere enough can cancel, at one stroke, a deed already done. It cannot. What it *can* do is a different thing, real and immense: to widen the continuum-river that bears the karma, to thin the saltiness to where it can be borne, to turn the destination toward liberation. Seeing clearly the line "it can lessen and redirect; it cannot erase" in fact frees remorse from the anxiety of "vainly trying to scrub out the past," and sets it back in the place where it is truly powerful — the forward guard (no further doing) and the widening of the continuum. The salt is still in the water; only the water has widened. This is at once phenomenal repentance's limit and its whole, sufficient truth.

**The question left for the next door.** That salt still in the water, then, that Dharma-eye unrefunded on the seat — the ultimate purification of the root of the five-逆 karma — is it truly sealed off here? The Āgama stops at this point and plants a signpost, its arrow pointing toward a door not yet opened. The good blade of phenomenal repentance can cut, but it cannot cut to the root of the karma; the floor ends here, and the door opens onto something deeper. That door is named principle-repentance (理懺) — how it can, at the very place where phenomenal repentance withdraws, return to the root of the karma and ask its question anew, is the separate matter of this volume's Part VII, not stepped onto here. This paper's task is only to mark, precisely, the floor's end up to exactly this point.
