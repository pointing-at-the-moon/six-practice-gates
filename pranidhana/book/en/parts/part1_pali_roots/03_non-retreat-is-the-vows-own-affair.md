## 1.3 Non-Retreat Is the Vow's Own Affair

By what does the world keep a long-held aim? Most people would say: by will. Bite down on the heart, clench the jaw, force through the moments of wanting to quit—enough years of forcing through is called perseverance; forced through to the end is called staying true to one's first resolve. In this picture, whether an aim can be kept depends entirely on how hard the will is.

To carry this picture straight onto the bodhisattva path is the most natural thing in the world. Beings are boundless, the Buddha-way unsurpassed, the road measured in incalculable aeons—surely a road that long takes a will forged like steel to walk to the end?

What this chapter has to say is the very opposite. The vow's non-retreat does not come from hammering the will into steel. Steel tires, steel snaps; hold out however long, there is a day it wears through. The vow's non-retreat is not seated on "holding out" at all. It is seated on the vow itself.

### What the Lamp Buddha Sealed Was Non-Retreat

First pick up the thread left at the end of the last chapter. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, recounting the scene of Dīpaṃkara's prediction, puts it this way:

> "From the time he saw the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, scattered five stalks of flower over the Buddha, and spread his hair upon the ground, the Buddha gave him the prediction of *avaivartika*; he rose into the air and praised the Buddha in verse."

*Avaivartika* means non-retreating—not turning back. Please notice: what Dīpaṃkara gave was not only the prophecy "you will become a Buddha," but the sealing of *non-retreat*—from that moment, this vow could not be retreated from, could not be taken away.

And this forces the question out into the open. A prediction is a confirmation arriving from outside; how could the Buddha seal, on the spot, that this vow shall never retreat? An outer seal must have an inner root. What the Buddha sealed must be some quality this vow itself already possessed—a quality that needs no outside guard, that of itself does not waver.

That quality has a name in the treatises.

### Three Things Make a Bodhisattva

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* once set down what a bodhisattva is using only three things:

> "He has a great vow; his mind cannot be moved; his effort does not retreat. By these three things he is called bodhisattva."

*Bodhisattva*—*bodhi-sattva*—means a being given over to awakening. The order of the three things asks to be read closely: the great vow stands first—it is the arousing; the mind that cannot be moved sits in the middle—it is the non-retreat; effort that does not retreat stands last—it is the walking.

The three are not three talents laid side by side; they are one structure. The vow stands at the front and fixes the direction. "The mind cannot be moved" is the quality of that vow itself—not a separate firmness trained up outside the vow and then brought in to clamp it tight; a vow genuinely aroused toward awakening is, of itself, unmovable. And effort? Effort is not what manufactures non-retreat; effort is what this vow—non-retreating from the start—looks like as it walks, step by step, through the years.

The treatise has another line that lays this bare:

> "The resolved vow is called the great adornment."

Adornment, here, means armor. The bodhisattva enters the sea of birth and death wearing the great armor—and this armor is not a weapon forged separately; it is the very resolvedness of the vow. The vow's being unalterable, unmovable—that is the armor that carries a bodhisattva across measureless aeons. The armor does not stand outside the vow; the armor is the shape of the vow.

### To Vow Is to Enter the Assured

There is another treatise that compresses this whole matter into a single line. The *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*—the treatise on the ten stages—says:

> "To raise the great-minded vow is to enter the assured company."

The "assured company" stands against the "uncertain company"—it names that class of persons whose course is already settled: bound for bodhi, never again to fall back. The eye of the sentence is the word *is*. Raising the great-minded vow and entering the ranks of the assured are not two stations, one after the other—not first the vow, then aeons of hard practice, slowly earning one's way up into the seat of the non-retreating. The treatise says *is*: the vow raised, the person already stands among the assured.

This turns the world's picture clean over. Non-retreat is not a reward far down the road of practice, to be earned inch by inch through effort; non-retreat is the vow's root—the great mind once aroused, it is already there. The measureless aeons of effort that follow do not purchase non-retreat; they are this non-retreating vow, unfolding naturally through time.

Look back now at Dīpaṃkara's prediction, and the whole of it comes through. The Buddha could seal *avaivartika* on the spot precisely because Megha's vow was a great-minded vow fully aroused, with nothing held in reserve—and such a vow, in the moment of its arousing, has already entered the assured. What the prediction stamped was not an extra surety added on by the Buddha; it was the root the vow possessed of itself.

In the southern transmission of the Dharma there is likewise a name kept for just this quality of the vow: *adhiṭṭhāna*—resolve. Among their ten perfections it stands as one; and in the ten perfections of the Chinese transmission, the very same position is held by the perfection of vows. Two names, one face: how a vow, once aroused, cannot be moved. North and south, the two streams saw the same thing in this place.

### The Ground of Non-Retreat Is No-Self

But—on what ground? On what ground can a vow, of itself, stand unmoved?

Set the will of steel beside it, and it comes clear. An aim held up by will has its ground in the "I"—it is "I" who hold on, "I" who must persist, "I" who must not lose. And whatever is seated on the "I" carries the "I"'s weaknesses: I grow tired, I lose heart, I wear down under enough years. Weightier still, such an aim has an endpoint waiting ahead of it—the day "I" achieve it, the day "I" collect. Where there is an endpoint to reach, there is an endpoint to abandon; where there is a thing to be gotten, there is the despair of not getting it. The road of retreat is buried in the ground together with the "I."

The vow's ground is not there. The last chapter saw it plainly: this vow faces a direction that will empty the very one who vows—on the day of harvest, the "I" of today is long gone. Such a vow has, in its very structure, no "gain of mine" standing at the end waiting to be redeemed. With no private endpoint, there is no place to retreat to; not held up by the strength of "I," there is nothing to be worn out. The hardness of steel is the hardness of a fortress—and a fortress, however hard, has its day of falling. The vow's unmovedness is having no fortress to defend at all. Its firm place is precisely its selfless place.

This layer can be brought down into daily use. Keeping a long vow—a Buddha-name recited for years, an aspiration held for years—when it begins to feel past holding, do not first ask "is my will strong enough?" Go back instead and check another thing: what does this vow face—some arrival of "mine," or a direction larger than "I"? If the former, the failing is natural; that ground was always going to tire. If the latter—if the direction has truly been handed over, no longer ending at "I"—then its non-retreat does not lean on your forcing through. Its unmovedness is its own affair. Your part is only to keep the fire trained on the true direction; the non-retreat that remains, the vow keeps for you.

### Part One, to This Point

With this, the three chapters of Part One have set the vow's root down firm.

Chapter one made it clear: what a vow turns is a direction, not an exchange for a thing—the vow founds the direction, the pledge holds the reception, transference turns the fruit. Chapter two returned to the scene before Dīpaṃkara: a lamp's worth of oil, five flowers, a spread of hair laid in the mud—the vow is aroused in the moment it is fully said, and in the moment of its arousing the person is already different. Chapter three made it clear: this vow cannot retreat—non-retreat is not wrung out of the will; it is the vow's own nature, and its ground is no-self.

From a point of arousing, to the unshakable root of that point, the ground of the vow is now laid. Yet one question stands unanswered. The vow is made; the road must still be walked one day at a time. On every ordinary morning, what force in the heart pushes a person through that day's stretch of road? And how is it told apart from craving?

The end of chapter one—Ānanda and the brahmin in the monastery—has already shown a corner of it: there is a kind of desire that, on reaching the place it was going, goes out of itself. In the Āgamas this force has a name of its own. In the next part we enter the country of the Āgamas, and look at it until it is plain.
