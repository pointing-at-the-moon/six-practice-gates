## 1.1 The Vow: Turning the Direction Around

On the first morning of the new year, the temple is crowded. The incense is lit, the palms come together, and each person silently says one sentence in the heart—asking for peace, for health, for a child's affairs to go a little more smoothly. We have watched this gesture since childhood, and done it ourselves more times than anyone could count.

In the Buddha's gate there is a gesture whose outward look is almost identical. Also before the Buddha, also with palms joined, also one sentence spoken. What the bodhisattva says is: "I vow to liberate the boundless beings."

Two gestures, alike to the last degree. Yet what moves inside them are two different roads. The "vow" this volume is about begins with telling these two roads apart.

### What the Vow Reaches For Is Not a Thing

First, a line of scripture. The *Ekottarāgama*—the "Numbered Discourses" among the Āgamas—records a practitioner who, having seen a Buddha, was so overjoyed he could not contain himself, and on the spot—

> "gave rise to this vow: 'May I in a future age become a Buddha, even as the Buddha of Lamplight—with disciples in attendance, all just the same.'"

The full scene will be told in the next chapter. For now, please notice only two places.

First, the words *gave rise to*. A vow is not a faint longing parked in the heart, not the thought "if only things could be so." It is *aroused*—like an arrow leaving the string, like a ship putting its rudder over: an act actually and concretely performed.

Second, what this vow asks for. He asks not for long life, not for wealth, not for smooth fortune. What he says is "may I in a future age become a Buddha"—turning his whole person around, toward awakening.

This is the most fundamental dividing line between vowing and wishing. A wish asks for a thing: health, safety, the success of some affair. The thing arrives, and the "I" is still the same old "I," only one item the richer. The vow is different. What the vow reaches for is not a thing that can be taken in hand; it is the matter of *where I am turned* itself. The moment the vow is aroused, this person is no longer the one who merely wanted something—he has become a person whose direction is awakening.

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*—the great treatise on the perfection of wisdom—says it without ceremony: at the moment the mind is first aroused and the vow made to become a Buddha, this person is already named a bodhisattva. Not that after years of cultivation and beings liberated the title is conferred on him in retrospect; in the very moment of vowing, the identity stands founded.

So it can be put this way: a wish changes what I have; a vow changes what I am. The one adds a thing; the other turns a direction. The same joined palms, the same single sentence—the whole difference lies here.

### Vow, Pledge, Transference: Three Moments of One Turning

Turning the direction around is only the beginning. Standing beside "vow" in the Buddha's gate are two more words: the pledge, and transference. The three are often run together; in fact each governs one stretch, and only together do they make one complete thing.

The vow governs the arousing. It stands at the very front and fixes the direction—in this life, and in life after life, toward awakening, toward beings.

The pledge governs the holding. The direction once fixed, something must hold it, or what is quickly aroused is quickly scattered. The *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra*—the sūtra on the layman's precepts—says, speaking of how the precepts come to be received:

> "In matters such as these, if one does not make the undertaking, and does not receive it from another, then it is not established."

Why must receiving the precepts be pledged, received with full solemnity from a teacher's hands? Because a thing merely intended in the heart does not yet stand; only when it is spoken before others, taken out of the mouth, shouldered, does it become established. The pledge is that shouldering. The vow is the arrow; the pledge is the fletching on its tail—an arrow without fletching leaves the string but cannot fly straight.

Transference governs the pouring-in. Along the road of practice, the work done and the merit gathered must be turned, time after time, back into that first direction. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* has a weighty line on this matter, that "the substance of the good cannot be given to another"—a wholesome root is a thing grown in one's own heart, like strength grown in one's own body; it cannot be passed from my hand into yours the way an object is. Then what does transference turn? It turns the direction the fruit will face when those roots ripen. I do not keep the fruit for my own enjoyment; I aim it at beings, at bodhi.

So transference is not carrying a thing across; it is turning a direction across. Hold on to this point first—when we come, much later, to the ten great vows of Samantabhadra Bodhisattva, it will open out in full.

Set the three side by side and look: the vow founds the direction, the pledge holds the reception, transference turns the fruit. One before, one in the middle, one after, running the whole length of the road. And this tells one thing more—a vow is not made once and finished. A wish is a one-time act: wished, and then awaited. A vow is to be walked a whole lifetime, walked through measureless lives—once aroused, the pledge keeps watch over it, and transference draws the heart back to it, one time after another.

### The Force a Vow Uses Comes to Rest of Itself

A doubt may still linger in the heart: to hold one direction in mind day and night—is that not also a kind of craving? Does the Dharma not teach the putting down of desire?

There is a dialogue in the *Saṃyuktāgama*—the "Connected Discourses"—that settles this matter through. The Buddha was staying in the country of Kosambī, in Ghosita's Park, and the venerable Ānanda was there as well. A brahmin came to see Ānanda and asked him: you who practice under the Buddha—for the sake of what? Ānanda said: for the cutting off of craving. The brahmin asked: by what means is it cut? Ānanda answered: relying on desire, craving is cut—by the very desire to cut it.

The brahmin would not have it: if you use desire to cut off craving, then does this desire itself not go on without end? Ānanda did not argue with him. He asked him a single question:

> "Was it not with a desire that you first set out to come to this monastery?"

You arose this morning with the thought "I will go to the monastery," and so you walked here—is that not so? The brahmin said it was so. Ānanda asked again:

> "Now that you have arrived at the monastery, has that desire not ceased?"

The man is here; the thought "I will go"—does it remain? The brahmin answered: it has ceased.

That is the answer. Worldly craving, having gotten one thing, wants the next, and is never filled—because what it feeds is the "I." But there is a kind of desire that, on reaching the place it was going, goes out of itself—the thought of coming to the monastery ceases at the monastery; the desire to cut craving ceases when craving is cut. The force a vow uses is exactly this latter kind. It pushes a person along the road, and when the road is walked, it does not stay behind to burn.

So the vow is nothing to fear. It too is a fire—but the direction this fire faces is the place of its own going-out.

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

Having come this far, there is one thing to see clearly, lest the very first step go crooked.

There is a way of thinking in the world that treats vowing as a transaction: I promise this before the Buddha, I offer up that, and in time it is exchanged for something in return—as though an order had been placed at an unseen window, and one waits for the goods to be delivered. The thought is natural; in the incense-smoke of any temple there is some measure of it.

But the vow of the Buddha's gate does not face that way. A transaction requires three things: a deliverable that changes hands, two parties to the contract, an agreed price. The vow has none of the three. What it hands over is not goods but a direction; what it faces is not a counterparty waiting to perform but awakening itself; and it demands no price in return—the one who vows never set out, from the start, to "get back" anything at all.

Then what of blessings? Vowing, doing good—will blessings come? They will. But blessings are fruit set along the way, not the destination of the walking. As a person walks toward a direction, the shadow follows along behind of itself—yet no one walks for the sake of the shadow. Fix the eyes on the shadow, and the road can no longer be walked; fix the heart on the direction, and the shadow follows of its own accord.

This distinction—the vow turns a direction; it does not trade for a thing—is the spine of this entire volume. In the nineteen chapters ahead, through the four great vows, through Amitābha's primal vows, through Samantabhadra's vows of practice, the scene grows grander chapter by chapter; but underneath, the question being asked never changes: this vow—where does it face?

### Walking On

Where, then, was such a vow first aroused?

It is not a principle hanging in mid-air. It has a concrete starting point—a time, a place, a person, an event. Ages and ages ago, in a city, a young practitioner with five flowers in his hand came face to face with a Buddha. Śākyamuni Buddha later told this event, with His own mouth, to His disciples.

In the next chapter we return to that scene—before Dīpaṃkara, that one vow.
