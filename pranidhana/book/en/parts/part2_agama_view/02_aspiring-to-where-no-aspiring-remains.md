## 2.2 Aspiring to Where No Aspiring Remains

There is a discourse whose very name is the *Discourse on Wishes*.

At that time, the Buddha was wandering in the country of Śrāvastī, staying in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park. A certain monk, sitting alone in a secluded place in quiet reflection, had a thought arise in his heart: would that the World-Honored One might comfort me, speak with me, teach me the Dharma. In the late afternoon he rose from his seat and went toward the Buddha.

The World-Honored One saw him coming from afar, and knew the wish in his heart. The Buddha did not answer him alone; through him, He spoke to all the monks an entire discourse—a discourse that lays the vow out, from low to high, as a single ladder.

Even a wish as small as "would that the World-Honored One might speak with me," the Buddha caught and held, and set it at the foot of the ladder. The bearing of this discourse shows itself from that alone.

### A Ladder of "May I"

The sentence-form of the discourse, from beginning to end, is one and the same: "May I." What changes is only what follows it.

The start is near at hand, and very human:

> "Monk, may I have kinfolk such that, when their bodies break up after death, they will surely rise to a good destination, reborn among the gods…"

May my kinfolk, on my account, be born after death in a good destination, among the gods. Further up: may the donors who support me reap great blessing; may I myself bear hunger and thirst, cold and heat, endure sickness and harsh words; may discontent, fear, and evil thoughts, when they arise, not bind the mind. Further up: may I attain the first absorption, and so to the fourth. Further up still, into the ranks of the noble:

> "Monk, may I, with the three fetters exhausted, become a stream-enterer…"

May I cut the three fetters and realize the first fruit—never again to fall to the evil destinies, with at most seven more rounds among gods and humans before the end of suffering. Further up: may I be a once-returner; may I not return to this world. All the way to the ladder's crown:

> "Monk, may I, with the taints exhausted, attain the taintless, the liberation of mind and the liberation by wisdom."

May every affliction be forever exhausted, mind and wisdom utterly freed—the station of the arahant, the highest rung of this ladder.

Now step back and look at the ladder whole. Two things ask to be seen.

First, the Buddha kicked away not a single rung. The wish that kinfolk be born among the gods is a vow; the wish that every taint be exhausted is a vow. The Buddha did not call the lower rungs grasping and have them put away; He set them in order, rung upon rung, and gathered every one into a single discourse. The low vows are not wrong; they are the foot of the road.

Second, from lowest to highest, the sentence-form never changes, and the aspiring mind never changes. What changes is only the thing aspired to—the object climbing level by level, from the felicities of gods and humans all the way to the taintless liberation. The height of a vow lies not in the strength of the willing but in the vow's direction. This locks, head to tail, with the last chapter's word that the two kinds of wanting differ in direction.

To cultivate the vow, then, is not to press the vow smaller and smaller until it is gone—it is to lead the thing aspired to upward, one rung at a time.

### Sought to the Summit, the Seeking Loosens

But at the top of this ladder, something marvelous happens.

The crowning rung aspires to nibbāna—the utter stilling of all grasping and affliction. Consider it closely: when what a person seeks is precisely the stilling of seeking itself, what becomes of that seeking in the moment it arrives?

It loosens of itself. There is a line in the *Saṃyuktāgama* that states this turn with utter economy:

> "For one who grasps nothing, there is nothing sought; for one who seeks nothing, nibbāna reveals itself."

When the mind no longer clutches at the world, the seeking subsides with it; the seeking subsided, nibbāna is naturally present. No one comes to pinch this seeking out—it is the sought object itself that loosens the seeking. Ordinary seeking, once it attains, possesses; possessing, it breeds the next seeking. Only the seeking of nibbāna, on attaining, falls away.

The Buddha treated the vows of others along this same principle. The discourses record a wanderer of another path who came to the Buddha set with all his heart on rebirth in the Brahmā heaven. The Buddha did not rebuke him with "vowing is grasping," nor tell him to put his vow away; the Buddha did one thing only—He exchanged the terminus of the man's vow, from the Brahmā heaven to nibbāna, and taught him to rely on the eightfold path, inclining toward nibbāna. The frame of the vow, the Buddha left him; what was changed was only the far end of the direction.

### The Undirected Is the Vow's Highest Rung

Follow this principle one step further up, and we reach one of the deepest words in the Āgamas.

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park, and told the monks: there are three concentrations—the concentration of the empty, of the undirected, and of the signless—three doors leading toward liberation. The second of these the discourse defines thus:

> "What is the concentration of the undirected? The undirected means: toward all phenomena whatsoever, one neither aspires nor seeks. This is called the concentration of the undirected."

Toward all phenomena, no further aspiring or seeking—the mind no longer pours itself toward any "becoming," no longer leans on any object. And the Buddha charged them: one who does not gain these three concentrations remains long in birth and death, unable to awaken of himself.

At first hearing, this sounds like the abolition of the vow entire. In truth it is the exact opposite. Set the ladder of the *Discourse on Wishes* alongside it, and the place of the undirected comes clear: it is not some other thing standing outside the ladder; it is the ladder's highest rung. The vow's object climbs and climbs; climbing to nibbāna, the seeking begins to loosen of itself; loosened to the very end, it is the undirected—the mind pouring its whole strength toward "no longer pouring toward any becoming" as its final facing. The highest vow is the vow aspired to where no aspiring remains.

It does not bypass the vow; it passes through the vow—it is reached by climbing the ladder of "may I," rung after rung, to where the ladder ends.

Then what of the bodhisattva's original vow? If the undirected is the crown, must the vow made before Dīpaṃkara go out as well? The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* has a line that meets this question head-on. The bodhisattva, the treatise says—

> "though dwelling in the three doors of liberation and contemplating nibbāna, does not cut off the original vow and good conduct."

Standing in the door of the undirected, contemplating nibbāna—and the original vow and good conduct unbroken. For what the undirected withdraws is grasping—the seeking that aspires to rebirth, aspires to possess; it cannot withdraw, and does not touch, the original vow that seeks nothing for itself and stands for the sake of beings. What goes is the clutching, not the vow. How a mind that grasps at nothing can still raise a vow at all—that deeper layer must wait for the later part of this volume on the vow and emptiness. For now, only fix the boundary: the undirected and the original vow do not obstruct each other.

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

The word "undirected" has one place where it is most easily read crooked.

It is not the heart gone cold; not the collapse of "I no longer want anything." A person who can summon interest in nothing, and a person abiding in the concentration of the undirected, may look equally quiet from outside; inside, they are two different worlds. The first has lost the direction; the second has walked the direction to its furthest point—still inclined toward nibbāna, still the working of the path's power: the stillness of a fire brought to its perfect heat, not the cold of a fire gone out.

What the undirected withdraws is "wanting in the mode of possession"; it does not withdraw the facing-toward. One word of difference—and a whole road of difference.

### Part Two, to This Point

With these two chapters, the vow's root in the Āgamas has been seen whole.

Chapter one told two kinds of wanting apart: craving is boundless, wanting more once it has gotten; wholesome desire is bounded, going out on arrival—and what the vow relies on is the latter, the engine of the path, to be nourished and not cut. Chapter two watched the ladder of the vow: one and the same mind, its object climbing rung by rung, the Buddha kicking not one rung away; climbed to the summit, the seeking loosens of itself, and the undirected appears—while the original vow remains unbroken.

By now the vow has its fuel, and the vow has its spectrum. But a new question is already waiting at the crossroads. If the vow can climb all the way to "no aspiring remains," then what is its actual standing in the bodhisattva's training? Giving, discipline, patience, effort, meditation, wisdom—on the list of the six perfections, there is no entry called "vow." Is the vow an idle stroke outside the six—or is it, in its own right, a way of crossing?

The next part enters by that question—watching how the vow, from a force and a ladder, comes to be established formally as a perfection.
