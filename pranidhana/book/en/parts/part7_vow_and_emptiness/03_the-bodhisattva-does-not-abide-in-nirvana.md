## 7.3 The Bodhisattva Does Not Abide in Nirvāṇa

Picture a long-distance runner who, one last stride from the finish line, suddenly stops, turns, and runs back.

Not because his legs have failed—his strength is full. Not because the line is false—the line is right there; half a step more crosses it. He stops and turns because, looking back, he sees others still running. He will not cross the line alone.

There is something in this picture close to unintelligible: he can plainly arrive; arriving is the meaning of the whole long run; and at the last step, he chooses not to arrive.

The deepest and most moving posture in the Mahāyāna is this picture: the bodhisattva does not abide in nirvāṇa.

### By Prajñā, Not Abiding in Saṃsāra; by Great Compassion, Not Abiding in Nirvāṇa

The Yogācāra treatises, classifying nirvāṇa, established four kinds; the fourth was named precisely for this posture of the bodhisattva—the nirvāṇa of no-abode. The *Cheng Weishi Lun* says:

> "Fourth, the nirvāṇa of no-abode: this is suchness freed from the cognitive obscuration, ever supported by the two wings of great compassion and prajñā. By this it abides neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa, benefiting and gladdening sentient beings to the end of all future time—functioning, yet ever still; and so it is called nirvāṇa."

The wording is dense; the frame is clear. This nirvāṇa has two wings: great compassion and prajñā, "ever supporting"—at its two sides at all times. Borne on these two wings, the bodhisattva "abides neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa": neither end is dwelt in.

Each "not abiding" has its own root. By prajñā, not abiding in saṃsāra: wisdom sees the five aggregates as empty at root, the round of rebirth without substance—and the prison-gate of saṃsāra stands open. One still driven along by karma cannot be said to "not abide in saṃsāra"; he is shut inside it, unable to leave. By great compassion, not abiding in nirvāṇa: and once the prison-gate is open? Wisdom suffices for him to walk out into cessation; but at that moment great compassion speaks—the beings are still inside. And so this one, who could walk out in peace, will not walk out alone.

What must be seen is this: it is no shortfall of attainment. The treatise says that of the four nirvāṇas, even the adepts of the two vehicles possess the first three; this fourth alone—only the World-Honored One can be said to possess all four. It is the highest of the four, not the lacking one. The bodhisattva's not-abiding is not "not yet qualified to enter"; it is "fully qualified, and not entering." One who could never leave cannot be said to "stay"—he is merely trapped; only one who truly can go gives his not-going the name of staying.

Like a physician practicing in a plague-stricken city. The gates stand open; he has every means of leaving; outside is a safe home. He does not go—not because he cannot, and not out of fondness for a city of suffering and danger, but because patients still lie in the wards. His staying has "being able to leave" as its very premise.

### Within the Actionless, by the Power of Effort, He Liberates All

But compassion and wisdom are two wings—what is it that beats them? The line from the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* met once in Part Three must now show its other face:

> "Though he knows the true mark of dharmas to be unconditioned and actionless, because by the original vow and great compassion he wishes to liberate beings, within the actionless, by the power of effort, he liberates all."

Part Three read it for the vow holding the bodhisattva back from falling into cessation. Now read its word-order. "By the original vow and great compassion"—original vow first, great compassion after. The order is not casual. Said with compassion alone, the not-abiding is easily imagined as a moment's tenderness: seeing beings suffer, the heart softens, and one stays. The feeling is real; but feeling rises and falls, and tires—a moment's tenderness will not hold up the words "to the end of all future time." The treatise-master sets "original vow" before "great compassion" to drive the root of this bearing into the vow fixed from the first arousing of the mind—a vow already seen through as empty, and unretreating. The vow stands first, and compassion gains an inexhaustible source; it is the vow that casts a moment's tenderness into an unrelinquishing that runs to the end of future time.

The Vimalakīrti Sūtra gathers this posture into a formula of eight characters:

> "Not exhausting the conditioned; not abiding in the unconditioned."

The conditioned is the world of dependent making—the bodhisattva does not cultivate it into emptiness and wash his hands of it. The unconditioned is quiescent nirvāṇa—the bodhisattva does not moor there to enjoy the rest. Both sides declined. Declined how? The text answers itself: not departing from great kindness, not abandoning great compassion—"teaching and transforming beings, never for a moment wearying." And what is "not abiding in the unconditioned"? "To train in emptiness, yet not take emptiness as one's realization"—to learn emptiness through and through, and not sit down hugging it. Part Four's "one attached to emptiness, whom the buddhas cannot transform" here receives its positive fulfillment: the bodhisattva learns emptiness to the very end, and exactly therefore does not make emptiness his resting place.

And inside the words "within the actionless, by the power of effort" hides the secret of the bodhisattva's unweariness. Where does weariness come from? From the mind that has something to attain—"all I have done, and no end in sight." The bodhisattva, having seen that not one dharma can be apprehended, has also no "liberating I" left to be worn down—the vow runs inside emptiness, hence inexhaustible, hence never for a moment weary. Part Six said unweariness comes from the self's lightness; the ground of that sentence is here.

### Rest Declined—Not Because It Is Impossible, but Because of the Vow

Now the chapter's word can be said at its center: rest is declined, not because it is impossible, but because of the vow.

The whole weight of the sentence rides on "not because it is impossible." To see it, set two look-alike postures aside.

The world has people who cannot stop: success achieved and still restless, one goal in hand and instantly replaced by the next chase. Their not-resting is pushed by the inertia of desire; they do not know how to stop. That is not the bodhisattva—prajñā long ago dismantled that engine; if he chose to rest, he could rest utterly, on the spot. The world also has people who cannot reach rest: the end they want is in principle out of reach, the horizon forever receding. Their not-resting is helpless futility. That is not the bodhisattva either—for him the resting place is entirely real; he stands at its gate.

The bodhisattva is neither. With both "able to rest" and "the rest is real" complete, he does not rest. No restless engine, no trick of fate—only one clear-eyed decision: the gate is open, the rest is true, I see it plainly—and I, because of the vow, do not go in.

This alone is freedom. The trapped man who stays put is not free; the futile man circling is not free; only one who truly can go, and knows the road ahead to be true rest, makes of his not-going a freedom entire. Return to the runner at the opening: his turning moves us not because he could not reach the line—that is failure—nor because the line was an illusion—that is futility—but because the line is real, the strength is enough, and in the moment he could cross, he does not. The bodhisattva's not abiding in nirvāṇa is a decision deeper than nirvāṇa—it lowers nirvāṇa by nothing; it only will not let nirvāṇa become the full stop.

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

Two things to nail fast.

First, not abiding in nirvāṇa is in no way a fondness for saṃsāra. The texts decline both sides: not abiding in saṃsāra, and not abiding in nirvāṇa. The bodhisattva stays within birth and death to liberate beings by the freedom prajñā has won, not by the worldling's bound "cannot get out." To read "does not abide in nirvāṇa" as "the bodhisattva cannot quite let the world go" is to flatten the dignity of the double refusal into a one-sided clinging.

Second, if anyone would say "nirvāṇa has turned out a dead end," the words must be weighed with great care. Nirvāṇa itself is no dead end—it is reality, it is coolness, it is the liberation every walker of the way should incline toward. What becomes the "end of the lane" is not nirvāṇa but the single act of "resting into it and never coming out." For one who seeks only his own deliverance, to arrive is fulfillment; but for one who has vowed "sentient beings are numberless, I vow to liberate them," any arriving-for-good means shutting the suffering outside the gate. One and the same quiescence: for the two vehicles it is home; for the bodhisattva, the place to turn—the difference lying not in nirvāṇa, but in the vow. It is the vow that re-marks a natural destination as a waypoint on the road.

### Part Seven, to This Point

With these three chapters, the meeting of the vow and emptiness has been seen whole. Look back, and emptiness has done one and the same thing throughout—it has relieved the vow of three loads in a row.

Chapter one: emptiness relieved it of "the I who vows." The vow needs no really existing self to prop it up—vowed with nothing to attain, it goes looser, farther, less grudgingly. Chapter two: emptiness relieved it of "the hidden worry of retreat." The vow was never arisen; no thing has arisen that could be lost—immovable for having no ground; its rootlessness is its unsnatchability. Chapter three: emptiness relieved it of "the lure of the terminus." Nirvāṇa too is actionless, no graspable resting place—seeing this far, the bodhisattva can turn from the gate unhurried, and within the actionless liberate all.

The burden of self, the worry of retreat, the lure of the end—the three places where a vow most easily goes bad, emptiness has unbound one by one. So the promise made at this part's opening can be honored now: emptiness was never the vow's adversary; emptiness is the vow's deepest ally. Seeing the vow's nature to be originally empty does not subtract one measure of the vow's power—it completes it.

Only one last question hangs, lightly.

That bodhisattva who turned from the gate—walking on, where does he walk to? Even nirvāṇa he does not abide in: does this road still hold an "arrive"? At the end of the Heart Sūtra, the answer is in fact written—the buddhas of the three periods, relying on the prajñā-pāramitā, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi: unsurpassed, perfect awakening. The word "attain," standing in full view after "no wisdom and no attainment."

In the scripture of nothing-to-attain, the last word set down is "attain." What is this "attain"? And what has it to do with the "vow" this book has spent seven parts on?

The next part is the last of this volume. The bottom of the whole book is about to be turned over.
