## 3.3 Merit Is the Ox, the Vow the Driver

In the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, Nāgārjuna asked the question to its bottom on behalf of a practical man:

> "If one makes no vow, does one then gain no merit?"

Without a vow, is there no blessing to be had? The question is asked in good order. Sow melons, reap melons; sow beans, reap beans. Merit done brings its blessed recompense, evil done brings its bitter one—cause and effect answer of themselves, even-handed and automatic, never missing a debt. That being so, what use is a vow raised over and above the merit done? Make the vow, and does the blessing grow by a share? Leave it unmade, and is the merit already done discounted? It sounds as though making a vow were one optional extra line written outside the ledger of cause and effect.

Nāgārjuna's answer first grants half: without a vow, the merit is still gained—"one does gain it." Then comes the turn: but not as when there is a vow. Why not? He says:

> "Merit done without a vow sets up no aim; the vow is what steers and guides, and so something can be accomplished."

Merit done without a vow has set up no mark of its own—it is there, yet it does not know what it exists to accomplish. The vow's office is to steer and guide: driven, directed, the merit "can accomplish something." The treatise then strikes a simile: like the melting of gold—the gold melted down, it follows the craftsman's hand, and may be beaten into a hairpin, cast into a bowl, shaped into a buddha-image; the gold of itself does not decide what it becomes. Merit is that melted gold; the vow is the decision of what it shall be cast into.

### The Ox Has Strength; It Takes the Driver for the Cart to Arrive

Then comes the simile that sets the key. The treatise says the adorning of a pure buddha-land is a vast undertaking; scattered, piecemeal merits cannot pile up into it—vow-power must gather and command. For example—

> "The strength of the ox can indeed pull the cart, but a driver is needed if it is to arrive somewhere… Merit is like the ox, the vow like the driver."

An ox, however great its strength, however heavy the cart it can pull, does not itself know where the cart is to go. There must be a driver with whip and reins for this cart to "arrive somewhere"—to reach a fixed place. Merit is that strong ox; the vow is the driver on the cart.

This simile takes the practical man's doubt apart at the root. He had supposed that if the vow were useful, it must be useful as "one more ox beside the ox"—one more layer of merit laid on the merit. Nāgārjuna says: no. The vow is not a second ox. The driver does not himself pull the cart; the pulling is the ox's, first to last. But without the driver, the ox's strength only drags the cart toward no one knows where. The vow's power is not the same kind of pulling power as merit's; it is another kind—the power of setting direction. It adds no ox-strength; it fixes the destination.

So the treatise concludes that all the bodhisattva's surpassing fruits are "received by the power of the vow"—"by the vow one receives the surpassing fruit." Those words want careful reading. They do not say "trade the vow for the fruit"; they say: the fruit is surpassing precisely because it is the kind of fruit that grew up steered by a vow. One and the same store of merit, steered by a vow, gathers into an adorned buddha-land; with no vow to set its aim, the blessing is still there, yet it does not know what to coalesce into. Like one and the same sum of money—one heap sitting with no stated use, one made out to a named end: the amount identical, the meaning worlds apart. The vow does not buy the fruit; the vow shapes it.

### Having Seen Emptiness, Why Does He Not Rest?

The power to steer and guide—that is vow-power's first layer. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* digs one layer further down—to the sharpest place of all.

When prajñā is cultivated deep, it sees into all dharmas as unconditioned and actionless: no real doer anywhere, no real deed done; all dharmas quiescent from the very start. Seeing to there, what is the most natural place to go? To follow that quiescence in—to rest everything, and incline into cessation. Water runs downhill; that is prajñā's natural pull.

The bodhisattva also cultivates prajñā, also sees the actionless—what holds him back from sliding down that current into cessation? The treatise's answer pushes the vow to its deepest post:

> "Because the manifold merits are not yet complete he does not cease, and by great compassion and the power of the original vow he does not cease."

Two reasons for not ceasing: the merits of liberating beings and purifying the land are not yet full; and great compassion and the power of the original vow are present. Notice—vow-power here does not argue with prajñā. It does not say "dharmas are really conditioned after all"; it receives, whole, the emptiness prajñā sees. It simply will not halt upon that emptiness. Prajñā draws toward the far end of cessation; vow-power holds him steady on the near shore of liberating beings; where the two forces meet, the bodhisattva stands—dwelling in the actionless, and not seizing cessation.

The treatise has another line that says this station out in full:

> "Though he knows the true mark of dharmas to be unconditioned and actionless, because by the original vow and great compassion he wishes to liberate beings, within the actionless, by the power of effort, he liberates all."

Knowing full well that the true mark of dharmas is unconditioned and actionless—that seeing stands untouched. By the original vow and great compassion—that is the moving force. Within the actionless, by the power of effort, liberating all—the key is in the words "within the actionless." Not retreating to a view of "the conditioned" in order to liberate beings; not, having seen the actionless, letting go of the liberating; but knowing the actionless, and walking toward beings all the same. Emptiness and conduct, in this one sentence, stand together with neither first.

This is vow-power's deepest work. Without it, prajñā would swallow the conduct—the actionless seen, there is no one to liberate and no liberating to speak of. Without prajñā, the conduct would block out the emptiness—liberating with grasping at the real, falling back into something-to-attain. Vow-power stands between, one hand never disturbing prajñā's emptiness, one hand never letting go the conduct of liberating beings. The driver's charge is direction, always and only: on the ox of merit, it fixes where the merit shall come to completion; on the wilder horse of prajñā, it fixes that within emptiness one still walks toward beings.

### One Thing to See Clearly Here

"By the vow one receives the surpassing fruit"—nail those words down once more at the end, lest the whole circuit return to where it began.

Vow-power is not a deposit paid to the buddhas and bodhisattvas, and the fruit is not goods delivered against an order. The driver does not buy strength from the ox—the driver only fixes where the ox is going. The line this book drew in Part One has here a simile struck by the treatise-master's own hand to stand under it: the vow is not a transaction; it is the setting of direction.

Brought down to the daily reckoning, it is one sentence as well: do not first ask whether the ox of your merit is sturdy; ask first whether the driver is on the cart, and where the reins are pointed. The day's practice done, the good done, the palms joined at evening to transfer it—that moment is the driver taking up the reins. How much merit, each one's conditions differ; whether the direction is fixed rests with oneself alone.

### Part Three, to This Point

With these three chapters, the vow's name and standing on the bodhisattva path have been seen whole.

Chapter one saw the seat: among the ten perfections the vow sits eighth, the perfection of the not-yet-across—no prologue, but a power that must be wakened again and again, the power by which even the bodhisattva of the eighth ground comes out of his stillness. Chapter two saw the skeleton: the ten vows of the first ground, the cord that opens the net, sealed at the crown with ten clauses of "exhaustion" that can never come true—the vow holding the whole road open by never closing. Chapter three saw the power: merit is the ox, the vow the driver—it adds nothing to merit's measure, and fixes only merit's direction; and at the place where prajñā sees emptiness, it is the vow again that lets emptiness and conduct run side by side.

Seat, skeleton, power—with the three in hand, the vow is no longer only a thought. It is a force with name and seat, truly at work.

And yet, the ten grounds and their ten vows remain, for ordinary people, far away. To make offerings to all buddhas, to purify all buddha-lands—vows like these, an ordinary person can hardly recite to the end. The Chinese tradition of the Buddha's gate condensed this entire skeleton into four sentences—four sentences nearly everyone who has set foot in a temple has heard, and recited. Beings are boundless: I vow to liberate them. Afflictions are endless: I vow to cut them. Dharma-gates are measureless: I vow to learn them. The Buddha-way is unsurpassed: I vow to accomplish it.

Where did these four sentences come from? And by what right do they carry the skeleton of the ten grounds within four lines? The next part tells these four great vows.
