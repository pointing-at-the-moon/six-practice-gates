# The Patience of Non-Arising and the Vow That Does Not Turn Back

**Immovability through Groundlessness: How the Vow's Non-Regression Is Possible**

## Abstract

Taking up the previous paper's conclusion — that a vow without a vower still does not run dry — this paper asks the next question: if there is no self who vows and yet the vow is inexhaustible, then how is it that the vow does not *regress*? The thesis is that the irreversibility of the bodhisattva's vow (*avaivartika*) is secured not by the white-knuckled persistence of the will but by the patience of non-arising (*anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti*). The vow is irreversible not because the bodhisattva grips it most tightly and guards it against loss, but because, seeing that all things are unarisen and the vow itself is without own-being, *no thing has arisen that could be lost, and so no thing can make it turn back.* Drawing on the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* — where the patience of non-arising simply *is* abiding in the stage of non-regression — and on the Eighth Ground of the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* — where attaining that patience is, in the same instant, entry into the Immovable Ground and the obtaining of the dharma of non-regression — the paper argues that the true mechanism of non-regression is non-arising: a vow that *could* turn back is itself unfindable, so non-regression is not the achievement of holding fast but the natural consequence of non-arising. Two Western mirrors are engaged: Bernard Williams's "ground projects" and Gabriel Marcel's "creative fidelity." Both root irreversibility in a bond with personal identity or in the persistence of a present subject; the patience of non-arising sees through even that disintegrable self and that faithful subject. Immovability rests on the absence of ground, not on the firmest grip. Its very rootlessness is its unsnatchability. The paper closes by setting a signpost — where, then, does a vow that can no longer turn back finally go? — for the closing paper of the volume.

**Keywords:** the patience of non-arising (*anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti*), non-regression (*avaivartika*), the Immovable Ground (*acalā bhūmi*), the irreversible vow, own-being and emptiness, immovability through groundlessness, ground projects (Bernard Williams), creative fidelity (Gabriel Marcel)

## I. The Question: Is Non-Regression the Tightest Grip, or Having Nothing to Lose?

On the first day of the year a person makes a resolution; by February it is already forgotten. Lovers swear they will never change; some years later the vow has quietly drained away. Our everyday picture of *not turning back* is almost always the same one. We assume it runs on willpower — on clenched teeth, on the resolve to hold out a little longer, on gripping the goal tightly enough that the current cannot wash it loose. The whole genre of motivational writing about "perseverance," however varied its surface, shares a single underlying image: backsliding is what happens when the will goes slack, and steadfastness is what the will achieves when it stays taut. On this picture, not turning back is a contest of forces — the harder you hold, the less easily the thing slips.

Mahāyāna Buddhism, however, assigns to the bodhisattva path a station that is set extraordinarily high and that, on first hearing, sounds almost inhuman: *non-regression* (*avaivartika*; in the older transcriptions, the stage from which one does not turn back). Once a bodhisattva reaches this station, the vow that orients them toward supreme awakening is said to become *unsnatchable and inexhaustible*; they will no longer fall away, no longer reverse course. The question this paper presses is simple: *which kind* of not-turning-back is this? If it were merely the not-turning-back of "gripping the vow as tightly as possible," then it would differ from the steadfastness of an unusually stubborn ordinary person only in degree, not in kind — a more powerful engine, perhaps, but still trapped inside the same machine of *effort braced against slackness*. If, on the other hand, the Mahāyāna's non-regression has another root, then its immovability lies not in the strongest grip but somewhere else entirely.

The preceding paper in this series had already drawn out something still more paradoxical: that the deepest vow is precisely one *made in the light of non-attainment* — a supreme vow aroused with a mind that grasps nothing, in which the one who vows, the one vowed for, and the vow itself are all unfindable; no "self who vows" stands behind it propping it up, and yet the vow does not collapse for want of such a self — on the contrary, it runs without end.[^p16] That paper closed on the question of *why a vow without a vower does not run dry*. The present paper pushes one step further. Very well: grant that there is no self who vows, and the vow can still be inexhaustible. But *inexhaustible* and *irreversible* are, after all, two different things. To be inexhaustible is not to run out, not to be used up. To be irreversible is not to reverse, not to be taken away. A spring may be inexhaustible and still be dammed back by a single stone. So where does this vow — which has no one vowing it and seems to have nothing to brace against — get its guarantee against being dammed back, its assurance that it will not turn around?

The thesis can be stated in a single line. The bodhisattva's vow does not turn back, and the guarantee of this lies not in *the tightest grip* but in *non-arising*. Non-regression is not the will's victory over its own slackness. It is, rather, this: because all things are seen to be unarisen and the vow itself to be without own-being, there is simply *no thing standing there, having arisen, for you to lose* — and therefore no thing that could make it turn back. A vow that could regress is itself unfindable, and so "regression" loses both its object and its purchase. To put it in a phrase: the bodhisattva's immovability is *immovability through groundlessness*, not immovability through the firmest grip. What follows lays out, first, the canonical spine on which the patience of non-arising simply *is* abiding in the non-regressing stage (§II); then it draws from that spine the core reasoning that *non-arising is non-regression* (§III); it sets two discussions from action theory and existential philosophy alongside the argument as clarifying mirrors (§IV); it marks off the four readings most likely to lead astray (§V); and it closes by gathering the thread and planting a signpost (§VI).

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The Patience of Non-Arising Is to Abide in the Stage of Non-Regression: The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*

In the treatise literature of the Perfection-of-Wisdom tradition, "non-regression" and "the patience of non-arising" are not two free-standing notions that happen to be mentioned side by side. They are explicitly clasped together. In fascicle 77 of his *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (*Dà zhìdù lùn*), explaining why a bodhisattva is called *non-regressing*, Nāgārjuna identifies the patience of non-arising itself as the inner realization of one who abides in the non-regressing stage:[^1]

> "All the fruits and all the knowledge of the arhats and the pratyekabuddhas are, for the bodhisattva-mahāsattva, [encompassed within] the patience of non-arising.… Know that this bodhisattva … abides in the stage of non-regression."

The structure of the passage repays a slow reading. It says first that the highest fruit and knowledge realized by the two vehicles of disciples and solitary buddhas — what is, for *them*, the endpoint — is, for the bodhisattva, no more than one facet, one stretch of content, within the patience of non-arising. What the bodhisattva sees through and rests within, in other words, is more thoroughgoing than anything the two vehicles realize. It then states flatly that the bodhisattva who accomplishes this patience *thereby abides in the stage of non-regression*. The treatise here treats "the patience of non-arising" and "abiding in the non-regressing stage" as very nearly two names for one thing: the former named for *what it sees* (that all things are unarisen), the latter named for *what it accomplishes* (that one no longer turns back). The patience is the cause, non-regression the effect — and the two are the two faces of a single inner realization.

This gives us the first and most decisive clue. The bodhisattva's not-turning-back is guaranteed not by some additional gate of the will, bolted on afterward, but by the patience of non-arising itself. It is not that there is first a vow liable to regress and then the patience is brought in to guard it. Rather, when the bodhisattva sees that all things are unarisen and can rest at peace in that seeing, the very question of *will it / won't it regress* is relocated within that seeing.

### 2.2 Attaining the Patience, One Enters the Immovable Ground: The Eighth Ground of the *Avataṃsaka*

The same clasping receives a more systematic and more stately exposition at the Eighth Ground of the "Ten Grounds" chapter of the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*. In the eighty-fascicle recension, the treatment of the Eighth Ground first marks what is realized there as *the attainment of the patience of non-arising*, and then names the station itself the *Immovable Ground* (*acalā bhūmi*):[^2]

> "… This is called attaining the patience of non-arising. O sons of the Buddha! When a bodhisattva accomplishes this patience, that very instant he enters the Eighth Ground, the Immovable Ground."

Note the words *that very instant* — at once, with no interval. Between attaining the patience of non-arising and entering the Immovable Ground there is no intervening stretch of "now working further to make oneself immovable." The moment the patience is accomplished, immovability stands established along with it — as though immovability were not some further thing done after the patience, but simply another name for the patience itself. The text then ties the *name* "Immovable" directly to *non-regression*:[^3]

> "Because he is able to obtain the dharma of non-regression, it is therefore said that he abides in the Immovable Ground."

This states the reason for the name with perfect plainness: the ground is called *Immovable* precisely because, here, the bodhisattva *obtains the dharma of non-regression*. And by the preceding line, this "obtaining the dharma of non-regression" is itself tied to "attaining the patience of non-arising." Read the three lines together and the *Avataṃsaka* gives us an unambiguous chain of equivalences: **attain the patience of non-arising → thereby enter the Immovable Ground → so named because the dharma of non-regression is obtained**. The ultimate reason that non-regression *is* non-regression is traced, all the way back, to a single source: the patience of non-arising.

The *Avataṃsaka* adds, at the Eighth Ground, one further description of great depth: here the bodhisattva *relinquishes all effortful activity and attains the dharma of effortlessness*.[^4] "Effort" (*功用*) here means contrived, strenuous exertion; "effortlessness" (*無功用*) means what proceeds without strain, spontaneously and of itself. This confirms the paper's thesis from the reverse side. The immovability of the Eighth Ground does *not* come from exertion driven to its limit, from gripping the vow as tightly as it can be gripped. On the contrary, it is precisely *in relinquishing effort and attaining effortlessness* — in setting down the very contrivance of "bracing to hold it fast" — that true immovability comes forth. Immovability is not the summit of effort. It is the falling away of effort.

### 2.3 The Stage of Non-Regression as a Structure: Corroboration from the Easy Path

It is worth adding that "the stage of non-regression," as a structural coordinate within the bodhisattva's career, is not the property of the Perfection-of-Wisdom and *Avataṃsaka* literatures alone. In the "Easy Practice" chapter of his *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* (*Shízhù pípóshā lùn*), Nāgārjuna likewise treats the non-regressing stage (under another transcription of *avaivartika*) as the pivotal station toward which the bodhisattva travels, and from this distinction opens the well-known division between a "difficult path" and an "easy path."[^5] The present paper takes from this only the structural fact — *that the non-regressing stage functions as a key coordinate of the bodhisattva path* — so as to register the weight that "not turning back" carries across the Mahāyāna traditions. The further sense in which the "easy path" involves attaining non-regression through *rebirth* by *other-power* belongs to the specific axis of Pure Land teaching, treated at length in this project's papers on Amitābha's vows; it is not restated here and is not enlisted as a premise for the present argument.[^p11] What this paper takes up, from beginning to end, is the *prajñā / patience-of-non-arising* face of non-regression, not the *other-power / rebirth* face. The two senses share a name but point to very different things — as §V will set out in detail.

## III. The Mechanism of Non-Regression Is Non-Arising, Not Holding-Fast

### 3.1 Regression Presupposes Something That Could Arise and Be Lost

To see clearly the reasoning by which *non-arising is non-regression*, we must first take apart what "regression" is. Grammatically the word names a transitive action: one always regresses *from* somewhere, loses *something*. It presupposes three things: a subject who regresses (the one who turns back), an object that is lost (the vow forfeited, the station fallen from), and a process running "from advance to retreat." Without these three, there is nothing for "regression" to mean.

The crux is this: every one of the three presupposes that what it concerns is *real, self-subsisting, and capable of arising and ceasing*. To say "I have lost my vow," one must first have a real "I," a real "vow," and a vow that arose at some time and can be taken away by some set of conditions. Regression, in its very bones, depends on a real structure of *arising–abiding–ceasing*: first the vow arises (arising), is held for a time (abiding), and then, under adverse conditions, fades away (ceasing).

Here the leverage of *prajñā* shows itself. It does not collide head-on with the *ceasing* link — does not stage a contest of strength over "I can hold it, you cannot wrest it away." It dismantles the *arising* link directly. If all things are seen to be, from the first, unarisen — and the vow is among all things, equally without own-being, with no real arising — then the object "that once arose and can now be taken away" never stood there as a real thing in the first place. With no real arising, there is no real ceasing for anything to undergo; with no thing that ever arose to be lost, there is no thing that can be made to turn back. Regression fails to occur, then, *not because a stronger force has blocked it*, but because it has lost the entire real structure on which it depended for its very possibility.

### 3.2 Immovability through Groundlessness

From this the paper's central thesis can be drawn out: **the bodhisattva's not-turning-back is *immovability through groundlessness*, not *immovability through the firmest grip*.**

The immovability that ordinary imagination pictures is that of a tree whose roots run very deep: the stronger the wind, the deeper the roots must drive and the tighter the trunk must be braced; the guarantee of immovability lies in the *solidity of the ground* and the *force of the grip*. Such immovability carries a fatal fragility. It is staked precisely on that ground — and once the ground itself is shaken (by impermanence, by adverse conditions, by some greater force), the whole tree topples, roots and all. For one who builds non-regression on "the tightest grip," not-turning-back is only ever "has not yet met a greater force." In principle it remains snatchable; it has simply not yet been snatched.

The immovability that the patience of non-arising accomplishes is of another kind. It does not drive its roots deep, because it sees that *there is no plot of ground to root in, and so none that could be overturned*. Since the vow is without own-being, there is no "real foothold" anywhere for adverse conditions to pull out from under it; precisely because it is not founded on any arising-and-ceasing real thing, there is nothing in it for any force of arising and ceasing to move. This is a paradoxical and thoroughgoing security: *unsnatchable*, not because it is guarded to the death, but because *the thing that could be snatched is itself unfindable*. Its very rootlessness is its unsnatchability. Once this layer is understood, not-turning-back ceases to be an arduous task of perpetual vigilance and unrelenting exertion and becomes, under the seeing called *non-arising*, a natural, effortless accompaniment.

### 3.3 Non-Regression Is Not a Victory of Voluntarism

A reading that slips in all too easily must be cleared away at once — the reading that quietly converts the non-regression of the patience of non-arising back into "an especially seasoned, especially powerful persistence of the will." This turns the whole foregoing argument upside down. The voluntarist non-regression places all its weight on *the subject who can hold fast* and *the force of that holding*; it is the achievement of an "I," the "I" that holds firmer and firmer. But the insight of the patience of non-arising is precisely that *even that "I" who can hold fast* falls within the seeing that all things are unarisen and is, equally, without findable own-being. If non-regression still had to lean on an ever-stronger "I" to keep guard, it would not yet have stepped out of the real structure that §II saw Nāgārjuna take apart; it would not yet be non-regression in the sense of the patience of non-arising.

The difference between the two can be put this way. Voluntarist non-regression is "the subject gripping the object as tightly as possible." The non-regression of the patience of non-arising is "subject and object alike seen to be unarisen, so that within this non-arising the vow cannot be snatched away." The former is the saturation of force; the latter is the falling away of the very ground that force depended on. To borrow the formula of the preceding paper: there it was *the vow of one who does not vow does not run dry*; here it is *the vow of one who cannot regress does not turn back*. With no "I" liable to turn back, and no real vow there to be turned back from, "regression" loses both its agent and its object, and the vow does not turn back of itself. And this is where the Pāli foundation laid down at the very start of this volume — in the discussion of *resolve* (*adhiṭṭhāna*), where the ground for the "non-regression" face was placed in reserve — finds its final redemption at the Mahāyāna's prajñā layer.[^p03]

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Reflection: How a Commitment Becomes Irrevocable

In the Western context, the nearest counterpart to "not turning back" is perhaps the cluster of discussions around *what makes a commitment, a project, irrevocable and constitutive*. Within contemporary action theory and existential philosophy, two thinkers have pressed this question with unusual depth, and they serve as two clean mirrors — and it is exactly at the point where each presses the question to its depth that the patience of non-arising parts ways with them.

### 4.1 Williams's "Ground Projects": One Does Not Turn Back Because Losing It Would Be One's Collapse

In his critique of utilitarianism, the British philosopher Bernard Williams introduced the celebrated notion of *ground projects*. A ground project is one of those core undertakings that give a person a *reason to go on living* — not "things I happen to want to do as long as I'm alive anyway," but, on the contrary, the kind of fundamental desire that *gives me a reason to go on living at all* (Williams calls these *categorical desires*: unconditioned, non-instrumental). On this basis Williams pushes back against the impersonal utilitarian demand that an individual sacrifice everything for the aggregate good: to require a person to abandon their ground projects is to require them to be estranged from *the very source of all their moral energy* — an assault on their integrity.

**The resemblance.** The bodhisattva's vow does indeed have something of the character of a ground project. It is unconditioned (not an instrumental wish that stops once a target is hit); it gives the whole bodhisattva path its direction; one might even say it *constitutes* the bodhisattva as a bodhisattva. On the point that the vow is constitutive and cannot be commanded away by external force, Williams and the Mahāyāna are remarkably in tune. A bodhisattva ordered to give up the great vow to liberate beings is absurd in much the way that a person ordered to give up a ground project is absurd; the two cases share a structure.

**The fundamental difference.** Yet where is the root of *Williams's* irrevocability driven? Into the bond with *personal identity*. A ground project may not be relinquished precisely because to relinquish it would be to lose that self — the person would *no longer be themselves*, would fall into the estrangement and personal disintegration Williams describes. In other words, the guarantee behind Williams's not-turning-back is exactly "lose it and I collapse": one does not turn back because turning back would mean the dissolution of the self, and the self resists its own dissolution with the utmost force. This is precisely §III's "immovability through the firmest grip" — and indeed the very firmest, since what is gripped is identified directly with "what makes me me." The patience of non-arising reverses course exactly here. Its not-turning-back is not secured by binding the vow to a self that *could* collapse, because even that collapsible, dissolvable self falls within the seeing that all things are unarisen and is, from the first, unfindable. Williams: one does not turn back because losing it would mean one is no longer oneself. The bodhisattva: one does not turn back because no thing has arisen to be lost, and there is no "I" there to collapse. The former drives the root of irreversibility into the deepest self-identity; the latter sets the root of irreversibility in *no root at all*.

(A note in passing: Harry Frankfurt's *volitional necessity* — the sense in which a person "cannot but" will as they do, because that willing constitutes a self they deeply care about and identify with — shares the same structure as Williams's, locating the root of irreversibility in the identified-with self; the response to both is therefore the same. This axis of *orders of the will and the identified-with self* has had its own dedicated treatment in earlier papers of this series and is not expanded here.)

### 4.2 Marcel's "Creative Fidelity": One Does Not Turn Back Because One Keeps Being Present to You

The French existential philosopher Gabriel Marcel's analysis of *fidelity* comes a step nearer still, and is finer-grained. Marcel distinguishes two kinds of fidelity. One is the mechanical keeping of a past oath — as though a promise were locked in a strongbox and thereafter merely, inertly, not violated. The other he calls *creative fidelity* (*fidélité créatrice*): not inert adherence but a continuous, ever-renewed *presence* (*présence*) — the faithful one becoming present to the one they are faithful to again and again, creatively, openly (his *disponibilité*, an available openness). For Marcel, true fidelity belongs to the register of *being* (*être*) rather than *having* (*avoir*): not "I possess a promise" but "I continue to *be* the one who is present to you."

**The resemblance.** This mirror sits closer than Williams's. The continuity of the bodhisattva's vow is indeed nearer to "creative fidelity" than to "mechanical adherence." The vow is not a contract sealed once and thereafter merely not broken by inertia; it is, rather, an openness — present anew, moment after moment, to all living beings. (The "moment-to-moment continuity" of the vow has its own dedicated treatment in this project's paper on Samantabhadra's vows of conduct, and is not restated here; only the point that *fidelity is living re-presence, not inert lock-down* is borrowed as a mirror.)[^p14a] On the point that fidelity is not inert sealing but living re-presence, Marcel is far more apt than voluntarism.

**The fundamental difference.** And yet Marcel's creative fidelity still presupposes a *continuously present subject* — an "I" who is always there, who becomes present to you again and again. He moves the root of not-turning-back from "the oath in the past" to "presence in the present," which is a great advance; but "presence" is, in the end, *someone's* presence. The patience of non-arising presses one layer further: even that "I who is present" is itself unarisen, itself without findable own-being. And so the ultimate point of the bodhisattva's "fidelity" is not "I keep being present to you" but — to borrow the previous paper's idiom — "with no one there who can hold faith, the vow still does not run dry, still does not turn back": this is *fidelity without a faithful one*, just as the previous paper set out the *vow without a vower*. One clarification must be added. An earlier paper in this series took Paul Ricœur as a mirror, correcting the misreading that "fidelity is the strenuous keeping of one's promise."[^p14b] Marcel's contribution is to move fidelity from "the tension of holding fast" to "presence"; what the present paper advances is to move further still, from "the present subject" to "even the present subject is unarisen." Three steps, each layered on the last, each with its own point of landing; they must not be run together.

### 4.3 The Common Turning-Point of Both Mirrors

Held up together, the two mirrors reveal a common turning-point. Whether Williams drives the root of not-turning-back into "the bond with self-identity" or Marcel moves it to "the persistence of a present subject," the West's most penetrating answers to "what makes a commitment irrevocable" still hang that irrevocability on *some subject* — whether identified-with or present. Their not-turning-back is, in the last analysis, the not-turning-back of *some "I."* What the patience of non-arising sees is precisely that even that subject is unarisen: not "I hold most firmly, therefore I do not turn back," but "no thing can be turned back from and no 'I' can turn back, therefore there is no turning back."

It must be stated emphatically: the foregoing is purely a heuristic analogy, not a claim of theoretical equivalence. The analyses of Williams and Marcel are each philosophical descriptions of the structure of human commitment, and neither carries any ontological commitment to non-arising or non-self. The paper enlists them only to illuminate the point that "not turning back" need not run along the single track of "gripping the vow most tightly," and to throw into relief how differently, in principle, the non-regression of the patience of non-arising is constituted. A mirror can show an outline, but it is never the thing reflected; one must not reason in reverse and say that the patience of non-arising amounts to some position in action theory, or that the Mahāyāna's non-regressing stage can be reduced to a doctrine of personal integrity or of presence.

## V. Corrections toward the Middle Way

This section marks off the four readings most likely to slip astray — using ❌ for doctrinal error and ⚠️ for traps of text and terminology — as boundary-stakes for keeping to the middle.

**❌ The non-regression of *prajñā / non-arising* is not the non-regression of *Pure Land / other-power*.** The easiest confusion is to take the non-regression discussed here as the same thing as the non-regression Pure Land teaching speaks of when it says "to be reborn in the Pure Land is to attain non-regression." The two share a name but run on different axes. What this paper takes up is the *prajñā / patience-of-non-arising* face: the guarantee of not-turning-back lies in the self-realized wisdom that *sees all things as unarisen and the vow as without own-being*. The non-regression Pure Land speaks of has, on one of its faces, its guarantee in the sustaining power of Amitābha's original vow — abiding in non-regression *in reliance on other-power*. This latter sense involves a whole body of teaching — the vow "should I attain buddhahood," the embrace of those reborn, the easy path — treated at length in this project's papers on Amitābha's original vow.[^p11] The present paper, throughout, enlists no other-power as its ground and advances no claim that "Amitābha's vow-power makes one non-regressing." The reader must not, because the term "non-regression" is shared, graft the premises of one axis onto the other.

**❌ The patience of non-arising is not the "repentance of non-arising," nor the "sign-less silence" of meditative absorption.** "Non-arising" is one of the Mahāyāna's most central seeings, and in different gates of practice it lands as different operational faces, which must be kept distinct. In the gate of repentance, "non-arising" lands as the *repentance of non-arising* — "the nature of wrongdoing is intrinsically empty; the one who repents and the wrong repented of are alike unfindable" — that is, non-arising spoken of with respect to *wrongdoing*, and given its own dedicated treatment in this project's volume on repentance. In the gate of meditative absorption, "the patience of non-arising" connects with the realization of *the signlessness of all things, the silent non-duality* — as with the five hundred bodhisattvas who attain the patience of non-arising at Vimalakīrti's assembly, and his thunderous silence — given its own dedicated treatment in this project's volume on absorption. What this paper takes up is the *third* face of that same "non-arising": **the patience of non-arising *qua* the vow's non-regression** — a vow that could turn back is itself unfindable. The three faces share one source but are used differently and may not be borrowed back and forth. This paper will on no account expand the repentance-face argument that "the nature of wrongdoing is empty," nor the absorption-face kōan of "the five hundred bodhisattvas' silence"; where it touches on those two faces, it only names them and points to the volume in which they are treated, without restating their content.

**⚠️ The *Vimalakīrti* alone is drawn on by three gates, each for a different sense; they must not be conflated.** One more stake follows from the last. The *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa* is a scripture drawn upon by several gates of practice, and each takes something different from it. The volume on repentance takes its "Upāli chapter" (the emptiness of the nature of wrongdoing, the defilement and purity of mind); the volume on absorption takes its "Seated Meditation" passage and the sign-less silence of the five hundred bodhisattvas' patience of non-arising; the present paper takes only the third sense, *the patience of non-arising qua the non-regressing vow*. Three gates cite one scripture and each keeps to its own sense, none trespassing on another; the reader must not, on seeing the *Vimalakīrti* cited in three places, suppose that the same passage or the same sense is at issue.

**⚠️ The placement of "the non-regressing stage" within the grounds is not uniform across the texts.** It must be said honestly: exactly which of the bodhisattva's ten grounds "the patience of non-arising / the non-regressing stage" corresponds to is not adjudicated uniformly across the scriptures and treatises. The "Ten Grounds" chapter of the *Avataṃsaka* clearly ties it to the Eighth, the Immovable Ground (as cited in §II); the Perfection-of-Wisdom treatises, in clasping "the patience of non-arising" together with "non-regression," sometimes speak across the junction of the seventh and eighth grounds, and sometimes speak in the general sense that "to attain the patience is to be non-regressing," without pinning it word-for-word to one specific station. What this paper rests on is *the mechanical clasping* — that wherever the patience is, there non-regression is — and not *the precise numbering* of which ground it falls in. The reader should not, on account of small divergences in the numbering across the various accounts, come to doubt that the core clasping, *non-arising is non-regression*, can be established.

## VI. Conclusion

**The argument in brief.** The vow's not-turning-back — its unsnatchability, its inexhaustibility — is guaranteed not by the clenched-teeth persistence of the will but by the patience of non-arising. Regression presupposes a real vow and a real self that could arise and be lost; the patience of non-arising dismantles the *arising* link directly — seeing that all things are unarisen and the vow itself is without own-being, so that no thing ever arose to be lost, and no thing can make it turn back. The bodhisattva's immovability is immovability through groundlessness, not immovability through the firmest grip: its very rootlessness is its unsnatchability. Following the previous paper's *the vow of one who does not vow does not run dry*, this paper completes the parallel: *the vow of one who cannot regress does not turn back* — with no "I" liable to turn back and no real vow to be turned back from, "regression" loses both agent and object, and the vow does not turn back of itself.

**A note on practice.** From this a directional pointer follows. For one who works at "the vow's not-turning-back," the point of application is not "guarding the vow harder, bracing the will tighter." That road — immovability through the firmest grip — is in principle always snatchable; it has merely not yet met a greater force. What truly makes the vow non-regressing is the seeing of the vow's own emptiness: seeing clearly that the very anxiety of "fearing it will be taken away" still presupposes a real vow that could be taken away — and when that presupposition loosens under the seeing called non-arising, the tension of "can I or can't I hold it" falls away of itself, and the vow does not turn back, effortlessly and of its own accord. So the emphasis, in working at non-regression, is not on reinforcement but on seeing; not on straining to guard a real vow but on seeing through the very illusion that "a real vow could be taken away." This is the deep import of the *Avataṃsaka*'s Eighth Ground: *relinquishing effortful activity, attaining the dharma of effortlessness*.

**An open question.** Yet the paper also leaves, at its close, a question still unanswered. A vow that is both inexhaustible and irreversible — where, finally, does it go? Since even nirvāṇa — the utter quieting of all seeking and all contrivance — is something the bodhisattva is plainly already able to enter, why does this non-regressing vow not simply come to rest peacefully at the gate of nirvāṇa and there fall still? Why should a vow that can no longer turn back and can no longer be snatched away still refuse to take final cessation as its terminus? This question reaches into the last layer of the dealing between vow and emptiness — the vow as a force that *will not take cessation for its end*. The present paper only sets the marker and lays the road; the full treatment is reserved for the volume's closing paper, on the bodhisattva who does not abide in nirvāṇa.
