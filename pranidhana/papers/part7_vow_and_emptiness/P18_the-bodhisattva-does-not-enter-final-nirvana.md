# The Bodhisattva Does Not Enter Final Nirvāṇa

**Vow as the Refusal of the Cul-de-Sac**

## Abstract

The bodhisattva's path contains one turn harder to read than any other. When the deep insight of prajñā has already made it possible for the practitioner to enter nirvāṇa—to still, once and for all, the whole turning round of suffering—the bodhisattva turns back at the very gate, declining a cessation that lies right there to be had. This paper does not approach that non-abiding from the side of meditative posture (the "not actualizing the real" of deep *samādhi*, treated elsewhere in this project) but from the side of the vow itself: what is it that makes an attainable rest be set aside? Reading the *Cheng weishi lun*'s definition of the *nirvāṇa of no-abode* through the joint working of compassion and wisdom—by prajñā, not abiding in saṃsāra; by great compassion, not abiding in nirvāṇa—together with the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s thesis that "though knowing all dharmas to be unconditioned and actionless, yet by the original vow and great compassion he acts within the actionless to liberate all," and the *Vimalakīrti*'s twofold refusal, "not exhausting the conditioned, not abiding in the unconditioned," the paper argues that the vow is precisely the power unwilling to make cessation the end. The bodhisattva's non-abiding in nirvāṇa is neither because nirvāṇa is unreachable (it is fully reachable) nor a restless inability to stop, but a freedom of no-abode sustained by vow and great compassion: rest is declined not because it is impossible, but because of the vow.

**Keywords:** non-abiding nirvāṇa (*apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa*), the union of compassion and wisdom, vow-power, the original vow, not exhausting the conditioned and not abiding in the unconditioned, the freedom of no-abode, the cul-de-sac, retaining afflictions to moisten rebirth, the cognitive obscuration (*jñeyāvaraṇa*), *Cheng weishi lun* (T1585), *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa* (T0475)

## I. Where Does the Non-Regressing Vow Go? The Puzzle of Turning Back at the Gate

Picture a long-distance runner. One stride from the finish line he stops short, turns, and runs back the way he came. Not because his legs have given out—they have not—and not because the line is a mirage; the line is exactly where it appears to be, and one more step would carry him across. He turns because, glancing back, he sees others still on the course, and will not breast the tape alone. There is something almost unintelligible in the image: arrival is plainly within reach, arrival is plainly the whole point of the race, and at the last step he chooses not to arrive.

This is the most baffling and the most moving posture in all of Mahāyāna Buddhism: the bodhisattva does not abide in nirvāṇa.

The preceding paper treated the question of how the vow does not turn back. A vow raised with no acquisition—where the one who vows, the thing vowed, and the vow itself are alike ungraspable—how does it not disintegrate, not regress? The answer lay in the patience of non-arising: since the vow has no self-nature, no dharma arises in it that could later be lost, and so no dharma can make it turn back. The vow's non-regression is not the clenched persistence of the will but the consequence of seeing that dharmas were never, at bottom, born. Such a vow is unsnatchable and inexhaustible.

But that answers only half. A vow that is unsnatchable and inexhaustible—where, in the end, is it headed? Toward what does it point?

On the common map of the Buddhist path, every road ends in nirvāṇa: the cool repose in which greed, hatred, and delusion are spent for good and the round of birth and death is stilled. The whole weight of practice rests, from the first, on the single word *release*. And at a certain stage the bodhisattva can indeed enter that cessation. The gate stands before him. One step, and the long race is over, with no further suffering to be borne.

And yet the bodhisattva turns back at the gate.

This turning is not what the present paper sets out to explain from the side of meditation. In the contemplative context, "non-abiding" appears as the fine posture of "not actualizing the real," of "not taking up the realization of cessation," of abiding-yet-not-abiding—like a bird tracking through open air, neither falling nor settling—and that is the technical face of one who wields deep *samādhi*, already treated in a dedicated study.[^5] The question here lies closer to the vow itself: when an attainable terminus is set squarely before him, what power makes the bodhisattva decline it? Not "he cannot"—on the contrary, "he can, and does not." Between the ability to stop and the not-stopping there stands precisely the vow.

So the question narrows to a single line: why does the non-regressing vow refuse to reach the terminus it could reach?

## II. The Nirvāṇa of No-Abode: Prajñā Does Not Abide in Saṃsāra, Compassion Does Not Abide in Nirvāṇa

To answer, we need first a precise term. In sorting the kinds of nirvāṇa, the Yogācāra tradition sets out four, of which the fourth is named for this posture as though tailored to it: the nirvāṇa of no-abode (*apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa*). The *Cheng weishi lun*, fascicle ten, reads:

> Fourth, the nirvāṇa of no-abode: this is suchness freed from the cognitive obscuration, ever supported by the two wings of great compassion and prajñā. By this it abides neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa, benefiting and gladdening sentient beings to the end of all future time—functioning, yet ever still; and so it is called nirvāṇa.[^1]

The passage first names this nirvāṇa's substance—suchness freed from the cognitive obscuration—then names its two wings: great compassion and prajñā, "ever supporting." The hinge is the clause that follows: "by this it abides neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa." Just because it has these two wings to support it, the bodhisattva is able *not* to abide in either.

Note that the non-abiding here is double: he abides neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa, and each of the two has its own root.

*By prajñā, not abiding in saṃsāra.* Wisdom sees the true mark of dharmas—the five aggregates empty at root, the round of rebirth without substance—so the bodhisattva is not bound or drowned by the flow of birth and death. One who still craves the three realms and is shoved along by the force of his own karma cannot be said to "not abide in saṃsāra"; he abides in it, unable to get out. Only when prajñā is present does the prison door of saṃsāra truly open. This face is the wisdom of release.

*By great compassion, not abiding in nirvāṇa.* But once the door is open, what then? His wisdom is already enough to walk him out, into cessation. And here compassion acts: beings are still inside the cell. So the one who could have walked out alone, in safety, will not walk out alone. Compassion does not keep him from reaching nirvāṇa; it keeps him from *taking* a nirvāṇa he has fully reached. This face is the compassion that does not abandon.

The two wings together give the full sense of the nirvāṇa of no-abode: it is not some place one can "move into"—on the contrary, it is an activity that abides in no place at all, functioning yet ever still, still yet ever functioning. Within the same Yogācāra system, the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* establishes the same name and sketches its two faces in a single phrase, "casting off defilement yet not casting off saṃsāra":[^2] what is cast off is defilement (the work of prajñā), and what is not cast off is the beings within saṃsāra (the work of great compassion).

It is worth pausing on the fact that the four nirvāṇas are not four parallel options but ranked. The first three—nirvāṇa of intrinsic purity, nirvāṇa with remainder, nirvāṇa without remainder—are roughly common to all the noble ones, and even the two-vehicle arhat who enters the nirvāṇa without remainder is held to have reached a kind of nirvāṇa. The fourth, the nirvāṇa of no-abode, is the bodhisattva's alone, and its decisive difference is buried in the words "freed from the cognitive obscuration." The two-vehicle practitioner severs the afflictive obscuration; he resolves the suffering of "my" own round of rebirth, and so can take up cessation in safety and enter repose. The bodhisattva, beyond the afflictive obscuration, must also exit the *cognitive* obscuration (*jñeyāvaraṇa*)—that subtle veil over total knowledge of all dharmas and of the capacities of all beings. Just because he must wholly exit the cognitive obscuration, must exhaust his knowing and his rescuing of beings, the bodhisattva cannot stop at a cessation that merely ends suffering for himself. In other words, "non-abiding" is not the bodhisattva falling short, not yet qualified to enter nirvāṇa—on the contrary, his nirvāṇa is deeper than the two-vehicle's by a whole degree, so deep that no "abode" can hold it.

This point is critical, for it shuts down a common misreading: that the bodhisattva is still turning about in saṃsāra because "his practice falls short and he cannot get into nirvāṇa." The truth is the reverse. His non-abiding is the upshot of *completing* the exit from the cognitive obscuration, not the residue of unfinished work. One who has not yet attained has no standing to say "I decline the realization"; only one already fit to attain can have his not-taking mean anything at all. The nirvāṇa of no-abode is the highest of the nirvāṇas, not a deficiency of nirvāṇa.

Take an image. A physician practices in a plague-struck city. The gates stand wide; he may leave the infected city whenever he likes—he has the antibodies, the pass to leave, a safe home outside. That he *can* leave is his competence, not his chain. But he does not leave. Not leaving—not because he cannot get out (he can), and not because he is enamored of the city (the city is wretched and dangerous), but because there are still people lying in the wards. His "not leaving the city" rests precisely on his "being able to leave it": a man who could never have gotten out cannot be said to "stay"—he is merely trapped. Only one who truly can go can have his not-going count as staying.

The nirvāṇa of no-abode is exactly this "able to go, yet not going." Prajñā gave him the power to leave the city; great compassion turned that power into a reason to remain.

## III. Vow-Power Is the Power That Refuses the Terminus

But we have so far spoken only of compassion and wisdom. These are the two wings of the nirvāṇa of no-abode—yet what is it that beats those wings, that keeps the bodhisattva from sinking down into the "actionless" ground where he might so easily come to rest? The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* supplies the word that compassion-and-wisdom overlie, and that is more fundamental than either. The treatise says:

> Though [the bodhisattva] knows the true mark of all dharmas to be unconditioned and actionless, yet because of the original vow and great compassion, wishing to liberate sentient beings, within the actionless he liberates them all by the power of energy.[^3]

This sentence presses the question forward from "compassion and wisdom" to "the vow." Look closely at its layers.

"Though he knows the true mark of all dharmas to be unconditioned and actionless"—this is prajñā. The bodhisattva knows full well that the true mark of dharmas is unconditioned, actionless; that is, he has already seen the realm of the actionless, which is precisely the face of nirvāṇa. Cessation lies before his eyes; he not only sees it, he understands it to be the ultimate truth.

"Yet because of the original vow and great compassion, wishing to liberate sentient beings"—this is the pivot of the turn. A single "because of" marks the ground for not sinking into the actionless: the original vow, joined to great compassion. Note that the *original vow* comes first. It is not that compassion comes first and a vow is added afterward; rather, the original vow laid down at the very first arising of the aspiration, carrying compassion with it, is the reason the bodhisattva will not rest before the actionless.

"Within the actionless he liberates them all by the power of energy"—this is the result. Knowing all to be actionless (knowing he need do nothing at all), he insists on raising energy "within the actionless," on liberating beings. In the very place where "nothing need be done," he insists on doing.

This brings the last motive force of the nirvāṇa of no-abode into the open: the vow is precisely the power "unwilling to make cessation the terminus." Compassion and wisdom explain *why the bodhisattva is able* not to abide in either extreme; the vow explains *why he means* not to abide—above all, why he will not make the path's end of that nirvāṇa already within reach, already his to take.

There is a paradox worth dwelling on here: "within the actionless, by the power of energy, he liberates all." "Actionless" and "energy" are, taken literally, contradictory—if all is unconditioned, actionless, what room is there for energy? Is energy not exactly "doing something"? The treatise sets the two side by side not by oversight but to mark the finest tension in the bodhisattva's life: his energy is energy built squarely upon "knowing the actionless." One who does not understand the actionless can only exert worldly effort—grasping, seeking, more wearied the more he does. The bodhisattva raises energy "within the actionless": he does every deed of liberation, yet plants in none of them a real grasping at "I am doing it, there is something to be done, there are beings to be liberated." And so he is "never wearied." Where does weariness come from? From the acquisitive mind that says, "I have done so much and still see no end." Having already seen ultimate emptiness, with no dharma to be gained, the bodhisattva has no fatiguing "I-who-liberates" left to be worn down. The vow turns within emptiness, and so it is inexhaustible, unwearying.

One more ordering deserves notice: "the original vow and great compassion"—the vow first, compassion after. The sequence is not arbitrary. Were one to say only "great compassion," it would be easy to take the bodhisattva's non-abiding for an emotional impulse: he sees beings suffer, cannot bear it, and so stays. The feeling is real enough, but feeling rises and falls and tires; feeling cannot bear up a charge that runs "to the end of all future time." By setting "the original vow" before great compassion, the treatise roots that charge in the vow laid down at the first arising of the aspiration—a vow that has already seen into emptiness and so cannot regress. The vow is prior; only then does compassion have an inexhaustible source. It is the vow without self-nature, the vow that does not turn back, that casts a momentary unbearableness into an unabandoning that runs to the end of all future time.

The *Vimalakīrti*, in its Chapter on Bodhisattva-Conduct, casts this "unwillingness" into the most compressed of formulas:

> As for the bodhisattva: he does not exhaust the conditioned, nor does he abide in the unconditioned. What is "not exhausting the conditioned"? It is not departing from great kindness, not abandoning great compassion … teaching and transforming beings, never for a moment wearying.[^4]

"Not exhausting the conditioned, not abiding in the unconditioned"—these eight characters are the Mahāyāna's most economical statement of the bodhisattva's double refusal. The "conditioned" is the world of dependent arising and contrivance; the bodhisattva does "not exhaust" it—does not, having worked it through to emptiness, let go his hold. The "unconditioned" is the cessation of nirvāṇa; the bodhisattva does "not abide" in it—does not dock there to enjoy his rest. And the sutra at once asks and answers itself: what bears up the "not exhausting the conditioned"? "Not departing from great kindness, not abandoning great compassion … teaching and transforming beings, never wearying"—what bears it up is exactly that vow-power, carrying great compassion, that never wearies.

So three texts interlock. The *Cheng weishi lun* gives the systematic name, "the union of compassion and wisdom." The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* digs, beneath compassion and wisdom, to the "original vow" as the final mover. The *Vimalakīrti* gathers it into the double refusal, "not exhausting the conditioned, not abiding in the unconditioned." The vow is not a fourth thing standing beside compassion and wisdom; it is the motive force by which those two become "an activity directed toward beings" rather than "two capacities at rest."

## IV. Rest Is Declined Not Because It Is Impossible, but Because of the Vow

We can now state the paper's central thesis plainly—and we must be careful to mark it off from two postures it superficially resembles but in substance utterly differs from.

The thesis is: **rest is declined not because it is impossible, but because of the vow.**

The whole weight of the sentence rests on the words *not because it is impossible*.

There are many in the world who "cannot stop." Some, having made their name and fortune, go on chasing in the same anxious way—not because they are unable to stop, but because they have no idea how to stop. The inertia of desire pushes them on; each goal attained is at once replaced by the next, and their "not resting" is a driven restlessness. This is not the bodhisattva's non-abiding. The bodhisattva's prajñā long ago dismantled the engine that drives; the instant he wished to rest, he could rest down to the root.

There is another kind: those who "cannot rest" though they would. Such a one wants to reach some terminus that is, in principle, unreachable—the ideal forever recedes over the horizon, the resting place simply does not exist. His "not resting" is a helpless tragedy, a futility to which fate has sentenced him. This too is not the bodhisattva's non-abiding. For the bodhisattva the resting place is altogether real; he even stands at its gate, one step from entering.

The bodhisattva is neither. His non-abiding holds in a case where both premises—"can rest" and "the rest is real"—are fully met, and still he does not rest. Here is no restless engine and no trick of fate, only a lucid, free choice: the gate is open, the rest is real, I see it perfectly clearly—and I, because of the vow, do not go in.

This is the freedom of no-abode. It is called freedom precisely because it rests on "it could have been otherwise." A trapped man staying put is not free; a futile man circling is not free; only one who truly *can* go, and knows the road ahead to be real rest, has in his not-going a freedom that is total. The bodhisattva's turning back is the highest freedom that awakening can hold—not the freedom of "I can at last be liberated," but the freedom of "I could be liberated, and for the sake of beings do not."

Return to the runner of the opening. We can now say more precisely why his turning back so moves us. It moves us not because "he could not reach the line and so turned around"—that would be mere failure; nor because "the finish line was an illusion all along"—that would be mere futility. It moves us because the line is truly there, and he is truly able to cross it, and in the very moment he could cross it he chooses not to. His turning back converts "arrival" from a thing he must do into a thing he may decline; and in the "may decline" he makes a decision deeper than arrival. The bodhisattva's non-abiding in nirvāṇa is just such a decision—deeper than nirvāṇa itself. It does not deny that nirvāṇa is real and precious; it only refuses to let nirvāṇa be the full stop.

Two corrections toward the middle way must be set here, lest the reading slide off course.

⚠️ **Not abiding in nirvāṇa is not the same as clinging to saṃsāra.** The bodhisattva's "not abiding in nirvāṇa" must never be read as a preference for the round of rebirth, an inability to part with the three realms. The texts are explicit: it is "not abiding in saṃsāra, and likewise not abiding in nirvāṇa"—both abodes refused. He stays among beings to liberate them by the freedom of "retaining a trace of afflictions to moisten rebirth" that prajņā makes possible, not by the worldling's bondage to affliction and karma, his "abiding in saṃsāra." To read non-abiding in nirvāṇa as "the bodhisattva really prefers the dust-world" is to shrink a lofty twofold refusal into a lopsided fixation. The two refusals stand together; neither side may be clung to.

⚠️ **The "non-abiding" here is not the non-abiding of meditative skill.** That this paper enters again and again by way of vow-power and great compassion is a deliberate marking-off from another approach. In the context of contemplative realization, "non-abiding" names the subtle posture by which the practitioner, wielding the concentration on emptiness, "does not actualize the real" and "does not take up the realization"—a matter of *samādhi*, the technical face of "how to abide without abiding,"[^5] already given its own study. The present paper asks an altogether different question: not "how does one not abide," but "why does one not abide"; not the posture of meditative power, but the choice of vow-power. To run the two together would hollow out the thesis—"vow as the power that refuses the terminus"—into a mere meditation slogan.

## V. A Dialogue with Western Refusals of the Terminus

Set this posture before the mirror of Western thought and its distinctive outline shows itself. Modern Western thought has its own profound refusals of the terminus—but the terminus they refuse is either tragic (no rest is to be had) or restless (one cannot stop). The bodhisattva's non-abiding in nirvāṇa is, precisely, neither. Three mirrors, each reflecting differently.

**The first mirror: Hannah Arendt on "action" and "natality."** For this political thinker, the deepest mark of the human is *action*—and the nature of action is never to arrive at a terminus. Every action is a new beginning, because human beings are born into the world "in the plural," and each birth (what she calls *natality*) brings something unprecedented into it. Action therefore opens new situations endlessly and can never be totted up as a finished product. One may gladly grant that Arendt names with precision the fact that "the human condition refuses finality," and that this is strikingly like the bodhisattva's posture of "continuing toward beings, never wearying"—both are refusals of a "once-and-for-all completion."[^7]

But here too is where they part. The "un-finalizable" in Arendt is at bottom *tragic*: action has no terminus because it is fragile, irreversible, its consequences beyond recall, the actor forever in a situation whose outcome he cannot master—a human fate of "wanting to complete and being unable to." The bodhisattva's non-abiding is the reverse: the terminus is wholly attainable, and he chooses not to take it. Arendt's human acts amid "no rest to be had"; the bodhisattva liberates beings amid "rest available, not taken." The one is judged by fate unable to stop; the other is free to stop and does not. The former is the dignity of human finitude; the latter the dignity of an awakened one's freedom—alike in shape, opposite in substance.

**The second mirror: Kojève and Fukuyama on "the end of history" and "the last man."** This Hegelian lineage holds that human history moves toward a terminus; when that terminus arrives—every fundamental struggle resolved, every craving satisfied—history "ends," and the one who lives after the end is called "the last man." The last man rests in completion: there is nothing left to strive for, nothing to surpass, only comfortable satisfaction. One may gladly grant that this construction honestly voices the human heart's deep longing for a terminus, a consummate rest—people do want a place where all is well and one can lay the load down.

But the bodhisattva's posture is precisely the refusal of the last man. Mark the key to that refusal: the bodhisattva continues not because the terminus cannot be reached (that would fall back into Arendt's tragedy), but because "resting in that terminus" is itself, in his eyes, a cul-de-sac—a cul-de-sac of compassion. When a man settles into his own completion while the suffering of the world still rolls on, that completion is no longer awakening but a refined self-enclosure. The comfort of the last man is exactly what the bodhisattva turns back at the gate to refuse. He would rather be a "walker" who never reaches the end than a "last man" resting in completion.

The title of this paper calls the nirvāṇa the bodhisattva refuses a "cul-de-sac," and the figure must be read with care. Nirvāṇa itself is no cul-de-sac—it is true, it is cool, the liberation toward which everyone who practices ought to turn. It becomes a cul-de-sac for the bodhisattva not through any flaw in nirvāṇa but through the *act* of "taking it as the terminus and, having stepped in, never coming out." A lane is a dead-end not because the lane is bad but because it leads nowhere further: walk in, and you are at the end. For one who seeks only his own crossing-over, the end is consummation; but for one who has vowed "beings without limit, I vow to liberate," any "end" means shutting still-suffering beings outside the gate. So the same cessation that is, for the two-vehicle traveler, a resting home is, for the bodhisattva, a cul-de-sac he must turn from at the gate. The difference lies not in nirvāṇa but in the vow—it is the vow that re-marks a place meant for homecoming as a lane not to be entered.

A word, in passing, on the classical source. Aristotle made *eudaimonia*—flourishing—the final end of a human life: the self-sufficient terminus that wants nothing further, toward which the goodness of all things at last points, the consummate resting place. "The end of history" and "the last man" are only this ancient teleology's modern echo: always a self-sufficient terminus up ahead, waiting to be reached and rested into. The bodhisattva-path shakes that schema at the root—not because no terminus exists, but because, for an awakened one, to treat any terminus as a place to rest into is to turn one's back on beings.

Three mirrors reflect one and the same contrast. The Western refusals of the terminus either hold the terminus to be unreachable (Arendt) or describe the terminus as desirable (Kojève, Fukuyama, Aristotle); the bodhisattva's non-abiding in nirvāṇa faces a terminus that is truly reachable and truly desirable, and—held by vow and great compassion—calmly declines a rest it could have enjoyed.

## VI. The Vow Is the Power Unwilling to Make Cessation the End (Conclusion)

Draw the three papers of this part, "Vow and Emptiness," together, and one line comes clear.

We began by establishing the vow of the one without a vow: with a mind of no-acquisition, the supreme vow is raised, and the one who vows, the thing vowed, and the vow itself are all empty in their three wheels—there is no "I who vows," and yet the vow is inexhaustible; to see that the vow is empty in nature is, exactly, not to dismiss vow-power. We then asked: if there is no one who vows and yet the vow is inexhaustible, how does it not regress? The answer lay in the patience of non-arising—the vow having no self-nature, no dharma can make it turn back, and so it is unsnatchable and inexhaustible. Now we arrive at the last question: where does the non-regressing vow go?

The answer is: it goes toward a refusal. It goes toward the refusal of "the terminus" itself.

The vow is the power unwilling to make cessation the end. Prajñā gave the bodhisattva the power to depart saṃsāra; great compassion gave the bodhisattva the reason not to take nirvāṇa; and the vow—the original vow—is the motive force that beats both into an activity running "to the end of all future time." When the gate of cessation stands open before him, it is the vow that turns the bodhisattva back at the gate. Rest is declined not because it is impossible, but because of the vow.

And so "Vow and Emptiness" can be sealed. Emptiness was never the ground for voiding the vow; seeing that all dharmas are ultimately empty, seeing that nirvāṇa is actionless, is exactly the ground on which vow-power can "act within the actionless to liberate all." The vow raised without acquisition, the vow without self-nature that does not regress, shows itself finally as a power unwilling to make any cessation its end. It does not arrive, because its very nature is to be unwilling to arrive alone.

Look back over the road these three papers have walked, and one finds that emptiness has all along played not the vow's opponent but its ally. In the first, emptiness freed the vow from the burden of "an I who vows," so that the vow need not lean on a real vowing-subject and yet still stand. In the second, emptiness freed the vow from "the lurking fear of regression," for no dharma arises and so none can be lost. And in this last, emptiness frees the vow from "the seduction of the terminus"—just because the bodhisattva sees that nirvāṇa too is actionless, not to be grasped as a real resting place, he is not detained by it and can turn, unhurried, at the gate. In all three the same principle holds: emptiness does not hollow the vow out but liberates it from everything that would turn it, weary it, or halt it. To see that the vow is empty in nature is, far from dismissing vow-power, precisely the completion of vow-power's thoroughness.

And the bodhisattva who turns back at the gate—toward what does he continue? What he does not abide in includes even the "ultimate nirvāṇa" that he himself could enter—that cessation which, on an earlier stretch of the road, was duly settled as the inward consummation within the bodhisattva's own ground.[^6] He has not stopped there. He turns, and presses on, toward a perfection farther than cessation—one for which even "ultimate nirvāṇa" is still only the midpoint of the way.

As for where that road finally leads, and what that perfection is—that is the last page of this book, still unopened. Here only a half-step signpost is set, left for what follows.
