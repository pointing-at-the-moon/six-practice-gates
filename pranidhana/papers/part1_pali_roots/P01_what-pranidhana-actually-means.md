# What Praṇidhāna Actually Means
**Vow, Pledge, and Transference: Distinguishing Praṇidhāna, Samādāna, and Pariṇāmanā**

*中文主題：願不是許願——願、誓、迴向三詞辨義與願門命題之界*

## Abstract

This is the opening chapter of the *praṇidhāna* (vow) volume of the *Six Practice Gates*, and its task is to draw the propositional boundary for the volume as a whole. Contemporary Western (and Chinese) self-help culture habitually misreads *making a vow* as a more devout form of *goal-setting* or *wishing*; this chapter argues instead that *praṇidhāna* is not a wish, not craving, and not manifestation — the difference lying not in the intensity of will but in the object the will is oriented toward, and in whether the one who vows presupposes a self to be realized. The argument proceeds by distinguishing three terms: the *Ekottarāgama* and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* establish the vow as the founding of an orientation toward awakening; the *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra* establishes the pledge as the decisive undertaking of what is received; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s chapter on sympathetic-joy transference and the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra*'s chapter on the ten transferences establish transference as the re-direction of merit's aim rather than a hand-over; and a single line of the *Saṃyuktāgama* sets the boundary between vow and craving. From these the chapter argues that the vow is a constitutive rather than an acquisitive act, that transference is the turning of an aim rather than the transfer of a thing, and that the three terms, mutually entailing, each block one of three misreadings — *the vow is a wish*, *the vow is craving*, *the vow is a transaction*. The conclusion: the three terms together define the gate of vows' thesis that *the vow is not a wish*, and lay the semantic ground for the chapters that follow.

**Keywords:** praṇidhāna; samādāna; pariṇāmanā; vow; aspiration; transference; primal vow (pūrva-praṇidhāna); craving (taṇhā); wholesome desire (chanda); constitutive vs acquisitive; three-term distinction; the gate of vows

## I. The Problem

Every turn of the year, the same gesture repeats. People write resolutions in fresh notebooks. They paste images of the house, the body, the title they want onto a vision board. *Place your order with the universe* circulates as folk wisdom — name the desire clearly enough, hold it with enough conviction, and it will be drawn to you. All of these share a single shape: they point a life toward something not yet realized, and they hold that pointing with certainty.

Seen through that shape, the bodhisattva's *I vow to liberate the boundless beings* looks like one more instance of the same thing. It too throws the will toward a future; it too holds with unshakable resolve. And so a perfectly natural — and perfectly dangerous — misreading is born: that *making a vow* is just a more devout, more grandiose form of *goal-setting*, or a more sacred kind of *wishing*. The danger lies precisely in the resemblance. The closer two things look, the sharper the line that must separate them; without it, the entire volume of *praṇidhāna* will go astray from its very first step, turning a training in *re-orienting a life* into a method for *getting what one wants more effectively*.

For this reason the thesis of the volume has to be set first as a negation: **the vow is not a wish.** The difference is not one of intensity of will — not a matter of who wants harder, holds longer, or believes more fully. Measured by *throwing the will toward a future and holding it with conviction*, the New Year's resolution, the vision board, and the law of attraction need yield nothing to any maker of vows. The difference lies in two deeper places. First, *what the will is oriented toward*: a future in which an *I* will arrive, receive, or become — or a horizon (awakening, and the beings) that empties the one who vows. Second, *whether a self is presupposed as the thing to be realized*: wishing always installs, on the far side of that future, an *I who will arrive*, an *I who will receive*; the vow throws the same fire toward a destination at which no *I* is waiting.

To make these two layers clear, one cannot begin by laying out the whole doctrine of the vow. This chapter does the groundwork first — the standing convention with which each gate of this series opens: it distinguishes a few near-synonyms that are habitually run together and that in fact each carry their own sense. In the gate of vows, the three are: *praṇidhāna* (the vow, aspiration), *samādāna* (the pledge, the undertaking), and *pariṇāmanā* (transference, dedication). They are not three names for one thing. They are three moments of a single act of orientation. To distinguish them is to draw the propositional boundary for the whole volume: the vow is not a wish, not craving (*taṇhā*), not manifestation. What this chapter establishes is exactly that boundary, and the semantic ground on which the later chapters will build.

## II. The Scriptural Basis

### 2.1 *Praṇidhāna*: The Founding of an Orientation toward Awakening

The root sense of the vow is preserved in its plainest form in a first-person aspiration from the *Āgama* corpus. The *Ekottarāgama*, in the *Chapter on Hearing the Dharma*, records a practitioner who, on seeing a Buddha, was so overcome with joy that he gave rise to this vow: *May I in a future age become a Buddha, even as the Buddha of Lamplight (Dīpaṃkara).*[^1]

The verb deserves attention: he *gave rise to* the vow. The vow is not an admiration suspended in the heart; it is an act of orientation that is *aroused* — set in motion. Nor is its content *to obtain some thing*; its content is *may I in a future age become a Buddha* — the whole of oneself turned toward awakening. The vow is grammatically transitive, but what it reaches is not an acquirable object; it is a *direction of one's existence*. The point looks minor and is in fact the seed of the entire distinction between vow and wish: the object of a wish is a thing (health, wealth, the wanted circumstance); the object of the vow is *one's own orientation itself*.

The treatise layer states this more sharply. Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, expounding the bodhisattva's arising of the mind, speaks of *binding the mind to the wish: may I become a Buddha*, and of how *at the moment the mind is first aroused, one vows: may I become a Buddha*.[^2] The phrase *binding the mind* points to the constitutive character of the vow: it ties scattered intention to a single mark, and that bound orientation, of itself, founds the identity of the one who vows. Once the vow is aroused, the one who vowed is no longer the person who merely *wanted some thing*; he has become a person *whose entire life-direction is awakening*.

### 2.2 *Samādāna*: The Decisive Undertaking of What Is Received

In the Chinese translations, *shì* (誓) is a word of many senses (the trap created by that polysemy is treated in §V). This chapter takes only the one sense that stands next to the vow without being identical to it: the *decisive undertaking* of what one receives and holds. The *Upāsaka-śīla-sūtra*, in the *Chapter on the Five Precepts*, discussing how the precepts come to be established, says: *In matters such as these, if one does not make the undertaking, and does not receive it from another, then it is not established.*[^3]

Here *making the undertaking* and *receiving it from another* are set side by side as two faces of one act: the pledge is a *decisive reception* — receiving from another in such a way that what is received becomes established in oneself. It is not the founding of an orientation toward a distant mark — that is the vow. It is the present-moment shouldering and sealing of what has been received. The vow sets the mark; the pledge holds the reception. Their directions differ: the vow faces outward and far, the pledge faces inward and now; the vow declares *where I shall be oriented*, the pledge resolves *what I take up at this moment*. An orientation that is only aroused and never held is like an arrow loosed without fletching — it leaves the string but cannot fly straight; the pledge is the fletching that keeps the vow from scattering. (This sense of receiving-and-holding touches on the reception of precepts; this chapter takes only the semantic distinction and does not address the institution or ritual of precept-reception.)

### 2.3 *Pariṇāmanā*: The Re-Direction of an Aim

The third term, transference, is the one most easily read as *giving merit away*. But the treatises set its structure with great precision. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, in the *Chapter on Transference through Sympathetic Joy*, distinguishes what in merit can and cannot be transferred: *This merit cannot be given to all beings; what can be given is its fruit … the substance of the good cannot be given to another.*[^4]

This sentence draws the form of transference: what can be transferred is the *orientation of the fruit* that merit ripens into; what cannot be transferred is *the substance of the good* itself. Why can the substance of the good not be given to another? Because a wholesome root, as a virtue already arisen, is a fact of the vowing person's own mental continuum, and cannot be passed from one hand to another like an object; the only thing that can be re-aimed is the direction in which its future ripening will tend. The *Avataṃsaka-sūtra*, in the *Chapter on the Ten Transferences*, makes the object of intention the criterion: a wholesome root that is not oriented toward beings *is not called transference*; only when it takes all beings as its object *is it called transference*.[^5]

Read together, the two passages show that transference is transference by virtue of *whether its orientation extends to all beings*, not by virtue of *how much has been sent out*. The vow founds an orientation; transference continually re-channels the fruit accumulated along the way back into that orientation. It is not *handing over a thing*; it is *turning an aim* — a formal distinction that will become, in §III and §V, the key to keeping the vow from being read as a transaction.

### 2.4 The Boundary between Vow and Craving (Setting the Boundary Only)

A last boundary must be set first, while its mechanism is reserved for later: the difference between the vow and craving. The *Saṃyuktāgama*, in its 561st discourse, records a brahmin asking whether the practitioner's desire comes to rest, and the discourse answers with a counter-question: *Once he has reached the monastery, does that desire not cease?*[^6]

The sense is this: wholesome desire (*chanda*), once it reaches its object, comes of itself to rest; craving (*taṇhā*), whose nature is the perpetuation of the self, having reached one thing seeks another, and never rests of itself. The will on which the vow rests is of the former kind, not the latter — a structure of will that *uses desire to exhaust desire* and will finally dissolve itself. Since the vow takes the cessation of craving as its very direction, it cannot be a higher form of craving, just as one whose aim is to put out a fire cannot be a still fiercer fire. The full mechanism of this distinction — how wholesome desire and craving differ at the level of mental factors — is reserved for a dedicated chapter of this volume; here the boundary is marked only, so that *the vow is not craving* stands from the opening.

## III. The Core Argument

### 3.1 The Three Terms Are Mutually Entailing: One Orientation in Three Moments

Vow, pledge, and transference are not three independent wholesome acts; they are three temporal moments of a single act of orientation. The vow comes first — the founding of a direction toward awakening and the beings. The pledge comes in the middle — a decisive reception that lets that orientation be held and not scattered. Transference comes after — the continual turning of the fruit accumulated along the way back into the mark. The three entail one another: without the vow, the pledge has nothing to hold and transference nothing to return to; without the pledge, the vow is easily aroused and easily lost, the orientation set and at once forfeited; without transference, the accumulated virtue is severed from the mark, degenerating into a private deposit of merit no longer bearing on the original orientation.

To hold the three apart and yet see their mutual entailment is the first structural insight into the gate of vows. It tells us that the vow is not an isolated thought, aroused once and counted done, but an orientation that must be held and continually replenished. A wish is a one-time act — wished and then awaited for fulfilment. The vow runs the whole length of the way — once aroused, it relies on the pledge to hold it across long stretches of time, and on transference to re-aim the fruit of each step back into it. The durative character of the vow is already contained in the three-moment structure of *vow–pledge–transference*.

### 3.2 The Vow Is Constitutive, Not Acquisitive

The most fundamental division between wishing and vowing is that the former is acquisitive and the latter is constitutive.

A wish seeks a thing: health, smooth fortune, the wanted circumstance. Its grammar is *I want to get X*. In that grammar the *I* is the constant — before and after the wish, the *I* remains the same *I*, and all that changes is whether the *I* has gotten X. The vow seeks no thing. *May I in a future age become a Buddha* does not take *Buddha* as a target to be acquired; it is an act that *redefines the orientation of the one who vows*. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* says that *at the moment the mind is first aroused, one vows: may I become a Buddha* — and thereby is named a bodhisattva.[^2] The vow once aroused, the identity of a bodhisattva is founded with it. There is no structure here of *a ready-made I that then goes to seek a Buddha-fruit*; there is *the arising of the vow itself constituting a new orientation of existence, and that orientation redefining the one who vows*.

The difference can be put this way: a wish changes *what I have*, while the *I* stays fixed; the vow changes *what kind of existence I am oriented toward*, and the *I* itself is redefined accordingly. Further still, when the object of that orientation is *I vow to liberate the boundless beings*, the redefined *I* points finally toward a horizon on which it is itself emptied — the mark of the vow does not complete the one who vows but undoes him. This *self-emptying* layer is only touched on here, reserved for the *prajñā* chapters of later volumes; but even on the level of sense and grammar alone, the constitutive character of the vow already suffices to set it apart from the acquisitive character of the wish.

### 3.3 The Primal Vow Is Not the Ordinary Arising of a Vow

Precisely because the vow is constitutive, the *primal vow* (*pūrva-praṇidhāna*) spoken of in the Pure Land tradition must be strictly distinguished from the ordinary *arising of a vow*. The *Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha* makes the establishment of a Buddha-field rest on the power of the *primal vow*, by which the measure of a lifespan becomes *long or short at will*.[^8] The *primal* (*pūrva-*) in *primal vow* points to a root already accomplished across past ages and possessing efficacy — not the present act of *arising a vow* in a single moment of thought.

To read *primal vow* flatly as *the vow he made* erases the word *primal*, and erases with it the whole layer in which *the power of the vow is an already-accomplished efficacy* — and the mechanism of the Pure Land teaching lies exactly in that layer: what one who is reborn relies on is not the force of one's own present vowing, but the root vow-power that the monk Dharmākara accomplished across past ages. This chapter only sets the distinction and does not unfold the doctrine of vow-power and other-power; its function here is to remind the reader that the word *vow*, in the Chinese tradition, carries at least two registers — *present arousing* and *vow-power already accomplished across past ages* — and that confusing the two will blur many later arguments.

### 3.4 Re-Direction, Not Hand-Over

Return to transference. §2.3 has already shown the limiting clause: *the substance of the good cannot be given to another; what can be given is its fruit.*[^4] This limit is decisive for the argument, because it determines whether transference — and the gate of vows as a whole — can hold the line of *no transaction*.

Imagine the common picture of transference: merit as a bank balance, transference as a wire transfer, by which I move the merit in my account into yours. The picture is seductive because it is intuitive, quantifiable, with a giver and a receiver. But the treatises plainly deny it: the substance of the good cannot be handed over. Once a wholesome root arises in my continuum, it is a fact of my continuum and cannot change hands like money; what can be turned is only the *orientation of the fruit* its future ripening will tend toward. So the true form of transference is not the *transfer of a thing* but the *re-direction of an aim* — I do not send my merit to beings; I take the ripened fruit of the virtue I have accumulated and re-aim it toward beings and *bodhi*, no longer aiming it at my own ease.

This formal precision is the semantic ground of the red line *the vow is not a transaction*. A transaction requires a deliverable thing, two contracting parties, and the redemption of consideration; transference has no deliverable thing (the substance of the good cannot be given), and the vow demands no consideration in return (what it hands over is an orientation). To read transference as a trade in merit, and to read the vow as an order placed with a Buddha or with the universe, is the same single error — mistaking the *turning of an aim* for the *handing over of a thing*.

### 3.5 How Each of the Three Terms Blocks One Misreading

The distinction of the three terms can now be drawn to a close: the precise definition of each — vow, pledge, transference — blocks exactly one of the three misreadings listed at the opening.

That the vow is a *constitutive founding* and not an *acquisitive want* blocks the error *the vow is a wish* — a wish acquires a thing while the *I* stays fixed, the vow redefines the orientation of the *I*. That the will on which the vow rests is the *desire that rests on reaching its object*, the wholesome desire, and not the *craving that, having reached, seeks more*, blocks the error *the vow is a higher form of craving* — the vow takes the cessation of craving as its direction and cannot be a fiercer craving. That transference is the *re-direction of the fruit's aim* and not the *hand-over of the substance of the good* blocks the error *the vow is a transaction* — there is no deliverable thing, and no consideration is demanded in return. The three terms, mutually entailing, are therefore not merely a dictionary-style classification but a threefold firewall: from three directions, it separates the vow from the wish, from craving, and from manifestation. The propositional boundary of the gate of vows is hereby set.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Bridges

The vow stands adjacent to three Western bodies of thought on *the directed will*. Examining each for its resemblance and its fundamental difference sharpens the contour of the vow. One caveat first: all three comparisons below are heuristic analogies (⚠️), used to illuminate one face of the vow, not to offer any modern theory as its theoretical equivalent.

**First, second-order desire and intentionality (a deep resonance, with a blind spot to mark).** The philosopher Harry Frankfurt distinguishes first-order desires (wanting some thing directly) from second-order desires (wanting to want something — having a desire about one's own desires). Under that distinction, vowing is indeed a second-order orientation of the will — not a first-order grasping impulse but a re-desiring of *one's whole direction of wanting*; what the one who vows desires is *to become a person whose wanting is for awakening*. The resemblance runs deep, deep enough to show that the vow is no low-grade emotional impulse.

And yet second-order desire theory still anchors itself, in the last instance, on a self: it is *I* who am to become the person *I* aspire to be, and the second-order structure serves a self's integrity and autonomy. The object of the bodhisattva's vow — *I vow to liberate the boundless beings* — runs precisely the other way: its mark dissolves the very self that vows. The vow can thus be called a second-order will *whose subject will finally dissolve itself*, for which Frankfurt's framework has no corresponding place. **Irreducible:** second-order desire theory can illuminate the *orientation* face of the vow (the vow is a desire about desire) but cannot reach its *self-emptying* face (the vow's destination empties the one who vows); to let the former stand for the latter is to demote a vow of no-self to a project of a self's integrity.

**Second, goal-setting theory (a misreading to correct).** The goal-setting theory of modern management and psychology (Edwin Locke and Gary Latham), together with the principle that goals be *specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound* (SMART), holds that clear, measurable goals drive behaviour effectively. The vow resembles this in that it is no diffuse daydream: it has direction, it has long-range commitment, it is not lax. A reader who imagines that *making a vow just means going with the flow and not taking it seriously* needs goal-setting theory as a corrective: the vow is solemn, it is carried through.

But the fundamental difference is this: a goal is completable, measurable, and held by a self; the vow — above all *I vow to liberate the boundless beings* — is structurally uncompletable, and its object is self-emptying. One common misreading needs special correction here: the vow's *uncompletability* is not its failure, nor a design flaw awaiting repair; it is its very form. A vow to liberate beings who can never be exhausted, measured by *completion rate*, is forever a failure — but the vow was never measured by completion rate; what it measures is the *truth of the orientation*. **Irreducible:** to read the vow as a *more efficient, more grandiose to-do list* is to lay a paradigm of *the completable, the measurable, the self-held* over an orientation that is *uncompletable, unmeasurable, and finally self-emptying* — a misplaced paradigm, off by a thousand miles.

**Third, manifestation and the law of attraction (where the cut must be sharpest).** *Manifestation* and *the law of attraction* — the *place your order with the universe* teaching, of which Rhonda Byrne's *The Secret* is the representative — name a real longing: to orient one's life by a vow, and to believe that this orientation can change one's circumstances. In *pointing toward something not yet realized and holding it with conviction*, it overlaps almost exactly with vowing in outward appearance, and is the very type of the *the closer they look, the sharper the line must be* warning of this chapter's opening.

But its structure is transactional: place the order, await delivery — the whole process presupposing a self *that will receive the result* and a universe *that will comply*. The vow is not transactional; as §III argued, what it hands over is an orientation, what it demands is not a result, and it has no deliverable thing and no contracting counterparty. The same fire, toward opposite objects: manifestation guards a future self that will arrive and receive; the vow points toward a destination void of the one who vows. **Irreducible:** manifestation and the vow share the *conviction directed at the future* outward form, yet stand exactly opposed on the point of *whether a self that receives the result is presupposed* — which is the core division of this volume's thesis, and cannot be erased by nearness of appearance.

## V. Corrections of the Middle Way

**❌ Misreading: the vow is manifestation, an order placed with the universe.** To read the vow as a transaction — *I vow, and the universe (or the Buddha) complies* — is the most fundamental misunderstanding of the gate of vows. §III and §IV have already set its ground from both sense and structure: transference is the turning of an aim, not the hand-over of a thing; the vow is the constituting of an orientation, not the acquiring of a result. A transactional structure presupposes a contracting self and a counterparty who will perform; the vow neither demands a result nor presupposes a contracting party. Any passage that can be read as *placing an order in exchange for delivery* has already departed from the gate of vows. This is a logical error in doctrine, not a question of the weight of a phrase.

**⚠️ The word *shì* (誓) does not map cleanly one-to-one onto *samādāna*.** The Chinese character *shì* can correspond to several source terms — *praṇidhāna*, *pratijñā*, *samaya*, *vrata* — and does not form a clean one-to-one correspondence with *samādāna*. This chapter isolates only its sense of *receiving-and-holding, the decisive undertaking* (i.e., *receiving from another so that what is received becomes established*, in the context of precept-reception), and does not claim that *shì* equals *samādāna*. A direct counter-instance: Amitābha's *I vow not to become a Buddha* — the *vow* there is in fact *praṇidhāna* at its height, not the undertaking-sense of the pledge. The distinction must therefore be pinned to context — it is the precept-reception context of *receiving from another* that gives this *shì* its undertaking-sense, and the character *shì* alone cannot bear it.

**⚠️ The *primal vow* is not the *arising of a vow*.** The primal vow (*pūrva-praṇidhāna*) names a vow-power rooted in past ages, already accomplished and possessing efficacy; the *arising of a vow* is the ordinary present-moment act of arousing. The two are of different registers and are not interchangeable. To slide *primal vow* into *the vow he made* erases the *primal* (*pūrva-*) that carries the sense of *vow-power as accomplished efficacy*, and strips the later arguments concerning other-power of their basis.

**⚠️ *Transference* is not the *arising of a vow*.** The arising of a vow comes first — setting the mark; transference comes after — turning the already-accumulated fruit into that mark. Their temporal order is the very reason for their mutual entailment: first an orientation, then a turning into it. To run the two terms together collapses the front-to-back structure of *the vow sets the mark* and *transference replenishes it* into a single mass, and the three-moment account (*vow–pledge–transference*) fails with it.

## VI. Conclusion

**In brief.** The vow is the structural founding that turns the whole will toward awakening; the pledge holds it by decisive reception; transference replenishes it by the turning of merit's aim. The three terms, mutually entailing, draw the propositional boundary of the whole volume: the vow is not a wish, not craving, not manifestation. The difference among the three lies not in the intensity of will but in the object the will is oriented toward, and in whether the one who vows still presupposes a self to be realized. The precise definition of each term blocks, from three directions, three misreadings, forming a firewall that guards the thesis of the gate of vows.

**For understanding.** This chapter teaches no technique of vowing or of transference — that is not the office of research. It points only to a turn in understanding: when *making a vow* is seen as a constitutive orientation rather than an acquisitive want, it is no longer bound to the anxiety of *whether I can actually achieve it*. The sincerity of a vow does not hang on whether the result is in hand; what it measures is whether the orientation is true, not whether the harvest is rich. Once this is seen clearly, the line between wishing and vowing — blurred at first — comes into focus: the two use the same fire, but toward opposite objects.

**Open question.** Yet this leaves a question unanswered. If the vow is *a point that is aroused*, how can it grow into *an entire path*? Where exactly does that founding first stand, and on what strength can it be held, crossing long stretches of time without retreat? To answer the first question, the next step must return to one of the oldest scenes of all — a practitioner, before a Buddha named Lamplight, and the primal vow he there made. From a point of arousing, this volume will watch the vow grow into the whole path of *bodhi*.

[^5]: *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* (《大方廣佛華嚴經》, 80-fascicle recension), fascicle 23, *Chapter on the Ten Transferences*, translated by Śikṣānanda (Tang), Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 10, no. 0279, p. 126b. The *is not called transference / is called transference* criterion turns on whether the object of intention extends to all beings; the rendering here is a summary of that sense. The full doctrine of transference is treated in a later chapter of this volume (P15, *Universal Transference*).

[^6]: *Saṃyuktāgama* (《雜阿含經》), fascicle 21, discourse 561, translated by Guṇabhadra (Liu Song), Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 2, no. 0099, p. 147a. The discourse distinguishes wholesome desire (*chanda*), which rests on reaching its object, from craving (*taṇhā*), whose nature is the perpetuation of the self. The mental-factor mechanism of wholesome desire and craving is treated in a dedicated chapter of this volume (P04); this chapter takes only the boundary.

[^7]: Pāli parallels (reference level, not cited inline): Sumedha's primal vow appears in the *Buddhavaṃsa* IIA, *Sumedha-kathā* (PTS edition); the self-cessation of wholesome desire on reaching its object appears in *Saṃyutta Nikāya* 51.15, *Uṇṇābha Sutta* (S v 271); the sense of *samādāna* as *self-reception, receiving-and-holding* appears in the *Pali-English Dictionary*, s.v. *samādāna* / *sīlasamādāna*. The volume's banner *from Pāli Roots* is true with respect to the root of the *concept* of the vow, and is not a claim to a verbatim Pāli base text; this source policy was declared at the head of the volume.

[^8]: *Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha* (《佛說無量壽經》), upper fascicle, translated by Saṃghavarman (Cao Wei), Taishō Tripiṭaka vol. 12, no. 0360. The *primal vow* (*pūrva-praṇidhāna*) names a root vow-power across past ages, distinct from the ordinary act of *arising a vow*; the full doctrine of vow-power and other-power is treated in the chapters of this volume on Amitābha's primal vow (Part V).

## Cross-References to Other Papers in the Series

This opening paper of the *Vow* volume establishes the three shared terms — vow, pledge, transference — that ground every paper that follows. What it points toward:

- **Signpost to P02 (*The Vow before Dīpaṃkara*)**: the "vow as the arousing of an orientation toward awakening" established here in §2.1 is unfolded in P02 as Sumedha's founding vow.
- **Signpost to P04 (*Chanda, Not Taṇhā*)**: §2.4 only draws the boundary that the vow is not craving; its mental-factor mechanism (*chanda* versus *taṇhā*) is taken up there.
- **Signpost to Part III (the vow as a perfection)**: §3.2, "the vow is constitutive, not acquisitive," prepares the vow's standing as a *pāramitā*.
- **Signpost to Part V (the primal vows of Amitābha)**: the distinction in §3.3 between the primal vow (*pūrva-praṇidhāna*) and ordinary vowing is unfolded there as the doctrine of other-power.
- **Signpost to Part VI P15 (universal transference)**: the lexical ground laid in §2.3, "transference re-directs an aim rather than handing over a thing," is completed there as the full doctrine of transference.

On the relation to the *Pointing-at-the-Moon Octalogy*: this paper's propositional boundary — the vow is neither manifestation nor craving — shares a root with the wisdom-axis treatment of "non-attainment" and "abiding nowhere"; the vow's self-emptying face (its terminus empties the one who vows) awaits the convergence in this volume's Part VII with the Prajñā papers.
