# The Active Voice of the Four Noble Truths

**The Four Great Vows as the Grammar of Universal Aspiration**

## Abstract

The four lines every Chinese Buddhist knows by heart — "Sentient beings are numberless; I vow to liberate them. Afflictions are inexhaustible; I vow to sever them. Dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to study them. The Buddha-Way is unsurpassed; I vow to accomplish it" — are recited until automatic, yet rarely interrogated: why precisely these four? Is there a grammar binding them? Zhiyi's answer is unexpected: these are not four wishes but the Four Noble Truths cast in another mode. Drawing primarily on the Tiantai sources (Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Diamond Sūtra*, T1699; the Tiantai *Digest of the Fourfold Teaching* recorded by Chegwan, T1931; and the *Mohe Zhiguan*, T1911), with the Chan *Platform Sūtra*'s (T2008) self-nature reading as corroboration, this paper argues that the Four Great Vows are the *active voice* of the Four Noble Truths: the same suffering–origin–cessation–path that the śrāvaka *knows* in order to escape (knowing suffering, severing its origin, longing for cessation, cultivating the path), the bodhisattva rewrites truth by truth into a vow (vowing, *because of suffering*, to liberate; *because of origin*, to sever; *because of path*, to study every gate; *because of cessation*, to accomplish the Buddha-Way). A further bifurcation of compassion structures the set: great compassion takes the suffering-and-origin pair and *removes* (the first two vows); great loving-kindness takes the path-and-cessation pair and *gives* (the latter two). What makes the four a *grammar* rather than a *checklist* is that they are a *form*: the object aspired-to may turn outward (Tiantai's objects of the Four Truths) or inward (Chan's sentient beings within one's own mind) while the form holds invariant — one and the same four-vow form fitted with different objects, which is precisely the proof that it is a syntax governing *how* a vow is made, not four targets to be hit. Using the speech-act category of the commissive and Wittgenstein's notion of grammar as mirrors, the paper illuminates how the vow makes the world conform to the word (its direction of fit), and corrects three misreadings. It closes by setting a signpost — vows are established first, deeds come to fill them (*xing-xing tian-yuan*) — for the volume's later parts, and leaves for the next paper the barb these four conceal: do not "numberless" and "inexhaustible" doom the vow never to be finished?

**Keywords:** the Four Great Vows, the Four Noble Truths, grammar, active voice, the gate of vows and the gate of deeds, vowing upon the Four Truths, the bifurcation of compassion, the self-nature Four Vows, commissive speech-acts, Wittgenstein, deeds filling the vow, Zhiyi, the *Platform Sūtra*

## I. The Question: Why Precisely These Four Lines?

Anyone schooled in the liturgy of Chinese Buddhism can recite these four lines:

> Sentient beings are numberless; I vow to liberate them. Afflictions are inexhaustible; I vow to sever them. Dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to study them. The Buddha-Way is unsurpassed; I vow to accomplish it.

Chanted in dharma assemblies, repeated at morning and evening services, voiced again at the taking of precepts — in frequency these four lines come second only to the Three Refuges. The better one knows them by heart, the easier it becomes to let them slide past as a set formula not worth a second thought. Yet it is exactly such a formula — one that everybody recites and nobody questions — that most deserves a pause and a question: why precisely these four? Would three do? Would a different fourth do as well? Among the four lines themselves, is the ordering arbitrary, or does some inner rule govern it?

A modern reader will naturally hear the four lines as "four grand objectives" — a wishlist of magnificent scope, or a checklist of ultimate targets to be ticked off one by one: deliver every being, sever every affliction, finish studying every dharma-gate, attain buddhahood, and the merit is complete. Today's culture of self-improvement reaches for the four readily and files them under "goal-setting" or the "vision board": write down what you want, pin it to the wall, and work toward it. But the slightest reflection shows the reading jamming at every turn. Beings are *numberless*; afflictions *inexhaustible*; this is a checklist whose boxes can never all be ticked. Treat the four as objectives to be achieved, and they are doomed to failure from the first day. One is then forced either to demote the lines to a kind of pious hyperbole (a thing one says, not a thing one means) or to take the person who makes the vow for a self-deceiving dreamer. Either road leaves the four lines suspect.

Where does the trouble lie? This paper will argue that the trouble lies in the very premise — *reading the four vows as a checklist*. Zhiyi, the founder of the Tiantai school, gives an answer that tears that premise out by the root. As he sees it, the four lines are not four wishes at all but a familiar old document rewritten in another mood. That old document is the Four Noble Truths the Buddha set turning at the first turning of the dharma-wheel: suffering, origin, cessation, path. The Four Great Vows are not four fresh objectives raised on new ground; they are the grammar that rewrites, truth by truth, that diagnosis of the human condition — suffering, origin, cessation, path — into a vow.

To put it another way, what makes the four vows the four vows is not *which four things they vow* but the fact that they exhibit *how a vow is made at all*. They are a grammar, not a list. The paper's claim can be stated in a single sentence: the Four Great Vows are the *active voice* of the Four Noble Truths. The same suffering–origin–cessation–path that the śrāvaka receives in the mood of *knowing* (knowing suffering, severing the origin, longing for cessation, cultivating the path, all in order to win his own escape), the bodhisattva takes up by flipping the mood of each truth from *knowing* to *vowing* (vowing, because of suffering, to liberate all beings; because of origin, to sever all afflictions; because of path, to study every dharma-gate; because of cessation, to accomplish the unsurpassed Buddha-Way, all in order to deliver the multitude). The map is one and the same; the śrāvaka uses it to leave the field, the bodhisattva to stay.

The argument proceeds thus. Section II lays the textual ground: how Tiantai divides the bodhisattva path into the two gates of vow and deed, and how it ties each of the four vows, in plain words, to one of the Four Truths (Sections II and III together form the spine of the argument). Section III distils the central thesis — the active voice of the Four Truths — and supplies the inner rule of the bifurcation of compassion that runs through it. Section IV holds up two mirrors, the speech-act theory of the commissive and Wittgenstein's notion of grammar, to make clear what "the four vows are a form, not a list" actually means. Section V corrects three misreadings into which one easily slips. Section VI closes with the strongest corroboration — the Chan *Platform Sūtra*'s self-nature Four Vows, where the form holds while the object changes — sets a signpost, and leaves one question open.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The Gate of Vows and the Gate of Deeds: The Four Vows as the Summa of "Vow"

To see where the four vows stand, one must first see how Tiantai zones the bodhisattva path as a whole. Zhiyi's *Commentary on the Diamond Sūtra*, lower fascicle, gives an exceedingly spare division:[^1]

> "The bodhisattva's path, though it has many gates, in its great gathering comes to no more than vow and deed. The gate of vows, though many, is summed up as the Four Great Vows; the gate of deeds, though measureless, is summed up as the six pāramitās. The Four Great Vows are these: first, those who have not yet crossed the sea of suffering, to bring them across; second, those not yet freed from the bonds of karmic affliction, to free them; third, those who have not yet gained the peace of the truth of the path, to settle them in it; fourth, those who have not yet gained the nirvāṇa of the truth of cessation, to bring them to nirvāṇa."

The passage first draws the whole bodhisattva path into two great gates — vow and deed: however many its threads, they reduce to these two kinds. Then it gives the summa of each. The gate of vows, many as it is, is headed by the Four Great Vows; the gate of deeds, measureless as it is, is headed by the six pāramitās. With that, the skeleton of the entire bodhisattva path stands clear: the four vows are the leading thread on the side of vow, the six pāramitās the leading thread on the side of deed. The vow establishes the direction first; the deed comes to accomplish the matter. (The six pāramitās as the summa of the gate of deeds is the subject of an earlier paper in this series; here it is cited only for its structural position over against the four vows, and its content is not rehearsed.[^p06])

More telling is the second half of the passage. Zhiyi takes the four vows apart line by line, and beneath each he marks the object that is "not yet crossed / not yet freed / not yet settled / not yet gained" — the sea of suffering, the bonds of karmic affliction, the peace of the truth of the path, the nirvāṇa of the truth of cessation. In other words, behind each line of the four vows stands one of the Four Noble Truths. This already lays the groundwork for the explicit assignment, in the next section, of each vow to its truth.

### 2.2 Vowing the Four Great Vows upon the Objects of the Four Noble Truths

The text that states the line-by-line tying of the four vows to the Four Truths most neatly is the systematized handbook of Tiantai teaching — the *Digest of the Fourfold Teaching*, recorded by the Korean monk Chegwan. The work is not from Zhiyi's own hand; it is Chegwan's faithful redaction (c. tenth century) of the Tiantai doctrine of the five periods and eight teachings — when cited, it is best described as "a digest of Tiantai teaching (recorded by Chegwan)," not flatly attributed as "Zhiyi says." Its account of the four vows runs:[^2]

> "From the first arising of the mind, taking the objects of the Four Truths as one's field, one raises the Four Great Vows and cultivates the conduct of the six pāramitās. First, the not-yet-delivered, to deliver them — that is 'sentient beings are numberless; I vow to liberate them,' which takes the truth of suffering as its object. Second, the not-yet-released, to release them — that is 'afflictions are inexhaustible; I vow to sever them,' which takes the truth of origin as its object. Third, the not-yet-settled, to settle them — that is 'dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to study them,' which takes the truth of the path as its object. Fourth, the not-yet-in-nirvāṇa, to bring them to nirvāṇa — that is 'the Buddha-Way is unsurpassed; I vow to accomplish it,' which takes the truth of cessation as its object. The mind once aroused, deeds must come to fill the vow."

This passage sets out, word for word, the correspondence at the heart of this paper:

- Sentient beings are numberless; I vow to liberate them — upon the truth of **suffering**
- Afflictions are inexhaustible; I vow to sever them — upon the truth of **origin**
- Dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to study them — upon the truth of the **path**
- The Buddha-Way is unsurpassed; I vow to accomplish it — upon the truth of **cessation**

Four vows, four truths, one to one. The "suffering–origin–cessation–path" that the śrāvaka cultivates, in the bodhisattva's hands, is rewritten line by line into the four vows of "liberate, sever, study, accomplish." The same Four Truths in a different syntax: the four truths of diagnosis (what suffering is, what origin is, what cessation is, what the path is) are rewritten as the four vows of aspiration (because of suffering, the vow to liberate; because of origin, the vow to sever; because of the path, the vow to study; because of cessation, the vow to accomplish).

The closing words — "the mind once aroused, deeds must come to fill the vow" — are worth holding onto, for they reconnect the division of the two gates set out above: the vow is established first (the four vows arise at the first stirring of the mind), and deeds come to fill it (the six pāramitās fill the vow). A vow is not a slogan to be raised once and then shelved; it asks to be made full by deeds, and deeds in turn are not directionless toil but are steered by the vow. This "deeds come to fill the vow" is the seed of the non-duality of vow and deed, but its unfolding is not the business of this paper; here only the signpost is set.[^3]

(One assignment, easily led astray by surface wording, must be cleared up here. For the correspondence of the four vows to the Four Truths, the two Tiantai sources [the *Digest* and the *Mohe Zhiguan* of the next section] agree on: deliver-beings ← suffering / sever-afflictions ← origin / study-the-gates-and-settle ← path / accomplish-the-Buddha-Way-and-nirvāṇa ← cessation. There circulates an alternative arrangement that, going by the bare word "Buddha-*Way*" (*dào*), pairs "the Buddha-Way" with the truth of the *path* (*dào*) and "dharma-gates" with cessation. This paper holds throughout to the two Tiantai sources, and its reason is this: the truth of the path *is* the "measureless dharma-gates that can be cultivated and studied," and so pairs with "dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to study them"; the truth of cessation *is* "the ultimate accomplishment that is nirvāṇa," and so pairs with "the Buddha-Way is unsurpassed; I vow to accomplish it." One must not be pulled off by the verbal echo of "Buddha-Way" and "path." See the correction in Section V.)

### 2.3 The Bifurcation of Compassion: Compassion upon Suffering-and-Origin, Loving-Kindness upon Path-and-Cessation

The grammar of the four vows has a deeper layer still. The *Mohe Zhiguan*, fascicle six — spoken by Zhiyi, recorded by Guanding — ties the raising of the four vows to a bifurcation of loving-kindness and compassion:[^4]

> "Thereupon, raising great compassion, one raises two vows: 'sentient beings are numberless; I vow to liberate them; afflictions are without number; I vow to sever them.' … Now, for the first time, comprehending, one therefore raises great loving-kindness and raises two vows, namely: 'dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to know them; the unsurpassed Buddha-Way; I vow to accomplish it.'"[^5]

The same work gives a more compact summation: "Raising great loving-kindness and compassion, one raises the four great vows, drawing out the two sufferings and bestowing the two joys."[^6]

Read together, the two passages cut the interior of the four vows into two clear halves:

- The **first two vows** (deliver beings, sever afflictions) are raised by **great compassion**, and their objects are the two truths of **suffering and origin** — this is the side that *removes* suffering: beings are sunk in the sea of suffering, bound by affliction, and so the compassionate mind draws them out.
- The **latter two vows** (study the dharma-gates, accomplish the Buddha-Way) are raised by **great loving-kindness**, and their objects are the two truths of **path and cessation** — this is the side that *gives* joy: the path is the road toward joy, cessation the ultimate joy, and so the loving mind bestows them.

The bifurcation is no arbitrary symmetry; it is internally coherent. Suffering and origin are the "illness" (the condition and its cause), and so pair with the *removing* of great compassion; path and cessation are "remedy and cure" (the therapy and the recovery), and so pair with the *giving* of great loving-kindness. To draw out the suffering first and bestow the joy after is exactly the physician's order before a patient. The Four Noble Truths are themselves a medical structure of "illness–cause–cure–remedy" (suffering as illness, origin as cause, cessation as recovery, path as the prescription); the four vows take this medical structure over, fit it with the two wheels of loving-kindness and compassion, and so the diagnostic mood of "know the illness, sever the cause, long for the cure, take the remedy" is flipped wholesale into the aspiring mood of "remove suffering, bestow joy."

## III. The Argument: The Four Vows as the Active Voice of the Four Noble Truths

### 3.1 The Same Four Truths, Two Moods

Gathering up the textual ground of the two preceding sections, the central thesis can now be set up formally: **the Four Great Vows are the *active voice* of the Four Noble Truths.**

By "active voice" a single thing is said through a grammatical figure. The Four Noble Truths are, in themselves, a *description* — they state the reality of the human condition objectively (there is suffering, suffering has its origin, suffering can cease, cessation has its path). This description the śrāvaka receives in the mood of *knowing*: know suffering, sever origin, realize cessation, cultivate the path, the aim being to let *oneself* escape suffering. The same description, in the bodhisattva's hands, has each truth rewritten into a vow turned toward *all beings* — no longer merely "I know there is suffering" but "beings are numberless; I vow to liberate them"; no longer merely "I sever my own origin" but "afflictions are inexhaustible; I vow to sever them."

The point is this: the *content* of the Four Truths is not altered by a single word; what is altered is only the *mood*. From passively conceding the reality (knowing) to actively shouldering it (vowing). This is exactly where the figure of the "active voice" bears its weight: the passive says "suffering is known," the active says "I vow to liberate all beings in this sea of suffering" — the subject turns from the looking-on "knower" into the shouldering "vower," the object widens from "my own self" to "all sentient beings." The four vows are the very rule of syntax that performs this flip of mood.

### 3.2 Why a Grammar and Not a Checklist

Now the challenge of Section I can be answered head-on. Reading the four vows as "four grand objectives" jams at every turn because the category "objective / checklist" simply does not apply to them. The four vows are not a *list* (a roster enumerating four items to be done) but a *grammar* (a syntax governing *how a vow is made*).

The difference between the two is categorial. A list means by "which items it enumerates" — its items stand side by side, addable and subtractable; accomplish one and you strike it off, strike them all off and the work is complete. A grammar is not like this: what it governs is *form*, not *content*; it does not tell you which concrete thing to vow but tells you what shape a bodhisattva's vow must take. The four vows are the latter — what they exhibit is this: any complete bodhisattva vow must be raised upon the full span of the reality that is "suffering–origin–cessation–path" (remove suffering, sever the cause, study the dharma, accomplish the fruit), and to lack any one face is to fail of being "great."

Precisely because the four vows are a form and not a content, they can be fitted with different objects while the form holds invariant — a point for which the next section but one (Section VI) will give the most forceful corroboration, where the Chan *Platform Sūtra* swaps the object from "the outward objects of the Four Truths" to "the sentient beings of one's own mind" while the syntax of the four vows does not shift in the least. A thing that can change its content and yet remain itself must be a form, a grammar, and not a list.

### 3.3 The Vow as a Turning of Mood

A last thing must be made plain: the flipping of the Four Truths from "knowing" to "vowing" is itself the secret of what makes a vow a vow. A vow is not some extra emotion or act of will added on outside the Four Truths; it *is* the very act of "receiving the reality in the mood of shouldering it." Śrāvaka and bodhisattva face the same world, the same suffering–origin–cessation–path; what divides them is not "how much they know" but the mood — *knowing* or *vowing* — they take toward one and the same reality.

This is also why the four vows are always tied to the "first arising of the mind": they mark exactly the moment a mind turns from "knower" to "vower." To make the four vows is not to set up four future targets to be hit but to complete, here and now, one conversion of mood: from this point on, the way this mind receives suffering–origin–cessation–path is no longer "so that I may escape" but "so that all may be delivered." The vow is a turn of *mood*, not the entries of a *list*.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Bridges: The Grammar of Promising and the Figure of "Grammar"

To press the thesis — that the four vows are a grammar, the active voice of the Four Truths — to a finer point, two discussions in Western philosophy of language offer two unusually clean mirrors: the speech-act category of the commissive, and Wittgenstein's notion of grammar.

**The first mirror: the commissive speech-act.** J. L. Austin, in *How to Do Things with Words*, proposed that speaking is not only describing the world; some utterances are themselves a "doing." John Searle then sorted speech-acts into kinds, one of which he named the *commissive* — by which the speaker commits himself to some future course of action ("I promise," "I vow"). This kind of speech-act has a directional feature: its *direction of fit* is world-to-word (the world is to be brought into accord with the words), the very reverse of the *assertive* sentence, whose direction is word-to-world (whether it is said rightly is judged by whether it accords with the facts).

This mirror reflects with great precision. The statement of the Four Noble Truths ("suffering–origin–cessation–path is thus") is just such an assertive, its direction of fit word-to-world: it aims to be *said in accord with reality*. The Four Great Vows ("I vow to liberate, to sever, to study, to accomplish") are just commissives, their direction of fit flipped to world-to-word: in making the vow one does not describe how the world already stands but commits oneself to bringing the world (the beings' deliverance, the afflictions' severing) into accord with the words of the vow. What Section III called "the active voice of the Four Truths" is, in the vocabulary of speech-act theory, precisely "a flip from the assertive to the commissive" — and the whole reversal of the direction of fit *is* the grammatical substance of "knowing" turned into "vowing."

**A fundamental difference, and irreducibility.** Yet a mirror is, after all, only a mirror. The promise Searle treats is the ordinary interpersonal commitment: its addressee is finite (I promise *you* one thing) and what is promised can be completed (the matter done, the promise kept, the promise discharged). The addressee of the Four Great Vows, by contrast, is "all sentient beings," "the unsurpassed Buddha-Way" — a promise whose object is set to no upper bound in number (beings numberless, the Buddha-Way unsurpassed), spilling far past the borders Searle's account of promising envisages. A promise that "can never be fully discharged and yet is sincerely made" is well-nigh anomalous within the frame of everyday speech-acts; for the four vows it is precisely the norm. (How this "numberless / unsurpassed" keeps the vow from coming to nothing and makes it, on the contrary, all the more a vow — the side of the commissive's *incompletability* — is a question requiring separate treatment; here only the spilling-over of the border is noted, the rest left to the next paper.) Hence one must say plainly: invoking speech-act theory serves only to throw light on the outline — that the vow is a shouldering that makes the world conform to the word, not a description of facts — and in no way licenses the inference that the four vows reduce to a taxonomy of pragmatics. A mirror can reflect an outline; it cannot stand in for the thing reflected.

**The second mirror: Wittgenstein's "grammar."** In his later philosophy Wittgenstein distinguished two kinds of sentence: empirical propositions (which describe facts and may be true or false) and "grammatical propositions" (which fix the formal rule for *how a concept is used* and themselves describe no fact). The word "grammar," used repeatedly in this paper, takes just this sense: the four vows are a "grammar" not because they state four verifiable facts or targets but because they fix "the form a bodhisattva's vow must take." It is a syntax in the sense proper to a doctrine of practice — telling you *how a vow is made* (upon the full span of the Four Truths, by the two wheels of loving-kindness and compassion, toward all beings), not telling you *what concrete result to vow for*.

This is just what explains the foreshadowing left in Section III: why the four vows can change their object while the form holds invariant. A grammar, qua grammar, is independent of the concrete content poured into it — one and the same syntax can make countless different sentences. This "syntax of the vow" that is the four vows, filled with Tiantai's "outward objects of the Four Truths," makes the vow to deliver the outward multitude; filled with Chan's "sentient beings of one's own mind," makes the vow to deliver oneself of the delusions within one's mind (see the next section). The content differs, the syntax is one — and this is the most forceful proof of "grammar" and not "checklist." One difference must be added: Wittgenstein's "grammar" is a description of the deep rules of language, while the "grammar" of the four vows is one of practice — it governs not only *how a vow is said* but *how a vow is raised, held, and filled*. The mirror reflects the outline of "form before content," and the dharma pours into that outline something the philosophy of language has not: a turning of the mind.

## V. Corrections of the Middle Way

This section marks three readings into which one easily slips (❌ for a doctrinal swerve, ⚠️ for a textual trap), as boundary stakes for holding to the Middle Way.

**❌ The four vows are not "a checklist of four objectives."** This is the misreading the whole paper works against, and the most deeply rooted: reading the four vows as a wishlist of grand scope or a roster of life-targets, even filing them readily under the modern self-improvement vocabulary of "goal-setting," "vision boards," or "manifestation." The swerve here lies not in seeing the four vows as too big but in using the wrong category — taking a *grammar* for a *checklist*. What the four vows govern is the form of the vow (upon the full span of the Four Truths, by the two wheels of compassion, toward all beings), not four results to be ticked off. The vow is a *mood* (a turning of voice), not a *list* (an enumeration of items). Once this is seen, "the boxes can never all be ticked" ceases to be a flaw in the four vows and becomes, instead, the very evidence that they are "the grammar of a vow" and not "a checklist of goals" — though the full unfolding of this step is the business of the next paper; here only the boundary is set.

**❌ The four vows are not the Three Pure Precepts.** Zhiyi has another place where he maps the four vows onto the Three Pure Precepts (restraining-conduct, gathering-the-good, gathering-beings); that argument is made on the side of the *precept-essence* — how the four vows are gathered into the threefold of the bodhisattva precepts. That side belongs to the topic of the precepts, treated in a dedicated paper in the precept volume of this series.[^p_sila] What the present paper takes up is the *vow*-side grammar of the four vows *upon the Four Truths*; the threefold correspondence on the precept side is a different axis and must not be conflated with it. Should a reader find the four vows expounded elsewhere by way of the threefold, let it be understood as an assignment within another frame of questions — running parallel to this paper's "active voice of the Four Truths" without conflict, each with its own concern.

**⚠️ The alternative assignment of the four vows to the Four Truths.** As argued in Section 2.2: in pairing the four vows with the Four Truths, the two Tiantai sources agree on deliver-beings ← suffering / sever-afflictions ← origin / study-gates-and-settle ← path / accomplish-the-Buddha-Way-and-nirvāṇa ← cessation. There circulates an alternative that, going by the bare three words "accomplish the Buddha-*Way*" (*dào*), pairs "the Buddha-Way is unsurpassed; I vow to accomplish it" with the truth of the *path* (*dào*) and "dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to study them" with cessation. This paper holds throughout to Tiantai (the truth of the path = the measureless dharma-gates that can be studied; the nirvāṇa of cessation = the ultimate accomplishment of the Buddha-Way). The correction is no nitpicking: invert the assignment, and the inner grammar of the bifurcation of compassion — *remove* (suffering-and-origin) / *give* (path-and-cessation) (Section 2.3) — is thrown wholesale out of joint. So it must be nailed down.

**⚠️ Variant wording in the citations.** It must be said honestly: the wording of the four vows differs slightly across texts. The *Mohe Zhiguan* has "afflictions are without **number**; I vow to sever them" and "dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to **know** them," diverging in wording from the form current in today's dharma assemblies — "afflictions are **inexhaustible**; I vow to sever them" and "dharma-gates are measureless; I vow to **study** them" (number / inexhaustible, know / study). Wherever this paper quotes directly, it keeps the wording of each source as it stands, without retrofitting to the current form; in the running prose it uses the current phrasing for ease of reading. The reader need not doubt, on account of small verbal differences, that it is one and the same set of four vows — what differs is the words, what is the same is the grammar of "raised upon the Four Truths, arising from loving-kindness and compassion."

## VI. Conclusion

**The Chan corroboration: the self-nature Four Vows, the grammar invariant.** The paper's thesis that "the four vows are a form, not a list" finds its loveliest corroboration in the Chan *Platform Sūtra* (Zongbao recension). When the Sixth Patriarch transmits the four vows, he prefixes "one's own mind" or "one's own nature" to each line:[^7]

> "The sentient beings of my own mind are numberless; I vow to liberate them. The afflictions of my own mind are boundless; I vow to sever them. The dharma-gates of my own nature are inexhaustible; I vow to study them. The unsurpassed Buddha-Way of my own nature; I vow to accomplish it."

The Patriarch then says plainly that "sentient beings" here are not others outside the body but the minds within "the mind" — minds of "wrong views and delusion, falsehood and deceit, the unwholesome, jealousy, malice" — "all of these are sentient beings, and each must deliver itself by its own nature; this is what is called true deliverance." And how does one deliver them? "When the wrong comes, deliver it with the right; when delusion comes, deliver it with awakening; when folly comes, deliver it with wisdom; when evil comes, deliver it with the good — to deliver in this way is called true deliverance." The closing line is most worth savoring: "to think constantly of practice — this is the dharma of vow-power."

The structural significance of this passage lies exactly in this: it moves the *object* of the four vows entirely from the outward to the inward, and the syntax of the four vows does not budge. Tiantai's four vows take "the outward objects of the Four Truths" for their object (sentient beings being the multitude in the sea of suffering); Chan's four vows take "the sentient beings of one's own mind" (sentient beings being the wrong views, delusions, falsehoods, and evils within the mind); the objects differ, but the syntax "numberless, I vow to liberate / inexhaustible, I vow to sever / measureless, I vow to study / unsurpassed, I vow to accomplish" is wholly the same. One and the same syntax can be fitted with utterly different objects and remain itself — this is the living evidence for the very point set up in Section III and reflected in Section IV by Wittgenstein's mirror: the four vows are "a grammar of the vow," not "a checklist of the vow." Form precedes content, and so the object can change while what remains is still the four vows.

**A concise restatement.** To draw the whole together: the Four Great Vows are not four grand objectives laid side by side but the active voice of the Four Noble Truths. The same suffering–origin–cessation–path the śrāvaka receives in the mood of *knowing*, to win his own escape (knowing suffering and severing origin, longing for cessation and cultivating the path), the bodhisattva flips, truth by truth, from *knowing* to *vowing* — vowing because of suffering to liberate, because of origin to sever, because of the path to study, because of cessation to accomplish — and with great compassion *removes* the two truths of suffering and origin, with great loving-kindness *gives* the two truths of path and cessation. What makes the four vows "great" is not which four grand things they vow but the form they exhibit that a bodhisattva's vow must take: upon the full span of reality, by the two wheels of compassion, toward all sentient beings. They are a *grammar*, not a *list*; a *mood*, not a *target*.

**A pointer for practice.** From this a directional pointer follows. In reciting the four vows day by day, the point is not to "take stock, item by item, of how many beings I have delivered and how many afflictions severed" (that is the old habit of mistaking the grammar for a checklist) but that with each recitation one completes anew one turning of mood — recalibrating the way one receives suffering–origin–cessation–path from "to win my own escape" back to "to deliver all beings." A vow is not for tallying progress; it is for correcting one's bearing. And once the recitation is done, one must remember Tiantai's eight characters all the more: "the mind once aroused, deeds must come to fill the vow" — the vow establishes the direction first, deeds come to make it full. A vow not filled by deeds is mere syntax left hanging; deeds not steered by a vow are directionless toil. How vow and deed prove, in the end, to be non-dual is a topic for the volume's later parts; here only the signpost is set.[^8]

**An open question.** Last, there is a barb hidden in these four lines that the paper has steered around throughout and yet cannot leave unmarked at the close. Beings *numberless*, afflictions *inexhaustible*, dharma-gates *measureless*, the Buddha-Way *unsurpassed* — each of these four quantifiers pushes the object aspired-to toward the "inexhaustible." Then is "sentient beings are numberless; I vow to liberate them" not a vow doomed, from its very first instant, never to be finished? To make a vow one knows one cannot reach — is this not, if not self-deception, then futility? This sharp challenge strikes exactly where "numberless / inexhaustible" is most easily misread. To set out the grammar is enough for this paper; whether this *incompletability* is a flaw in the vow, or rather the very criterion that makes it a "vow" and not an "objective" — that is the business of the next paper.
