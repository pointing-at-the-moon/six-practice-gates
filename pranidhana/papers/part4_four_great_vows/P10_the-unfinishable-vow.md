# The Unfinishable Vow

**"Boundless" as a Structure, Not a Target**

## Abstract

"I vow to ferry across the boundless beings" — if beings are truly *boundless*, is the vow not doomed to be unfinished from the moment it is made? This paper argues that the "boundless / inexhaustible / measureless / unsurpassed" of the Four Great Vows is not intimidating hyperbole but a structural component: it deliberately designs the vow's terminating condition so that it can never be met, and therefore the vow never retires. Unfinishability is not a malfunction of the vow; it is precisely the criterion that marks it as a *vow* (an orientation) rather than a *goal* (a to-do list). Building on the prior paper's claim that the Four Vows are the "active voice" of the Four Noble Truths, the paper shows that the vows' objects are coextensive with the realm of birth-and-death, so their inexhaustibility is structural rather than a sign of personal insufficiency. It then takes Zhiyi's "uncontrived Four Great Vows" (*wúzuò sìhóng shìyuàn*) in the *Mohe Zhiguan* as its hinge — "though beings are like empty space, I vow to ferry beings-like-space"; "within the nothing-whatsoever, I therefore seek it"; "like planting a tree in empty space, yet making it flower and fruit" — to show that the posture of vowing while fully knowing the object to be empty and inexhaustible is not futile striving but the *uncontrived*: doing-not-for-completion, and doing while knowing the object to be empty. Three Western mirrors — Setiya's atelic activity, Callard's aspiration, Carse's infinite game — illuminate this structure, yet all three halt at "without endpoint" and do not reach the Dharma's further turn of "vowing even while the object is empty." A vow is not a list that gets crossed off; it is an orientation that forever orients. With this the paper seals Part IV (the Four Great Vows) and sets a signpost toward the "other-power" face of Amitābha's vows in Part V.

**Keywords:** the Four Great Vows, unfinishability, boundlessness, the uncontrived Four Vows, contending with empty space, "within the nothing-whatsoever I therefore seek it," vow versus goal, orientation, the *Mohe Zhiguan*, Zhiyi, atelic activity, aspiration

## I. The Question: What Is a Vow Doomed to Be Unfinished?

Every Chinese Buddhist can recite the four lines: I vow to ferry across the boundless beings; I vow to sever the inexhaustible afflictions; I vow to study the measureless gates of Dharma; I vow to realize the unsurpassed buddha-way. They come easily to the tongue, and yet almost no one stops to press on the barbed word lodged in the first line — *boundless*. If beings are truly boundless, is "I vow to ferry them across" not, from the very instant it is uttered, doomed never to be finished? To raise a vow whose object one knows cannot be reached sounds like either self-deception or wasted effort. A man who writes "ferry across all beings" into his ledger as an item to be done can never, in this life or across measureless aeons, put a checkmark beside that line.

The doubt deserves to be taken seriously, for it strikes exactly where "inexhaustibility" is most easily misread. But its two faces must first be pried apart. One is the emotional, the psychological face — does doing what one knows cannot be finished collapse into a Sisyphean despair, the stone rolled up only to roll forever back? That face has already been met head-on elsewhere in this series, and the worry of "futility" dismantled from several angles; I will not rebuild the argument here, but only borrow its conclusion in a single line.[^ksama] The face this paper takes up is the other one, more basic and far less often asked: what *kind of thing* is a vow that has been *constructed never to be finished*? And wherein, exactly, does it differ from a goal that is "large enough but in principle completable"?

Put the question this way and the matter grows interesting. We assume, by reflex, that "not finishing" is a bad thing — a failure of the plan, a shortfall of capacity. Yet the Four Great Vows write "not finishing" into their very definition: not finished by accident, but made deliberately never to finish. This can no longer be read under the heading of "failure." A thing designed never to arrive at its endpoint, if it is still solemnly called a *vow* and judged by generations of masters to be the genuine raising of the awakening-mind, must mean that "not finishing" is here no defect but some positive structural feature.

The thesis can be stated in one sentence at the outset. The "boundless" of the Four Vows is not a goal so large as to terrify; it is a structure — it designs the terminating condition so that the condition can never be met, and so the vow never runs dry. Unfinishability is not the vow's flaw; it is precisely the criterion that marks it as a *vow* (an orientation) rather than a *goal* (a to-do list). And Zhiyi's "uncontrived Four Vows" pushes this step to the bottom: knowing full well that what is vowed is like empty space, one still says "within the nothing-whatsoever, I therefore seek it" — this is not the wasted toil of Sisyphus but the uncontrived's contending with empty space. What follows takes apart the claim that "the quantifier *is* the structure" (§II), then sets the *Mohe Zhiguan*'s uncontrived vows at the heart of the paper (§III), holds up three mirrors from the philosophy of action (§IV), corrects four misreadings into which one easily slips (§V), and finally gathers the whole, seals Part IV, and plants a signpost for the next (§VI).

## II. The Quantifiers Are the Structure: Designing a Terminating Condition That Can Never Be Met

### 2.1 The Four Quantifiers Are Not Rhetoric but Structural Components

Look first at the four quantifiers of the Four Vows: beings *bound*less, afflictions *in*exhaustible, Dharma-gates *measure*less, the buddha-way *un*surpassed. Read carelessly, these four words look like the exaggerations one reaches for when urging others to raise the mind — "very, very many," "great beyond telling." But if one truly reads them as adverbs of degree, the whole vow collapses at once, collapses into an empty boast. This paper argues that the four quantifiers must be read as structural components, not as figures of rhetoric.

To call them structural components means this: each of them fixes, for its line of the vow, a terminating condition, and that terminating condition has been deliberately constructed never to be met. "Ferrying beings to the *end*" awaits the day the number of beings falls to zero; but beings are *boundless*, and by definition the number has no day of reaching zero. "Severing afflictions to the *end*" awaits the exhaustion of affliction; but affliction is *inexhaustible*, and the day of its exhaustion does not exist. "Studying the Dharma-gates *through*" awaits their being learned entire; but the gates are *measureless*, and the hour of learning them all never comes. "Realizing the buddha-way" awaits a summiting of that way; but the way is *unsurpassed* — above it there is nothing further to climb, so that what is realized is not one fixed terminus but the very limit of "nothing above." Four quantifiers, four thresholds that never close.

What follows from setting the terminating condition so that it can never be met? The answer is blunt: the vow will never "expire and retire." A to-do list loses one line with each crossing-off, and once every line is crossed it has done its work and goes into the bin. But the Four Vows are not a list. Precisely because each of its lines has fixed a terminus that cannot be reached, it remains forever in the state of "unfinished" — where "unfinished" means not "not yet done well" but "forever doing." The power of a vow lies not in its eventually coming due but in its forever orienting. To turn "not finishing" from a temporal failure (not yet done) into a structural feature (by design not to be done) is the first key to understanding the Four Vows.

### 2.2 The Vow's Object Is Coextensive with the Realm of Birth-and-Death

Are these four "boundlessnesses," then, exaggerations out of thin air? They are not. They are "boundless" because the objects of the Four Vows are themselves boundless. The prior paper in this series has already shown that the Four Great Vows are not four separate wishes but the "active voice" of the Four Noble Truths — the same suffering, origin, cessation, and path, which the disciple takes up in the mood of *knowing* in order to depart, and the bodhisattva takes up in the mood of *vowing* in order to shoulder: ferrying beings upon suffering, severing delusion upon origin, establishing the Dharma-gates upon the path, realizing the buddha-way and nirvāṇa upon cessation.[^p09] That layer need not be restated here; I take only one of its corollaries. Since the objects of the Four Vows are the whole quantity of suffering-origin-cessation-path, and since these are coextensive with the entire current of birth-and-death — so long as a single being still turns in saṃsāra there is still suffering, still origin — then the objects of the Four Vows are as large as the realm of birth-and-death itself.

This step matters, for it rewrites "boundless" from a personal boast into an objective structural fact. "Beings are boundless" says not "my ambition is large, I would ferry very many people" but "the realm of beings has, in itself, no edge." The vow's inexhaustibility is therefore not the mark of a personal shortfall — "I am forever not diligent enough," "I am forever not worthy" — but the faithful registering of an objective vastness: "the object is, from the first, without margin, as boundless as birth-and-death." An image: the horizon forever recedes ahead of you, not because you walk too slowly, but because the horizon was never a *point* you could step onto; it is the *line* where vision meets the earth, moving as you move. To blame yourself for failing to catch the horizon is to misread what the horizon is. By the same token, to read "the unferriable" as one's own failure is to misread what "boundless" is.

### 2.3 The Vow Is a Never-Closing Frame, Not a List That Gets Crossed Off

Put 2.1 and 2.2 together and a criterion comes clear. To judge whether a thing is a *vow* or a *goal*, one need not ask how large it is, but whether its terminating condition can be met.

A goal, however grand, has an in-principle reachable terminus — distant though it be, and though this life may not arrive at it, it is still a coordinate, a point that, once reached, is complete and may be struck off. A vow is otherwise. A vow such as the Four Vows has its terminating condition constructed never to be met, and so it was never a thing to be *arrived at* but a thing to be *continuously oriented toward*. A list that gets crossed off retires once crossed off; a never-closing frame, precisely because it never closes, can go on bearing weight to the very end of the future. This is the very thesis planted in this series' paper on the ten vows of the ten grounds and left for the present paper to redeem:[^p07] the reason vow-power is a *power* is not that it will at last come due, but that it forever orients.

One qualification must be stated plainly. The "unfinishability" treated here is taken purely from the side of the object — the boundlessness and inexhaustibility of beings and afflictions as objects. Whether "the mind that vows, the matter vowed, the act of vowing itself" are, on the side of the subject, *also* empty and *also* unattainable, is a deeper negotiation, one that bears on the vow's frontal encounter with emptiness; a dedicated paper later in this volume treats it in detail, and I do not touch it here. This section establishes only one line: as regards the object, the Four Vows are designed never to be finished; and that "never finished" is precisely their certificate of being a vow.

## III. The Uncontrived Four Vows: Vowing While Knowing the Object to Be Empty

### 3.1 The Signature Line: "Contending with Empty Space"

Section II established "unfinishability" as the structural feature of a vow. But this is not yet deep enough, for a sharp follow-up rises at once: granted that "boundless" is objective and not my failure, still — when I "vow to ferry while knowing full well that the ferrying cannot be finished," is this temper of "doing what one knows cannot be done" not, even so, a tragic self-consumption? To answer it one must summon the true heart of this paper — Zhiyi's "uncontrived Four Great Vows," as he expounds them in the *Mohe Zhiguan*.

Zhiyi retells the Four Vows under the horizon of the Perfect Teaching, where "all dharmas are emptiness, the object is unattainable":[^1]

> "Though beings are like empty space, I vow to ferry beings-like-space; though I know the afflictions to be nothing-whatsoever, I vow to sever afflictions that are nothing-whatsoever. Though I know beings are exceedingly many, I ferry beings exceedingly many; though I know afflictions are boundless and bottomless, I sever afflictions that have no bottom. Though I know beings are thus, as the suchness of the Buddha is thus, I ferry beings that are as the suchness of the Buddha."

Look closely at the syntax. Every clause has the structure "though … I vow …": *though* one knows full well that beings are like empty space and afflictions are from the first nothing-whatsoever, one *still* vows to ferry these beings-like-space, to sever these afflictions-that-are-nothing. Zhiyi does not flinch from the fact that "the object is like empty space." He sets it squarely on the table, and then, directly above it, raises the single word *vow*. This parts ways utterly with the naïve reading that "supposes there really are that many beings awaiting my ferrying." The one who vows does not fail to know that the object cannot be exhausted — he knows it completely — and in the very midst of knowing, he vows.

Then comes the signature line:[^2]

> "Though empty, one ferries; though ferrying, it is empty — therefore it is called 'contending with empty space,' and therefore it is called 'the genuine raising of the awakening-mind.'"

"Contending with empty space" — the strangeness of the image is worth pausing over. To contend with empty space is to face an adversary that is boundless, bottomless, formless, never to be "beaten"; viewed from the angle of winning and losing, it is a contention bound never to end. And yet Zhiyi's verdict is: precisely *this* contention with the void is what is called "the genuine raising of the awakening-mind." That is to say, the truth of a vow lies not in its being able to win, to be completed, but in its daring to keep lifelong company with an object that is by nature unwinnable, unfinishable. Contending, but not for the win; ferrying, but not to ferry-to-the-end — this is no longer the Sisyphean "rolling of the stone uphill." Sisyphus is punished, unwilling, and takes "reaching the top" for a goal forever falling away; the bodhisattva's "contending with empty space" is self-chosen, lucid, and from the very first does not take "beating the void" for its aim. The difference lies not in what is done (both do endlessly) but in the *mood* of the doing — the one treating its aim as a failed goal, the other treating its doing as the vow itself.

### 3.2 "Within the Nothing-Whatsoever, I Therefore Seek It"

Zhiyi pushes this turn to the utmost. He continues at once:[^3]

> "Though I know awakening to be nothing-whatsoever, within the nothing-whatsoever, I therefore seek it. … Though I know the buddha-way is neither a realizing nor a thing realized, it is like planting a tree in empty space, yet making it flower and fruit. Though I know the Dharma-gates and the buddha-fruit are neither cultivated nor not-cultivated, yet cultivated; neither realized nor attained, yet by realizing-nothing it is realized and attained."

"Within the nothing-whatsoever, I therefore seek it" — these are the eye of the whole paper. Knowing full well that the awakening one seeks is wholly unattainable, nothing-whatsoever, one nonetheless "therefore" seeks it within that very nothing — and the word "therefore" (deliberately, even so, knowing-thus-and-still) buckles shut, in a single stroke, the seemingly unbridgeable chasm between "knowing it empty" and "still seeking." It is neither "seeking because one does not know it is empty" (that is the seeking of ignorance), nor "not seeking because one knows it is empty" (that is the not-seeking of annihilation), but "knowing it empty, *therefore* still seeking" — upon a thorough knowing, a wholly lucid vow is raised anew.

The simile "like planting a tree in empty space, yet making it flower and fruit" makes the posture more concrete. To plant a tree in empty space sounds absurd — the void has no ground, no soil, no place to set a root; whence should a tree grow? Yet Zhiyi's point is: in this very "nowhere to plant," one still plants, and makes it flower and fruit. This bids no one to attempt the physically impossible; it points to the vow's quality of "not depending on a real, completable place to land." The tree is planted as before, the flower opens as before, the fruit forms as before — everything is done as before, only the doing is no longer bound to the grasping after "a real fruit that will at last be completed, at last be possessed." Hence his summation: the buddha-way is "neither a realizing nor a thing realized" (not some made, completed accomplishment), and the Dharma-gates and buddha-fruit are "neither cultivated nor not-cultivated, yet cultivated; neither realized nor attained, yet by realizing-nothing realized and attained." All cultivation and realization proceed exactly as before; only the stake of a "terminus" has been pulled out.

### 3.3 The Weight of "Uncontrived"

For the Four Vows so read, Zhiyi gave a technical name: the *uncontrived* Four Great Vows. The Tiantai teaching-manual, in laying out the ten modes of contemplation, marks the second mode, "the genuine raising of the awakening-mind," in just these terms:[^4]

> "Second, the genuine raising of the awakening-mind. That is, upon the wondrous object, one raises the uncontrived Four Great Vows; pitying self and pitying others, seeking upward and transforming downward."

"Uncontrived" is the key to the whole paper, and also the term most easily misheard, so it must be nailed down at once. "Uncontrived" here does not mean "no need to do," "no need to act." Quite the reverse. "Uncontrived" renders "non-fabricated," "not arisen from a conditioned doing" — it names a vow not built upon the fabricating cognition that "takes the object for a real, completable thing." What it negates is the conditioned mind that "grasps the object as real and grasps completion as the aim"; it by no means negates the *acts* of ferrying, severing, studying, realizing. The lines above — "within the nothing-whatsoever, I therefore seek it," "planting a tree in empty space, yet making it flower and fruit" — have already shown, again and again, that the uncontrived Four Vows still seek, still plant, still ferry, still sever. They do everything as before; only the root of the doing is no longer "I must finish this matter, reach it, hold it in hand."

And so the first section's question — "to do what one knows cannot be done, is this not a tragic self-consumption?" — receives here its frontal answer. It is not self-consumption, because it never looks to "completion" as a reward to be hoped for; with no hope laid up, there is no expenditure when hope falls through. Nor is it tragic, for tragedy presupposes the heaviness of "fighting on though one knows one will lose," whereas the uncontrived Four Vows stand outside the coordinates of winning and losing altogether — it is not that they "will lose," but that they are "regardless of win or loss." To turn "unfinishability" from a bitter task one must grit one's teeth to bear (Sisyphus) into a form of vow that never took completion for its condition in the first place (the uncontrived) — this is the deepest stroke Zhiyi left to the Chinese tradition of the vow, and the step this paper means to redeem and to carry one pace beyond the static frame the earlier papers laid down.

## IV. Three Mirrors: Atelic Activity, Aspiration, and the Infinite Game

To say more fully why "a vow that never finishes" is not wasted effort, recent discussions in Western philosophy of action — on the "purposiveness of activity" and on "self-transformation" — offer three unusually clean mirrors. It must be said at the outset that these mirrors are for casting an outline, not for equating doctrine; the end of this section is given over expressly to gathering up their irreducible limits.

### 4.1 Setiya: The Telic and the Atelic

The first mirror comes from the philosopher Kieran Setiya, who in *Midlife: A Philosophical Guide* distinguishes two kinds of activity.[^5] One is *telic* — directed at an endpoint, finished once attained, where the finishing also means the activity loses its object: write a book, and the activity of "writing this book" terminates. The other is *atelic* — not directed at an endpoint that would terminate itself; the doing of it is its own meaning: walking, keeping company, practising — you do not walk in order to "have walked." From this Setiya diagnoses one root of the midlife crisis: a person who lives life as a string of telic projects loses one with each completed, until the emptiness sets in; the remedy is to cultivate atelic activities, so that meaning falls on "the doing" rather than on "the done."

This mirror casts the Four Vows in especially clear outline. The Four Vows are atelic through and through: "ferrying beings to the end" was never an endpoint to be attained, after which one knocks off; the ferrying itself — that continuous orientation and shouldering — is the vow's realization. To read the Four Vows as telic (an index to be hit) is just the naïve misreading of §I, made academic; to read them as atelic turns "not finishing" at once from failure into the norm. An atelic activity has no slot for "completion" in the first place; you would not say a man "failed at walking" because he did not walk to the end — walking has no end. By the same token, you would not say the Four Vows "failed" because they did not ferry all beings — they never took ferrying-to-the-end for an endpoint at all.

### 4.2 Callard: Aspiration — Reshaped by a Value Not Yet Possessed

The second mirror comes from the philosopher Agnes Callard, who in *Aspiration: The Agency of Becoming* analyzes "aspiration."[^6] Callard distinguishes aspiration from ordinary "goal-pursuit." In pursuing a goal, you already know what you want and roughly why it is good, and you set out to secure it; in aspiration, you move toward something you do not yet fully possess and whose value you do not yet fully understand, and in the moving you let yourself be gradually reshaped, gradually made into a person who can appreciate it. The one who aspires does not "acquire" a known good; within the aspiring, she becomes another person.

This maps directly onto the theme that runs through this volume: a vow is a reshaping of the mind's orientation. The Four Vows do not pursue a clearly defined, plainly priced "result"; they orient a whole life toward the "buddha-way not yet realized," and in that orienting let the self be reshaped, piece by piece, by it — reshaped from a mind concerned only with its own liberation into a mind that "vows to ferry the boundless beings." In this sense the "unfinishability" of the Four Vows is no problem at all, for the gist of aspiration was never "attaining the value" but "being reshaped by the value," a thing that goes on continuously happening. You would not ask one in the midst of aspiring "have you hit the mark yet," because the person she is becoming is itself the fruit of the aspiring.

### 4.3 Carse: The Infinite Game — Played to Keep Playing

The third mirror comes from James Carse's famous distinction in *Finite and Infinite Games*; I take it here as a positive lens, and set against it, by way of contrast, a familiar popular cast of mind.[^7] Carse says the finite game is played to *win*, played to *end* — it has clear boundaries, rules, and a final whistle; the infinite game is played to *keep playing*, its one purpose being that the game not end. In the finite game, to win is to terminate; in the infinite game, the very thought of "winning" is to be avoided, for the moment someone "wins," the game dies.

The Four Vows are exactly an infinite game. Their "win" — ferrying all beings across — would, were there ever such a day, terminate the vow. So the wisdom of the vow lies precisely in this: its object is designed never to be "won out," and thus it can go on playing to the end of the future. This sets the Four Vows clearly apart from a popular cast of mind: the one that treats life's ideals as "goal-setting," that treats vowing as "placing an order with the universe" (manifestation), that demands the vow be broken into quantifiable, attainable items to be checked off one by one — that is precisely to mistake an infinite game for a finite one, to demote a vow that ought to be "played to keep playing" into a list "itemized to be struck off." In this mirror the unfinishability of the Four Vows shows its true reading: they are not a poorly designed finite game whose target can never be met; they are not a finite game at all. Their "unfinishability" is just another way of saying that they "can be played forever."

### 4.4 Irreducibility: Where the Three Mirrors Stop

The three mirrors each light up, from one side, the outline of "never finishing yet not wasted": Setiya gives the *form* of "no endpoint," Callard the *dynamic* of "being reshaped by a value," Carse the *posture* of "playing to keep playing." All three are most useful, and all three must honestly be marked at the point where they stop — for all three halt at "without endpoint," and none reaches the further turn the Dharma takes.

Setiya's atelic activity, Callard's aspiration, Carse's infinite game all share a presupposition: that the object moved toward, the game, the value — though it has no endpoint — is still something "real, worth orienting toward." They handle "having no endpoint," but they do not handle "the thing oriented toward being itself like empty space, wholly unattainable." Zhiyi's uncontrived Four Vows, by contrast, vow upon precisely this — "knowing full well that what is vowed is like empty space" — and still raise a vow. To this slot none of the three mirrors reaches. Callard's aspirant still presupposes the value to be "really aspirable"; Carse's infinite player still presupposes the game to be "really playable"; the uncontrived Four Vows alone are "though I know awakening to be nothing-whatsoever, within the nothing-whatsoever I therefore seek it" — they stand up a lucid vow even after the reality of the object has been wholly drained away. This is where the Dharma is the Dharma, and not a philosophy of action.

It must therefore be said gravely: the foregoing is purely heuristic analogy, not theoretical identity. The work of these three philosophers is descriptive analysis of human action and self-formation; in none of it is there the ontological commitment of "vowing though dharmas are like empty space." This paper borrows them only to illuminate the one point that "a vow that never finishes can be not wasted," and one must on no account reason backward to say that the Four Vows equal some position in the philosophy of action, or that the uncontrived Four Vows reduce to the sum of "atelic activity" plus "aspiration" plus "the infinite game." A mirror can cast an outline, but it is not the thing cast; and the deepest layer of the thing this paper casts — the uncontrived — lies precisely where the light of the three mirrors does not reach.

## V. Corrections of the Middle Way

This section marks four misreadings into which one easily slips (❌ for a swerve of doctrine, ⚠️ for a trap of textual scope and bounds), as boundary-stakes for holding to the middle way.

**❌ "Unfinishability" is neither failure nor wasted effort.** The readiest misunderstanding is to read "forever not finished" as a tragedy — the bankruptcy of the vow, or a Sisyphean grinding-on in vain. The whole of this paper works to block that fork: the unfinishability of the Four Vows is structural (§II), and under the horizon of the uncontrived it does not take completion for its condition at all (§III), so it is neither failure (it never set up a terminus that could be judged "unmet") nor wasted effort (it never looked to completion as a reward, and so suffers no expenditure from its falling through). As for the emotional worry of "Sisyphean despair," this series' volume on repentance has met it head-on from several angles; I do not rebuild that argument here, but take only its conclusion as a single signpost.[^ksama] What the vow learns here is to see "not finishing" not as a problem to be overcome but as a norm that was always so.

**❌ "Uncontrived" is not lying flat, not a passive doing-nothing.** The second misreading hears the "uncontrived" of the "uncontrived Four Vows" as "no need to act," and so reads out a slackness — "since it can't be finished anyway, just take it easy and not be too earnest." This is the most dangerous mishearing of "uncontrived." As §3.3 nailed down: "uncontrived" renders "non-fabricated," "not arisen from grasping-as-real"; what it negates is the conditioned mind that "grasps the object as real and grasps completion as the aim," and by no means the act itself. Zhiyi's own words — "within the nothing-whatsoever I therefore seek it," "planting a tree in empty space, yet making it flower and fruit" — say, word for word, "still seek, still plant, still ferry." The uncontrived Four Vows are not doing less, but doing as before without grasping; not lowering the burden shouldered, but shouldering it more purely upon a thorough knowing. To let "uncontrived" slide into a quietist "not-doing" is precisely to drop Zhiyi's load-bearing word, "therefore."

**⚠️ "Boundless" must not borrow the four-similes of inexhaustibility from the repentance gate.** A boundary of textual scope must be honestly marked. Within the strand of Samantabhadra's vow-conduct there is another famous set, the "four similes of inexhaustibility" (using the inexhaustibility of the realm of space, the realm of beings, beings' karma, and beings' afflictions to figure the inexhaustibility of vow-conduct); that material and its treatment of "unfinishability" are already fully held and deeply discussed in this series' volume on repentance, and this paper neither cites nor restates them. This paper grounds its account of the Four Vows' unfinishability purely on the four quantifiers of the Four Vows themselves — boundless, inexhaustible, measureless, unsurpassed — which is a different textual base and a different approach. The reader must not, because both places speak of "inexhaustibility," fold them into one; throughout, this paper holds strictly to the quantifiers of the Four Vows and does not cross over to borrow that set of four similes.

**⚠️ This paper takes only the object-side boundlessness; the subject-side emptiness is reserved for later.** Finally, the bounds of this paper must be fixed. Zhiyi's lines — "within the nothing-whatsoever I therefore seek it," "by realizing-nothing it is realized and attained" — in fact touch two dimensions at once: one, the object (beings, afflictions, awakening) as boundless and empty on the side of the object; two, "the mind that vows, the matter vowed, the act of vowing" as also empty and also unattainable on the side of the subject. What this paper takes is strictly the former — the object-side boundlessness-and-unfinishability, and the uncontrived posture raised upon it. The latter — the emptiness of the three wheels (the one who vows, the thing vowed, the power of the vow), and the question of "how a vow can be raised with a mind of nothing-to-attain" — bears on the vow's frontal encounter with emptiness, and a dedicated paper later in this volume treats it in detail; here I take only the object-side and do not move the subject-side emptiness in advance. And it must be said plainly: this paper makes no forward citation to texts not yet written, marking the place softly only as "later in this volume."

## VI. Conclusion

**A concise restatement.** The "boundless" of the Four Vows is not a goal so large as to terrify; it is a structure: it designs the terminating conditions (ferrying-to-the-end, severing-to-the-end, studying-through, summiting) deliberately so that they can never be met, and so the vow never retires. Unfinishability is not the vow's flaw; it is precisely the criterion that marks it as a *vow* (a continuous orientation) and not a *goal* (a list that gets crossed off). And Zhiyi's uncontrived Four Vows push the step to the bottom: knowing full well that beings are like empty space and awakening is nothing-whatsoever, one still says "within the nothing-whatsoever, I therefore seek it" — this is not the Sisyphus who contends with the void and must at last be defeated, but the uncontrived who, lucidly and by choice, contends with empty space while never speaking of winning or losing. A vow is not a list that gets crossed off; it is an orientation that forever orients. Unfinishability is not its flaw; it is its certificate of being a vow.

**A note for practice.** From this follows one directional hint: there is no need to be dispirited that beings cannot be ferried to the end or afflictions severed entire, and still less need to contrive to break the Four Vows into a few small "achievable this month" goals to be struck off one by one — that is precisely to demote the vow into a list. The real practice is to learn to settle the mind on the very fact of "orienting now," rather than to leave it hanging on the never-arriving point of "at last attained." To contend with empty space and not speak of defeat, to seek within the nothing-whatsoever "therefore" — this is the deepest watershed between raising a vow and placing an order. The work of the vow lies not in pressing it to come due, but in correcting its orientation day by day and resting in its unfinishability.

**Sealing Part IV.** This series has treated the Four Great Vows in two papers: the prior one set their *grammar* — the Four Vows as the active voice of the Four Noble Truths, the same diagnostic chart rewritten line by line into the syntax of vowing; the present one carries that forward to discern their *structure* — the "boundless" of the Four Vows as an unfinishable structure, not a target to be attained. Grammar plus structure: with this, Part IV (the Four Great Vows: the skeleton of the bodhisattva's general vow) is sealed.

**An open question and a signpost.** Yet this paper also leaves a signpost at its summit. What it has treated is the "self-power" face of the vow — knowing full well the unattainable, one still seeks it, continuously, by the force of one's own vow. But when this "seeking-though-knowing-it-unattainable" is pressed to its limit, another face of the vow surfaces: to lay the accomplishing of the whole direction not entirely on one's own shoulders, but to entrust it to the power of a buddha's original vow — this is "other-power." A vow designed never to be finished — besides "I bear it by my own vow-power to the end of the future," can it also be "I entrust this direction outward, and rest within the field constituted by another, greater vow"? How do the vow of self-power and the vow of other-power stand together without each devouring the other? That question is what the next part takes up frontally; the present paper only sets its marker and lays its road, leaving the details for what follows.
