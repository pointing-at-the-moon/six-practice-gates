## Transition ② · From the Concentration in the Āgamas to the Abhidharma

Part Two has walked up to the door of a workshop, and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath.

With Part Two done, you hold three more things in your hands: a position (right concentration is the eighth factor of the noble eightfold path, upheld by the other seven—its "right" is given by the whole road); an order (concentration sits sixth among the seven awakening-factors, the channel forming when the water has arrived—a fruit that is brought forth); and a seclusion (the content of concentration is the absence of the five hindrances, and that absence is suppression, not severance). Together with Part One's three words, one wall, one pair of tracks, one scale, and one map of doors—the concentration of the Āgama stratum is, at this point, fully told.

Is that enough?

If all you ask for is the broad outline, it is enough. But there is one thing the language of the Āgamas cannot handle, and in these two parts it has in fact surfaced again and again.

That thing is: the Āgamas give seeds, not a system.

Think back over the seams met along the way. The five hindrances: named five, yet seven items are listed—dullness-and-drowsiness counts two things together, restlessness-and-remorse counts two together; this ledger the Āgamas themselves never reconcile. The jhāna-factors: the first-jhāna text points out awareness, examining, rapture, ease, and "one mind" does not appear until the second-jhāna sentence—how many factors there are in all, which belongs to which jhāna, which cures which hindrance, the Āgamas draw up no table. The meditation-subjects: scattered across the discourses, "each sutta as told above"—the Āgamas keep no file. Even the word "concentration" itself—is it a quality pervading every mind, or a function that arises only in moments of focus? The Āgamas never so much as ask such a question.

This is not carelessness in the Āgamas. It is what the genre of the Āgamas was always like: the Buddha taught to the occasion—one time, one place, one person, one matter; the medicine was given to fit the illness, and no pharmacopoeia was compiled. The sutta is a prescription, not a pharmacopoeia.

But when prescriptions have piled up for centuries, thousands of them, their agreements and differences, their fullness and brevity, their surfaces and depths—someone, in the end, has to sit down and sort them out. Which words point to the same thing? Which numbers must be aligned? Which definitions must be fixed to the last immovable word? This work of sorting, in the centuries after the Buddha's passing, generation after generation of treatise-masters carried forward as a relay—classifying, counting, defining, debating—until it became a vast system, named the Abhidharma, "facing the dharma"—to stand facing the dharma and comb it clear, item by item.

At the end of Part One we saw one product of this workshop: the map of the thirty-eight fields-of-practice. That was only a corner. Part Three walks in formally, to look at three things.

The first: the full catalog of meditation-subjects—dozens of doors, how they are classified, how they are matched to a person's temperament, how fine this map was drawn. The second: the anatomy of the jhāna-factors—awareness, examining, rapture, ease, unification of mind, the five factors taken apart one by one: what each is, what each cures, at which jhāna each is shed. The third, and the deepest: the dispute over the substance of absorption—is "concentration" a universal property of the mind, or a particular function? Two camps of treatise-masters contended over this for centuries, and the dispute bears on the most basic understanding each of us has of the word "concentration."

Before walking into the workshop, take along the ruler the first two parts have ground out again and again: what the treatise-masters drew is a map, and the map is exceedingly fine, exceedingly useful—but the map is not the terrain. The star-chart is drawn by men; the stars were in the sky all along. In reading the treatises, honor their precision; in using them, remember their place.

We turn the page, to enter the workshop.
