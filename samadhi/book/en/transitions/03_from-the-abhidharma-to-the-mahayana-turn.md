## Transition ③ · From the Abhidharma to the Mahāyāna Turn

Part Three has walked up to the back door of the workshop, and stopped. The page is about to turn.

This middle page is a breath.

Three chapters walked inside the workshop, and you hold three more knives in your hands: the knife that compounds medicine (matching the remedy to the illness—the object is assigned to the affliction in the way, not to the person's preference); the knife that dissects (applied and sustained thought are the stirring of language, and the second jhāna stills them into noble silence; the deepening of concentration is the removal of stirring, layer by layer); and the knife that questions the substance (is concentration a thing, a momentary dharma, or only a phase of the mind?—three schools, three readings). The treatise-masters took the word "concentration," a word the suttas use in passing, and dissected it into a science precise to the hairsbreadth.

This precision is a real achievement. But step out of the workshop and look back, and you will find the whole workshop shares one common shape: the concentration on the bench—compounded for, dissected, weighed—was, from first to last, one person's concentration.

One person discerns his own afflictions and compounds his own medicine. One person sits down and lets his own applied and sustained thought grow still. One person enters absorption, emerges, maintains, increases. The subject of the entire science, from beginning to end, is "the practitioner"—singular: one person who cultivates alone, realizes alone, finishes alone. There is nothing wrong in this; liberation has always been an intimate affair, and no one can do it for another.

Yet there is one question the whole workshop never asked: for whose sake is this concentration cultivated?

Concentration cultivated deep, the hindrances suppressed, the mind bright and clear—and then? Keep watch over that clarity until this life ends? The Buddha was not like that. He brought forth the three knowledges in the fourth jhāna, and after His awakening His first thought was to go and deliver His two teachers; the two teachers had passed away, so He walked to the Deer Park—and from then on, for forty-five years, He walked roads thick with dust, with people always around Him. His concentration was never the concentration of a study or of the deep mountains; it was the concentration of one who taught into the night and at dawn entered the town again with His bowl.

It was the Mahāyāna that put this question formally into words.

The Mahāyāna gave concentration a new name: the perfection of dhyāna (*dhyāna-pāramitā*). *Pāramitā* means "crossing over"—crossing to the far shore, and not one person crossing alone, but carrying living beings across together. With the change of name, the center of gravity of the whole science of concentration shifted: concentration is no longer only a work of finishing oneself; it became one of the bodhisattva's six perfections—before it giving, keeping the precepts, patience, and energy; after it, prajñā—concentration was woven into a long road walked for the sake of living beings.

And so the new questions came one after another. When the bodhisattva enters absorption, does he come out again? If deep absorption is so lovely, how could a bodhisattva bear to leave it and return among the clamor of beings? The mind must abide settled, and yet move freely among ten thousand people—is such a concentration still the one dissected in the workshop? And there is that knife of substance still hanging: is the substance of absorption a thing or a name?—the Mahāyāna's prajñā will take this question, scale and all, place it into emptiness, and ask it again.

Part Four walks this road of the turn: first to see how Nāgārjuna speaks of the perfection of dhyāna; then how the Yogācāra school lays out the long staircase of taming the mind, the nine stages of mental abiding; then the bodhisattva's concentration that "does not abide"—not abiding in birth-and-death, not abiding in nirvāṇa, and not abiding in concentration either.

Concentration is about to come down the mountain.

We turn the page, to follow it down the mountain.
