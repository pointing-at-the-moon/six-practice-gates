## 9.1 Two Failure Modes

Take sitting meditation as a button: when the mind frets, sit a while; the sitting done, the mood leveled, go back to the day's work—this is very nearly the most popular image of "cultivating concentration" today. It is clean, measurable, available on demand, like a stress-relief tool that fits in a pocket.

The other image is less noisy, but no less common: a person who has sat long, meeting a grief that ought to be faced, an anger, a real conflict with another person, retreats instead into that interior stillness, taking "I am no longer disturbed" for the matter settled; or, seeing within concentration some brightness, some spaciousness, concludes that he has "realized" something.

The first takes the "bliss" of concentration too much to heart and draws it outward for use; the second takes the "sign" of concentration too much to heart and guards it inward. They look opposite—and what this chapter has to say is this: they are one and the same fissure opening in two directions. The name of that fissure is "concentration severed from wisdom." And the tradition, long before these two modern faces appeared, had seen both directions clearly, named them both, and made up for both the same one medicine.

### The First Failure Mode: Held Fast by the Flavor

The ancient prototype of the first is described with great clarity in the *Dazhidu lun*. Speaking of the gods of the form realm, the treatise says:

> Though they first heard the Dharma and gave rise to the aspiration, because they relish concentration so deeply, they cannot seek *prajñā*.

The weight of this line lies in its causal structure: not that "concentration is bad," but that the act of relishing concentration, deepened to a certain point, blocks off the mind that would seek prajñā. The form-realm gods are not those who never heard the Dharma, never roused the aspiration—they are those held fast by the long, refined bliss within concentration, a bliss far surpassing the human world's; and once held fast, they "cannot seek *prajñā*"—not that they refuse: the impetus to go forward has been worn away by an abiding too comfortable to leave. Elsewhere the treatise adds another cut: the form-realm gods, toward the bliss of concentration, "having no mind of aversion, are hard to convert"—precisely because not one thread of disenchantment remains, they are hardest of all to bring across.

The key word is "flavor." Concentration of itself gives rise to bliss—this is concentration's true merit, not its defect; the trouble lies in the mind's stance toward that bliss. When the bliss is taken as a flavor to be tasted again and again, drawn upon again and again, concentration turns from a gate that opens onto prajñā into a room one can settle into for a long stay—once moved in, the gate closes.

The reader of Part Five will recognize this structure: the honey is truly sweet; the error is the hand stuck in the jar. And aim this ancient mirror at the present, and the true face of "concentration as tool" shows itself: the person who treats sitting as a stress-relief button wants exactly that flavor—calm itself has been made the product, the deliverable. Where the ancients relished the long bliss of form-realm dhyāna, the people of today relish a stretch of quiet that can be slotted into a schedule. The form of the bliss has changed; the structure of the clinging is identical.

How does the tradition keep from condemning the bliss of concentration wholesale as poison? It has a built-in test, and the reader of Part Two has met its kin—the three questions. The *Madhyama Āgama* puts three questions to every felt object: What is the gratification? What is the danger? What is the escape? "Gratification" is the genuine flavor it does have, not denied; "danger" is the harm that follows once one is held fast; "escape" is the road that sees through it and departs from it. Set the three upon the bliss of concentration: the bliss of concentration does have its flavor—this the first limb concedes; relished, it blocks off prajñā—this is the danger; not to be held fast, but to go on walking toward wisdom—this is the escape. The failure mode of "concentration as tool" is exactly the taking of the first limb alone: making the gratification the whole of the matter, halting at the flavor, never asking after the danger, never asking after the escape.

### The Hinge: One Sentence, Two Poles, One Medicine

Before entering the second failure mode, set in place the hinge of this chapter—a single sentence that diagnoses both poles at once and points out their shared medicine. Speaking of root dhyāna methods such as the four dhyānas, Zhiyi's *Móhē zhǐguān* (the Great Calming-and-Contemplation) says:

> The four *dhyānas* are root, blind-attainment, and relished *dhyāna*; common to ordinary and sage alike, gained by slight cultivation. The Distinctive Dharmas alone retain some discerning wisdom—neither relished nor blindly attained.

One short sentence, and the whole chapter's skeleton is inside it. The danger of the four dhyānas runs in two directions: one is "relished dhyāna"—sunk in the bliss of concentration, taking concentration for a room to settle into, exactly the pole of the previous section; the other is "blind attainment"—groping in the dark without the clear awareness of contemplation, unwittingly mistaking what appears within concentration for the fruit of what has been realized. And the second half of the line gives the two poles their common solution: the line of the Distinctive Dharmas falls into neither relished dhyāna nor blind attainment precisely because it "retains some discerning wisdom"—with the wisdom of contemplation present within, the bliss of concentration is not relished, and the state of concentration is not misrecognized.

Relished dhyāna and blind attainment are concentration, severed from wisdom, spoiling in two opposite directions; discerning wisdom is the one medicine that treats both poles at once. In another place of the same work, Zhiyi states the pair still more symmetrically: a certain deep method of contemplation, he says, is "not what the blind-attainment dhyāna-master or the text-reciting Dharma-master can know"—on one side the blind-attainment dhyāna-master, who only sits and lacks discerning wisdom; on the other the text-reciting Dharma-master, who only recites, his mind moving along with the text. One has concentration without wisdom; the other has letters without concentration; neither arrives. And Yongjia's four characters from Part Seven stand here as a ready-made anchor: "concentration grown long sinks."

### The Second Failure Mode: Claiming as Attained What Is Unattained

Now the second can be looked at head-on. Its classical naming, in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra*, holds just eight characters:

> Claiming as attained what is unattained, as realized what is unrealized.

This is the conceit of over-attainment—taking what has not in truth been gained for the gained, taking what has not in truth been realized for the realized. Aimed at the state of concentration, it is exactly the core mechanism of blind attainment: seeing within concentration brightness, spaciousness, pliancy, all manner of perceptions unlike the ordinary, one concludes that one has realized something—mistaking a "state" within concentration for an "achievement" in the sense of a fruit of the path.

The reader of Part Six will recognize at once the full chart of this illness: the criterion formula of the fifty skandha-māras—Form no notion of sagehood, and it is a good state; construe it as sagehood, and you fall prey to the host of māras. The three characters "construe it as sagehood" differ not by a hair from "claiming as attained what is unattained."

And aim this ancient mirror at the present, and the structure of the failure mode "using sitting to dodge what ought to be faced" shows itself too. The person who hides inside the stillness relies on exactly that sense concentration can produce—"I am no longer disturbed"; and this sense is often precisely a misrecognized state: taking a temporary non-reactivity for real liberation, taking the suppression of an emotion for the severance of an affliction. Taking what is not truly settled for the settled—in structure, this is "claiming as attained what is unattained."

The two failure modes can now be set side by side. One covets the flavor, one lays false claim; one prizes the bliss of the concentrative state, one prizes its sign. Opposite in direction, identical at the root: both, in the absence of discerning wisdom, over-value what appears within concentration. Where discerning wisdom is present, the bliss of concentration is seen through as "flavor"—not relished; the state of concentration is seen through as no realization—not blindly attained. Where discerning wisdom is lost, the former becomes relished dhyāna, and the latter the conceit of over-attainment.

### A Mirror

What this medicine points toward is a boundary set down at this volume's very opening. Remember the scene at the volume's head: the Buddha personally cultivated the deepest formless concentration then known, yet stood at the very top of concentration and said—This dharma does not tend toward knowledge, does not tend toward awakening, does not tend toward nirvāṇa—and then left. The two failure modes, at the very root, are both a forgetting of this boundary: taking concentration for a place to settle into for a long stay, or for a destination already reached. Concentration is not the end-point.

This chapter leaves no techniques. It does not say "how to sit so as not to relish"—that is not a diagnosis's business. It leaves a mirror: when you find that you have made sitting a button to be used and walked away from, or find that you are using that stretch of stillness to dodge some matter that ought to be faced—this mirror reminds you: these are not two ailments, but two faces of one and the same fissure.

And the medicine, from first to last, is one alone. Zhiyi's five characters are left to the two chapters ahead:

> Neither relished nor blindly attained.
