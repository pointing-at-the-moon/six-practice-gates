# Part Nine · The Modern Return · The People of Today Step In

Eight parts walked, and the two poles of concentration stand firm: gathered inward to the single thought free of signs, reaching outward to the dharma-realm manifest all at once. Yet along this whole road, one figure has never once stepped in—the people of today.

Concentration did not die in the scrolls. It changed its clothes and came back into the lives of millions: the timer in the phone, the stress-relief course at the company, the quiet cushion in the room. Concentration has come back—and the moment it came back, it brought back two ancient dangers, wearing modern faces.

Part Nine has three chapters.

Chapter one, two failure modes. To treat concentration as a stress-relief tool taken up at will is one failure mode; to use concentration to dodge what should be faced, and to mistake a state within concentration for a measure of realization, is the other. They look opposite; the root is one and the same—concentration severed from wisdom. For both illnesses the tradition long ago had names, and long ago made up the medicine.

Chapter two, concentration in the age of AI. When the machines at our side make the contest for attention their business, the work of gathering the mind to a single point has met a new external condition the ancients never had. Is it concentration's enemy? Is it concentration's helper? Both questions ask wrongly—and where they go wrong, the treatises hold a ready-made ruler.

Chapter three, the final chapter of the whole volume—the inward gate opens back. This volume opened with the scene of the Buddha relinquishing the formless attainments; thirty chapters walked, it returns to that scene, to bring to fruit the sentence planted at the volume's head: concentration is not final; it must return to prajñā.

First, one covenant for this part: these three chapters diagnose only; they prescribe nothing. It is a mirror, not an assignment.

Look in the mirror.
