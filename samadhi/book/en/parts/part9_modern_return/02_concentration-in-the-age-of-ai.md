## 9.2 Concentration in the Age of AI

You sit down, meaning to settle the mind in one place. But the device in your pocket decides for you, every few minutes, "the next thing worth looking at."

This is not the private problem of a weak will; it is a structural problem. The previous chapter diagnosed the inward failure modes; this chapter looks at something peculiar to the modern situation—a new external condition the ancients never had.

### The Nature of Concentration: The Word "One"

First set the ruler upright. The nature of concentration is defined nowhere more cleanly than in the *Cheng weishi lun* (*Treatise on the Establishment of Consciousness-Only*):

> What is concentration? Upon the contemplated object, its nature is to focus the mind undistractedly; its function is to be the support of wisdom.

"To focus the mind undistractedly"—gathering the scattered mind into one focused point. What makes concentration concentration lies in the word "one"; this volume has read from the Pāli one-pointedness of mind all the way to here, and this sense has never changed. And the second half, "its function is to be the support of wisdom"—concentration's work is to be that on which wisdom rests—set it down for now; it is a thread planted for the final chapter.

### The Question Must Be Asked Rightly

Facing AI, the two easiest questions to slide into are: "Will AI destroy concentration?" and "Can AI help people gain concentration?" One is the doom, the other the utopia; they look opposed, but in fact they commit one and the same error—both take AI for concentration's "cause."

Yogācāra has a ready-made ruler, finely divided. The *Cheng weishi lun* analyzes dependent arising into four conditions, and two of its concepts answer this case exactly. One is the direct condition (*hetu-pratyaya*)—"a conditioned dharma that directly accomplishes its own fruit": to accomplish one's own fruit oneself is to be the direct generative cause. The other is the dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*):

> If there is a dharma that has potent force, able, with respect to other dharmas, either to favor or to oppose.

Standing outside another dharma, with potent force, lending help toward it or standing against it—this is the dominant condition.

Lay the ruler on, and the rank is clear at once. The direct cause of concentration is always the *samādhi* accomplished by the practitioner's own mind—it is the mind itself gathering itself to a single point. This, no external thing can do in its stead: no machine, however refined, can complete for you the act of "focusing the mind undistractedly." At most it can increase or decrease the help or the hindrance on the side of circumstance—and that is exactly the dominant condition's business. So AI, with respect to concentration, is a condition, not a cause; it can favor, it can oppose, yet it can neither destroy concentration nor make it. To shift the question from "make-or-break" to "favor-or-oppose" is no rhetorical nicety; it is a setting-back-in-place within the structure of dependent arising—and this one repositioning blocks both sides at once: to say AI must destroy concentration is to mistake a potent opposing condition for a cause that can sever concentration; to say AI can cultivate concentration in a person's stead is to mistake an external condition that may help for the cause that directly accomplishes.

### The Opposing Side: A Structure Whose Business Is Distraction

Then is this contemporary condition mainly favoring, or opposing?

First, one term made clear: "the attention economy" is an analytical label, not a settled term of the sūtras and treatises. It names a structure of this kind—one whose operating core is to contest for and monetize human attention. Its livelihood rests on three acts: capture attention, switch attention, and let the targets available for switching endlessly multiply.

Set these three acts beside the nature of concentration, and the opposition is plain at a glance. The crux of concentration lies in the word "undistractedly"; and the output of this structure is precisely distraction—it does not scatter the mind by accident; it takes distraction as its normal mode, as its purpose. One is accomplished by not scattering; the other profits by scattering. This is not a difference of degree; it is a reversal of direction. And for the same reason, this is not the deviation of any one product or any one company (this book names no platform and no application); it is the operating logic of this kind of structure itself: its very standing depends on the non-accomplishment of one-pointedness.

But the measure of the diagnosis must be held exactly: what is eroded is the external condition on which one-pointedness depends, not the essential mechanism of concentration. That "focusing the mind undistractedly" is what concentration is has not changed from ancient times to now; between forest and marketplace, between brush-and-paper and screen, what changes is only the tension of the external condition. The ancients' conditions for scattering were mostly sporadic and passive—a dog's bark, a gust of market noise; the present age's conditions for scattering are designed, optimized day and night, living by the contest for attention. To gather the mind to a single point—the thing itself remains achievable; only the headwind has grown structurally stronger. The diagnosis stops here: this is a pressure of erosion, not concentration's death sentence.

### The Favoring Side—and Who Judges

"Either favoring or opposing"—being a dominant condition, it cannot be looked at on the opposing side alone. The same set of technological conditions can also turn into a favoring condition: barring intrusions for a person, guarding a stretch of silence, aiding a place of solitude. The division between favoring and opposing lies not in the technology itself, but in whether its force, in a concrete situation, helps a person gather the mind into one or helps them scatter it into many.

And a deeper question hides here. Who is it that can tell favoring from opposing? Not concentration itself—concentration only focuses without scattering; it has no way to turn back upon the condition it stands in and judge it favoring or opposing. What can make that judgment is the wisdom that takes concentration as its support—the wisdom in "its function is to be the support of wisdom." The discernment of favoring from opposing must in the end come back to the relation between concentration and wisdom—and so even the answer to this chapter's question of external conditions points to the same one place.

The covenant holds as before: this chapter opens no manual of "how to turn off notifications, how to digital-detox." Diagnosis and prescription are two different things; the proper road of cultivating concentration the first eight parts of this volume have already told, and it is not repeated here, nor stood in for.

### One Sentence That Has Never Changed

To gather this chapter in: concentration is one-pointedness of mind; AI is concentration's dominant condition, either favoring or opposing, not a direct cause able to destroy or to make; the dominant force of the contemporary attention-structure leans to the opposing side, because its business is distraction, the reverse of the direction of the "one"; but the same condition can favor—and what judges favoring from opposing is wisdom.

The mechanism of concentration has not changed. Whatever external condition the age supplies, favoring or opposing, the one sentence that defines concentration as concentration stands as it always has —

> Focusing the mind undistractedly.

Only—a mind that has held to the "one," if it does not open onto the wisdom that sees through, is still not concentration's final sense, whatever the external conditions. Of this volume's thirty chapters, only the last remains. The inward gate is about to open back.
