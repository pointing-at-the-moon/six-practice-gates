# Part Three · Abhidharma and the Path of Purification · The Treatise-Masters' Workshop

The transition page said it: the sutta is a prescription, not a pharmacopoeia. The Buddha taught to meet the person; the medicine was given to the illness—one time, one place. Over a few centuries the prescriptions piled up by the thousand, and someone had to sit down and put them in order. That work of ordering became a vast system—the Abhidharma, "facing the dharmas": to face the dharmas and set them straight, one by one.

Part Three walks into this workshop, for three chapters.

Chapter one, compounding. Dozens of meditation objects—matched to whom? The treatise-masters' answer is a physician's method: matching the remedy to the illness—what it reads is not what this person likes, but which affliction now holds sway in his mind. Chapter two, dissection. A jhāna-factor goes onto the dissection table, to be taken apart: what that subtle stirring of the mind is, why it comes to stillness in the second jhāna, and why its stilling is called "noble silence." Chapter three, the question pressed to the bottom. The treatise-masters ask a question the suttas never ask: is concentration, in the end, a thing?

Each of the three chapters holds a knife: one compounds, one dissects, one questions substance. All three are exceedingly sharp. Only, on the way in, bring along the ruler the first two parts have ground true—what the treatise-masters drew is a map; the map is exquisitely fine; the map is not the terrain.

Into the workshop.
