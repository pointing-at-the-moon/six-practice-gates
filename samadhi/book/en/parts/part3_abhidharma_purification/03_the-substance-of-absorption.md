## 3.3 The Substance of Absorption

This chapter asks a question that sounds a little strange: is concentration a thing?

Put it another way. You have attained concentration—then is what you "attained" an it that can be kept? Like a possession lost and found again, recoverable when mislaid, the same one each time you re-enter? Or is there no such "it" at all, and the concentration of each moment must arise anew?

The Āgamas do not ask this question. The suttas call concentration the concentration-faculty, the concentration-power, right concentration; they tell you what concentration does, what it looks like, what it is for—the sutta is a prescription, and a prescription does not discuss the molecules of the drug. It was the treatise-masters who set concentration on the scale and asked whether it has a weight of its own. This is the deepest cut the Abhidharma chiseled in the place the suttas left unopened. Three schools of treatise-masters; three readings of the scale.

### The Sarvāstivāda Says: It Is a Thing, and It Is Always There

The Sarvāstivāda's answer is brisk: concentration is a really existent dharma. "What is concentration? One-pointedness of mind." The very one-pointedness by which the mind fixes on its object is itself a mental factor with its own-nature, distinct from the mind, standing on its own feet.

The Sarvāstivāda pushes this dharma further, to an unexpected position: it is a universally-present factor (*mahābhūmika*)—present in every mind. Which is to say, concentration is not only there when you are in absorption; even the scattered mind has it. At this moment your mind may be a restless ape, your thoughts running wild—yet in that very thought there is a thread of concentration, only too weak for you to notice. The treatise-masters say it plainly: the concentration in the scattered mind is merely *weak*, not absent.

Weigh what this step means. It means concentration is not something that exists when you enter absorption and is gone when you rise from it. It is a faculty always on the scene, differing only in strength. To cultivate concentration is not to manufacture a new thing out of nothing; it is to take that thread of one-pointedness everyone already has, too weak to be felt, and nurse it strong, nurse it pure, nurse it deep. The treatise-masters point out, further, that this same substance of concentration wears different names on different rosters: among the five faculties it is named the concentration-faculty, among the five powers the concentration-power, among the seven awakening-factors the concentration awakening-factor, among the eight path-limbs right concentration—one substance, named in four places. All those words "concentration" Part Two met have been gathered, in the treatise-masters' hands, into one and the same it.

### The Sautrāntika Says: Nothing Extra on the Scale

Yet in the very same *Abhidharmakośa*, Vasubandhu records, in his own hand, the voice of the opposition—the Sautrāntika masters say:

> it is just the state of the mind being fixed on a single object … there need be no further, separate mental factor … its substance just is the mind.

The meaning: what you call "concentration" is nothing more than "the way the mind is when fixed upon one object." That the mind is one-pointed—this fact itself is named concentration; there is no need to set up, apart from the mind, a further thing called "samādhi" to explain it. Set concentration on the scale—the Sautrāntika says all you are weighing is this unscattered mind itself; no extra weight has appeared in the pan.

Vasubandhu's attitude toward this position is worth savoring. He writes one short sentence: "Though this reasoning has merit, it is not the tenet of my school."—the argument has its point; but it is not the position I hold. Recorded, respected, not adopted. A fault-line runs, just so, straight through the *Kośa*'s own pages.

Saṃghabhadra, the Sarvāstivāda's later champion, answered hard. Among his reasons one cuts closest to the practice: the "association" of mind and mental factors is the same in every state—it admits of no more or less; yet concentration plainly has its depths and shallows—the concentration of the first jhāna is shallower than that of the fourth. If concentration has degrees, while "the way the one-pointed mind is" can give no account of degree, then concentration cannot be merely the name of a way of being; it must be a dharma of which depth and shallowness can themselves be said.

### The Theravāda Says: It Is a Thing, but Gone in a Moment

The Theravāda of the southern transmission also set concentration on the scale, and the scale, too, read "there is": one-pointedness of mind is, in the southern treatises, an ultimate real (*paramattha-dhamma*)—what remains when analysis has gone all the way down, not an empty name. But its "there is" is not the Sarvāstivāda's "there is." The Sarvāstivāda's substance of concentration perdures across time, a thing that can be kept; the Theravāda's substance of concentration arises and perishes momently (*khaṇika*)—each instant it falls, and instant upon instant it rises anew. The weight on the scale, as the Theravāda reads it, flickers.

Set the three readings side by side: the Sarvāstivāda says there is an ever-present it; the Theravāda says there is, but each moment's is new; the Sautrāntika says there is no it at all, only the one-pointed mind. Who is right? This chapter does not judge. What matters is to see that the everyday sentence "the concentration I have attained" does not mean the same thing on the three scales—a keepable possession, a train of momentary sparks in succession, or a way the mind is. One word put in question, and the question reaches down to the practitioner's most basic understanding of his own work.

### Three Words to Tell Apart, and One Fault to Recognize

Before leaving the workshop, two small matters remain.

One is a set of three near-synonyms. *Samādhi*—the "even-holding" met in chapter 1.1—is just that mental factor of one-pointedness of mind, present even in the scattered mind; what it measures is "how widely present." *Samāpatti*, "attainment," is the *zhèngshòu* recognized in Part One—the concentrated state entered; what it measures is "which level entered"; it is broader than *samādhi*, since even mindless states such as the attainment of non-perception and the attainment of cessation count as *samāpatti*. *Samāhita*, "the composed state," is the even, continuous station induced by the mind that precedes it. Three words, three referents; in sutta and treatise they often stand shoulder to shoulder, and once they are told apart, nothing you read will tangle them.

The other is a fault within concentration that the treatise-masters named. One and the same absorption can stand in three states: stuck fast with craving, it is the relished absorption; the clean, mundane, wholesome one is the pure absorption; the one conjoined with the noble path is the undefiled absorption. The word "relished" is set down with great precision—to crave the very state of absorption one has entered, like a tongue lingering on a taste: that absorption has been stained through with attachment. The treatise-masters class the relished absorption as "obstructive": it can obstruct the noble path. Look closely at the weight of this: even concentration can become an object of craving; the lovelier the state, the faster it sticks. This name, the Prajñā part will come back to visit—and heavily.

### The Workshop Door Opens Outward

Three chapters, three knives, gathered into one place. The compounding knife: the object is matched to the affliction in power—matching the remedy to the illness. The dissecting knife: applied and sustained thought are the motion of language; still them, and there is noble silence; concentration deepens by removing motion layer by layer. The knife that questions substance: is concentration a thing, a momentary dharma, or merely a state of the mind—three schools, three readings, left open.

The treatise-masters dissected concentration to the very end. But the concentration on their table is, from first to last, one person's affair—cultivated by oneself, realized by oneself, finished by oneself. There is one question the whole workshop never asked: for whose sake is this concentration cultivated?

The moment this question is spoken aloud, a great turning in the history of the Buddha's Dharma begins. The next part, the Mahāyāna.
