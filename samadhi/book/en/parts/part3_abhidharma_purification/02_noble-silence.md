## 3.2 Noble Silence

There is a doubt that many who sit in meditation have known: the mind has settled, the hindrances have thinned, and yet the mind is not wholly still—it is still moving, subtly, like a beam of attention lightly aiming at the object, lightly resting there. And so the suspicion arises: have I still not entered?

What the texts say is exactly the opposite. When sutta and treatise list the components of the first jhāna, what stands at the head of the list is not a blankness but precisely a motion—awareness and examining; the new translations call them applied thought and sustained thought. In other words, that subtle aiming and resting is not the noise of "not yet in concentration"; it is the very factor the first jhāna plainly lists, and the first to be set down. Part One met its name when reading the four jhānas; this chapter lays it on the dissection table and takes it apart: what exactly this moving factor is, how it comes to stillness, and why its stilling carries so beautiful a name—noble silence (*ariya-tuṇhībhāva*).

### The Coarse Stirring and the Subtle Stirring of the Mind

The treatise-masters' definition of applied and sustained thought is plain to the point of austerity. The *Prakaraṇapāda*:

> What is applied thought? The coarse-stirring nature of the mind. What is sustained thought? The subtle-stirring nature of the mind.

Two sentences pin the vital point: applied and sustained thought are both "stirring"—a motion of the mind, not a content of the mind. They are not "having thought of something"; they are "the mind moving toward somewhere, then adhering there a while." Applied thought is the coarser cast outward; sustained thought is the finer holding that follows. The treatise adds that sustained thought "follows upon applied thought"—it is the stretch glued on behind, turning in the wake of the cast.

Tradition left two similes that bring this coarse and fine to life. One is the bell: at the stroke, the sound bursts out coarse and strong, and the lingering hum thins after—the first strike is applied thought, the lingering hum is sustained thought. The other is the bird: at takeoff it beats its wings with effort, then glides at ease—the wing-beat is applied thought, the glide is sustained thought. Both similes say one thing: applied and sustained thought are not two acts but two stretches of one act—a stretch of effortful casting, a stretch of easeful holding.

So the meditator at the opening of this chapter has her answer. That beam of "lightly aiming, lightly resting" she perceived is precisely applied and sustained thought—the casting and the resting of attention, not the thinking of thoughts. See this clearly: applied and sustained thought are a motion of attention, not conceptual thinking. Though the first jhāna contains this moving factor, the mind is not spinning fantasies; and its later stilling is the setting down of that last aiming force, not the extinguishing of the mind.

### Why It Is Called Noble Silence

Where do applied and sustained thought come to stillness? In the second jhāna—the sutta says "awareness and examining already stilled." The treatise-masters did not stop at "practice that far and they stop"; they gave a mechanism. The *Mahāvibhāṣā*:

> The second dhyāna ceases the root of speech. The root of speech is applied and sustained thought. As the discourse says: "One must have applied and sustained thought to be able to issue speech, not without them."

Applied and sustained thought are the root of speech. A person can open the mouth and speak because the mind has first stirred with applied and sustained thought—first the aim is taken, first the hold is fastened, and only then can words be assembled. Without applied and sustained thought, there is no root from which speech issues. In the second dhyāna applied and sustained thought have ceased; the root of language is severed—and so it received a name: noble silence, the silence of the noble ones.

The Āgamas had planted this name long before. The *Saṃyukta Āgama* records the World-Honored One teaching the Venerable Maudgalyāyana:

> If a monk stills awareness-and-examining, inwardly clear, the mind one, without awareness, without examining, the concentration born of rapture-and-bliss, and dwells endowed with the second jhāna, this is called noble silence … Maudgalyāyana, you should keep noble silence; give rise to no heedlessness.

Here one thing must be seen clearly: noble silence is not a vow of silence. It is not the discipline of keeping the mouth shut. Even one who never opens his mouth all day, so long as applied and sustained thought still stir within—still aiming, still adhering, still assembling sentences inwardly—is not silent in this sense. And the reverse holds: this silence is an affair of the mind's ground; the whole mechanism that pushes the mind toward language has been stilled. Quiet of the mouth, anyone can manage; silence of the mind waits for the second jhāna. Stillness, it turns out, has a layer this deep.

### Two Traditions, the Same Ridgeline

Applied and sustained thought—one coarse, one fine, arising glued together. Then when they cease, do they cease at once, or one before the other?

The suttas carry word of it. The *Madhyama Āgama* plainly lists three concentrations:

> to train in the concentration with awareness and examining, the concentration without awareness and with little examining, the concentration without awareness and without examining.

The middle one—"without awareness and with little examining": awareness already gone, a little examining still left. The coarse departs first; the fine remains. The sutta saw this transitional instant.

Later, two treatise traditions that never exchanged a word each gave this instant a place. The Sarvāstivāda of the northern transmission set up for it a separate "ground" (*bhūmi*), named the intermediate dhyāna—above the first jhāna, short of the second, wedged between, with even a karmic fruit all its own. The southern treatises added for it a "reckoning" on the ladder—a fivefold reckoning (*pañcaka-naya*) set beside the current four, inserting between the first and second jhānas the level "without applied thought, with sustained thought alone."

One adds a ground, one adds a reckoning—different architecture; but two pairs of hands that never met drew the same ridgeline. This fact itself carries great weight: if the jhāna-factors were merely a list later men tidied up, with nothing real beneath, two traditions would not have set their chisels, unprompted, at the same fine point. Two independently drawn maps rise at the same spot—and that spot is a mountain the terrain itself owns.

### From Coarse to Subtle, Removing Motion Layer by Layer

After applied and sustained thought, rapture and ease have their own anatomy too. Rapture is the coarser exultation, carrying a tremor of elation, so the third jhāna sets it down first; ease is the finer, quieter receiving, so only the fourth jhāna sets it down. The treatise-masters pressed even the question "what, exactly, is the ease of the first two dhyānas"—the *Kośa* judges it to be pliancy, that supple, unagitated settledness of body and mind, and not a pleasant feeling; the southern judgment runs exactly the other way, taking ease to be feeling. That the two schools each hold one end of this factor shows the very point: the jhāna-factors are not a checklist of feelings to be scored "reached / not reached," but an anatomy that can be taken apart factor by factor and questioned to the end.

Gather the whole line in: applied and sustained thought are the motion of language—the second jhāna stills them, hence the name noble silence; rapture is the motion of exultation—the third jhāna leaves it; ease is the motion of enjoyment—the fourth jhāna relinquishes it. From coarse to subtle, three layers of motion removed layer by layer—the scale Part One set up shows its inner workings on the dissection table: the direction of deepening concentration was never to amass more, but to set down ever finer motion, layer by layer. One sentence can close this chapter: When applied-and-sustained thought grows still, concentration is born.

The person has been matched; the factors have been taken apart. One last knife remains in the workshop, and it cuts deepest—the treatise-masters ask: concentration, this thing taken apart and taken apart again—is it, after all, a "thing"?
