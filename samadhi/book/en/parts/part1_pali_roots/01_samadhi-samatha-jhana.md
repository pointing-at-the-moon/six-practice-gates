## 1.1 Samādhi, Samatha, Jhāna

Open the *Saṃyukta Āgama*—of the Chinese Āgamas, the one closest to the Pāli Saṃyutta Nikāya—and where the text speaks of a practitioner entering concentration, the word it uses most often is not the *chándìng*, the "meditative concentration," familiar today, but *zhèngshòu*; next most often, *sānmèi*; after that, "right concentration." As for "śamatha," "vipaśyanā," "*chánnà*"—the words so common in today's Buddhist books—in this entire collection they never once appear.

This small fact is the best possible starting point for this book. It tells us that "concentration," in the scriptures, was never something one word could exhaust. To enter the gate of concentration, we must first take the several words that look alike and are constantly mixed together, and tell them apart, one by one.

### Three Words, Three Faces

First, set up three words.

The first word is concentration—*samādhi*, transliterated *sānmódì* or *sānmèi*. It names a faculty: the mind's capacity, set facing one object, to gather in, to stay unscattered, to abide. Ordinarily the mind flows everywhere like water; concentration is what gathers this water in and lets it stay.

The second word is calm—*samatha*, transliterated as śamatha. It names a method: countering scatteredness with stilling, letting the mind grow quiet, and walking by this toward concentration. Samatha is the road; samādhi is the faculty the road brings to completion.

The third word is *jhāna*—rendered in meaning-translation as "quiet consideration." It names an attainment: when the mind, following the road of calm, has gathered in to some definite depth, that level has a name—the first jhāna, the second jhāna, the third, the fourth. A jhāna is one notch on the scale.

To gather it into one sentence: samādhi is a faculty, samatha is a method, jhāna is an attainment. The three are not three names for one same thing; they are three faces of one same field. Knead them into a single lump, and the whole book after this reads as a blur; tell them apart, and every chapter has its place to land.

### The Definition in the Discourses: One-Pointedness of Mind

Then how do the discourses define "concentration" itself?

In the Pāli *Majjhima Nikāya* there is a short discourse which records a layman named Visākha going to question a nun of great wisdom—the nun Dhammadinnā. He asks: what is concentration? Her answer is a single sentence: the one-pointedness of the mind—this is *samādhi*.

"One-pointedness of mind" (*cittassa ekaggatā*—the mind's unification upon a single object): the mind abiding upon one object, not scattering, not wavering, not running off. This short phrase later became the definition shared by a thousand years of treatises. Xuanzang's translation of the *Abhidharmakośa* says: "*Samādhi* is the one-pointedness of mind." The *Cheng weishi lun* says that concentration, "upon the contemplated object," is what "focuses the mind undistractedly." Different treatises, different schools—the skeleton of the definition is one and the same.

One treatise speaks more densely still. The *Prakaraṇapāda*, explaining what the faculty of concentration is, sets a whole string of words in a row:

> The mind's abiding, even abiding, close abiding, settled abiding, non-scattering, non-distraction, calming, even-holding, and unification of mind upon one object: this is called the faculty of concentration.

Abiding, non-scattering, non-distraction, calming, even-holding, one-pointedness of mind—this string of words is like a chain, drawing all the translation-names that ever surfaced, early and late, across the history of the translated canon back onto one same referent. *Zhèngshòu*, *sānmèi*, even-holding—they all say this one thing: the mind gathered in upon a single object.

Here one thing must be seen clearly: one-pointedness of mind is a gathering-in, not a blank. Concentration is not pressing the mind down until nothing is left; it is settling the mind in one place—settled steady, and settled clear. When we come to the four jhānas this will show still more plainly: the deeper the concentration, the brighter the mind, not the duller.

### One Thing, Many Names

As said at the opening, when the *Saṃyukta Āgama* speaks of entering concentration, its most frequent word is *zhèngshòu*. The word deserves a longer look. *Zhèngshòu* names the state of being in concentration—within it, receiving its object—what the later treatises call the state of attainment (*samāpatti*). So when the text says "at the time of the first jhāna's *zhèngshòu*," it means "while within the concentration-state of the first jhāna." We will meet this word in quantity in chapter four; recognize it here first.

*Sānmèi* is the transliteration of *samādhi*, and the most widely current of all: it can name concentration broadly, and it can stand as the proper name of this or that samādhi-state—the many samādhis we will meet later in the book all wear this word. "Right concentration," for its part, names specifically the eighth factor of the noble eightfold path: concentration's formal seat on the path of liberation. With Xuanzang's new translations, two more were added—"even-holding" and "quiet consideration." Even-holding renders *samādhi*, taking the sense of an even, balanced holding; quiet consideration renders *jhāna*—the quiet is concentration, the considering is wisdom, and inside the one word hides the news of concentration and wisdom running together.

So many names—a first-time reader of the sutras and treatises easily goes dizzy. There is no need for alarm; just remember that chain: abiding, non-scattering, non-distraction, calming, even-holding, one-pointedness of mind. A thousand names, ten thousand titles, pointing to one same thing.

### The Word "Chan," Set Aside for Now

There is one more word that must be settled plainly at the opening of the book: *chán*.

At the level of the translated scriptures, *chán* is simply the abbreviation of *chánnà*—of *jhāna*—naming an attainment such as the four jhānas; through the first six parts of this book, wherever the word appears, it carries this sense. But Chinese Buddhism later grew another "Chan": the Chan of the Chan school—Bodhidharma's Chan of facing the wall, the Sixth Patriarch's Chan in which concentration and wisdom are one substance. That is a great house of learning which took conscious shape only around the Tang, with its own claims and its own family style.

That Chan of the Chan school this book holds back until Part Seven, with a whole part to speak of it. Until then, the reader is asked to set it to one side. Walk the Pāli roots, the Āgamas' concentration, and the treatises' analysis steadily first, layer by layer, and when Part Seven comes to look at the Chan school, you will be able to see what it truly inherited—and what it overturned.

### Mindfulness and Concentration: A Line Drawn First

For readers who have read volume one of this series, *Mindfulness*, one more line must be drawn here.

Mindfulness (*sati*) is the work of awareness—remembering, keeping watch, knowing what is happening right now. Concentration (*samādhi*) is the work of gathering in—collecting the mind onto a single object. The two are neighbors and often appear side by side, but they are not one thing. Mindfulness is the foundation; concentration is the layer above the foundation that gathers inward. This book speaks of concentration, and will not talk concentration back into mindfulness; whatever belongs to mindfulness, volume one has already said—here we only touch the border where the two meet, without repeating.

### When the Water Grows Still, the Moon Was Already There

Last, one picture, to close the chapter.

Concentration, when it comes about, has most often added nothing to the mind; it has removed the disturbance. A basin of water, while it is being stirred, reflects nothing; leave off stirring it, and the water grows still of itself—the moon was already there at its bottom. Nothing was added to produce the reflection. To cultivate concentration is not to put the moon into the water; it is to let the water come to rest. Removing the disturbance—this is the first verb of everything the study of concentration will go on to say.

The three words are now told apart: samādhi is a faculty, samatha is a method, jhāna is an attainment. But all of this is still a matter of words. Concentration, in the Buddha's own person, was a road walked in the flesh—He realized the deepest concentration of His day, and then set it down with His own hands.

In the next chapter, we go to look at that road: a rose-apple tree, and two teachers.
