## 2.2 The Channel Forms When the Water Arrives

Anyone who has wrestled on the cushion with a restless mind has most likely tasted this flavor: the harder you push, the less the mind will settle. You pull the mind back, pass after pass; pass after pass it runs out again; you grit your teeth and sit on, the legs go numb, the mind grows wilder still. And then the doubt is hard to keep out: is it that I am not trying hard enough?

What this chapter has to say is this: most likely it is not too little effort; it is effort spent in the wrong place. On how concentration comes, the texts have a sequence stated with great clarity—and in that sequence, concentration is not at the end where the effort goes.

### The Seven Awakening-Factors: Concentration Sits Sixth

The Buddha taught a list called the seven awakening-factors—the seven factors of awakening, in order: mindfulness, investigation of states, energy, rapture, tranquility, concentration, equanimity. (The *Saṃyukta Āgama* renders it "awakening-portion"—the same word.)

Concentration stands sixth. Before it are five factors: mindfulness is awareness; investigation is discernment; energy is the applying of effort; rapture is delight in the Dharma; tranquility is the settling of body and mind—the old character rendered "tranquility" speaks of lightness, of steadiness, of agitation laid to rest, and the later treatises would render the same word "pliancy." And after concentration there is one factor more: equanimity, abiding in evenness.

Sixth is not only an order in the list; it is, even more, an order of arising. The *Saṃyukta Āgama*, teaching how the cultivation of mindfulness of in-and-out breathing fulfills the seven awakening-portions in sequence, comes to the passage where the concentration awakening-portion arises—and the causal run between its verbs deserves to be read word by word:

> The mind being gladdened, body and mind grow tranquil … body and mind being tranquil, one gains the ease of body and mind; having gained the ease of body and mind, one gains concentration.

Rapture gives rise to the tranquility of body and mind; tranquility gives rise to the ease of body and mind; "having gained the ease of body and mind, one gains concentration"—ease gives rise to concentration. On this chain, concentration is the second-to-last fruit. It is not a first cause the practitioner grits out of nothing; it grows out of the ease before it, and that ease has itself grown up out of tranquility, out of rapture, out of the investigation and energy further back still—all the way along.

### Need Not Intend: The Sequence Unfolds by Natural Law

And this chain can be traced still further back. The *Heyi-jing* (*Discourse on What Is the Purpose*) of the *Madhyama Āgama* begins from the keeping of the precepts and presses all the way to concentration:

> Keeping the precepts has non-regret as its meaning … non-regret has joy as its meaning … joy has rapture as its meaning … where there is happiness, the mind gains concentration.

Keep the precepts, and the mind has no regret; no regret, and the mind is joyful; joy gives rise to rapture, rapture gives rise to tranquility, tranquility gives rise to happiness—"where there is happiness, the mind gains concentration." What moves one most in this discourse is its tone. It says the precept-keeper "need not intend"—need not stir up the thought "let me be without regret"; non-regret arises of itself along with the keeping of the precepts. In the same way, one who has come to happiness need not form the intention "let me enter concentration"; the mind settles of itself. The Pāli parallel makes this layer of the meaning more thorough still: from non-regret to joy, to rapture, to tranquility, to happiness, to concentration, everything arises of itself in due sequence, "by natural law" (*dhammatā*)—such is the nature of things; this is simply how the matter grows, joint by joint.

Even the grammar of the text testifies to this meaning. The Pāli says "for one at ease, the mind is concentrated"—and that "is concentrated" stands in the medio-passive voice: the mind is what gets concentrated, not "I go and do the concentrating." Concentration comes upon a mind already at ease, the way water flows toward a channel already dug.

And so this chapter's title can now stand up: the channel forms when the water has arrived. The channel is not concentration itself; the channel is the precept, is mindfulness, is investigation and energy, is rapture and tranquility. Digging the channel is the business upstream; the flowing of the water is the fruit downstream. Effort is truly to be spent—keeping the precepts takes effort, rousing energy takes effort, telling wholesome from unwholesome takes effort—but all of it is spent on digging the channel. Dig the channel well, and the water comes of itself. Cultivate the cause with diligence, and the fruit ripens of itself; diligence in planting the cause; forcing the fruit, labor wasted.

And this is exactly the answer to that wrestling match on the cushion. To strain at the fruit called "concentration" is to force the fruit; the fruit will not be forced, and the straining only winds the mind tighter and tighter. The road the texts point is the reverse: go back upstream—why is the mind not joyful? Where is there regret? Where would rapture come from? Has tranquility arrived yet? Once the channel runs clear, the water arrives of itself.

### Agitated or Sunken: Concentration Stands on the Calming Side

Within the seven awakening-factors, concentration has one finer position still. The *Saṃyukta Āgama* observes that the mind's deviations are of two kinds: one is "agitation"—floating, restless, unable to stop; the other is "feebleness"—sunken, unable to lift, drowsy and dim. To counter the two deviations, the seven factors are to be used by sides.

When the mind is sunken, one should cultivate the three factors of investigation, energy, and rapture, to rouse it; when the mind is agitated, one should cultivate the three factors of tranquility, concentration, and equanimity, to settle it. Used the wrong way round, the trouble is real: to cultivate the calming three when the mind is low—the text says, "this is untimely"—is like a small fire just catching, which one wishes to make blaze: heap wet fuel onto it, and the fire goes out; to cultivate the rousing three when the mind is agitated is to give the gale another fan. Only mindfulness, the one factor, serves at all times.

Look at which side concentration stands on: the calming side. Its function in the Dharma is to settle an over-active mind. And this says once more why concentration cannot be forced—a restless mind, precisely because it is over-active, only moves the more under doubled effort; the text says outright that to press concentration onto such a mind is "untimely." Concentration cannot be seized out of turn; it must wait for tranquility to soothe the stirring down first, and only then is it born of itself, within the ease.

### Concentration Is Not a Product of the Will

What this chapter has been saying, at bottom, is one point of doctrine: concentration is not a thing the will can directly manufacture.

It has a place: sixth among the seven awakening-factors. It has a sequence: mindfulness, investigation, energy, rapture, tranquility—factor ripening upon factor, and only then does concentration come to fruit. It has a side: between agitation and sinking, it stands on the side of calming. Put the three together, and concentration's identity is plain—it is a fruit that arises held up by the prior factors, not a state forced out by technique.

Here too this book's measure of proportion must be stated: what this chapter has given is the structure of how concentration arises, not the operating steps for entering it. To know that the channel forms when the water has arrived is one thing; to force the water along by the diagram is another—and the moment the latter is attempted, the fruit has been smuggled back into a technique.

Concentration has arisen. Then this fruit that has arisen—what, exactly, is its content? The texts' answer is a strange one—they do not count, from the front, what concentration *has*; they count, from the reverse, what it does *not* have. The five things it does not have are called the five hindrances.
