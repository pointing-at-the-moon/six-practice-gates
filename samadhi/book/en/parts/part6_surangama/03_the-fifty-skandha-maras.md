## 6.3 The Fifty Skandha-Māras

By the ninth fascicle of the Śūraṅgama the Dharma was all but fully spoken, and the World-Honored One was about to leave the seat. But He turned back—out of compassion for the practitioners of the latter age, who in samādhi do not recognize the māras, He deliberately sat back down and proclaimed afresh a stretch of "subtle māra-matters." This teaching, added with a turn of the body, is the fifty skandha-māras.

First set the reading of this stretch straight. It is commonly read as one of two things: read as a bestiary of monsters—the text does carry the names of deva-māras, ghosts, spirits, and sprites, so the weight falls on "what demons one will meet on the road"; or read as a pathology list of meditation's side effects—a catalog of fifty symptoms of "fire-deviation, entering the demonic." The text's own organization is another matter: it is a chart of criteria—by what standard the states appearing in concentration are adjudged good or demonic. What it answers is not "what will go wrong, and how to prevent and treat it," but "a state has appeared—how is it to be seen?"

### If the Host Be Deluded, the Guest Finds Its Opening

The keystone of the whole discourse is these lines:

> Though those māras have great wrath, within their dust-and-toil and within your wondrous awareness it is as wind blowing on light, as a knife cutting through water—nothing touches at all … relying merely on spirit-power, they are but guests; the rupture is accomplished by the host of the five skandhas within your mind. If the host be deluded, the guest finds its opening. Awake and unconfounded right where you stand in dhyāna, and the māra-matters can do nothing to you.

Though the māras have great wrath and great power, within the practitioner's wondrous awareness it is as wind blowing on light, as a knife cutting water—nothing touches at all: the māra is "but a guest." What truly accomplishes the rupture is the "host of the five skandhas" in the practitioner's own mind. Only if the host be deluded does the guest find its opening; let the host be awake right where he stands, without confusion, and the māra-matters can do nothing.

These lines take apart, at one stroke, the reading of "harm inflicted by outer māras." The wrong turn was never a wound dealt by something outside; it is one's own mind misrecognizing a state of concentration. One commentator has a figure: a thousand lamps sharing one room—when one lamp is about to leave the room, the light of every lamp trembles at it. The practitioner's concentration, on the point of dissolving the five skandhas, shakes the dharma-realm he shares with all beings, and the māras, stirred by it, appear. The māras' arising is not because the practitioner has done some evil; it is the natural effect of true concentration shaking the shared realm—and the key to success and failure, from first to last, lies only in whether the host is deluded or not.

### Form No Notion of Sagehood, and It Is a Good State

As the five skandhas—form, feeling, perception, formation, consciousness, from coarse to fine—dissolve layer by layer, each skandha presents ten kinds of states; five times ten makes fifty. And the text adjudges every one of these states with one and the same sentence-form. Take the first state of the form-skandha:

> This is but a temporary effect of the work and is no sagely realization. Form no notion of sagehood, and it is a good state; construe it as sagehood, and you fall prey to the host of māras.

This sentence-form is the criterion formula of the whole chart; weigh it word by word. A state arising in concentration is, in itself, a transitional mark in which the power of contemplation temporarily prevails over the knot of the skandha—a flash and it is past, in itself no fault. Raise toward it no thought of "I have realized sagehood," and it is merely "a good state"; only on "construing it as sagehood"—fixing that flash of a transitional mark as a sagely stage already realized—does the state turn: "you fall prey to the host of māras."

What makes the māra was never "what state appeared"; it is "what construal was placed upon the state." The state is neutral; the wrong turn lies only in the one thought that construes it as sagehood. The old masters set a threefold measure here: do not sweep every good state aside as māra (that would smother the genuine working of the practice); do not at once acknowledge it as sagehood (the practitioner's delusion and karma are not yet severed); discern it well, with neither grasping nor rejecting—"the moment the mind sets out to claim realization, that is when it becomes a māra."

### From Coarse to Fine: Three Layers of Terrain

The wrong turns of the five skandhas change mechanism three times as the dissolving deepens. What this section describes is terrain, not a route-guide for the walking—the text itself keeps to that boundary: it says "this is what it is," not "this is what to do."

At the coarsest, the form-skandha and the feeling-skandha, the states that appear are projections of one's own mind; nothing from outside has slipped in. The verdicts carry a self-correcting safety valve—"if understood, there is no fault; awake to it and unconfused, in time it dies away of itself": one thought of awareness, and the state recedes on its own. The old masters diagnose the root sickness of this layer in four plain words: concentration present, wisdom absent—concentration arrived first, and wisdom has not yet caught up.

At the perception-skandha the safety valve is gone, and the text's wording turns heavy: "the deva-māra, seizing its chance"—the outer condition rides in through the gap; sprites and spirits of every kind either possess a person's body or appear in forms of their own, and the mouth, too, can expound the sūtras and the Dharma. Yet the root is still in the practitioner himself: only when the perceiving mind wavers is there a gap to be ridden; and when the possession has gone on long, its appetite is sated and it departs of itself—even the māra of this layer has its limit.

At the finest, the formation-skandha and the consciousness-skandha, the terrain turns over once more, and the outer māra leaves the stage: "the ten kinds of deva-māra finding no opening"—the practitioner's power of concentration now deep, the mind clarified and upright, the outer condition has no gap left to ride. The wrong turns of this layer arise purely from one's own mind "engaging in [false] reckoning" upon the profound state of concentration—erecting verdicts upon the marks one sees—and so tumbling into the wrong views of a heterodox path. Here the text passes its heaviest sentence: "He turns away from perfect penetration, turns his back on the city of nirvāṇa."

Set the three layers side by side: form and feeling are the mind-māra; perception is the deva-māra; formation and consciousness are the view-māra. What must be seen clearly is this: the three layers are a difference of kind, not of clinical severity—what the chart adjudicates is "which kind of wrong turn this is," not "how grave the symptom is." And the deepest layer gives most to ponder: the deeper the concentration, the more powerless the outer māra—and the more hidden the wrong turn: the last fork lies not with ghosts and spirits but with the view one has erected oneself.

### The Remedy Lies in Wisdom, Not in Technique

The pivot of the whole chart surfaces here.

The essence of "construing it as sagehood" is grasping—fixing a transitional concentration-state, without nature of its own, as a real and substantial sagely realization. So its remedy, from first to last, is one medicine only: prajñā. The treatment the old masters prescribe is "to contemplate, with the markless wisdom, that the skandha-body has from the first never arisen"—wisdom seeing the five skandhas as never graspable, the grasping loses its footing of itself. Another figure says it through to transparency: in the dark of night a man mistakes a stump for a ghost and is frightened out of all measure; one sentence breaks the spell, and the ghost-thought is wholly gone—the deluded thinking is scattered by awakening; it does not retreat before force of arms.

The reader of Part Five will recognize this medicine. The prajñā corpus cut the conceit of "I have attained concentration," cut the dharma-clinging within concentration-states—that was the theory; the fifty skandha-māras put the same theory to work as a skandha-by-skandha chart of remedies: at every layer, the remedy for the wrong turn is not a deeper concentration (the deeper the concentration, the harder the forks of formation and consciousness are to detect), not a finer technique, still less the subduing of outer māras—it is wisdom's not-grasping. Meeting a state in concentration, the adjudication is one line only: form no notion of sagehood.

The whole chart gathers, at the last, into one place. Each of the five skandhas has one layer of deluded thinking for its root—the fifty states in all their profusion are, at bottom, one deluded thinking unfolding from coarse to fine. The sūtra closes with a handful of words that illumine sudden and gradual both at once:

> In principle, awakening is sudden, and with awakening [the delusion] is dispelled at once; in practice, it is not removed all at once but exhausted in sequence.

In principle, one awakening and the delusion of the five skandhas is dispelled along with it, then and there; in practice, the habit-energies admit no sudden removal but are exhausted in sequence, step by step. In principle sudden, in practice gradual.

### Part Six Is Walked

Gate, path, map—the three chapters compose one complete terrain of the Śūraṅgama's teaching of concentration: the precept as base—gathering in the mind is the precept, and the gathering begins from the upholding of it; perfect penetration as entrance—reverting the faculty, entering the stream, turning the hearing back to hear its own nature; the criterion as guard—form no notion of sagehood, and it is a good state. A great samādhi already complete from the first, together with the road that leads to it and every fork upon that road, has all been handed over.

Only—those few words at the sūtra's end, in principle sudden awakening, in practice no sudden removal, raised waves in the history of the concentration gate that run far beyond one chapter. If principle can be suddenly awakened to, then after the awakening, is the ladder still needed? Is the map still needed? There was a company of people who carried this question all the way to the bottom.
