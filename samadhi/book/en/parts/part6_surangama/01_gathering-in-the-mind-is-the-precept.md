## 6.1 Gathering In the Mind Is the Precept

Ānanda had asked for the concentration by which bodhi is attained; by the sixth fascicle, the World-Honored One gives the master outline of cultivation:

> You have long heard me, in my Vinaya, expound the three decisive senses of cultivation—namely, gathering in the mind is what I call the precept; from the precept concentration is born; from concentration wisdom is unveiled. These are called the three trainings free of outflows.

The sequence of the precept, concentration, and wisdom is no stranger to the reader of this book—when Part Two read the Āgamas, this order had long since been set. But the Śūraṅgama's raising of it is not a general discourse on the three trainings; the sentence that follows hard upon it is the true eye of this chapter:

> Ānanda! What is the gathering-in of the mind that I call the precept?

The wording of this line must be heard precisely. The Speaker of the sūtra does not say "what is the keeping of the precepts"; He does not say "what is restraining and forbidding"; He says "what is the gathering-in of the mind that I call the precept"—the precept, here, is defined directly as gathering in the mind: the mind that gallops and scatters outward, drawn back and gathered inward.

This definition by a single word carries enormous weight. For the movement "the mind gathering inward" is precisely concentration's own movement—every work of calming-and-contemplation, without exception, sets out from the mind's gathering inward. So "gathering in the mind is the precept" is as much as to say: the precept and concentration, at this one point of "gathering in," are one and the same movement; what differs is only the stage of that one movement—the precept is the gathering begun, concentration is the gathering completed.

Take a figure. The mind climbing along outer conditions is like objects scattered across a desk, drifting farther the more they scatter; gathering in the mind is like reaching out and drawing the scattered things back into the palm, one by one. The drawing-in movement is already "gathering" from the instant the fingertips first touch the first object—there is no such thing as two disconnected works, "first sort through the scattered things, then set about drawing them in." The precept as gathering-in is exactly the opening of this "drawing in": it does not come before the gathering; it is the gathering's first portion.

The common reading takes "from the precept concentration is born" as the succession of two things different in kind: first the moral precept, afterward the meditative concentration, and between them a seam to be stepped across. By the sense of "gathering in the mind is the precept," that seam simply does not exist—the precept is already the first portion of concentration. A person whose precept-conduct is immaculate is not merely someone who "has done nothing bad"; his mind is already gathering in, already standing inside the threshold of concentration—only not yet settled deep. In the very moment of upholding the precept, what is being done is not some preparatory homework outside concentration; it is the first portion of concentration's own work.

### Four Ropes Leading Outward

Ānanda having asked about gathering in the mind, the Speaker of the sūtra straightway instructs:

> You asked about gathering in the mind; I now first set forth the wondrous gateway of cultivation for entering samādhi. To seek the bodhisattva path, one must first uphold these four kinds of restraint, pure as ice and frost … the mind will not even cling to form, scent, taste, or touch—how then could any demonic affair arise?

The four kinds of restraint are none other than the four pure and clear instructions the reader of the previous volume knows well: cutting off lust, cutting off killing, cutting off stealing, cutting off the great lie—the Śūraṅgama's order sets lust at the head, departing from the usual sequence of the śrāvaka Vinaya canon; this is its arrangement matched to the ailment of the concentration gate.

See clearly why these four obstruct concentration. Their obstruction of concentration lies not in the moral judgment that they are "bad deeds," but in this: each of them is a rope binding the mind fast to an outer object. Once the mind raises craving at these objects, raises aversion, raises grasping, it is hauled along by that object, racing outward with no way back. The four kinds of restraint cut these four off, and for concentration their work is exactly this: to loosen these outward ropes one by one—the ropes all loosened, the mind drawn by nothing, only then can the movement of gathering inward truly begin. So the Speaker's eye is not on "uphold these four precepts, and conduct will be upright," but on "the mind will not even cling to form, scent, taste, or touch"—whether the mind can come back. The precept here clears the ground for the gathering; and the clearing of the ground is itself the gathering's opening move.

"How then could any demonic affair arise?"—set this line down first. The mind once purified and gathered in, no longer racing outward, what would disturb concentration has no ground from which to arise. As for what māra-matters are, the third chapter of this part holds the full picture.

### Steaming Sand to Make Rice

That the four kinds of restraint are the foundation of concentration, the Speaker says down to the very bottom with one simile:

> If one cultivates dhyāna without cutting off lust, it is like steaming sand and gravel hoping to make rice: through hundreds of thousands of kalpas it is still only hot sand. Why? Because this is not the stuff of rice, being made of sand.

The key of this simile lies not in admonition but in pointing out an impossibility at the level of the foundation. Steam sand however long, it never becomes rice—not because the fire is too weak, not because the hour has not yet come, but because the material is wrong: sand is not the stuff of rice. Just so with cultivating concentration upon an unpurified mind: though hundreds of thousands of kalpas pass, what comes of it is not true concentration, for the foundation does not stand. To read the steaming of sand as moral admonition ("do not do this") is to read it shallow; what it states is the actuality of the gathering movement—if the mind still climbs and races outward to the objects of lust, killing, stealing, and lying, the gathering has never begun at all, and concentration naturally has no base on which to stand.

### The Precept Volume's Brick Is Built into the Wall

At the close of the previous volume, *The Precept*, this same passage of the four pure and clear instructions was read from the precept's side, and a brick was handed toward the concentration gate: the precept as the ground of concentration, to be formally entered upon in the concentration volume. Now that brick is built into the wall—the same passage of scripture: the precept volume read the precept's completeness (why the four instructions must be received and upheld as one whole set); the concentration volume reads the precept's first step (what the precept has done for concentration). Only the two faces together make the full breadth of "gathering in the mind is the precept."

And this sequence of the precept giving birth to concentration is no solitary light of the Śūraṅgama's. The chain Part Two walked—upholding the precept, non-regret, joy, rapture, tranquility, happiness, "where there is happiness, the mind gains concentration"—the Indian canon's own "the channel forms when the water has arrived": the Śūraṅgama takes that whole chain over and sets it beneath the great samādhi by which bodhi is attained. It is not founding a new doctrine; with a greater samādhi, it receives that sequence of long descent.

The gate is set. Within the gate, how is the mind to be gathered in? In the sūtra, twenty-five sages rise one after another, each recounting from which point he entered in his own day.
