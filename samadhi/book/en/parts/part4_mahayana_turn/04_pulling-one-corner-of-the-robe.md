## 4.4 Pulling One Corner of the Robe

Open the Mahāyāna sūtras and treatises to the study of concentration, and the first-time reader is mostly startled by one thing: what comes at you is not a clean, countable list like the four dhyānas and eight attainments, but samādhis with a hundred names—the Heroic-March samādhi, the Ocean-Seal samādhi, the lion's-roused-vigor samādhi, the jewel-seal samādhi, the Pratyutpanna samādhi, the lion's-play samādhi—titles in such profusion that at first sight a doubt almost rises: is there real doctrine here, or an extravagance growing more occult the longer it speaks?

This chapter answers that doubt head-on. The conclusion first: these hundred names are not further, higher concentrations stacked on top of the four dhyānas and eight attainments; nor are they a pile; nor a new hearth built elsewhere. What the Mahāyāna has done is enlarge the same one "concentration" into a field with names and entries—and the naming itself is its doctrinal workmanship.

### Two Grammars: One Side Subtracts, the Other Adds

The *Dazhidu lun* states its own method: to explain the perfection of prajñā, it uses "the eighteen emptinesses"; to explain the perfection of dhyāna, it uses "the hundred-and-eight samādhis."

The symmetry carries great weight. On prajñā's side, a string of negations—eighteen kinds of emptiness, closing in on the true nature by veiling, by sweeping, by "not this, not that": subtraction. On the side of dhyāna, a string of namings—a hundred samādhi-names unfolding concentration's inexhaustible functions by coining and by listing: addition. One subtracting, one adding—the two hands of a single tradition. Naming is not the decoration of the study of concentration; it is the mode of disclosure by which that study stands face to face with prajñā: prajñā opens by subtraction; dhyāna opens by naming.

First pin down an easy misreading: "hundred-and-eight" is not exactly one hundred and eight items in an inventory to be counted off. It is a round number—like the hundred-and-eight afflictions, the hundred-and-eight beads—signifying a multitude that is its own completeness. Read it as a precise roster of powers, and you miss exactly what the naming is doing. The treatise's author draws the line plainly: the disciples' samādhis are finite and countable; the bodhisattva's samādhis are "measureless, incalculable"—too many ever to finish counting. And this "too many to count" is precisely the dharma it teaches: the function of concentration has no exhausting.

Then why are all the new names called "samādhi," not "dhyāna"? The treatise's author has bounded the breadth of the three words: all meditative concentration may be called *concentration*, may be called *samādhi*; "dhyāna" names only the root absorptions with the factored structure of the four dhyānas. Samādhi is the widest; dhyāna the narrowest. To open an endless roll of new titles, one needs the container that was widest from the start—"dhyāna" is locked shut by the structure of the four dhyānas; only "samādhi" has room for the hundred-and-eight. The enlargement happens at the word where the language has left the most room.

### Each Name Locks In a Function

How are these names established? The scriptures keep to a highly regular formula. The Larger *Prajñāpāramitā*, listing the bodhisattva's samādhis:

> this samādhi can seal all samādhis; this is called the jewel-seal samādhi.

"What is the samādhi named so-and-so?—this samādhi can do such-and-such—this is called the samādhi so-and-so." First the name is given, then the function glosses the name, welding name and function fast together. The jewel-seal is called jewel-seal because it can seal and certify all samādhis. Every name locks in, this way, one capacity of the bodhisattva. The hundred names together are not a drift of floating titles; they are a map of functions on which every node carries its own definition.

But will cutting it up this way dismember the seamless whole of concentration into a hundred fragments? The *Dazhidu lun* answered long ago, and with the most beautiful simile of the chapter:

> Like pulling one corner of a robe, and the whole robe comes up.

A robe cannot be lifted whole—there is nowhere to take hold; but grasp one corner, and the whole robe rises with it. All samādhis are from the first one body; naming marks out, on this measureless robe of concentration, corner after corner that can be taken hold of. Cultivate the Heroic-March, or cultivate the Pratyutpanna: take hold of any named entrance, and the whole of concentration is drawn along. The treatise adds one more simile: or like tasting honey—taste a single drop and you know the flavor of the whole jar.

This is the doctrinal workmanship of naming: the measureless itself gives no purchase—no one can practice a "measureless"; naming turns the inexhaustible into door after door by which one can enter.

### The King Among Samādhis: The Heroic-March

Carried to its limit, naming forces out one question: above all the named samādhis, is there one name whose function is precisely "to hold all the rest together"? There is. It is called the Heroic-March samādhi.

First get one bibliographical matter clear, lest the wrong hat land on the wrong head: what is meant here is the *Śūraṅgama-samādhi-sūtra* translated by Kumārajīva, and it is not the same scripture as the *Great-Crown Śūraṅgama-sūtra* known to every household in China (commonly known simply as "the *Śūraṅgama*")—the latter has its own history, and Part Six of this book is given entire to it. Here we speak only of "Śūraṅgama" as the name of a samādhi.

The sūtra states what sets this samādhi apart: "The Heroic-March samādhi cannot be known by one thing, one object, or one meaning." An ordinary samādhi can be grasped by one affair, one object, one meaning—the samādhi of emptiness takes emptiness as its object; the samādhi of loving-kindness takes the happiness of beings. The Heroic-March cannot, because its range is "everything." The sūtra unfolds its hundred aspects, and the first is: "Cultivating the mind to be like space." It is not a bounded state of absorption; it is a holding-together that contains all things the way space does. And from this its name is established:

> All factors aiding bodhi follow in its train; therefore this samādhi is named the Heroic-March.

The ground of the name is command: all the dharmas that aid the Way follow it, as ministers follow a king. And here the most important thing must be seen clearly: the Heroic-March is "the king among samādhis," but the kingship means containment, not transcendence—it does not stand "one rung higher" on the vertical ladder of the four dhyānas and eight attainments; it is a horizontal holding-together, the bodhisattva's ripened command of the whole field of concentration. Read it as one more story added at the ladder's top, and you have fallen back into that tower of powers stacked ever higher.

### The Two Ends of the Field

This named field shows its breadth in one name at either end.

One end reaches toward the dharma-realm. The "Worthy Leader" chapter of the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* speaks of "the spiritual power of the Ocean-Seal samādhi": the great sea, wind and waves stilled, printing in one moment the myriad images of the sky—the marks of all the dharma-realm appear at once in this absorption, as the sea mirrors the heavens. The containing capacity of concentration can be as wide as the whole dharma-realm. Of this name we take here only one passing edge; its home ground is Part Eight, in the dharma-realm of the Huayan.

The other end reaches toward mastery. The lion's-roused-vigor, the transcendent, the lion's-play—the previous chapter saw them demonstrate the bodhisattva's mastery in entering and leaving; on this map they are the named nodes where the field stretches toward the end called "mastery."

One end the breadth of containing, the other the liveliness of entering and leaving; and holding the two ends together, the Heroic-March. The grammar of naming has here unfolded into a complete map.

### Part Four Is Walked

Look back at the arc of these four chapters. Concentration re-natured: prajñā plus great compassion, and dhyāna becomes a pāramitā. Concentration re-modeled: the nine stages of mental abiding, a ruler that measures the mind's force. Concentration re-held: neither falling nor resting, and this is what mastery means. Concentration re-enlarged: a hundred names, each name a corner of the robe, the myriad concentrations gathered under the Heroic-March.

But everything this part has done is construction—transformation, modeling, enlargement; the grandeur of concentration built ever greater. A reader steeped in prajñā, arriving here, will feel a doubt rise: this vast field with the Heroic-March for its king—what of the field itself? Once concentration has been named, empowered, built into so magnificent a map, will prajñā not turn back and ask one question—this "mark of concentration" itself: is it, too, empty?

It will. The next part, prajñā turns back.
