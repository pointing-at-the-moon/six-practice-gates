# Appendix A · Academic Notes Index

The main text was written to be spoken to you. So the catalog numbers, the fascicle references, the source locations—all of these have been ushered out of its pages and gathered here.

This index serves two purposes. First, it lets the reader who wants to look closer go from any chapter of the book back to the exact place in the sūtras and treatises on which that chapter rests. Second, it gives a single overview of the principal texts cited across the book's thirty-one chapters. Nothing in this book is invented—every chapter's argument presses down on the original wording of the texts listed below. All Chinese-canon quotations have been collated against the CBETA Electronic Tripiṭaka (the Taishō canon and the Manji Zokuzōkyō); the Pāli canon is cited by Pali Text Society (PTS) numbering, and in the main text it is mostly rendered by sense rather than transcribed word for word.

Numbering conventions: **T** refers to the Taishō Shinshū Daizōkyō (volume and text number); **X** to the Manji Shinsan Zokuzōkyō; **MN / AN / SN / DN** to the Pāli Majjhima, Aṅguttara, Saṃyutta, and Dīgha Nikāyas.

Several adjudications concerning attribution and wording are also recorded here, and the whole book abides by them. First: the *Fahua jing anlexing yi* (The Meaning of the Lotus Sūtra's Course of Peaceful Practice, T1926) is headed "spoken by the great dhyāna master Si of Nanyue, of the Chen"—the form of a record of oral teaching; throughout, this book writes "attributed to Huisi of Nanyue as its speaker" and does not assert that he penned it himself. Likewise the *Zhengdao ge* (Song of Realizing the Way, T2014): scholarship has reached no settled verdict on its single authorship, and the book writes throughout "traditionally attributed to Yongjia Xuanjue." Second: Dazhu Huihai's *Dunwu rudao yaomen lun* (Treatise on the Essential Gate of Entering the Way by Sudden Awakening) is numbered X1223, in the Manji Zokuzōkyō; it must not be confused with the esoteric text that bears the number T1223 in the Taishō—the same number in a different canon. Third: the dharma-realm lion-samādhi of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* reads, in the sūtra's own text, "the Lion's-Stretch samādhi" (*shizi pinshen sanmei*, *siṃha-vijṛmbhita*); the name "the Lion's-Roused-Vigor samādhi" (*shizi fenxun sanmei*) issues from the *Dazhidu lun* and Larger *Prajñāpāramitā* line—different scriptures, different senses, and the two are never conflated. Fourth: the phrase "just-sitting" (*shikantaza*) is a later coinage of Dōgen in Japan, not Hongzhi Zhengjue's own name for his practice—Hongzhi's own name was "silent illumination" alone; and "infinitely multiplied, endlessly interpenetrating" is an expository coinage of the commentators Fazang, Chengguan, and their line, not the wording of the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* itself. Fifth: the dialogue in which Huike begs to have his mind pacified is not recorded in Daoxuan's Tang-dynasty *Xu gaoseng zhuan* (Continued Biographies of Eminent Monks); it is a developed form that crystallized in the *Jingde chuandeng lu* (1004), and the book quotes it throughout as the Chan school's self-interpretation, not as sixth-century historical fact. The *Lengqie shizi ji*'s text of the entrance by principle writes "wall-gazing" with a loan-graph (a *pi* standing in for the *bi* of "wall")—a variant of transmission. And the *Shaoshi liumen* (T2009) is of doubtful authorship; the book uses it only as corroborating witness.
