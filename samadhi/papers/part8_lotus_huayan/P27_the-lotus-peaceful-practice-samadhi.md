# The Lotus's Peaceful-Practice Samādhi

**Concentration as Conduct — Huisi's Formless Peaceful Practice and the Half-Walking-Half-Sitting Lotus-Samādhi**

*中文主題：法華〈安樂行品〉之行門定——慧思無相安樂行與半行半坐之法華三昧*

> "The formless practice just is the peaceful practice. Amid all dharmas, the mark of mind is stilled and in the end does not arise; this is why it is called the formless practice. One abides ever in every kind of profound and subtle dhyāna: walking, standing, sitting, lying, eating, speaking — in all four postures, the mind is ever in concentration."
>
> — *The Meaning of the Lotus Sūtra's Course of Peaceful Practice*, transmitted as spoken by Nanyue Huisi, T1926, p. 0700a18–a21

---

## Abstract

The "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" of the *Lotus Sūtra* reconstructs "concentration" into something that runs through walking, standing, sitting, and lying, through speech and silence, motion and rest — a samādhi practiced as a mode of conduct, which this paper calls *concentration-as-conduct* — and not merely a station one enters and leaves while seated. The paper first examines the concentration-face of the scripture itself: it names dhyāna as a source-state ("entering a quiet room … rising from dhyāna") and then spreads the vocabulary of dhyāna across the *sphere of intimacy*, which belongs to the category of conduct ("ever fond of seated meditation, in a quiet place, gathering and disciplining the mind"; "abiding unmoved as Mount Sumeru … abiding ever in one mark"). It then takes as its keystone Huisi's *Meaning of the Lotus Sūtra's Course of Peaceful Practice* — "the formless practice just is the peaceful practice … in all four postures the mind is ever in concentration" — and sets against it, as its explicit foil, the *practice-with-marks* (reciting the sūtra with a scattered mind, "not entering samādhi"); of special note is that Huisi expressly cuts this profound dhyāna off the three-realm ladder ("it does not rest on the desire realm, nor abide in form or formlessness … this is the bodhisattva's pervading practice"), so that it is no higher rung but a different modality. The paper then takes Zhiyi's half-walking, half-sitting Lotus-samādhi, his reading of "gathering and disciplining the mind = the concentration-gate," and his three-action calming-and-contemplation of "governing amid violence, at peace amid alarm," as the Tiantai consolidation of this concentration-as-conduct. Inheriting the preceding paper's *living concentration of non-abiding in signs* (Chan), this paper discloses that the *Lotus* holds a separate source for, yet the same conclusion as, the thesis that "concentration is not a seated station": Chan folds concentration inward to the limit of a single thought free of signs; the *Lotus* spreads concentration outward to fill the four postures. The two meet, from different scriptural lineages, at one place — the starting point of the Lotus-Huayan "totalizing" spine.

**Keywords:** peaceful practice (安樂行), concentration-as-conduct (行門定), the formless practice (無相行), profound and subtle dhyāna (深妙禪定), Huisi (慧思), Zhiyi (智顗), the Lotus-samādhi (法華三昧), half-walking, half-sitting (半行半坐), gathering and disciplining the mind (修攝其心), one mark (一相), the four postures (威儀), samādhi (三昧)

## I. From Concentration-as-a-Tower to Concentration-as-Conduct

To picture "concentration" as a tower one can climb storey by storey is the most natural image for one who cultivates it, and the one most familiar to the contemporary reader who treats sitting as a measurable inner attainment to be accumulated. The longer one sits, the deeper one enters, the higher one seems to have climbed; concentration thus becomes an inner yield that can be counted, compared, and signed off. This volume's running question — what is concentration (*samādhi*)? — has been from the start an effort to loosen that picture: the deepest reach of concentration is not on the top floor of that tower.

The preceding paper (Part VII) closed one stretch of this inquiry by way of Chan. Chan's concentration is not some station achieved while seated, but the *living concentration of non-abiding in signs*, one substance with wisdom (*prajñā*): it folds concentration inward, into a single thought, into that limit where one is free of signs without leaving the world. That is a thorough turning inward — concentration is not in the seated form but in the present thought that meets objects without being turned by them.

This paper opens another door. It comes not from a Chan record but from a scripture of an altogether different lineage — the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" of the *Lotus Sūtra* (*Miaofa lianhua jing*). What is striking is that this door opens onto the same conclusion: concentration is not a seated station. But it travels the opposite direction — not folding concentration into a single thought, but spreading it open to fill the whole comportment of walking, standing, sitting, lying, speaking, and falling silent, and naming this the *peaceful practice* (*anlexing*). If Chan's living concentration folds concentration inward to its limit, then the *Lotus*'s peaceful practice spreads concentration outward to its full reach: across the four postures, the mind is ever in concentration.

So the volume's question — what is concentration? — takes a new form here. Concentration is not the building of some Sumeru-like attainment by sitting, but a mode of conduct in which, through walking, standing, sitting, lying, eating, and speaking, "the mind is ever in concentration." This paper calls that form **concentration-as-conduct** — concentration as a practice running through the four postures, not merely a station of entering and leaving. It should be said at the outset that what this paper undertakes is not to set out a method for practicing the peaceful practice (that lies outside this volume's descriptive scope), but to clarify two things: how the tradition read the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" as just such a reconstruction of "concentration," and what, doctrinally, that reconstruction means. What follows examines three layers of material in turn — the concentration-face of the scripture itself, Huisi's definition of it, and Zhiyi's systematic consolidation — then sets these in dialogue with three Western scholars, and finally returns concentration-as-conduct to the volume's arc to close.

## II. The Concentration-Face of the Chapter on Peaceful Practices

The "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" (the fourteenth) is, on its face, the Buddha's teaching to bodhisattvas who would keep the sūtra in the latter age of four peaceful practices — of body, speech, mind, and vow. Under the bodily peaceful practice it opens out a *sphere of action* and a *sphere of intimacy*, listing a long sequence of what the bodhisattva should abide in and what he should keep far from. Within that long list two leaves grow together, facing in different directions. One is the precept-face — the spheres of action and intimacy as a precept of conduct, a roster of restriction-precept and practice-precept; that leaf has been taken up as the home ground of a companion paper in the *Precepts* volume (SĪLA P26), and this paper does not restate its precept-reading. What this paper takes is the other leaf of the very same passage, the one steeped in the vocabulary of dhyāna: the concentration-face.

The scripture first names "dhyāna" as a source-state. The "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" describes the continuity of the bodhisattva's teaching: "entering a quiet room, with right recollection, contemplating dharmas according to their meaning. Rising from dhyāna …"[^1] — entering the quiet room, right recollection, contemplating dharmas according to their meaning, and then "rising from dhyāna" to teach. The sequence repays a close look: dhyāna is not the interruption or the opposite of teaching, but the place teaching "rises from"; teaching is the thing one does upon rising from concentration. Concentration is here identified as the source of conduct (teaching) — concentration first, and conduct then flows out of it. This is the scripture's first placement of "concentration": a source-state one may enter and leave, and out of which conduct issues.

More telling is the second placement: the scripture lays the vocabulary of dhyāna across the *sphere of intimacy*, which belongs to the category of conduct. It says the bodhisattva is "ever fond of seated meditation, in a quiet place, gathering and disciplining the mind"[^2] — "seated meditation," "a quiet place," "gathering and disciplining the mind," all three set within the sphere of intimacy, set in a row alongside other norms of conduct. Which is to say, seated meditation and the disciplining of the mind are not isolated here as a self-standing "stretch of concentration practice"; they are woven into one aspect of the bodhisattva's daily conduct, one strand of the sphere of intimacy. Concentration is not some further thing outside daily life, but a mode of conduct that daily life draws near to and abides in. The verse-portion gathers this face into a famous image: "in a quiet place, gathering and disciplining the mind, abiding unmoved as Mount Sumeru … abiding ever in one mark"[^3] — the unmovingness of Mount Sumeru figures the steadiness of that abiding, and "abiding ever in one mark" points to the mark of one-pointedness: the mind rests on a single object and never shifts. Sumeru unmoved, abiding ever in one mark, is the portrait of "gathering and disciplining the mind" pushed to its limit.

The scripture's close takes "entering deep into dhyāna, seeing the buddhas of the ten directions"[^4] as the fruit toward which the peaceful practice tends — when concentration is deep, one sees the buddhas pervading the ten directions. So within the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" concentration answers to itself head and tail: it is both the source of conduct (rising from dhyāna to teach) and the fruit of conduct (entering deep into dhyāna and seeing the buddhas).

There is a detail here easy to slide past. The four words "gathering and disciplining the mind" sit, in the place they occupy in the scripture, embedded within the roster of the sphere of intimacy, shoulder to shoulder with the prohibitive language of keeping far from various sources of disturbance. That is: the same passage, read from the prohibitive side, is a precept of conduct (what the bodhisattva should keep from, what he should draw near); read from the gathering side, it is a concentration of conduct (how the bodhisattva should abide his mind). Precept and concentration are, in the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices," two leaves of one and the same passage — which is why this paper and the *Precepts*-volume companion can draw from the same passage, each taking one leaf, without colliding: the precept-reading is the precept-reading, the concentration-reading is the concentration-reading, each with its own home. The leaf this paper follows is the gathering sense of "gathering and disciplining the mind."

Taken together, the concentration-face of the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" presents a double structure. On one side, "rising from dhyāna" identifies dhyāna as a source-state one may enter and leave, a side not far from the ordinary understanding of "a stretch of concentration practice." On the other, "ever fond of seated meditation, gathering and disciplining the mind" and "abiding unmoved as Mount Sumeru, abiding ever in one mark" spread that dhyāna into the sphere of intimacy that runs through the bodhisattva's daily life, so that it is no longer only one enterable, leavable concentration but a constantly abiding aspect of the bodhisattva's conduct. Between the two sides a tension already lies hidden: is concentration "a station of entering and leaving," or "a constantly abiding practice-gate"? The scripture lays both faces out, yet does not, with a single term, name and gather this double structure. It lets "dhyāna" be both the source and the constantly abiding mark across the postures, without yet saying how the two are gathered into one meaning. The work of naming and gathering it falls to Huisi.

## III. Huisi: The Formless Peaceful Practice Just Is the Peaceful Practice

The one who gathered the concentration-face of the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" into a single defining sentence is Huisi (515–577), as transmitted in *The Meaning of the Lotus Sūtra's Course of Peaceful Practice*. The work's opening line ascribes it to "the great meditation master Si of Nanyue of the Chen, as spoken" — a record of oral teaching — and so it is referred to throughout as "transmitted as spoken by Huisi," with no claim of his own hand. In a single sentence Huisi fixes the root sense of the peaceful practice, and that sentence is this paper's pivot:

> "The formless practice just is the peaceful practice. Amid all dharmas, the mark of mind is stilled and in the end does not arise; this is why it is called the formless practice. One abides ever in every kind of profound and subtle dhyāna: walking, standing, sitting, lying, eating, speaking — in all four postures, the mind is ever in concentration."[^5]

This sentence pushes the volume's running "concentration" to a new position. First, the formless practice *just is* the peaceful practice — the two are one thing, not two methods set side by side, but two names for a single practice. Second, what makes this formless peaceful practice a *concentration* lies precisely in the two clauses "abiding ever in every kind of profound and subtle dhyāna" and "in all four postures the mind is ever in concentration." Note "abiding ever" and "all four postures": concentration is no longer some station of entering and leaving, no longer a seat attained while sitting, but that state in which, across the whole range of conduct — "walking, standing, sitting, lying, eating, speaking" — "the mind is ever in concentration." Here Huisi resolves the tension seen in §II — is concentration an entering-and-leaving station, or a constantly abiding practice-gate — plainly in favor of the latter: concentration is constantly abiding, pervading the postures, the mind ever unmoved. This is just the root sense of what this paper calls concentration-as-conduct: concentration as a mode of conduct running through the four postures.

Huisi then sets it off against its opposite pole. He posts the *practice-with-marks* and the *formless practice* as two pegs:

> "Again, the practice-with-marks — this is, in the 'Chapter on the Encouragement of Samantabhadra,' to recite the *Lotus Sūtra* with a scattered mind and to press on … not cultivating dhyāna, not entering samādhi; whether sitting or standing or walking, single-mindedly intent on the letters of the *Lotus*, pressing on without lying down as though to save one's burning head. This is called the lettered practice-with-marks."[^6]

The practice-with-marks is that fervor which keeps and recites the letters of the *Lotus*, presses on with a scattered mind, and does not lie down day or night as though to save one's burning head. Huisi does not deny its fervor, but he points to one crux in particular: this practice "does not cultivate dhyāna, does not enter samādhi." It is a fervor of the *scattered mind* — the strength of mind is fierce, yet it has not been gathered onto a single object, and so it is called "the lettered practice-with-marks." (The Samantabhadra figuration and the vow-conduct of sūtra-recitation here have their own home ground, which this paper does not unfold; it takes only the one sense of "scattered, not entering samādhi" as a foil.) Setting the scattered-mind recitation against the mind-ever-in-concentration of the formless, the "concentration-face" and the "letter-face" of the Chapter on Peaceful Practices part cleanly: what this paper takes is the concentration-face (the formless); the sūtra-letter face lies outside this paper's scope.

In his own verse Huisi binds these two clearly together — "the formless four peaceful practices, the profound and subtle dhyāna"[^7]: what the formless four peaceful practices answer to is precisely the profound and subtle dhyāna. And he firms the sense up with a gloss on the words themselves: "amid all dharmas the mind does not move — therefore 'peace'; amid all dharmas there is no aggregate of feeling — therefore 'bliss'"[^8] — "peace" is the mind not moving amid all dharmas, and this "mind not moving" is the same concentration as the earlier "in all four postures the mind is ever in concentration." One sees that Huisi reads the two words "peaceful bliss" from the start as a mark of concentration: peace is the mind unmoved, bliss is the absence of the aggregate of feeling.

The most decisive stroke is that Huisi expressly cuts this "profound and subtle dhyāna" off the ladder of the three realms' four jhānas and eight attainments:

> "The profound and subtle dhyāna within the peaceful practice is not like this. Why? It does not rest on the desire realm, nor abide in form or formlessness. To practice dhyāna thus is the bodhisattva's pervading practice — in the end without thought of mind, and therefore called the formless practice."[^9]

"Not like this" means that the profound and subtle dhyāna of the peaceful practice is not the same as the graded dhyāna of the three realms. It "does not rest on the desire realm," nor does it "abide in form or formlessness" — it is neither the scattered mind of the desire realm nor a resting within the concentration-states of the form and formless realms. In other words, it is not some higher layer of concentration-state above the four jhānas and eight attainments, not a further rung up that tower; it is "the bodhisattva's pervading practice" — a form of concentration that pervades all the postures and is bound to no single ground of the three realms. The phrase "in the end without thought of mind" deserves attention: it takes no mark and abides in no object, and so it does not fall within the graded stations of concentration that have an object to rest on.

This stroke answers exactly the spine this volume has been re-examining all the way from its Pāli ground: the four-jhāna ladder, shedding jhāna-factor after jhāna-factor as it rises, is a structure of orderly stations one can ascend rung by rung. The concentration of the peaceful practice is not on that ladder, and is not a higher rung of it; it is a different kind of concentration — concentration as a practice-gate. Up to this point the volume has discussed concentration as a "laddered concentration ascended rung by rung"; here it adds a non-laddered paradigm: concentration cannot, and need not, be slotted onto some storey of that tower, because it lies, from the start, spread across the level ground of walking, standing, sitting, and lying.

These three steps of Huisi's interlock. First, with "the formless practice just is the peaceful practice … in all four postures the mind is ever in concentration," he establishes the root sense — concentration is a constant concentration pervading the postures. Second, with the practice-with-marks of "scattered, not entering samādhi" as foil, he marks this "mind ever in concentration" off from mere fervent pressing-on — it is not that practicing fiercely counts as concentration, but that the mind gathered into one and ever unmoved counts. Third, with "does not rest on the desire realm, nor abide in form or formlessness," he cuts this concentration off the ladder of the three realms — it is not a higher layer of concentration-state but a bodhisattva's pervading practice bound to no single ground. Taken together, Huisi gathers the double structure the scripture left latent and unnamed into one clear proposition: the concentration of the peaceful practice is a formless, posture-pervading, non-laddered constant concentration. What this paper calls concentration-as-conduct is just another name for that proposition.

## IV. Zhiyi: The Half-Walking-Half-Sitting Lotus-Samādhi and the Concentration-Gate

After Huisi, the one who took the peaceful practice up into a larger system of cultivation and realization was his disciple Zhiyi (538–597). Zhiyi fixed the samādhi of the Lotus line under the name "the Lotus-samādhi," and assigned it to the half-walking, half-sitting class among the four kinds of samādhi. The four kinds of samādhi, as a systematic classification of concentration-cultivation by bodily posture, have had their groundwork laid out in detail in a companion paper of the *Mindfulness* volume (NIAN P23); this paper does not re-enumerate the four forms, but takes only the half-walking, half-sitting slot directly relevant to the peaceful practice, as the dwelling-place for the concentration of the Lotus-samādhi. The crux of "half-walking, half-sitting" lies in the one word "half": it is neither the full stillness of pure sitting nor the full motion of pure walking, but a concentration between walking and sitting, between motion and rest — which accords exactly with concentration-as-conduct's sense of "concentration pervading the postures."

Zhiyi's reading of the sphere of intimacy in the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" divides it three ways. In the *Words and Phrases of the Lotus Sūtra*, expounding the sphere of intimacy, he distinguishes three gates: keeping far from the ten kinds of disturbance is the precept-gate, "gathering and disciplining the mind" is the concentration-gate, and contemplating all dharmas as empty is the wisdom-gate.[^10] The precept-gate (the face of keeping from disturbance, of restriction-precept and practice-precept) is just what the *Precepts*-volume companion treats in detail, and that paper has expressly handed the concentration-gate leaf among these three over to this one; the wisdom-gate (contemplating all dharmas as empty) belongs to the matter of *prajñā*, and this volume keeps to the single pole of stilling, the systematic handling of wisdom having its own home elsewhere. What this paper takes is the "gathering and disciplining the mind = the concentration-gate" leaf among the three. Under this concentration-gate Zhiyi says: "Now the concentration-gate sets out at length the cultivating mind of concentration, the place of cultivating concentration, the essential gate of cultivating concentration; and by the power of concentration, one governs amid violence and is at peace amid alarm."[^10]

"Governing amid violence, at peace amid alarm" is the phrase most worth turning over. Concentration is not the drawing of a person out of clamor and dread and the setting of him down in some untroubled quiet; quite the reverse — it is the capacity to "govern" within violence and to "be at peace" within alarm. Concentration appears within objects, not by leaving them behind. The clamor need not first be quieted, the dread need not first be dispelled, and the mind is already able to govern within them, to be at peace within them — this is "in all four postures the mind ever in concentration" brought down onto a concrete circumstance. By this Zhiyi makes concentration-as-conduct concrete: the mind ever in concentration is not the setting up of some separate stretch of still concentration apart from conduct and circumstance, but the achieving of that peace within conduct itself, within the very circumstances of violence and alarm. It should be said that this is a description of the *mechanism* of concentration-as-conduct — an account of "what kind of concentration this is" — and not an instruction for how to use the mind when violence and alarm come.

Zhiyi then makes the sense concrete through the three-action method of "half-walking, half-sitting." In the *Great Calming and Contemplation*, expounding the method of the Lotus-samādhi, he says: "The body has its opening and restraint, the mouth its speaking and silence, the mind its calming and contemplation … all taking half-walking, half-sitting as the method."[^11] The body has opening and restraint (the opening and closing, the going and halting of walking, standing, sitting, and lying), the mouth has speaking and silence, and the mind runs calming and contemplation in tandem — the three actions of body, speech, and mind all taking half-walking, half-sitting as their method. The point here too lies in the "half": the body is not confined to a single sitting, the mouth not held to a single silence, the mind running between calming and contemplation; and so "in all four postures the mind ever in concentration" is no longer only Huisi's single line of doctrine, but comes to ground as the concrete mark of calming-and-contemplation in which body, speech, and mind all run between motion and rest. This is the Tiantai consolidation of concentration-in-motion: concentration is not outside motion and rest, but within them, amid the opening and restraint, the speaking and silence of the three actions.

One line must be drawn here. Zhiyi's "Lotus-samādhi" in fact carries two faces: one is the whole rite of repentance-practice set out in detail in the *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite*; the other is the profound and subtle dhyāna toward which one tends after carrying out that rite. The former (the procedural sequence of the repentance-rite) is the home ground of a companion paper in the *Repentance* volume (KSAMA P13), and this paper does not unfold its ritual procedure. This paper takes only the boundary-line Zhiyi himself drew: in the *Lotus Samādhi Repentance Rite* he says that the practitioner, after the various ritual practices, should "set aside what was done before, rely directly on the peaceful practice, and be ever fond of seated meditation"[^12] — "set aside what was done before" means gathering up the earlier ritual practices, and "rely directly on the peaceful practice, be ever fond of seated meditation" means entering directly into the profound and subtle dhyāna of the peaceful practice. In this one line Zhiyi parts, with his own hand, the repentance-rite from the deep-concentration destination it leads to; and the latter is just the concentration of the peaceful practice this paper treats.

## V. In Dialogue: Stevenson, Stevenson–Kanno, Swanson

Reading the peaceful practice as concentration-as-conduct both has points of dialogue with, and points to push beyond, the existing Western scholarship on Tiantai concentration. Three scholars are taken here, each summarized doctrinally rather than rendered word for word.

First, Daniel B. Stevenson on the four kinds of samādhi in early Tiantai. His account treats the four kinds of samādhi as four forms of concentration-cultivation classified by bodily posture, with the Lotus-samādhi belonging to the half-walking, half-sitting class. To bind "concentration" to the postures of the body for classification is exactly the ground this paper builds on — once concentration can be classed by posture, "concentration pervading the postures" is no idle claim. The point to push beyond: Stevenson mainly situates the Lotus-samādhi along the line of the repentance-rite (the very face the *Repentance*-volume companion treats), and stops at a typological description of bodily posture, without going on to the doctrinal proposition that "the Chapter on Peaceful Practices reconstructs concentration into a mode of conduct (of body, speech, mind, and vow)." The systematic groundwork of his posture-typology is already laid in the *Mindfulness* volume's companion (NIAN P23) and is not re-argued here; this paper borrows only the half-walking, half-sitting slot as the dwelling-place for the concentration of the Lotus-samādhi.

Second, the complete translation and study of Huisi's *Meaning of the Lotus Sūtra's Course of Peaceful Practice* by Stevenson and Hiroshi Kanno. Their exposition treats the division of the practice-with-marks from the formless practice, and identifies the formless peaceful practice as a practice that is formless, not apart from the sense-faculties and sense-objects, with concentration and wisdom not two. This runs the same way as this paper — the formless peaceful practice is the concentration of "concentration just is conduct, not something achieved while seated." The point to push beyond: their book stops at the philological interpretation of "what Huisi taught," without going on to a doctrinal synthesis with "concentration" as its axis. That this paper takes Huisi's text as the pivot of its primary documentation, and threads the axis of concentration-as-conduct through it, is just what the scholarship has not yet done with it.

Third, Paul Swanson on the philosophical groundwork of Tiantai's perfectly interfused three truths. The three truths of "at once empty, at once provisional, at once the middle" make it so that concentration need not be bound to sitting alone — if all dharmas are, in their very substance, at once empty, provisional, and the middle, then the achievement of concentration too is not bound to any one posture. This explains, from the side of the philosophy of the three truths, why concentration need not be seated. The point to push beyond: it is a general account of the philosophy of calming-and-contemplation, not specific to the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices"; and its three-truths groundwork is already laid in the *Mindfulness* volume's companion (NIAN P23), cited here only as corroboration and not restated.

It must be stated honestly: to date there is no Western monograph that takes as its central thesis "the Lotus peaceful practice = a reconstruction of concentration into concentration-as-conduct (concentration as a mode of conduct), with Huisi's formless peaceful practice as its root sense, resonating with Chan's living concentration while issuing from a different scriptural lineage." To establish the peaceful practice as the doctrinal proposition of "concentration as a practice-gate," and to make this synthesis with concentration as the axis, is what this paper attempts; the bricks it builds with lie scattered among the three scholars above and the primary texts themselves.

## VI. Conclusion: The Peaceful-Practice Samādhi

Taken together, the contribution of the "Chapter on Peaceful Practices" to "concentration" can be gathered in a single line: it reconstructs concentration into concentration-as-conduct — concentration is not some station of entering and leaving while seated, but a mode of conduct running through walking, standing, sitting, lying, speech and silence, motion and rest. The scripture spreads dhyāna into the sphere of intimacy with "ever fond of seated meditation, gathering and disciplining the mind" and "abiding unmoved as Mount Sumeru … abiding ever in one mark," and points to dhyāna as the source and fruit of conduct with "rising from dhyāna" and "entering deep into dhyāna, seeing the buddhas of the ten directions"; Huisi fixes the root sense with "the formless practice just is the peaceful practice … in all four postures the mind is ever in concentration," and judges this profound and subtle dhyāna to be no rung of the three realms' four jhānas and eight attainments; Zhiyi then gives it Tiantai consolidation with the half-walking, half-sitting Lotus-samādhi, with "gathering and disciplining the mind = the concentration-gate," and with the three-action calming-and-contemplation of "governing amid violence, at peace amid alarm." The three layers of material carry one another forward layer by layer, gathering the double structure the scripture left latent and unnamed into a paradigm with a name and a substance: concentration-as-conduct.

Set back into the volume's arc, this paper stands exactly opposite the preceding one. The preceding paper closed with Chan: Chan's concentration is the living concentration of non-abiding in signs, one substance with wisdom — folding concentration inward, to the limit of a single thought free of signs. This paper's peaceful practice turns the other way: it spreads concentration to fill the four postures, across walking, standing, sitting, lying, speech and silence. The two issue from wholly different scriptural lineages — one from a Chan record, one from the Lotus and Tiantai — yet meet at one place: concentration is not a seated station. One folds inward to the limit of a single thought, one spreads outward to the full round of the postures; and what the two together attest is that concentration need not, and in the end cannot, be locked inside a single seated form. To the contemporary reader who treats sitting as a measurable inner attainment to be accumulated, the *Lotus*, beyond Chan, strikes the same note once more: the depth of concentration is not in how long you sit or how deep you enter, but in that mind-ever-in-concentration which, amid walking, standing, sitting, lying, eating, and speaking, has never once left its seat.

This paper opens the Lotus-Huayan part. The *Lotus*, with its peaceful practice, spreads concentration across the comportment of conduct; the next paper (the second of this part) will turn to Huayan, to see how concentration is gathered up to the summit of the dharma-realm — there another kind of "summit-gathering" is at work, drawing concentration into the blazing, all-at-once appearing of the whole. That is the next paper's matter; here it is pointed at only from afar, its sense not borrowed in advance. And the larger question this volume must in the end answer — whether even the deepest concentration is likewise ungraspable, whether concentration must in the end return to *prajñā* — waits further off as well: even if the concentration of the peaceful practice fills the postures, it is still one pole of calming (*śamatha*), and the wisdom-face is yet for another place to take up. This paper keeps to the single pole of concentration and leaves that question at the door.

To close, if the concentration of the peaceful practice is to be gathered in a single line, Huisi's seven characters are the aptest:

> In all four postures, the mind ever in concentration.

Concentration is not a Sumeru built by sitting, but that stretch of mind, amid the postures, which has never moved.
