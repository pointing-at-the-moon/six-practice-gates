# Concentration in the Age of AI

**AI as a Dominant Condition of Concentration — The Attention Economy's Erosion of One-Pointedness**

*中文主題：現代診斷篇——AI 作為定之增上緣·注意力經濟對心一境性之侵蝕*

---

## Abstract

The nature of concentration is defined across the treatises as one-pointedness of mind (*cittass'ekaggatā*)—the gathering of a scattered mind onto a single point. This paper asks how an environment built on capturing-and-switching attention (here called, as an analytical term and not a canonical one, "the attention economy"), an environment now centred on AI, stands in relation to one-pointedness. Drawing on the *Cheng weishi lun*'s distinction within the four conditions between the direct condition (*hetu-pratyaya*) and the dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*), it argues that the direct cause of concentration remains the practitioner's own cultivated *samādhi*, while the AI-environment, acting upon concentration from without with potent force, is a *dominant* condition, not a direct one. Since the treatise defines the dominant condition as one that "either favours or opposes," the same condition can favour or oppose. The attention economy structurally fragments attention—its direction (the many) being precisely the opposite of one-pointedness (the one)—and so its dominant force falls mainly on the "opposing" side; yet the same technology can also favour, as an aid to silence and retreat. The mechanism of concentration is unchanged; what shifts is the external condition on which one-pointedness depends. The paper diagnoses this erosive pressure and locates it doctrinally; it issues no manual.

**Keywords:** concentration (定), one-pointedness (*cittass'ekaggatā*, 心一境性), the dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*, 增上緣), the direct condition (*hetu-pratyaya*, 因緣), "either favouring or opposing" (或順、或違), the four conditions (四緣), the attention economy, the erosion of one-pointedness, *Cheng weishi lun* (成唯識論), *samādhi* (三摩地), modern diagnosis, AI

## I. Gathering the Mind to One Point, and the Scattering of It into Many

You sit down meaning to settle the mind on one thing; but the device in your pocket decides, every few minutes, what the next worth-seeing thing will be. This is not the private failure of a weak will—it is a structural matter. The nature of concentration is one-pointedness: the gathering of a scattered mind into a single absorbed point. The information environment driven by today's AI algorithms runs, structurally, on the opposite logic—it works to break attention into many points, and to keep those points multiplying and switching. One side seeks the *one*; the other manufactures the *many*.

The preceding paper diagnosed two ways in which concentration fails when it is cut loose from wisdom: one that overvalues the meditative state, treating concentration as a tool of mood-regulation and productivity (the relished-flavour failure), and one that hides inside a misconstrued meditative state to avoid what ought to be faced (the failure of taking-it-for-attainment). Those were the two opposed terminals of the old error of concentration severed from wisdom. The present paper turns to a problem peculiar to the modern situation: beyond those inward failures, the present age carries something the ancients did not—a **new external condition**, a technological environment centred on AI and contesting for attention as its prize.

Everything turns on framing the question rightly. The easy slide is to ask "Will AI destroy concentration?" or "Can AI manufacture concentration?"—and both questions treat AI as the *direct condition* (*hetu-pratyaya*) of concentration, its generative cause. From there one falls either into the doom of "AI destroys concentration" or the utopia of "AI produces concentration." The two framings look opposed, but they share one error: each promotes a condition that should stand *outside* concentration, lending force toward it or against it, into the generative cause that directly accomplishes concentration's own fruit. Once the rank is misplaced, the arguer is left choosing between two equally untenable roads—"an external thing can destroy concentration" and "an external thing can accomplish concentration."

This paper holds that both framings misplace AI's rank within the structure of dependent arising. The right question is: as a *potent dominant condition*, does AI favour or oppose one-pointedness of mind? The answer—on the *Cheng weishi lun*'s definition of the dominant condition—is "either favouring or opposing." To shift the question from "make-or-break" (the language of the direct condition) to "favour-or-oppose" (the language of the dominant condition) is not a rhetorical nicety but a structural repositioning: only once AI is set back in its proper place as a dominant condition can one even ask in *which direction* it acts upon concentration. What this paper does is to mark that line of favouring-and-opposing clearly, and to show that what a technological structure built around contesting for attention mainly applies is the "opposing" side.

## II. The Scriptural Ground: Concentration Is One-Pointedness, and AI Is a Condition, Not a Cause

The nature of concentration is defined most cleanly among the object-specific mental factors in the *Cheng weishi lun*:

> What is concentration? Upon the contemplated object, its nature is to focus the mind undistractedly; its function is to be the support of wisdom.[^1]

"To focus the mind undistractedly" holds the essence of one-pointedness—settling the mind upon its object, single and unscattered; and "to be the support of wisdom" already buries a thread that concentration is the support on which wisdom rests (the redemption of that thread is left to this volume's closing paper). The full construction of one-pointedness, from the Pāli Abhidhamma through to the treatises of the Chinese tradition, has been set out earlier in this series[^2]; it is not repeated here. Only its essential sense is taken as this paper's anchor: **what makes concentration concentration is the "one."**

Having fixed that concentration is one-pointedness, one must then settle the rank of the relation between AI and concentration. The Chinese Yogācāra tradition analyses dependent arising by the "four conditions," and the distinction within them between the *direct condition* and the *dominant condition* suffices to set this paper's key. The *Cheng weishi lun* glosses the direct condition as "a conditioned dharma that directly accomplishes its own fruit"[^3]—to accomplish one's own fruit oneself is to be the direct generative cause. It glosses the dominant condition thus:

> If there is a dharma that has potent force, able, with respect to other dharmas, either to favour or to oppose.[^4]

The difference lies just here: the direct condition accomplishes its own fruit; the dominant condition, standing *outside another dharma with potent force*, lends help toward it or stands against it. **The direct cause of concentration—its direct condition—remains always the practitioner's own cultivated *samādhi***: it is the mind itself gathering itself into one point, and no external thing can perform this act in its stead. No technological environment, however refined, can complete for the practitioner the act of "focusing the mind undistractedly"; at most it can increase or decrease the help or hindrance on the side of circumstance—and that is precisely what the dominant condition does, "having potent force, able with respect to other dharmas either to favour or to oppose." The AI-environment stands outside concentration and acts upon its making-and-marring with potent force; its rank can only be that of a **dominant condition, not a direct one**. The full doctrine of the four conditions has a dedicated treatment elsewhere[^5]; it is not re-derived here. Only the single distinction—direct condition versus dominant condition—is taken as this paper's lever.

This distinction at once brings out the middle-way fulcrum of the whole paper: since the dominant condition is one that "either favours or opposes," the same AI-condition can, with respect to one-pointedness, oppose or favour. It carries a two-way firewall built in—blocking at once the doom of "AI must destroy concentration" and the utopia of "AI can cultivate concentration in our place." Should an arguer see only its opposing and declare concentration no longer possible in the present age, that mistakes a potent opposing condition for a condition that annihilates concentration; should one see only its favouring and suppose technology can gather the mind for the practitioner, that mistakes an external condition that may help into the generative cause that directly accomplishes. Hold to the one phrase—"the dominant condition, either favouring or opposing"—and both one-sided errors are avoided.

## III. AI as a Dominant Condition of Concentration: The Attention Economy's Erosion of One-Pointedness

This section is the one core argument of the whole paper: within the frame of "the dominant condition, either favouring or opposing," what the contemporary attention economy mainly applies to one-pointedness is the "opposing" side—and that opposition holds precisely because its structural direction (the many) is the reverse of one-pointedness (the one).

**First, the opposing side: the structural opposition between the attention economy and one-pointedness.** "The attention economy" is an analytical term borrowed here (not a canonical one), naming a technological structure whose operating core is to *contest for and monetise the user's attention*: its commercial incentive requires the system continually to **capture** attention, to **switch** attention, and to make the targets available for switching **multiply**. Capture, switch, multiply—these three together are exactly the structural opposite of one-pointedness. One-pointedness would settle the mind upon *one* object without scattering; the attention economy is structured to break attention into *many* targets and keep them in flux. To put the opposition more precisely: the nature of concentration is defined as "focusing the mind undistractedly," and the weight of it falls on the word *undistractedly*; whereas a structure whose operating logic is capture, switch, and multiply has, as its very output, *distraction*—it does not scatter the mind by accident but takes distraction as its normal mode and its purpose. One side is accomplished by *not scattering*; the other profits by *scattering*. The two differ not in degree but in direction. This is not the deviation of some one product or some one company (and so this paper names no platform, application, or model); it is the operating logic of the structure itself: its very functioning depends on the *non*-accomplishment of one-pointedness. When the practitioner's daily surroundings are filled by such a structure, the external condition that one-pointedness requires is structurally squeezed—this is what the paper calls "the erosion of one-pointedness" (likewise an analytical term, not a canonical one).

**Second, a condition, not a cause: the mechanism is unchanged, the external condition changes.** What must be held especially firm is this: what the attention economy erodes is the *external condition on which* one-pointedness depends, not the *essential mechanism* of concentration itself. That "focusing the mind undistractedly" is the nature of concentration does not change because the surroundings change; the act by which practitioners ancient and modern gather the mind into one is, in its nature, as it always was. Between forest and marketplace, between brush-and-paper and device, what changes is only the tension of the external condition; what does not change is the nature of gathering the mind into one. What has changed is only this: the tension of the external condition that must be contended with, in order to gather the mind to a point, has structurally intensified in the present age—the ancients' conditions for scattering were mostly sporadic and passive; the present age's conditions for scattering are designed, optimised, and dependent for their very survival on contesting for attention. So this paper passes no causal verdict of the form "AI has destroyed concentration" (that would be to misjudge a dominant condition as a direct one); it says only this: as a potent dominant condition, the contemporary attention economy structurally opposes one-pointedness, increasing the external resistance to the act of "gathering into one." What is diagnosed is a *pressure of erosion*, not the ruin of concentration's mechanism. One-pointedness remains achievable; only its headwind is stronger.

**Third, the favouring side is also present: the same condition can favour.** Holding to "either favouring or opposing," one cannot look at the opposing side alone. The same AI-centred technological condition can also turn into a "favouring" condition for one-pointedness—as a guard for silence, an aid to seclusion and solitude, a screen that bars intrusion. The division between favouring and opposing lies not in the technology itself, but in whether the force of that technology, in a concrete situation, helps the practitioner gather the mind into one or helps them scatter it into many. It is worth noting: what can discern whether that force favours or opposes is not concentration itself—concentration merely focuses without scattering; it has no way to turn back upon the condition it stands in and judge it favouring or opposing. What can make this judgement is the wisdom that takes concentration as its support, named in "its function is to be the support of wisdom." In other words, the discernment of favouring-from-opposing must in the end return to the relation between concentration and wisdom. This paper holds that the *dominant* force of the contemporary attention economy leans to the opposing side (its commercial-structural incentives being what they are); but this is a diagnostic judgement about the direction of a force, not a metaphysical pronouncement about the nature of AI. Whether a technology favours or opposes hangs on the discernment of the one who uses it, not on any own-nature of the technology.

A boundary must be drawn alongside: the fragmentation of attention, on the Yogācāra axis, further involves the grasping and the transformation-of-the-basis of the defiled *manas*; on the verbal-karma axis, it further involves the loosening of the joint between volition and speech. Each has its own dedicated paper[^6]; this one keeps to the axis of concentration and treats only the single face it presents to one-pointedness (the opposition of the one and the many), without crossing over to restate the rest.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Engagement: Attention as a Resource Restructured by Technology

Modern scholarship offers two lines worth engaging on the matter of "attention restructured by technology"; this paper draws on them briefly and does not unfold into a treatise on the attention economy.

First, Peter Hershock, early in the information age, already treated attention as a *relational resource* restructured by the technological environment, and examined intelligent technology through the vocabulary of Buddhist dependent arising and karma.[^7] His precision (**worth taking**) lies in this: he points out that attention is not the isolated private property of an individual but is structurally shaped and conscripted within a technological environment—which accords closely with this paper's judgement that "an external condition acts upon one-pointedness." The gain and loss of attention never hung entirely on individual will; it hangs on the conditions one stands within. What he leaves unfinished (**to be supplied**) is that Hershock's lever lies in relational ontology and "technological karma," attending to the relationality of attention and the ethical consequences of technology, yet without furnishing the precise criterion the axis of concentration requires—namely the line of structural opposition between one-pointedness (the one) and the fragmentation of attention (the many). That attention is "restructured" is one thing; that attention is "restructured into the reverse of one-pointedness" is another. What this paper supplies is precisely the latter—the concentration-axis opposition.

Second, the line that uses the term "the attention economy" to describe the structure in which attention is treated as a scarce resource to be contested and monetised.[^8] Its description (**worth taking**) is accurate at the structural level: attention is indeed systematically captured and switched, and this contest has its commercial necessity. What it leaves unfinished (**to be supplied**) is that this line's lever is political economy and commercial mechanism—it stops at describing "how attention is contested for" without reaching "why one-pointedness is, doctrinally, the thing lost when attention is contested for." It can make the mechanism of the contest clear, but cannot make clear *what* is lost; the latter can be made clear only through concentration's doctrinal sense (one-pointedness, focusing the mind undistractedly). This paper does not take its political-economic lever, but reconnects its structural description to the axis of concentration: what is contested for and scattered away is precisely the "one" of one-pointedness.

It should be honestly disclosed: so far as the present writer has seen, no existing Western work makes its thesis the *concentration-axis* reading of "AI as a dominant condition acting upon one-pointedness, either favouring or opposing, constituting an erosion of one-pointedness." To read the attention economy and one-pointedness together under the double frame of "dominant condition / direct condition" and "one / many" is the synthesis this paper attempts.

## V. Middle-Way Correction

**❌ Not to be reduced to "AI destroys concentration / AI manufactures concentration."** This is the error most in need of correction. To cast AI as concentration's destroyer or its maker is, in either case, to **misjudge a dominant condition as a direct one**—to suppose that an external thing can directly accomplish concentration's own fruit. The direct cause of concentration remains always the practitioner's own *samādhi*; AI is a condition outside, and its action is "either favouring or opposing," not "making" or "breaking." Hold to the language of the dominant condition (either favouring or opposing), and one is at once kept clear of both doom and utopia.

**❌ No digital-detox manual is opened here.** This paper is a diagnosis, not a prescription. To identify the attention economy's erosive pressure upon one-pointedness and to locate it doctrinally is one thing; to set out operational steps for "how to turn off notifications / how to digital-detox / how to cultivate concentration in the AI age" is another. The latter, once written, slides into the very failure this series has repeatedly warned against—diagnosing, then dispensing a cure. The proper road of cultivating concentration—how to focus the mind undistractedly—has been addressed in this series' papers on the cultivation of concentration[^9]; this paper does not repeat it, nor stand in for it.

**⚠️ "The attention economy" and "the erosion of one-pointedness" are analytical terms, not canonical ones.** These two phrases are analytical labels coined here for diagnostic convenience; no such fixed terms exist in the treatises, and readers should not read them back as the original verdict-language of any treatise. On the scriptural side, this paper establishes only two sets of original concepts: "one-pointedness" (the nature of concentration) and "the dominant condition / the direct condition" (the rank within dependent arising). "The attention economy" and "the erosion of one-pointedness" are analytical language that scaffolds these original concepts onto a contemporary phenomenon; their diagnostic force is rooted in the former and not grounded in themselves.

## VI. Conclusion

**A précis.** Concentration is one-pointedness—gathering the mind into one point. The AI-environment, with respect to concentration, ranks as a dominant condition (either favouring or opposing), not as a direct one: the direct cause of concentration remains always the practitioner's own *samādhi*. The contemporary attention economy structurally fragments attention (capture, switch, multiply); its direction (the many) is the reverse of one-pointedness (the one), and so what it mainly applies to one-pointedness is an *opposing* external condition; yet the same condition can turn into a *favouring* guard for silence. The mechanism of concentration is unchanged ("focusing the mind undistractedly" is as it always was); what changes is only the external condition on which one-pointedness depends.

**An unfinished question.** Since "either favouring or opposing" marks out that the same condition can favour or oppose, the line dividing favouring from opposing—at just what point the force of a technology turns over from helping one gather the mind into one to helping one scatter it into many—is not a line any treatise has drawn for us. This paper points out that the line exists, and that the dominant force of the contemporary structure leans to the opposing side; but it does not draw the line fast at any fixed point for the reader. The judgement of that point falls to the practitioner's faithful discernment within a concrete situation; the line is not ours to draw.

**An open question (toward the next paper).** This paper has diagnosed the erosion of concentration's external condition in the present age, yet it still rests within the single axis of "concentration." But the *Cheng weishi lun*, in defining concentration's function, already said "its function is to be the support of wisdom"—concentration is, in the end, the support on which wisdom rests. To take this inward gate, pushed to its limit and now squeezed by modern external conditions, and reconnect it to its matrix of wisdom is what this volume's closing paper will redeem[^10]. If one-pointedness were only one, and did not open onto the wisdom that sees through, then whether its external conditions favour or oppose, it would still not be concentration in its final sense.

And that one phrase defining concentration as concentration—set in whatever age, facing whatever external condition—has never changed: **focusing the mind undistractedly**.
