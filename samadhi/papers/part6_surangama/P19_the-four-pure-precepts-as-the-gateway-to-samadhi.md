# The Gateway to Samādhi

**The Four Pure Precepts of the Śūraṅgama and "Gathering the Mind Is the Precept"**

*中文主題：入三摩地之門——楞嚴四種清淨明誨與「攝心為戒」*

---

## Abstract

This paper takes up a question that looks simple and is regularly read awry: the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* is from end to end a manual of concentration — so why does its formal teaching on concentration begin with the precepts? The common reading lifts the Four Pure Precepts of fascicle six out as a self-standing chapter on moral discipline, and reads "gathering the mind is the precept; from the precept, concentration arises" as a staircase: do the moral preparation first, then sit and cultivate concentration, the precept being merely an external front-matter to the real work. This paper rereads the whole sūtra from the side of concentration. The Śūraṅgama opens with Ānanda — ever rich in learning, yet his power-in-the-Way not yet complete — narrowly escaping Mātaṅga's snare, and earnestly requesting "the foremost expedient of the wondrous śamatha, samādhi, and dhyāna" by which the Buddhas of the ten directions came to awakening: the whole text is a request for the *method of the concentration that awakens*, and the four precepts of fascicle six sit at the threshold of that manual. The reason they form a *gateway* to samādhi lies in this: the sūtra does not merely say "keep the precepts" but asks "how is it that the gathering of the mind — that I call the precept?" The precept is defined here as **the gathering of the mind**, the drawing of the mind inward — the very same movement as concentration, which is inward abiding. The precept is therefore no step outside concentration but the first phase of the inward turn; the precept is already where concentration begins. To cultivate concentration with an ungathered mind is to steam sand hoping for rice: the ground does not form, and so no concentration can stand. When the four codes of restraint are pure and bright as ice and frost, the mind no longer takes form, scent, taste, or touch as its objects, and no māra-deed has any place to arise. Carrying forward the seed-line left by *Ethics as the Ground of Samādhi* in the volume on the precepts, this paper makes the formal entrance within the volume on concentration, and opens this volume's sixth Part, the Śūraṅgama.

**Keywords:** the Śūraṅgama (楞嚴), the Four Pure Precepts (四種清淨明誨), the four codes of restraint (四種律儀 / *saṃvara*), gathering the mind (攝心), gathering the mind is the precept (攝心為戒), the three undefiled trainings (三無漏學), the gateway to samādhi (入三摩地之門), ethics as the ground of samādhi (戒為定基), the simile of steaming sand (蒸沙喻), the manual of concentration (定學手冊), inward-gathering (收攝), samādhi (三摩地)

## I. Introduction: A Sūtra About Concentration — Why Begin with the Precepts?

A Chinese reader of the *Śūraṅgama* will, somewhere around fascicle six, meet a juncture so familiar that it is easy to read past. There the sūtra sets out the Four Pure Precepts — severing lust, killing, stealing, and major false speech[^1] — in words severe and an atmosphere austere, and it is all too easy to lift the passage out as a self-standing "chapter on the precepts": as though, in the middle of teaching concentration, the speaker had broken off to insert a stretch of moral admonition, charging the student to put his conduct in order first and only then return to the matter of sitting. Following that impression, the line "gathering the mind is the precept; from the precept, concentration arises; from concentration, wisdom is brought forth"[^5] gets read as a **staircase mounted step by step**: the precept is the first stair, concentration the second, and the precept is no more than an outside preparation that paves the way to concentration.

The reading is not exactly wrong, but it misses the most consequential sentence in the sūtra. The thesis of this paper is that, within the Śūraṅgama's framing, the precept is **not** an external prerequisite for concentration but the first movement of concentration itself. To see this one must change one's standpoint — to ask, not from the side of the precept ("why must all four be taken up together?"), but from the side of concentration: **what, precisely, does the precept do *for* concentration?**

This layer of reading is worth naming at the outset because the reader steeped in the East Asian canon tends, at three points, to slip into reading the Śūraṅgama awry. First, by treating fascicle six's Four Pure Precepts as a detachable "precept chapter," cut off from the sūtra's governing axis of concentration, as though it were a set of bodily house-rules mentioned in passing before the cultivation of concentration. Second, by taking "gathering the mind is the precept; from the precept, concentration arises; from concentration, wisdom is brought forth" straightaway as a general treatise on the three undefiled trainings, so that the two words "gathering the mind" in "gathering the mind is the precept" are slid past, the "precept" recedes into its ordinary sense of "the restraint of body and speech," and the precept and concentration fall apart into two unrelated things. Third, by running "the *Great-Crown Śūraṅgama-sūtra*" together with Kumārajīva's "*Śūraṅgama-samādhi-sūtra*," stacking the doctrines of two texts without distinction. Of these three, the first two bear directly on the heart of this paper — the precept as the gathering of the mind, as the beginning of concentration — while the third must be cleared away at the threshold. The order below follows accordingly: first distinguish the two texts, then state the thesis from the side of concentration.

Once the standpoint shifts, the first thing to see clearly is the character of the whole sūtra. The Śūraṅgama is not a treatise on the precepts, nor a general account of the three trainings; from end to end it is a sūtra about **concentration**. Its occasion is Ānanda, who by the force of his much-learning came near, on his alms-round, to falling into the snare of Mātaṅga's daughter; freed from it, Ānanda reproached himself deeply — "ever rich in learning, yet his power-in-the-Way not yet complete" — and earnestly requested "the foremost expedient of the wondrous śamatha (*śamatha*), samādhi (*samādhi*), and dhyāna (*dhyāna*)" by which the Buddhas of the ten directions came to awakening.[^2] That request is a request for the **method of the concentration that awakens**; the layered teaching that follows turns wholly upon this one question. The Four Pure Precepts of fascicle six sit precisely at the threshold of this manual of concentration — not an aside dropped along the way, but the gate that must be passed before concentration is entered. To speak of a "gateway to samādhi" is to speak of a gate not as a barrier but as a passage in: the precept-as-gate is the entrance to concentration, not a stair outside its door.

One matter must be cleared first, lest it be confused with another sūtra already treated in this volume. In the East Asian transmission the name "Śūraṅgama" belongs to two distinct texts. One is Kumārajīva's *Śūraṅgama-samādhi-sūtra* (T0642), on which the fifth Part of this volume already argued at length that "Śūraṅgama" is the *name* of a concentration — one that subsumes the various samādhis rather than transcending them.[^3] The other is the *Great-Crown Śūraṅgama-sūtra* (T0945) on which this paper draws; the two are different texts of different import. The precept treated here under the rubric "the gateway to samādhi" belongs to the latter, within the framework of its wondrous samādhi — the subsuming sense of the name in the former text is not reopened here.

This paper also lands the seed-line left by *Ethics as the Ground of Samādhi* (SĪLA-P24) in the volume on the precepts. That paper treated the Four Pure Precepts in full from the side of **the precept** — each precept paired with its simile (steaming sand and the rest), the integral taking-up of the whole set, the East Asian gradual-versus-perfect reckoning — and closed on a seed-line: that the "ethics as the ground of samādhi" opened there awaits its formal entrance in the volume on concentration.[^4] This paper is that line's landing-point. One and the same passage: the precept-volume read it for the precept's own completeness; this paper reads it for concentration's first step — for what the precept does *for* concentration. Two papers, one passage, two axes — not a repeat.

## II. The Śūraṅgama's Self-Positioning as a Manual of Concentration

To argue that the precept is "the first phase of concentration," one must first establish that the Śūraṅgama is indeed a sūtra whose pivot is concentration, and that its sequence of the three undefiled trainings is stated from the very starting-point of concentration. The sūtra's self-positioning shows in Ānanda's request after his escape:

> Ānanda, ever rich in learning, his power-in-the-Way not yet complete, earnestly requested the foremost expedient of the wondrous śamatha, samādhi, and dhyāna by which the Buddhas of the ten directions came to awakening.[^2]

This single sentence gathers up the whole purport of the sūtra. Ānanda's failing is not a want of learning but a "power-in-the-Way not yet complete" — much-learning with no concentration-power to follow it, so that the moment a situation pressed upon him he could not hold himself. What he requests is not more doctrinal study but the **first expedient of the concentration that awakens**. The four words "the foremost expedient" repay attention: concentration has an entering-gate, a first approach, and what the sūtra unfolds thereafter is just how that first gate is stepped through. To read the Śūraṅgama as a manual of concentration is no latter-day interpretive imposition but the character the sūtra declares of itself.

For that very reason, the programmatic line of fascicle six must be read within the frame of a manual of concentration if its weight is to be felt:

> You have often heard me, within my Vinaya, set forth the three decisive meanings of cultivation — namely, gathering the mind is the precept; from the precept, concentration arises; from concentration, wisdom is brought forth. These are called the three undefiled trainings.[^5]

The sequence of the three undefiled trainings — śīla, samādhi, prajñā — is the shared frame of the cultivation-path across Indian Buddhism, and in no way begins with the Śūraṅgama. But the Śūraṅgama lifts it up *here* not to discourse generally on the three trainings, but to account for **the starting-hand of concentration** — the first of the three decisive meanings is the precept, and the reason the precept stands first, as the next line makes plain, is that the precept *is* where the gathering of the mind begins. This paper takes only the "precept→concentration" stretch of the sequence; the "from concentration, wisdom is brought forth" stretch belongs to the domain of wisdom, not unfolded in this volume and left to its proper place. The three undefiled trainings are cited here not as a general list but as the head of concentration's own sequence.

The context in which this programmatic line appears is especially worth marking. The speaker does not mention the three undefiled trainings in passing while setting out some general outline of cultivation; he brings them forward as the lead-in to his answer at the very moment Ānanda has put the direct question of how to *gather the mind* — for the sentence that follows immediately is "how is it that the gathering of the mind — that I call the precept?" In other words, the three undefiled trainings are produced here as the **answer** to the question "how is the mind to be gathered inward?"; and the reason the precept stands first answers not "what virtues should a practitioner have?" but "where does one begin to gather the mind inward?" A reader who lifts the line out on its own and takes it for a general treatise on the three trainings will miss the question it answers — and what it answers, from first to last, is the starting-hand of concentration, not a moral checklist. This is the whole point of a section on "self-positioning": the Śūraṅgama does not veer off mid-course from concentration into the precepts; it answers, when asked about the entrance to concentration, with the precept. The precept is the first word the speaker gives to the question "how is the mind gathered?"

Put otherwise, from the side of concentration the first sense of the three-trainings sequence is not "morality first, meditation after," but this: the work of gathering the mind inward begins at the precept and comes to fullness at concentration. The precept stands first not because it precedes concentration in time but because it is the first phase of one and the same gathering movement. The next section establishes this directly, on the words "gathering the mind is the precept."

## III. "The Gathering of the Mind — That I Call the Precept"

The pivot of the whole paper turns on a single question-and-answer. Ānanda having heard the three decisive meanings, the speaker at once puts the question to himself and answers it, giving the definition of the precept:

> Ānanda — how is it that the gathering of the mind, that I call the precept?[^5]

Note the wording with care. The speaker does not say "how does one keep the precepts," nor "how does one restrain," but "how is it that the **gathering of the mind** — that I call the precept?" The precept is here defined directly as **the gathering of the mind**. This one difference of wording is the very watershed between this paper and the common reading.

The word "gather" (攝) must be read true. The gathering meant here is the inward-gathering of the mind — drawing the outward-scattering mind back and binding it inward — not the "gathering" of *embracing* or *converting others* (that is the sense of leading others to the Dharma), and still less the precept read in the purely external sense of "restraining behaviour." If "gathering the mind is the precept" is read as "regulating the conduct of body and speech by the rules of discipline," then the precept recedes to a place outside concentration, a moral control heterogeneous to it; but the sūtra's wording runs the other way — with the two words "gathering the mind" it lodges the precept on the very act of the mind's *drawing inward*.

And that act, the mind's drawing inward, is precisely the act of concentration. Concentration is inward abiding; every work of stillness-and-insight begins from the mind's inward-gathering. So "gathering the mind is the precept" amounts to saying: the precept and concentration are, at the point of *gathering*, **one and the same act**; what differs is only the phase of that one act — the precept is the gathering's beginning, concentration its completion. As the gathering of the mind, the precept is already inward; it is not some other matter before concentration but the first step taken on concentration's inward road.

A homely image may make the principle plain. The mind running outward, latching onto its objects, is like things scattered across a table, farther flung the more they scatter; to gather the mind is to reach out a hand and draw those scattered things back, one by one, into the palm. The act of gathering *is* "drawing in" from the very instant the fingertips touch the first object — there is no such two-part labour as "first sort the scattered things through (the precept), then set to gathering them (concentration)." The precept, as the gathering of the mind, is just this *first reach* of the hand: it does not come before the gathering, it is the gathering's first phase. The common reading takes the precept and concentration for two affairs — "sorting" and "gathering" — the first preparing the second; by the sense of "gathering the mind is the precept," precept and concentration are the first phase and the fullness of one and the same gathering movement. The moment the precept stirs, the gathering has already begun; it is simply not yet complete.

From this it may be said: **the precept is not an external prerequisite for concentration but the place where concentration begins.** The common reading takes "from the precept, concentration arises" for a succession of two heterogeneous things (first a moral precept, then a meditative concentration), so that a gap stretches between precept and concentration that has to be crossed. By the sense of "gathering the mind is the precept," there is no such gap — the precept is already concentration's first phase, and "from the precept, concentration arises" is not a passage from one thing to another but the deepening of one and the same gathering movement from its first phase toward fullness. The precept is, in this sense, already "the first of concentration."

This repositioning also changes how we understand "purity in the precepts" itself. In the common reading, purity in the precepts means conduct without blemish, fit to be praised — an already-completed state of virtue; by "gathering the mind is the precept," the true content of purity in the precepts is that the mind no longer races out toward the four object-fields (the fields grasped at in lust, killing, stealing, false speech), that it has begun to draw in and to abide, in a first way, inward. A person of bright, unblemished precept-keeping is not merely one who "has done nothing wrong" — his mind is already gathering, already standing within the threshold of concentration, only not yet deeply settled. What the Śūraṅgama means by "gathering the mind is the precept" is just that one should take it so in the very act of precept-keeping: what you are doing is not a preparatory exercise outside concentration, you are already doing the first phase of concentration's own work. This is why the speaker dares to say that the four codes of restraint are "the wondrous gate of training for entering samādhi" — the gate is no separate thing; the mind made pure by the precepts, at the place of its inward drawing-in, is right there the gate.

This is just where this paper's axis parts from *Ethics as the Ground of Samādhi*. The precept-volume asks from the side of the precept: why must the Four Pure Precepts be taken up as a whole set, not picked and chosen among? Its concern is the internal demand of the precept as an integral whole. This paper asks from the side of concentration: why does the Śūraṅgama define the precept as the gathering of the mind — why are precept and concentration, at the point of *gathering*, one and the same act? Each question has its own charge: the precept-volume treats the precept's completeness, this paper treats the precept's being the beginning of concentration. From the one line "gathering the mind is the precept," the precept-volume reads out the precept's inner structure, this paper reads out the precept's constitutive bearing on concentration. Should this one line be read awry — should "gathering the mind" be mistaken for sheer restraint of behaviour — the whole paper collapses; and so it must be established here head-on:

❌ **Misreading:** to take "the gathering of the mind" as a purely external "restraint of behaviour" — as though "gathering the mind is the precept" meant "regulating the conduct of body and speech by the rules of discipline."

The gather of "gathering the mind, that I call the precept" is the inward-gathering of the mind, a drawing inward of one source with concentration; read as control of body and speech by precept-rules, the precept recedes outside concentration and this paper's thesis cannot stand. The gatherer gathers his mind; this is of one source with concentration; the precept is the first phase of concentration.

## IV. The Four Codes of Restraint as the Gate into Samādhi

With "the precept is the gathering of the mind, and the gathering of the mind is where concentration begins" established, return to the text of fascicle six's Four Pure Precepts and one can see why the speaker calls them "the wondrous gate of training for entering samādhi." Ānanda having asked about gathering the mind, the speaker answers:

> Ānanda — you asked about gathering the mind. I will speak first of the wondrous gate of training for entering samādhi. One who seeks the bodhisattva's Way must first keep these four codes of restraint, pure and bright as ice and frost … the mind no longer takes form, scent, taste, or touch as its objects — how, then, could any māra-deed arise?[^6]

Two points call for attention here. First, the order of the four codes of restraint runs, in the Śūraṅgama, severing lust, killing, stealing, and major false speech — with lust first, an order different from the killing-stealing-lust-falsehood order common to the disciples' Vinaya.[^1] This paper marks the difference only in passing and makes no separate inquiry into the reordering; what matters is why the four precepts are the gate of concentration, not how they are ranked among themselves. Second — and this is the weightier point — the speaker binds the function of the four codes of restraint plainly to *gathering the mind*: "pure and bright as ice and frost" is the mind's purity, free of stain; and a mind so purified "no longer takes form, scent, taste, or touch as its objects," no longer running out after the six dusts.

And "no longer taking form, scent, taste, or touch as its objects" is just another way of saying the inward-gathering. The reason the four codes of restraint are a gate into concentration lies not in the mere "non-transgression" of the event-level, but in their making the mind **sever the conditions of its outward grasping** — the conditions severed, the scattering mind has nothing to lean upon, the gathering can begin to accrue, and the field of concentration can stand. So the text goes on: "how, then, could any māra-deed arise?" — the mind being gathered and pure, no longer racing outward, whatever would disturb concentration has no place to spring up from. The word "māra-deeds" here points ahead, faintly, to the fifty skandha-māras treated later in this volume — the various byways that may branch off once concentration deepens; this paper touches the word only for the single sense "where the precepts are pure, no māra-deed is born," and leaves the doctrinal map of the fifty skandha-māras to be unfolded in its proper place within this Part.[^7] It must be said plainly: this paper offers a doctrinal description (why purity in the precepts is the ground of concentration), not a clinical guide to the meditative road; the recognition and handling of byways is not within its remit, nor within the author's competence.

The juncture here, again, must be seen from the side of concentration. Lust, killing, stealing, and false speech obstruct concentration not because they are "bad" — that is a moral judgment — but because each is a cord that ties the mind fast to its objects: once the mind raises craving, or aversion, or grasping upon those fields, it is dragged by the field, running outward with no way back. What the four codes of restraint accomplish, in severing these four, is — for concentration — to **loosen these outward cords one by one**; the cords loosened, the mind unpulled, the inward-gathering can at last truly get under way. This is why the speaker says not "keep these four precepts and conduct will be upright," but that keeping them means "the mind no longer takes form, scent, taste, or touch as its objects" — his eye is on whether the mind can be drawn back, not on whether conduct conforms. The precept, here, clears the ground for the gathering; and the clearing is itself the gathering's starting-hand, not some further preparation outside it.

That the four codes of restraint are the ground of concentration the speaker puts most thoroughly in a single simile — the simile of steaming sand:

> If one cultivates dhyāna-concentration without severing lust, it is like steaming sand and gravel hoping it will become rice: through a hundred thousand kalpas it is named only hot sand. Why? Because this is not the stuff of rice; it is made of sand and gravel.[^8]

The key to this simile is not exhortation ("you must not do thus") but its pointing to a kind of **ground-level impossibility**. Steam sand however long, it will never be rice, because sand is not the stuff of rice — the material is wrong, and so the outcome simply does not form, regardless of the heat or the timing. In the same way, to cultivate concentration with an ungathered mind, though it be for a hundred thousand kalpas, yields no true concentration, because the ground does not form. The speaker pairs each of the four precepts with a simile, building up the principle of this ground layer by layer; this paper takes the steaming-sand simile alone as representative, the other three having been treated in full in the precept-volume, and does not walk through them again here.[^4]

The point is this: what the steaming-sand simile discloses is not "one ought to keep the precepts before cultivating concentration" (a moral *ought*) but "without keeping the precepts, concentration has no way whatever to accrue" (a *fact* at the level of the ground). To read steaming sand as a moral exhortation is to fall back into the old frame of the precept-outside-concentration; by this paper's axis, steaming sand speaks of the material precondition of the act of gathering — if the mind still grasps outward at the fields of lust, killing, stealing, false speech, then the act of gathering has not so much as begun, and concentration of course has no ground on which to stand. Purity in the precepts is the ground on which gathering can begin; the precept-as-gate is the entrance to concentration, not a place outside its door.

## V. The Indic Roots of the Precept-to-Concentration Sequence, and the Limits of This Paper

The Śūraṅgama's sequence — "gathering the mind is the precept; from the precept, concentration arises" — is no isolated East Asian insight; it shares a lineage with the precept-to-concentration sequence already plain in the Indic canon. The *Kimatthiya* discourse of the *Madhyama-āgama* asks "what is the point of keeping the precepts?" and answers that one who keeps the precepts comes to non-remorse, and thence, by turns, to gladness, joy, calm, and ease — "from joy comes concentration."[^9] The Pāli parallel, *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 11.1, gives the same in an eleven-link sequence: from purity in the precepts arise, in order, non-remorse, gladness, rapture, tranquillity, ease, and at last concentration.[^10] That the precept is the antecedent cause of concentration, beginning from the unremorseful purity of the precepts, is a sequence already clearly drawn in the Indic canon. This paper offers only a single point of comparison and does not unfold the eleven links again — the precept-volume has already treated this Indic sequence in detail.[^4] The point worth making is this: what the Śūraṅgama does is to *crown* this Indic-canonical precept-to-concentration sequence atop the wondrous samādhi of awakening — it does not set up a new doctrine, but, with the Mahāyāna concentration that awakens, receives and gathers up this long-descended sequence.

The thesis being clear, its limits must be marked. This study sets its own correctives at the places where doctrine extends, lest the reader push the argument too far. For this paper, five things must be stated:

❌ **"Gathering the mind" is not the purely external sense of "restraining behaviour."** This is the paper's load-bearing pivot, and bears restating: the *gather* of the Śūraṅgama's "the gathering of the mind, that I call the precept" is the inward-gathering of the mind, a drawing inward of one source with concentration; read as the regulation of body and speech by precept-rules, the precept recedes outside concentration and this paper's thesis cannot stand.

⚠️ **This paper takes only the "precept→concentration" stretch and does not unfold "concentration→wisdom."** The three undefiled trainings are a complete sequence; this paper argues only its first stretch (the precept as the beginning of concentration). The "from concentration, wisdom is brought forth" stretch belongs to the domain of wisdom, not treated in this volume and left to its proper sources. The reader should not infer from this paper's "the precept is where concentration begins" the parallel claim "concentration is where wisdom begins" — the two must each be argued in their own right and cannot be carried over wholesale.

⚠️ **The four precepts touch on the precepts of nature; this paper is a doctrinal description, not a moral admonition or a manual of practice-safety.** On the lust, killing, stealing, and false speech to which the Four Pure Precepts pertain, this paper offers only a doctrinal account of "why purity in the precepts is the ground of concentration"; it does not exhort to good or warn against ill in matters of conduct, nor provide any safe-handling instruction for the road of practice. The text must be read within its own context, not lifted out in isolation as a moral verdict.

⚠️ **The word "māra-deeds" is a lightly touched seed-line; this paper is not a clinical account of meditative ailments.** The discernment of the fifty skandha-māras has its own proper place of doctrinal unfolding; on "māra-deeds" this paper touches only the single sense "where the precepts are pure, none is born," and does not enumerate, adjudicate, or teach how to avoid them or when to seek help — this is not within the paper's scope, and the author holds no relevant qualification.

⚠️ **A note on text and wording.** This paper draws on the *Great-Crown Śūraṅgama-sūtra* (not the same text as the *Śūraṅgama-samādhi-sūtra*), and the words it uses — "samādhi" (三摩地 / 三摩提), "gathering the mind" (攝心), "codes of restraint" (律儀) — are throughout the wording of the Śūraṅgama itself; they are not to be run together with the cognate terms of other texts and other translators (such as Xuanzang's *jinglü* / *dhyāna* renderings, or the samādhi-names of other versions).

## VI. Conclusion: In Dialogue with Three Interlocutors

The thesis "gathering the mind is the precept; the precept is where concentration begins" is not without its possible objectors. Three possible challenges, and a brief reply to each, may show the field within which this paper stands.

**First, the challenge of the secularized attention-discipline.** In recent years Western voices have observed that many contemporary "mindfulness" programs have lifted attention-training out of the Buddhist context of precept and purpose, leaving a bare technique of attention that can be drilled on its own; that being so, is the precept not a dispensable add-on to concentration?[^11] This paper grants at the outset that the observation is not unfounded: decontextualized attention, as a technique, does yield a measure of short-term effect. But on the Śūraṅgama's terms one must go a step further. The line "the gathering of the mind, that I call the precept" *identifies* the precept with the very act of gathering the mind inward. To strip the precept away is not to remove an external condition and so reach concentration the faster; it is to remove the first step of the inward turn. What is then called "concentration without the precept" is, in the sūtra's image, precisely steaming sand — the mind still runs outward (the root of lust, the māra-deeds of which the text speaks), the gathering has never truly begun, and what is produced is not the stuff of concentration. The effectiveness of secular focus is effectiveness at the level of *technique*; it is not the same order of thing as the concentration the Śūraṅgama speaks of, whose body is the inward-gathering.

**Second, the challenge of the Śūraṅgama's textual provenance.** Modern scholarship has long disputed whether the *Great-Crown Śūraṅgama-sūtra* is a Chinese composite.[^12] An objector might say: the provenance being suspect, on what does its "ethics as the ground of samādhi" rest? To this the paper takes one point only: the validity of the precept-to-concentration doctrine does not hang on the textual authenticity of any one sūtra but on its being *isomorphic* with the Indic canon's precept-to-concentration sequence (the *Kimatthiya* discourse, *Aṅguttara Nikāya* 11.1, of the preceding section). That sequence is Indic in origin; even granting the Śūraṅgama to be a Chinese composite, the doctrine it carries has Indic precedent to attest it, and is not shaken by the authenticity question. As for the textual-authenticity inquiry itself, this paper does not undertake to unfold it; the precept-volume and Śūraṅgama scholarship have already treated this at length, and it is not walked through again here.

**Third: is the precept an external prerequisite for concentration, or an internal constituent?** Common sense regularly takes the precept as external preparation — make the person good first, then sit. This paper grants that, as a matter of the ordinary experience of the cultivation-sequence, there is no great harm in saying so. But the line "the gathering of the mind, that I call the precept" makes the precept **internal to** the act of gathering: the precept is not some other matter before the gathering but its first phase. The precept is already within concentration, its first step, not its outer threshold — and this is what the sūtra's own wording directly supports, not an interpretation imposed from outside.

It must be said frankly: so far as the author can see, Western scholarship has, to this day, no dedicated study centered on "the Four Pure Precepts as the gateway to samādhi" or on "the precept-to-concentration continuity of inward-gathering established by 'gathering the mind is the precept.'" This paper argues from that empty seat — for the author a fitting occasion, and for the field perhaps a small gap filled; the emptiness itself is, from another angle, sign that this line of reading has rarely been brought out head-on.

In sum, this paper's core finding has three layers. First, **in brief restatement**: the Śūraṅgama is from end to end a request for the method of the concentration that awakens, and fascicle six's Four Pure Precepts are the threshold of that manual of concentration; the reason they are a "gateway to samādhi" lies in the Śūraṅgama's defining the precept as the gathering of the mind — the precept is the inward-gathering of the mind, the same inward movement as concentration, and is concentration's first phase, so that the precept is no prerequisite outside concentration but the place where it begins. Second, **as a bearing for cultivation**: if the precept is already the first step of the inward-gathering, then keeping the precepts and cultivating concentration are not two separate labours, nor a splicing-together of moral preparation and meditative technique in sequence; in the one act of gathering, precept and concentration are the first phase and the fullness of a single matter — which also means that to seek concentration quickly by leaving the precept behind is, in principle, incoherent. Third, **the question left to what follows**: the precept being the gate into concentration, what is cultivated within the gate? Once entered, how does concentration accrue toward depth, and open toward perfect penetration? This question is just what the sixth Part of this volume takes up next — the following paper will treat the twenty-five means of perfect penetration, from the side of concentration, as the path of realization once the gate has been entered.[^13]

The precept is no stair outside concentration's door; the precept is the first drawing of the mind inward.

To gather the mind is the precept; and that is where concentration begins.
