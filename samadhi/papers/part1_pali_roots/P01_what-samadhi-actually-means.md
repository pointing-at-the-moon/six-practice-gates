# What Samādhi Actually Means
***Samādhi*, *Samatha*, *Jhāna* — How Chinese Splits, and the Abhidharma Re-gathers, a Single Indic Field**

*中文主題：三詞辨義——定／止／禪那；漢譯之裂與阿毘達磨之合*

**Abstract**

Contemporary Western practitioners meet a single English umbrella — *meditation*, with its near-relatives *mindfulness* and *concentration* — where the early Buddhist analysis deployed a precise multi-term technical field. This paper, the opening chapter of the *SAMĀDHI* volume, prises apart three of the terms that the modern vocabulary and the Chinese translation idioms alike tend to press into one: *samādhi* (the mind's gathering, understood as a *faculty* — a genus), *samatha* (calm, understood as a *method* — a path of approach), and *jhāna* (absorption, understood as an *attainment* — a species). The argument runs in two opposed directions. The Chinese Āgama layer split a single Indic field into several words used side by side: in the *Saṃyukta Āgama* (T0099) the dominant renderings are *zhèngshòu* (正受, 249 occurrences), *sānmèi* (三昧, 222), and *zhèngdìng* (正定, 118), while *shēmótā* (奢摩他, *samatha*), *pípóshènà* (毘婆舍那, *vipassanā*), and the full disyllable *chánnà* (禪那, *jhāna*) appear zero times — a decisive datum for the datability of translation vocabulary. The Abhidharma layer then ran the opposite way, gathering the scattered words back onto a single defining referent, *unification of mind upon one object* (*cittassa ekaggatā* / 心一境性), in the *Abhidharmakośa* (T1558), the *Prakaraṇapāda* (T1542), the *Cheng weishi lun* (T1585), and the *Abhidharmasamuccaya* (T1605) — yet without flattening method and species back into the genus. The paper also makes one structural reservation explicit: the word *chán* (禪) in its later *school* sense — the Chan tradition's "concentration and wisdom are not two" — does not surface self-consciously in Chinese sources before the eighth century (T2837, T2007 / T2008, T2076), and is therefore held in reserve for Volume VII; in Parts I–VI, *chán* carries only its scripture-translation sense of *jhāna* as graded absorption. The chapter closes by seeding the volume thesis for chapter two (P02): the Buddha tasted his teachers' formless attainments and *abandoned* them — the deepest *samādhi* is one in which even the *samādhi*-state cannot be grasped.

**Keywords:** *samādhi*, *samatha*, *jhāna*, *vipassanā*, *cittassa ekaggatā* (心一境性), *sānmèi* (三昧), *zhèngshòu* (正受), *sammā-samādhi* (正定), *děngchí* (等持), *jìnglǜ* (靜慮, Xuanzang's *dhyāna*), the genus / method / species distinction, *mahābhūmika* (大地法) vs. object-specific *caitta* (別境), the *Fānyì míngyì jí* (翻譯名義集, T2131), *Saṃyukta Āgama* (T0099), the Chan-sense reserve, *mindfulness ≠ concentration* (念≠定)

## I. Introduction

You arrive at a weekend retreat. Within the first hour you have collected three English words. The teacher uses *mindfulness* for what you are doing now — watching the breath, noticing when the mind wanders, coming back. Later the same teacher uses *concentration* for something that sounds like more of the same, only harder and steadier. And at lunch, in a lowered voice, someone mentions *the jhānas* — named as if they were a rare and slightly dangerous country that advanced practitioners disappear into for hours. By the end of the day you have arranged the three words on a single sliding scale: *mindfulness* is the everyday entry-level version of paying attention, *concentration* is the focused version, and *jhāna* is the deep end. One activity, three intensities.

This picture is almost universal in the contemporary Western reception of Buddhist meditation, and it is wrong in a specific and instructive way. The three words do not name three intensities of one activity. They name three *different kinds of thing*. *Samādhi* (the term behind "concentration") is a **faculty** — a capacity of mind. *Samatha* (the term sometimes left untranslated, sometimes rendered "calm" or "serenity") is a **method** — a way of working. *Jhāna* (the deep-end word) is an **attainment** — a specific, graded state. To file all three under "degrees of focus" is to confuse a capacity, a technique, and a destination, as though *muscular strength*, *weight training*, and *a personal-best lift* were three amounts of the same thing.

The modern conflation has a recognizable source. The word *mindfulness*, having travelled out of the monastery and into clinics, schools, and corporate wellness programmes, now does the work of an entire vocabulary. Pressed beneath that single signboard are at least four distinct Pāli technical terms — *sati* (mindfulness in the strict sense, the recollective awareness analyzed in the first volume of this series), *samādhi* (gathering), *samatha* (calm), and *bhāvanā* (cultivation, "meditation" in the broad sense). The compression is understandable as a pedagogical and commercial choice; it is not a defect of character in the people who teach this way. But as scholarship it cannot be followed. The first task of a volume on *samādhi* is to unstack the words.

The historical record offers an unexpectedly sharp instrument for doing so. Open the *Saṃyukta Āgama* (《雜阿含經》, T0099), the Chinese translation that scholars have long regarded as among the closest surviving witnesses to an early *Saṃyutta*-style collection, rendered by Guṇabhadra in the Liu-Song period. Search it for the words it uses when a meditator enters the states under discussion. The most frequent rendering is not the term a modern reader would predict. It is not *chándìng* (禪定) and not even *sānmèi* (三昧). It is *zhèngshòu* (正受) — 249 times. *Sānmèi* (三昧) is second, at 222; *zhèngdìng* (正定) third, at 118. And the three terms that today's Buddhist-studies vocabulary treats as standard — *shēmótā* (奢摩他, for *samatha*), *pípóshènà* (毘婆舍那, for *vipassanā*), and the full disyllabic *chánnà* (禪那, for *jhāna*) — occur, across the whole of this text, **zero times**.[^1]

That zero is the chapter's first piece of evidence, and it is more counter-intuitive than the retreat-room scene. It tells us that the polished "samatha / vipassanā / jhāna" vocabulary that Western practitioners now take as the bedrock terminology of Buddhist meditation was *not yet in standard use* when one of the oldest Chinese witnesses to the early discourses was made. That vocabulary is a later sediment. What lay beneath it, even at the level of translation, was already a field divided among several words. The modern compression of the field into "mindfulness" is the most recent layer in a much longer history of the same field being repeatedly re-cut.

This volume opens, then, by loosening a few words that have been pressed together:

- **Samādhi** (定) is a **faculty** — the mind's gathering upon a single object: undistracted, unscattered, settled.
- **Samatha** (止) is a **method** — calming as the means by which agitation is answered and the mind brought to rest; the approach through which *samādhi* is reached.
- **Jhāna** (禪那) is an **attainment** — a particular graded state among the four absorptions and the four formless attainments.

These are not three names for one thing. They are three facets of one field: the name of a capacity, the name of a method, the name of a degree. In the grammar of the Pāli analysis, *samādhi is the genus, jhāna is a species, and samatha is a method* — and a genus, a species, and a method cannot be laid out as a flat list of synonyms.[^2]

Drawing this distinction has a protective function for everything that follows. It marks the border between *sati* (念) and *samādhi* (定). The previous volume of this series gave a full functional account of *sati* as the recollective engine of practice; this volume must not, at its outset, quietly write *samādhi* back into *sati*. The two are neighbours — both appear among the seven factors of awakening, both among the five faculties — and in some discourses and some meditation objects they cooperate closely. But functionally they differ: *sati* is the ground of awareness, the bare recollective registering of what is present; *samādhi* is the inward unification built on that ground. There is an overlap zone. There is not an identity. **"Mindfulness is not concentration" (念≠定) is a red line of this volume; any reading that collapses *samādhi* back into *sati* — including the common move of the modern mindfulness movement — is one this volume declines.**

A second word must also be fenced off at the door: *chán* (禪). In the Chinese Buddhist vocabulary, this single character carries at least two non-synonymous uses. The first is the scripture-translation use, *chán* = *chánnà* (禪那) = *jhāna*: the graded-absorption sense. The second is the *school* use, the *chán* of the Chan tradition (and its Japanese descendant, which English usually calls by its Japanese name) — the "living concentration" of *concentration and wisdom are not two*, *within appearances yet free of appearances*. The first sense belongs to the Pāli-roots and Abhidharma strata that occupy the first half of this volume. The second is a downstream development in the history of Chinese Buddhism, and **this volume reserves it for Volume VII**. Until then, wherever *chán* appears, it carries only the scripture-translation sense of absorption; wherever the Chan school's distinctive claims are at issue, they are deferred.

None of this is etymological housekeeping for its own sake. It is what allows the volume's central question to stand up: when we say someone is *cultivating samādhi*, what exactly are we saying? That a *faculty* is being trained? That a *method* is being practised? That a *state* is being reached? Each has its own canonical and doctrinal grounding, and conflating them produces avoidable disputes — including the modern dispute over whether the *jhānas* are "necessary," which often turns out to be two parties using one word for three different things.

A simple image of water can close this opening. The arising of *samādhi* is usually not a matter of *adding* something to the mind but of *subtracting* agitation. When water grows still, the moon was already there at its bottom; nothing was added to produce the reflection — only the disturbance was removed. That subtraction is the first verb of everything that follows in the study of *samādhi*.[^3]

## II. The Three Pāli Terms — *Samādhi*, *Samatha*, *Jhāna*

In Pāli usage *samādhi* is not a lone word but the centre of a cluster of mutually checking terms. Its most frequently cited definition appears in the *Cūḷavedalla-sutta* (Majjhima Nikāya 44), where the layman Visākha asks the nun Dhammadinnā what *samādhi* is, and she answers with a phrase that becomes the anchor of the entire later tradition: *samādhi* is *cittassa ekaggatā* — the **one-pointedness, or unification, of the mind**.[^4] This anchor — "unification of mind upon one object" — would become the shared skeleton on which every later Abhidharma school built its definition of *samādhi* (see §IV below). It names the mind's non-scattering, non-agitation, non-running-off in relation to a single object. Note what it does and does not say: it is the mind's *unity* upon an object, not the mind's *blankness*; it is gathering, not suppression.

*Samatha* (Chinese *zhǐ* 止, or transliterated *shēmótā* 奢摩他) stands, in Pāli usage, alongside *vipassanā* (insight, 觀), the two together forming the so-called twin track of calm and insight. (The relationship of these two as full *paths* of practice is a problem this volume takes up in later chapters and in conversation with the eightfold-path material; here only the term is being fixed.) *Samatha* is not a synonym for *samādhi*. It is the **method or approach** by which *samādhi* is reached — calming as the answer to scatter and restlessness, the settling of the mind. In the Aṅguttara Nikāya's Twos, calm and insight are listed as the pair of "things that partake of true knowledge" (*vijjābhāgiya*): the one conducing to liberation of mind (*cetovimutti*), the other to liberation by wisdom (*paññāvimutti*).[^5] But *samatha* here is still a term at the level of *method*, not the state itself.

*Jhāna* (Chinese *chánnà* 禪那, or *jìnglǜ* 靜慮) is the name for *samādhi* as it is realized, under the method of *samatha*, into a **specific graded attainment** — the four *jhānas* and the four formless attainments, eight stages in all. The standard description of the four *jhānas* occurs in the *Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna-sutta* (Dīgha Nikāya 22) and is repeated at *Satipaṭṭhāna-sutta* (Majjhima Nikāya 10). In paraphrase of the canonical formula:

> Secluded from sensual pleasures and from unwholesome states, a practitioner enters and dwells in the first *jhāna*, which has applied and sustained attention (*vitakka*, *vicāra*) and the rapture and ease (*pīti*, *sukha*) born of seclusion. With the stilling of applied and sustained attention, there is inner clarity and unification of mind, and one enters and dwells in the second *jhāna*, which has the rapture and ease born of *samādhi*, without applied and sustained attention. With the fading of rapture one enters the third *jhāna*; and with the abandoning of pleasure and pain, in the purity of equanimity and mindfulness, one enters and dwells in the fourth *jhāna*.[^6]

The decisive feature of this passage is not the internal structure of each *jhāna* — that is the proper subject of chapter four (P04) of this volume — but the fact that the formula appears precisely *as the definition of right concentration* (*sammā-samādhi*). When the Buddha teaches the eighth factor of the noble eightfold path, the content of "right *samādhi*" is given directly as the four *jhānas*. Here, in a single passage, *jhāna* and *sammā-samādhi* are tied to one anchor.

The interrelation of the three terms can now be stated cleanly:

- **Samādhi** is the **genus** — the mind's capacity for non-scattered gathering upon an object as such. It can be shallow or deep, can pervade every wholesome state, and can also refer narrowly to deep unification.
- **Samatha** is the **method** — the calming approach toward *samādhi*, paired with *vipassanā* as the insight approach.
- **Jhāna** is the **species** — *samādhi*, under the method of *samatha*, realized at a determinate degree of absorption, named accordingly.

To lay the three side by side as synonyms is to press the genus, the species, and the method into one undifferentiated heap. In the history of Chinese transmission that pressing did, in fact, happen — but it happened in a particular and traceable way, which is the matter of the next section.

One methodological note closes this section. The Pāli passages cited here (MN 44, AN 2.31, the *jhāna* pericope of DN 22) have received differing degrees of critical-edition scrutiny. Throughout this volume, citations of Pāli discourses are paired with their Chinese Āgama parallels where these exist, so that the same practice can be read along both traditions' trajectories; the conventions of this dual-track citation were set out in the opening apparatus of an earlier volume and are not repeated here.[^7]

## III. How Chinese Splits — A Single Indic Field Falls into Several Words

Return to the figures with which the chapter opened. In the *Saṃyukta Āgama* (T0099, Guṇabhadra, Liu-Song), *zhèngshòu* (正受) occurs 249 times, *sānmèi* (三昧) 222 times, *zhèngdìng* (正定) 118 times; *shēmótā*, *pípóshènà*, and the full *chánnà* occur zero times.[^1] These numbers can carry a single claim: **the Chinese Āgama layer had already, at the outset, broken a single Indic field into a terrain of several words used in parallel — and the "samatha / jhāna" vocabulary stratum so familiar to later readers had not yet appeared at this level.**

Take *zhèngshòu* (正受) first. The word has long been read as an alternative rendering of *zhèngdìng* (正定, "right concentration"), but its coverage is in fact wider. It carries *both* *samādhi* (concentration itself) *and* *samāpatti* (the act of entering, the attainment of, a meditative state). This is why the four-*jhāna* passage in fascicle 17 of the *Saṃyukta Āgama* reads "at the time of the *zhèngshòu* of the first *jhāna*… at the time of the *zhèngshòu* of the fourth *jhāna*…" — not a loose translation but a precise framing of "the state of having entered the *n*th *jhāna*" as *zhèngshòu*.[^8] To read *zhèngshòu* uniformly as a synonym of *zhèngdìng* loses the entering-the-attainment half of its meaning. This is the first philological caution of the volume: *zhèngshòu* is not flatly *samādhi*.

Next, *sānmèi* (三昧). This is a transliteration — a Chinese phonetic rendering of a Prakrit form of *samādhi* — and it is the most widespread and earliest-circulating of the renderings, which is also why it is the most easily over-generalized. *Sānmèi* can serve as the proper name of a particular state (as in "the *samādhi* of emptiness," "the *samādhi* of signlessness") and equally as a general word for concentration ("to cultivate *sānmèi*," "to dwell in *sānmèi*"). Its semantic feel is closest to Pāli *samādhi* itself: it can name the general capacity of one-pointedness, and it can name a particularized meditative state.

Third, *zhèngdìng* (正定). This corresponds precisely to Pāli *sammā-samādhi*, the eighth factor of the noble eightfold path. Where Guṇabhadra uses it, he tends to use it within structural, normative enumerations — the eightfold path, the thirty-seven factors of awakening — where it occupies a fixed, regulatory slot.

What, then, does the **zero count** for *shēmótā*, *pípóshènà*, and full *chánnà* mean? It means that these three transliterated terms entered the mainstream of Chinese Buddhist vocabulary as standard technical terms *later* — roughly in step with the translation of the sectarian Abhidharma treatises and, afterward, with the formation of the Tiantai calm-and-insight (*zhǐguān* 止觀) system. When Guṇabhadra translated the *Saṃyukta Āgama*, the "samatha / vipassanā / jhāna" triad had not yet been fixed as a standard set; he worked with the earlier menu of choices — *sānmèi*, *zhèngshòu*, *zhèngdìng*, and *chán* (the single character, mostly in compounds such as *chándìng* 禪定 and *chánsī* 禪思, not the full disyllabic *chánnà* with its determinate-absorption sense).

This gives the volume a small methodological tool: **Chinese Buddhist vocabulary can be stratified and dated.** The "concentration vocabulary" a reader meets across different treatises and different eras of translation is not a set of interchangeable slices of one stable glossary; it is an accumulation of different lexical strata laid down at different historical moments. The same *samādhi* is rendered in the Āgama layer as *zhèngshòu* / *sānmèi* / *zhèngdìng*; in the Abhidharma treatise layer (the *Abhidharmakośa*, the *Cheng weishi lun*, the *Abhidharmasamuccaya*) it is gathered under *dìng* (定), *sānmódì* (三摩地), and *děngchí* (等持) and redefined under a single rubric; in the Madhyamaka and Yogācāra Mahāyāna treatise layer the twin-track vocabulary of *shēmótā* / *pípóshènà* and the perfection of *dhyāna* is added; and in the Tiantai line the comprehensive frame of *zhǐguān* (calm-and-insight) is added again. Each layer has its own history of translation and its own history of doctrine.

One further reader's safeguard belongs here. Do not rush to read the phrase *wúsuǒyǒu* (無所有, "nothing-whatever") as *wúsuǒyǒu-chù* (無所有處, the third of the four formless attainments, *ākiñcaññāyatana*), nor equate it with the third member of the three liberations (emptiness, signlessness, undirected). *Wúsuǒyǒu* occurs some 71 times in the *Saṃyukta Āgama*; some occurrences are indeed the name of the third formless attainment (a meditative degree), while others sit in the slot of the three liberations, corresponding to *appaṇihita* (rendered elsewhere as "wishless" or "undirected"). The distributive passage in fascicle 3 does string together emptiness / signlessness / nothing-whatever, but the clean, lexically uniform compound triad "emptiness / signlessness / wishless" does *not* occur as a fixed unit in T0099 — the triad appears distributively, not as a uniform lexical block. Reading such passages requires confirming, case by case, what each instance denotes.[^9]

With this stratification made plain, the next section — the convergence in the Abhidharma layer — shows its force.

## IV. How the Abhidharma Re-gathers — *Samādhi* Recollected as "Unification of Mind"

If the Chinese Āgama layer displays the *splitting* of a single Indic field, the Abhidharma treatise layer displays the opposite motion: **the many parallel words are gathered back onto one unifying, definitional referent — the unification of mind upon a single object** (*cittassa ekaggatā* / 心一境性).

The most direct evidence is in the *Abhidharmakośa* (《阿毘達磨俱舍論》, T1558, translated by Xuanzang). In fascicle 4, expounding the universal mental factors, it states that *sānmódì* (samādhi) *is* the unification of the mind upon a single object.[^10] Two things here repay a pause. First, *sānmódì* (三摩地, the *new* transliteration of *samādhi*) has been adopted as the headword term in place of the older *sānmèi* — a fruit of the new-translation drive toward precision. Second, "unification of mind upon one object" lifts the definition cleanly out of the multi-word terrain and condenses it into a single phrase. The same definition recurs in the *Cheng weishi lun* (《成唯識論》, T1585, Xuanzang), fascicle 5, which defines concentration as having, with respect to the contemplated object, the nature of focusing the mind without scattering, and serving as the support of wisdom;[^11] and in the *Abhidharmasamuccaya* (《大乘阿毘達磨集論》, T1605), fascicle 1, which defines *samādhi* as having one-pointedness of mind upon the contemplated matter as its essence, and the support of wisdom as its function.[^12]

Three treatises, three systems — the Sarvāstivāda, the Yogācāra, and the *Abhidharmasamuccaya* lineage — agree on the definitional skeleton of *samādhi*: **the mind's one-pointedness, non-scattering, focus upon its object.**

But if the matter stopped there, the real claim of the Abhidharma layer would be missed. Its truly striking force lies in its **gathering of three distinct words — "calming," "even-holding," and "unification of mind" — onto a single chain.** The strongest single piece of evidence is the definition of the faculty of concentration (*samādhindriya*) in the *Prakaraṇapāda* (《阿毘達磨品類足論》, T1542), fascicle 8:

> What is the faculty of concentration? It is, with respect to the wholesome states arising from renunciation and seclusion, the mind's abiding, even abiding, close abiding, settled abiding, non-scattering, non-distraction, *calming* (攝止), *even-holding* (等持, *samādhi*), and *unification of mind upon one object* (心一境性): this is called the faculty of concentration.[^13]

This passage lays "calming" (*shèzhǐ*), "even-holding" (*děngchí*), and "unification of mind" (*xīn-yī-jìng-xìng*) side by side as *synonyms* of the faculty of concentration — a single chain binding together terms from different lexical strata and declaring that they point to one and the same referent. **The splitting performed by Chinese translation is here gathered back, by paraphrastic chaining, in the treatise layer.**

But gathering is not flattening. Though the Abhidharma schools share a definitional skeleton for *samādhi*, they diverge on its ontological placement:

- The **Sarvāstivāda** (the *Abhidharmakośa* tradition) files *samādhi* among the *universal mental factors* (*mahābhūmika*, 大地法) — those present in every state of mind whatsoever, wholesome, unwholesome, or neutral, concentrated or scattered alike. On this reading, *samādhi* is a pervasive quality of mind, not the proper name of a meditative state.
- The **Yogācāra / Abhidharmasamuccaya line** (the *Cheng weishi lun*, the *Samuccaya*) files *samādhi* among the *object-specific mental factors* (別境, *caitta* arising only on particular objects) — present only when a specific contemplated object is before the mind, abiding only in relation to that object, not pervading every state. On this reading, *samādhi* lies much closer to the modern general sense of "focusing on an object."

That one and the same word should have its ontological status placed differently by two systems is itself a piece of evidence: **"concentration" is not a small word.** It carries enough doctrinal load in the Abhidharma analysis of mental factors that distinct schools contend over its position. That contention later enters the Chinese discourse on mind and consciousness, bifurcating into a "concentration pervades all states of mind" reading and a "concentration arises only on a meditative object" reading.

More worth noticing still: although the Abhidharma layer gathers "concentration / even-holding / unification of mind" onto one chain, it does *not* also pull the practice-discourse of "calm-and-insight" or the state-discourse of "jhāna" into that chain. In the treatise layer, three lines of exposition remain distinct: first, unification of mind is the *definition* of concentration; second, calm-and-insight is treated separately as a matter of *practice approach*; third, *jhāna* is given its own structural analysis as a determinate *degree* (its constituent factors, the five hindrances, and so on). The three narrative lines are not pressed into one.

In other words: **the treatise layer supplied a unified definition for "concentration" (the genus) while preserving "calm" (the method) and "jhāna" (the species) as independent topics.** The three-layer distinction set out in §II still holds in the Abhidharma layer — the layer simply furnished one of the three, the genus, with precise content: unification of mind upon a single object.

The chapter's opening thesis is thus shown to be two-directional. The Chinese Āgama layer *splits* the field into a terrain of parallel words; the Abhidharma treatise layer *gathers* it into a single definition, while preserving the three-layer distinction. Between §II and this section, the chapter has now laid out a complete topographical map of the vocabulary: from the three Pāli terms, to the several Chinese words, to the treatise layer's single definition that nevertheless keeps the three layers intact.

## V. Translation History and the Reserve of the Word *Chán*

Push the map one notch later, into the history of the translated names themselves.

The most concentrated single source for the provenance of the concentration-vocabulary is the *Fānyì míngyì jí* (《翻譯名義集》, T2131), compiled by the Southern Song monk Fǎyún. In treating four key terms — *sānmèi*, *sānmódì*, *chánnà*, and *shēmótā* — Fǎyún lays out the old and new renderings side by side:

- **Sānmèi** (三昧, old transliteration of *samādhi*): glossed as "well-ordered concentration," also called "right concentration" (正定), also called "right reception" (正受) — that is, the three renderings *sānmèi*, *zhèngdìng*, and *zhèngshòu* set out as synonymous alternatives.
- **Sānmódì** (三摩地, new transliteration of *samādhi*): glossed as *děngchí* (等持) — the mind's even, balanced holding-of and resting-on its object.
- **Chánnà** (禪那, transliteration of *jhāna* / *dhyāna*): glossed as *jìnglǜ* (靜慮, "quiet consideration"), with the further note, citing the *Treatise on the Great Perfection of Wisdom*, that the old rendering was *sīwéixiū* (思惟修, "cultivation by reflection") — and then the gloss "*the quiet is concentration; the consideration is wisdom*."
- **Shēmótā** (奢摩他, transliteration of *samatha*): glossed simply as *zhǐ* (止, "calm").[^14]

Read together, the four entries reveal an internal logic of the history of Chinese Buddhist vocabulary.

First, **the old translations mixed transliteration with sense-translation** — *sānmèi* (sound) used alongside *zhèngdìng* (sense) and *zhèngshòu* (sense); *chánnà* (sound) alongside *sīwéixiū* (sense). The translators preserved the Indic word's original shape with one hand while, with the other, supplying a sense-rendering for the comprehension of Chinese readers.

Second, **the new translations (the Xuanzang line) drove toward precision** — using *děngchí* (even-holding) to capture the root *sam-ā-√dhā* of *samādhi* (to hold, to place, to set in place), and *jìnglǜ* (quiet-consideration) to capture the dual sense of *dhyāna* (the quiet that is concentration; the considering that is the precising of thought). The new translations aimed to capture the morphemic structure of the Indic term in a single Chinese compound. Corpus statistics show the drive had real effect: in the *Yogācārabhūmi* (《瑜伽師地論》, T1579, Xuanzang) the term *jìnglǜ* occurs 812 times and *děngchí* 100 times; in the *Abhidharmakośa* (T1558, Xuanzang) *jìnglǜ* occurs 322 times and *děngchí* 45 times. The new-translation terms are overwhelmingly dominant across the Xuanzang corpus.

Third, **the gloss "the quiet is concentration; the consideration is wisdom"** — Fǎyún's small note on *jìnglǜ* — lays the dual sense of *dhyāna* (quiet = concentration / consideration = wisdom) side by side. This pairing already foreshadows the later tendency of Chinese Buddhism (especially Tiantai and Chan) to read "concentration and wisdom are not two" as a semantic structure internal to the very word *jhāna*. But it is only a foreshadowing. One cannot read back from it to conclude that *jhāna* carried a "concentration-and-wisdom-not-two" meaning already in the Pāli layer; that is a later overlay, and it must not be pre-dated.

Looking back from this map of translation history, the documentary support for the three layers named in §I comes into focus:

- The **genus** (*dìng* 定 / *sānmódì* / *děngchí*) has its treatise-layer gathering (the *Kośa*, the *Cheng weishi lun*, the *Samuccaya*, the *Prakaraṇapāda*).
- The **method** (*zhǐ* 止 / *shēmótā*) is an independent term, later, and not part of the mainstream Āgama layer.
- The **species** (*chánnà* 禪那 / *jìnglǜ* / *sīwéixiū*) is the name for a determinate degree of absorption.

One word remains to be handled at the close of this section: *chán* (禪).

The difficulty of *chán* is that in the Chinese Buddhist vocabulary it carries at least two non-synonymous uses. The first is the scripture-translation use, *chán* as the abbreviation of *chánnà* (禪那), denoting the determinate absorption of *jhāna* — the Pāli-roots use, and the only sense in which Parts I through IV of this volume employ the character. The second is the *school* use, *chán* as the "living concentration" of the Chan tradition: *concentration and wisdom are not two*, *within appearances yet free of appearances* — a downstream development in the history of Chinese Buddhism.

Can the two senses be merged? Within the Chan tradition's own claims they are indeed deliberately merged — the *Platform Sūtra*'s declaration that *concentration and wisdom are one substance, not two* is precisely such a merger.[^15] But the opening of *this* volume cannot take that merger as its starting point; to do so would swallow the three-layer distinction of §I whole at the first step.

More to the point, the self-conscious formation of *chán* as a *school* (the Chan tradition) can be dated. Among the documents that serve as anchors:

- The **Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkāvatāra** (《楞伽師資記》, T2837), compiled by Jìngjué around 713, surviving in Dunhuang manuscripts. This text establishes a patriarchal lineage running Guṇabhadra–Bodhidharma–Sengcan–Daoxin–Hongren–Shenxiu, and contains the formula "single-practice samādhi: the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha."
- The southern-school movement of **Shenhui** (from roughly the 730s), with its critique of the northern school, which pushed the dispute between "sudden awakening" and "gradual cultivation" onto centre stage.
- The **Platform Sūtra** (the Dunhuang recension T2007, and the later received recension T2008), composed between the late eighth and the ninth century, which raises "concentration and wisdom are not two" to the status of a self-identifying doctrinal hallmark of the Chan school.
- The **Records of the Transmission of the Lamp of the Jingde Era** (《景德傳燈錄》, T2076), completed in 1004, the comprehensive lamp-history of the Chan lineage.

That is to say: the self-conscious emergence of *chán* as a *school* is a matter of the early-to-mid eighth century; the *chán* of the Chinese Buddhist texts before that point is, in every case, the scripture-translation abbreviation of *chánnà*, carrying no sectarian claim. **This volume therefore makes a structural decision: the sectarian sense of *chán* is reserved for Volume VII, and the character *chán* in the first six Parts carries only the scripture-translation sense of determinate absorption.**

A passing cross-volume thread may be noted in the same breath. The formula "single-practice samādhi: the very mind that recollects the Buddha is the Buddha," in the *Record of the Masters and Disciples of the Laṅkāvatāra*, and the "single-practice samādhi" of the *Platform Sūtra*, are the same term unfolded from different interpretive angles — the former reading it as "the mind that recollects the Buddha is itself the Buddha," with a faint bridge to the buddha-recollection field of the first volume of this series; the latter reading it as the "living concentration" of "constantly practising the straight mind in all postures and at all times." The many-facetedness of single-practice samādhi is merely flagged here, not developed — it returns in Volume VII.

## VI. Conclusion — Three Voices in Dialogue, and the Tee-Up for P02

Having unfolded the distinction among the three terms, and having traced the two-directional movement of the vocabulary — the splitting of Chinese translation, the re-gathering of the Abhidharma — the chapter closes by setting a middle-way correction within a frame of dialogue.

Scholarship on *samādhi* offers several principal approaches worth engaging.

The first is **Richard Shankman**'s detailed distinction, in *The Experience of Samādhi*, between the *jhāna* of the Pāli Nikāyas and the *jhāna* of the commentarial tradition.[^16] This is useful for the three-term distinction of §II: it reminds the reader that *samādhi* is not univocal even *within* the Theravāda tradition — the Nikāya-layer descriptions of *jhāna* and the later commentarial refinements differ in both intensity and breadth. Where this volume parts company with the approach is on horizon: Shankman's analysis works within Theravāda materials and does not reach the Chinese *Śūraṅgama* and Tiantai horizons that later chapters of this volume must take up. (As with all attributions in this section, the specific claims here are to be verified against the source before they are firmed up in print.)

The second is the body of work by **Rupert Gethin** and **Lance Cousins**, which places *samādhi* in its structural position within the eightfold path and the threefold training (ethics, concentration, wisdom — *sīla*, *samādhi*, *paññā*).[^17] This framing supplies the strongest foundation for this series' overall architecture: the series is ordered along the threefold-training spine — *sīla* (Volume III) → *samādhi* (the present volume) → *paññā* — and that ordering is continuous with the Gethin–Cousins reading. This volume does not re-argue the structural case for the threefold training (that belongs to the higher architecture of the series); it simply registers the framing as a premise it accepts.

The third is **Robert Sharf**'s "The Rhetoric of Meditative Experience," which supplies a crucial guardrail: do *not* reify *samādhi* into an inner state that an experiencing subject "collects."[^18] Sharf's caution applies throughout this volume. The characteristic error into which the study of concentration most easily slides is to speak of "the *jhāna*-state" as a psychological object that can be entered, possessed, and displayed. In describing the four *jhānas*, the meditation objects, *samādhi* states, the sudden-versus-gradual dispute, and silent illumination and the *huatou*, this volume holds to that guardrail: it describes their doctrinal structure and historical strata, and does not turn into a register of "experiential reports of meditative states" or a "ladder of attainments."

Bringing the three cautions together, the chapter restates, before closing, three red lines of this volume:

**First, mindfulness is not concentration (念≠定).** This volume does not press *samādhi* back into *sati*. The previous volume gave a complete functional analysis of *sati*; this one treats *samādhi* from the outset as the inward unification built upon that ground. The two have an overlap zone (both appear among the seven factors of awakening, both among the five faculties), but functionally they are not reducible to each other. Any reading that talks *samādhi* back into *sati* — including the common reduction of the modern mindfulness movement — is one this volume declines.

**Second, no meditation manual.** This volume treats the doctrinal structure, historical stratification, and conceptual load of *samādhi*; it offers no operational instruction on posture, breathing, choice of object, or steps for entering absorption. Descriptions of meditative states are confined to their structural exposition (the first *jhāna*'s departure from the five hindrances, the sequential dropping-away of the *jhāna*-factors, and the like); they do not become a "how to reach the first *jhāna*" register. Instruction in actual practice belongs to a different domain — the training in the three higher disciplines — not to the task of this research volume.

**Third, no reduction of *samādhi* to mindfulness (in the movement sense).** The modern mindfulness movement compresses many technical terms beneath one signboard; this is an educational trade-off and not to be scolded. But as scholarship, this volume refuses to follow the reduction. *Samādhi*, *samatha*, *jhāna*, and *sati* are distinct technical terms across the Pāli, Abhidharma, and Chinese layers alike; they are interrelated, but they cannot substitute for one another.

The last image is left to open the next chapter.

There is a celebrated narrative in the Pāli literature. The young Siddhattha, on the day of a ploughing festival before his renunciation, spontaneously and without instruction entered the first *jhāna* in the shade of a rose-apple tree; the memory of that boyhood, untaught *samādhi* he would later use as the clue to the path of awakening (*Mahāsaccaka-sutta*, Majjhima Nikāya 36). After his renunciation, he studied in turn under Āḷāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta, mastering the formless attainments of "the sphere of nothingness" and "the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception" — and *abandoned* them, on the grounds that these deep concentrations did not lead to liberation (*Ariyapariyesanā-sutta*, Majjhima Nikāya 26).[^19]

This origin narrative is the textual base of the next chapter (P02). It draws the line of a thesis that runs through the whole volume: **the Buddhist *sammā-samādhi* is not the formless absorption of the non-Buddhist teachers; the deepest *samādhi* is one in which even the *samādhi*-state cannot be grasped.** This thesis will be recovered at the volume's end, where it is fastened to the *Heart Sūtra*'s "ultimate nirvāṇa" and "supreme awakening."

But that is later. This chapter has done its work by loosening three words that had been pressed into one: **concentration is a faculty, calm is a method, *jhāna* is a degree; Chinese translation split the field, the Abhidharma re-gathered it, and the word *chán* in its sectarian sense is held in reserve for Volume VII.** The hand that distinguishes the three terms is open; the next chapter can begin.
