# Śamatha-Vipaśyanā in the Yogācārabhūmi

***The Nine Stages of Mental Abiding: How the Yogācārabhūmi Models Concentration as a Measurable Gradient***

*中文主題：九住心——《瑜伽師地論》如何把「定」建模為一道可丈量的次第*

---

## Abstract

Most readers in the Chinese-Buddhist world meet "the nine stages of mental abiding" (九住心) for the first time inside Tsongkhapa's *Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path*, or on that famous chart of a hunter chasing an elephant that turns from black to white — and they read it, naturally enough, as a progress chart for cultivating concentration, a checklist to be worked through rung by rung. This paper argues the reverse. The source of the nine stages, the *Śrāvakabhūmi* of the *Yogācārabhūmi*, is not teaching anyone *how* to meditate; it is building a measurable doctrinal model of what "calm abiding" (止, *śamatha*) is. With three mutually independent inventories — six powers (the causes), four kinds of attention (the texture of effort), and nine stages of abiding (the rungs) — it decomposes concentration, measures it, and engineers it into a graduated path. Following one self-contained, continuous passage of fascicle 30, the paper lays out the etymological format of the nine stages, the two-fold drive-structure of the six powers and the four kinds of attention, and then closes with the division of calm and insight, the yoking of the two, and the *Bodhisattvabhūmi*'s "concentration that benefits beings" and "deliberately being reborn back in the desire-realm." What the Yogācāra does to concentration is not the previous chapter's prajñā-style *fixing of nature*, but a wholly different language's *modeling*; and the same staircase of concentration, once the bodhisattva swaps the dedication from private bliss to the welfare of beings, turns entirely Mahāyāna.

**Keywords:** the nine stages of mental abiding (九住心), calm abiding (止 / 奢摩他 / *śamatha*), insight (毘鉢舍那 / *vipaśyanā*), the *Yogācārabhūmi*, the *Śrāvakabhūmi*, the six powers (六力), the four kinds of attention (四作意), one-pointedness of mind (心一境性 / *cittaikāgratā*), the yoking of calm and insight (止觀雙運), the perfection of dhyāna (靜慮波羅蜜多), the *Bodhisattvabhūmi*, the concentration that benefits beings (饒益有情靜慮), reborn back in the desire-realm (還生欲界), modeling

## I. Where You Have Likely Met This Staircase

Many readers in the Chinese-Buddhist world meet the nine stages of mental abiding for the first time in Tsongkhapa's *Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path*, set beside that famous chart: a hunter, rope and hook in hand, pursuing an elephant whose body shades from solid black to white by degrees, nine numbered rungs ranged along the mountainside as the path spirals up from the foot. The picture looks perfectly transparent. Surely this is a progress map — the mind is a wild elephant, here is how you tame it a rung at a time, here is what to counter at which stage. And so, in many minds, the nine stages of mental abiding settle into a how-to manual for concentration, to be drilled level by level.

But follow the staircase upstream, back to its source — the *Śrāvakabhūmi* of the *Yogācārabhūmi*, in the Tang monk Xuanzang's translation — and its grammar turns out not to be imperative but definitional. The treatise asks, "What is calm abiding?" and answers itself, "It is the nine stages of mental abiding";[^1] then it sets out nine stages one by one, each written in the same etymological mould: "What is such-and-such abiding? It is … therefore it is named such-and-such abiding." This is not a sequence of orders issued to someone sitting on a cushion. It is, slot by slot, a definition of the thing called calm abiding.

So this paper must first fend off three readings that are all too easy to slide into.

The first: the nine stages of mental abiding are not a manual of meditation steps to be carried out. The treatise is using an etymological formula to define nine stages, and its subject, from first to last, is "the treatise," "the *Śrāvakabhūmi*" — never the seated you.

The second: neither do the nine stages swallow insight, nor does calm abiding stand in for wisdom. As the text below will show, the treatise itself sorts calm abiding and insight into two distinct aspects, and lets insight depend explicitly upon calm abiding — the nine stages are the territory of the calm-abiding aspect alone, and do not cross the line to do the work of the wisdom teachings.

The third: still less is the bodhisattva cultivating some recondite concentration of a kind separate from the disciples'. What the *Bodhisattvabhūmi* changes is not the technique but the dedication: on one and the same staircase, swap the dedication out for the welfare of beings, swap the bliss of the absorption for a deliberate return into the desire-realm, and the meaning of the concentration turns Mahāyāna.

All three readings miss. The remaining truth is this: the Yogācāra has taken one and the same "calm abiding" and modeled it as a nine-rung, measurable gradient of engineering; and its Mahāyāna character lands on the dedication, not on the technique.

One slippery word should be pinned in advance. The "abiding of mind," the "one mind," the "one-pointedness of mind" of which this paper speaks belong to the gathering-in sense of the concentration line — the mind's capacity to settle on a single object. They are not the "mindfulness" (念) of the fourfold establishment, the *satipaṭṭhāna*. That is a different thread, already distinguished at length in the earlier chapters of this volume on recollection and on buddha-recollection; here it is enough to flag it once. Concentration is not mindfulness, and if that one word slips, the whole line goes crooked.

Set this paper back in its place in the volume. The previous chapter watched how prajñā fixes the *nature* of concentration — prajñā as an operator, plus the direction of great compassion, transforming bare *dhyāna* into the perfection of dhyāna. This chapter watches a different tradition — the Yogācāra — *model* concentration. The same staircase of absorption is about to be measured all over again, in a wholly different language.

## II. The Bedrock the Model Rests On

The *Yogācārabhūmi* divides the territory traversed by the practitioner of yoga into seventeen grounds, named all together "the grounds of the practitioner of yoga."[^2] The material this paper draws on falls within three of them. The sixth, the Ground of the Concentrated (*Samāhitā Bhūmi*, 三摩呬多地), is the ground devoted to concentration as such, and the sentence that opens this paper — "What is calm abiding? It is the nine stages of mental abiding" — comes from it. The thirteenth, the *Śrāvakabhūmi*, is where the nine stages live in their full body. The fifteenth, the *Bodhisattvabhūmi*, carries the Mahāyāna turn treated below. All three belong to the treatise's Basic Section (本地分), not to some later appendix — a point that matters for the argument to come.

The standard Chinese version of this treatise issued from Xuanzang's translation workshop. According to the colophon, on the fifteenth day of the fifth month of the twenty-second year of Zhenguan (648 CE), Xuanzang, by imperial decree, finished translating these hundred fascicles at the Hongfu Monastery translation cloister in Chang'an.[^3] One popular error is worth clearing in passing: the workshop is often pinned to Da Ci'en Monastery, but Da Ci'en was not completed until the third year of Yonghui (652), four years after this translation was done, and so cannot have been its site. Xuanzang did later direct translation work at Da Ci'en — but that was other scripture, and the two should not be confused.

The authorship must also be settled first, because it directly governs the grammatical person of everything that follows. The East-Asian tradition titles the treatise "spoken by the bodhisattva Maitreya, transmitted by the bodhisattva Asaṅga."[^4] Modern scholarship, by contrast, largely takes the *Yogācārabhūmi* to be a stratified composite text — there are discernible stratigraphic differences between the Basic Section and the *Viniścayasaṃgrahaṇī*, and it need not have come from a single hand. This paper takes no side in that dating dispute, but for safety's sake it keeps "the *Yogācārabhūmi*," "the *Śrāvakabhūmi*," "the treatise" as its subject throughout, and never scores a passage as "thus Asaṅga personally argues." The nine stages of mental abiding are a model the treatise builds, not one author's private invention.

One last boundary of vocabulary must be drawn. This treatise is Xuanzang's seventh-century rendering; its words for the concentration line are "the perfection of dhyāna" (靜慮波羅蜜多, *dhyāna-pāramitā* — with the extra syllable), "samādhi" (三摩地), "śamatha" (奢摩他), "vipaśyanā" (毘鉢舍那). This is not the same vocabulary as the previous chapter's, drawn from Kumārajīva's fifth-century workshop, which read "the perfection of dhyāna" as 禪波羅蜜.[^5] When this paper cites the *Yogācārabhūmi* it uses Xuanzang's terms uniformly and does not let Kumārajīva's 禪波羅蜜 in; the two chapters' citations run on permanently separate tracks.

With the bedrock laid this far, we can step inside. The first move the *Śrāvakabhūmi* makes, on opening, is to cleave the thing called concentration in two:

> Further, this one-pointedness of mind is either of the calm-abiding aspect or of the insight aspect. One-pointedness of mind within the nine stages of mental abiding is named the calm-abiding aspect; one-pointedness of mind within the four modes of discernment is named the insight aspect.[^6]

Note how the cleaving is done. Calm abiding (*śamatha*) and insight (*vipaśyanā*) are not two separate trees but two aspects branching from a single root — "one-pointedness of mind" (*cittaikāgratā*, the state of the mind fixed on a single object). One-pointedness of mind, falling on the nine stages of abiding, is the calm-abiding aspect; falling on the four modes of discernment, it is the insight aspect. With this one sentence the text seals insight into the position of a mere heading: what this paper unfolds is the whole nine-rung staircase of the calm-abiding aspect, while the insight aspect is taken by name only, its content left to the wisdom chapters. Whether this self-sealed mouth holds is something we will come back to look at below.

## III. The Nine-Rung Staircase: A Definition Written as Etymology

The whole body of the nine stages sits in one continuous passage of fascicle 30; there is no need to stitch it together across fascicles. Begin with the frame sentence:

> What are the nine stages of mental abiding? There is a monk who makes the mind dwell within, dwell evenly, dwell securely, dwell near, become tamed, become pacified, become thoroughly pacified, become single-pointed, and come to settled equipoise. These are called the nine stages of mental abiding.[^7]

Inner placing, continuous placing, repeated placing, close placing, taming, pacifying, thorough pacifying, single-pointing, settled equipoise — these are the names of the nine stages and their order (in Sanskrit, given here once for reference: *cittasthāpana*, *saṃsthāpana*, *avasthāpana*, *upasthāpana*, *damana*, *śamana*, *vyupaśamana*, *ekotīkaraṇa*, *samādhāna*). The treatise then defines each in turn, always in the same sentence-shape: "What is such-and-such abiding? It is … therefore it is named such-and-such abiding." That shape is worth pausing on. It is not the imperative "you shall do thus and so," but the etymological "the meaning of such-and-such abiding is …" From start to finish, the whole passage is the treatise glossing, defining, and sorting nine stages — not barking commands at a practitioner. Once that layer is clear, every word below that *looks* like an action can be set back in its proper place.

To take the measure of this staircase, the sharpest cut is not at the head but at the tail. Look at the seam between the eighth and ninth stages:

> What is single-pointing? It is the continuous abiding of a samādhi that has exertion, has effort, and is without gap and without interruption; therefore it is named single-pointing.
>
> What is settled equipoise? Because repeated cultivation, repeated practice, and much repeated practice serve as its cause and condition, one attains a course that runs of itself without exertion and without effort … therefore it is named settled equipoise.[^10][^11]

The eighth stage, single-pointing, the treatise defines as a samādhi "having exertion, having effort" that can run on unbroken; the ninth stage, settled equipoise, it defines as one that runs of itself "without exertion, without effort." The edge of this cut falls exactly on the presence or absence of exertion and effort (加行, *prayoga*; 功用, *ābhoga*): the first eight stages all still cost effort (have effort), and only at the ninth does it stop costing effort (no effort, running of itself). What the treatise's calibration of these nine rungs measures is nothing other than this — the presence, absence, and weight of *effort*. That is the first ruler of the model.

With the anchor set there, walk the nine again from the bottom and the whole thing comes clear. The first stage, inner placing, the treatise defines as gathering back the mind that had been reaching outward after every object, and binding it within, so that it no longer scatters:

> What is inner placing? It is the gathering-in of the mind from all outward objects and fields, binding it within, making it not scatter … therefore it is named inner placing.[^8]

"Gathering-in the mind" reads like an action; but the treatise rounds the clause off at once with "therefore it is named inner placing" — it is explaining why the name "inner placing" is the name it is, not telling you to go and gather anything. The second stage, continuous placing, the treatise defines by the freshly gathered mind passing from coarse to fine and abiding continuously upon its object; the third, repeated placing, it scores as the stage at which a mind that has briefly slipped away can be noticed at once and gathered back and reset within; the fourth, close placing, it defines by repeated attention drawing the mind close to its inner object so that it does not stray outward; the fifth, taming, it scores as the stage of recognizing the danger in the marks of form, sound, smell, taste, and touch and of greed, hatred, and delusion, and so subduing the mind; the sixth, pacifying, it defines by seeing the danger in unwholesome trains of thought and the secondary afflictions and bringing the mind to rest; the seventh, thorough pacifying, it scores as the stage at which a momentary lapse of recollection, once arisen, can be expelled at once.[^9] Above these come the eighth and ninth already seen.

Note this: wherever the effortful-sounding compound verbs of Xuanzang's translation appear — "gather in," "subdue," "expel" — this paper frames them within the etymological context, "the treatise defines this stage thus," and never reads them as "here is how you should operate." The treatise is measuring the nine gradations of a staircase, and the criterion of the gradation is the fineness of the mind's settling and the texture of the effort spent. It is not an instruction sheet on how to climb.

## IV. Two Drive-Inventories: Decomposing Concentration into Measurable Parts

If the nine stages of mental abiding were only the naming of nine rungs, they would not yet amount to a "model." What makes them a model is the two mutually independent drive-inventories the treatise supplies for the nine.

The first is the "six powers." The treatise states plainly that the nine stages of mental abiding are something one "is able to accomplish by the six powers":

> Know that here, it is by the six powers that one is able to accomplish the nine stages of mental abiding: first, the power of hearing; second, the power of reflection; third, the power of recollection; fourth, the power of clear comprehension; fifth, the power of effort; sixth, the power of familiarization … and by the power of familiarization, settled equipoise is brought to completion.[^12]

The two verbs "accomplish" (成辦) and "bring to completion" (成滿) are worth holding onto — they are an output grammar: the nine stages of abiding are *produced*, *completed*, by these six powers, as their output. The treatise also maps the six powers onto the nine stages one for one: the powers of hearing and reflection accomplish the first two stages (inner placing, continuous placing); the power of recollection the third and fourth; the power of clear comprehension the fifth and sixth; the power of effort the seventh and eighth; and the power of familiarization brings the ninth stage, settled equipoise, to fullness. (The power of recollection here is recollection as a power, the holding-in-mind of the calm-abiding line — not the *satipaṭṭhāna* mindfulness flagged above.) The six powers are the "cause" column of this model.

The second inventory is the "four kinds of attention" (四作意, *manaskāra*). The treatise says again:

> Within these same nine stages of mental abiding, know that there are also four kinds of attention: first, forcibly engaged attention; second, engaged attention interrupted by gaps; third, gapless engaged attention; fourth, effortless engaged attention. In inner placing and continuous placing there is forcibly engaged attention; in repeated placing, close placing, taming, pacifying, and thorough pacifying there is engaged attention interrupted by gaps; in single-pointing there is gapless engaged attention; in settled equipoise there is effortless engaged attention. Know that these four kinds of attention, within the nine stages of mental abiding, are the calm-abiding aspect.[^13]

The four kinds of attention partition the nine as two–five–one–one: the first two stages must drive the mind forcibly (forcibly engaged); the middle five can keep it moving but still with breaks and gaps (interrupted by gaps); the eighth can already run gaplessly unbroken (gapless engaged); the ninth costs no effort at all (effortless engaged). The word "gap" (缺) here is load-bearing — fascicle 30 reads "interrupted-by-gaps / gapless," and the gap, whether or not there is any interruption, is precisely the criterion separating the middle five stages from the eighth. The curve this draws is monotonically decreasing: the texture of effort slides all the way from "forced" down to "effortless." If the six powers are the "cause" column, the four kinds of attention are the "texture of effort" column — the phenomenological calibration of the model.

Here it is worth setting the Yogācāra's procedure beside a familiar way of thinking, to see it plainly.

For one and the same process — the mind moving from scatter to settled equipoise — the treatise offers two non-overlapping explanations that converge on a single end-point: one accounts for how it is *brought about* (the six powers, as drivers); the other describes its *appearance at each rung* (the four kinds of attention, as the texture of effort). To give a single thing a redundant, independent double description, and to break it down into nameable inputs (the six powers), distinguishable modes of operation (the four kinds of attention), and an orderable output of rungs (the nine stages) — this procedure is, in its form of thought, rather like the "systematic model-building" found across the modern disciplines: not a slogan handed over, but a process structured, parted out, made measurable. What the Yogācāra is doing is measuring a single "calm abiding," engineering-fashion.

But the resemblance stops there, and the difference beneath it is fundamental. ⚠️ Systematic model-building in the modern sense aims to describe, predict, and even control an external object, and its ontology presupposes an objectively operating mechanism. The Yogācāra's "model" is not like this. Its "output" is not a manipulable product but a mind oriented toward liberation; its ontology is not an objective mechanism but the transformation and purification of mind; its purpose is not to describe the world but to deliver beings across. Different in purpose, different in ontology, different even in the object measured (one an external thing, the other one's own mind).

So the point must be put bluntly. ⚠️ "Modeling," "engineering," "parts" are throughout a *heuristic analogy*, not a *reductive* theoretical equivalence. One must not turn it around and read the nine stages as an engineering process to be operated to spec — that would slide straight back into the "meditation manual" warded off in Section III; still less may one say the treatise is doing modeling in the sense of a modern science. The word "modeling" is borrowed only to mark the Yogācāra's particular discursive fingerprint — that decompose-name-measure gesture — and to set it apart from a bare exhortation to practise. The fingerprint is real; the analogy is on loan.

## V. How Calm and Insight Conjoin, and How Concentration Turns Mahāyāna

The nine stages of mental abiding are the business of the calm-abiding side. What, then, of insight? The treatise does not leave insight outside the door, but the place it assigns insight is unmistakable: insight depends upon calm abiding.

> What are the four kinds of insight? There is a monk who, grounded upon inner calm abiding, is able, among all dharmas, to investigate correctly, to investigate thoroughly, to examine comprehensively, and to analyse comprehensively; these are called the four kinds of insight.[^14]

The four kinds of insight — correct investigation, thorough investigation, comprehensive examination, comprehensive analysis — this paper lists by name and stops there, without unfolding the long string of detail beneath them concerning the [knowledge of] all that exists and of things as they are, the three gateways, the six bases; that belongs to the territory of the wisdom teachings, and is left to this volume's wisdom chapters. One clause alone is to be lifted out here: "grounded upon inner calm abiding." The four operations of insight can arise only when "grounded upon" the inner calm abiding; the directionality runs one way — insight depends on calm abiding, not calm abiding on insight. The self-sealed mouth of Section II — calm-abiding aspect, insight aspect — holds right here: insight has its own heading, but it must stand on calm abiding's shoulders.

When, then, do calm and insight truly conjoin and operate together as equals? The treatise pins the moment fast to the very summit of the nine stages:

> Question: at what point may one say that calm abiding and insight, the two, operate conjoined and in equal measure together? … Answer: when one has attained the ninth of the nine stages of abiding, namely the concentrated state. Taking such a perfected samādhi as its ground, one cultivates higher discernment within the contemplation of dharmas. At that time … insight, pure and bright, is gathered in, made supple by calm abiding, and runs as the path of calm abiding gathers it. Just here it is named the conjoined, equal operation together of calm abiding and insight, the two.[^15]

Look at the grammatical subject of this passage: it is calm abiding. Only when the ninth stage of abiding is attained — the nine stages run to the summit — does the yoking come into question at all; and within the yoking, insight is "gathered in, made supple by calm abiding, and runs as the path of calm abiding gathers it" — insight is the party carried along by calm abiding. The yoking is not two parallel tracks; it is something that happens only after the staircase of calm abiding has reached the top. (In passing: the treatise here writes "the nine stages of abiding" and "the ninth stage of abiding" — the same referent as fascicle 30's "nine stages of mental abiding," with a small variation in wording; this paper keeps "nine stages of mental abiding" as primary, "nine stages of abiding" being the calm-abiding section's synonym.)

And once conjoined, toward what does it return? The *Viniścayasaṃgrahaṇī*, quoting the *Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra*, presses the same question and gives a clean answer:

> World-Honoured One, at what point may one say that the bodhisattva's calm abiding and insight operate conjoined together? — Good son, when one rightly contemplates one-pointedness of mind.[^16]

The conjoined operation of calm and insight returns, in the end, to the one root of "one-pointedness of mind" — the same root that, back in Section II, gave rise at once to both the calm-abiding aspect and the insight aspect. The model has come full circle, back to its trunk.

This staircase of the *Śrāvakabhūmi* is not, in itself, Mahāyāna. What earns it Mahāyāna standing is what the *Bodhisattvabhūmi* does to the same concentration.

Here a firewall must be set first. ⚠️ The *Bodhisattvabhūmi* speaks of the "nine kinds of [bodhisattva] concentration" (九種相靜慮) — nine kinds of bodhisattva concentration (intrinsic, all, difficult, all-gateway, good-person, all-kind, wish-granting, bliss-in-this-and-other-worlds, pure); these are a completely different "nine" from the *Śrāvakabhūmi*'s "nine stages of mental abiding," belonging to a different register, and their numbers must never be cross-referred or conflated.[^17] When the *Bodhisattvabhūmi*'s material is used below, it is this "nine kinds of concentration" line that is in play, not the same thing as the "nine stages of mental abiding" of the first three sections.

How does the *Bodhisattvabhūmi* re-write this concentration? Look at the first aspect of "difficult concentration":

> What is the bodhisattva's difficult concentration? … When bodhisattvas, already able to abide in the vast and excellent … concentrations, freely and of their own wish relinquish that supreme bliss of the concentrations, and out of pity for beings … deliberately and after reflection choose to be reborn back in the desire-realm — know that this is named the bodhisattva's first difficult concentration.[^18]

A bodhisattva who has, plainly, already perfected the most excellent concentration and could rest in that highest bliss of absorption, instead, out of pity for beings, "deliberately and after reflection chooses to be reborn back in the desire-realm" — actively chooses to return to the desire-realm. This act of refusal is the Xuanzang-vocabulary version of that line in the previous chapter's Kumārajīva text, "not reborn by dhyāna": one and the same refusal — having perfected the highest concentration, refusing to be carried into rebirth by it, and turning back into the sea of suffering instead — said once in each of two translation-lineages' terms. The ordinary cultivator, once a deep concentration is perfected, is carried up to the high heavens by his own karma; the bodhisattva goes deliberately against that pull, and actively re-enters the desire-realm.

Harder evidence still: the *Bodhisattvabhūmi* writes benefit-to-others directly into the *definition* of concentration:

> The bodhisattva's concentration that benefits beings is of eleven kinds … that is, bodhisattvas, grounded upon concentration, are able to draw forth benefit and welfare for beings in this and that undertaking, to act as their helpers and companions, and for those who suffer to remove suffering … all of this together is named the bodhisattva's all-concentration.[^19]

"The concentration that benefits beings" — the very content of the concentration is, directly, drawing forth benefit for beings, acting as their helper, removing their suffering, all grounded upon concentration. Concentration is no longer only an inward gathering-in; an outward benefit-to-others has grown inside its definition. And so the *Bodhisattvabhūmi*'s closing praise of the concentration chapter comes to rest on the dedication:

> Concentration of this kind is measureless and boundless, and can win for the bodhisattva the great fruit of awakening. Relying on this, the bodhisattva brings the perfection of dhyāna to fullness, and upon unsurpassed perfect awakening swiftly has realized, will realize, now realizes it.[^20]

This sentence ties the whole thing together: it is still that same staircase of concentration, the technique unchanged — but the dedication changes, from private bliss and quietude toward the welfare of beings, toward unsurpassed awakening — and the meaning of the concentration turns wholly Mahāyāna. The hardest watershed between the bodhisattva's concentration and the concentration of the two vehicles lies not in which rung is cultivated deeper, but in this dedicatory act of going against the gravity and returning by choice. The same staircase: when the dedication changes, the definition changes.

Set side by side the misreadings this section has to correct, as the boundary-stones of the middle way:

❌ Reading the nine stages of mental abiding as a manual of meditation steps. The correction: the treatise defines nine stages with an etymological sentence-shape, and its subject is the treatise, the *Śrāvakabhūmi*, not the practitioner; the effortful-sounding verbs are glosses and criteria, not operating steps.

❌ Using the nine stages to swallow insight, or putting calm abiding in wisdom's place. The correction: the text itself sorts the calm-abiding aspect from the insight aspect, insight depends plainly upon calm abiding, and the yoking is pinned to occur only after the ninth stage — calm abiding does not overstep to do wisdom's work, and wisdom is not displaced by the nine rungs.

❌ Confusing the *Bodhisattvabhūmi*'s "nine kinds of concentration" with the *Śrāvakabhūmi*'s "nine stages of mental abiding." The correction: two different "nines," of different registers, whose numbers may not be cross-referred.

⚠️ Scoring the treatise's claims as some single author's personal assertion. The correction: this paper's subject is uniformly "the treatise"; the treatise is a stratified composite text, traditionally titled "spoken by Maitreya, transmitted by Asaṅga," but the prose does not, on that basis, assign particular arguments to a single author.

## VI. Conclusion: One Mountain, Two Rulers

First, draw the core of this paper into a few sentences. The nine stages of mental abiding are not an operating checklist for cultivating concentration but a doctrinal model the *Yogācārabhūmi* builds for "how calm abiding is raised up rung by rung": with three mutually independent inventories — the six powers (the causes), the four kinds of attention (the texture of effort), and the nine stages of abiding (the rungs) — it decomposes, names, and measures concentration, modeling it into a measurable graduated gradient; and when the *Bodhisattvabhūmi* swaps the dedication out for the welfare of beings, and the bliss of absorption for a deliberate return to the desire-realm, the meaning of the same staircase turns Mahāyāna. What the Yogācāra does to concentration is not the previous chapter's prajñā-style *fixing of nature* but a wholly different language's *modeling*.

A word, in honesty, about the state of research, and a salute to several modern scholars, with a note on where this paper stands relative to theirs. Western scholarship on Yogācāra calm and insight has been lopsided. The "mundane path" of the *Śrāvakabhūmi* has Florin Deleanu's two great volumes of trilingual Sanskrit-Tibetan-Chinese critical edition;[^21] but "the nine stages of mental abiding as a self-contained pedagogical model," read on its own from the Chinese side, has almost no dedicated study — most readers meet it, secondhand, through the Tibetan *Lamrim*'s retelling, on that elephant chart. This paper brings the nine stages into the foreground in the form the *Yogācārabhūmi* itself states them, precisely to fill that particular gap — not to boast of any new invention.

Deleanu's work this paper takes as the bedrock of its method. His reading of the text as accumulated "strata," and his caution against arbitrary verdicts on dates of composition, are the philological floor this paper should honour. But his focus is the "mundane path" (the seven kinds of attention, the step-by-step ascent through the four dhyānas); the nine stages are, in his vast collation, one item among many. What this paper does is exactly to lift the nine stages out of the scaffolding of the mundane path and read them on their own — as a self-contained pedagogy that "actually survived" into later ages. What stratigraphy is most apt to underrate is precisely how coherently this pedagogy went on working afterward.

Lambert Schmithausen's reminder that the treatise is a stratified composite this paper carries throughout.[^22] Saying "transmitted by Maitreya and Asaṅga" rather than "Asaṅga's own teaching," keeping the reservations of a composite text at every turn — that caution is right; it blocks a naive single-author attribution, and blocks the backflow of the later Tibetan ascription too. But his dating method is built on tracing some "doctrinal innovation" (such as the first source of the ālaya-vijñāna); the nine stages, by contrast, are a *practical-pedagogical* innovation, not a doctrinal one — they announce no new ontology. And so the ruler of "earliest source" gets almost no grip on the nine stages. Their centrality lies not in how early their text is, but in another kind of evidence: they travelled, intact, for more than a thousand years. Philology's tools can answer "when did this idea first arise"; they cannot answer "why did this map of practice outlive every vessel that carried it" — and the latter is this paper's real question.

Alan Sponberg analysed the reception of this meditation system by Xuanzang's own lineage — the East-Asian Faxiang (Dharma-characteristics) school — whose core is the gradually deepening "Five-Level Discernment of Vijñapti-mātratā," ending in "the world is nothing but cognition."[^23] This supplies precisely the East-Asian downstream the Mahāyāna turn needs: the meditation system of the *Śrāvakabhūmi* and this treatise did not die in India; it became the living spine of a Chinese contemplative gate. But the Faxiang lens is trained on the *insight* end-point (the five-level discernment) and largely steps over the *calm abiding* (the nine stages) that must come first — it treats the nine-rung calm abiding as so much prior plumbing. And this is the mirror-image reverse of the Tibetan reception: the Tibetan tradition foregrounded the nine-rung *calm abiding*, made it into an illustrated curriculum (that elephant chart), and enshrined it as canonical. One source, and each side took half — East Asia took the insight, Tibet took the calm abiding. The asymmetry is itself an observation worth writing down, and it is exactly the trace left by this model's travels.

Speaking of travels: this is the most interesting downstream line of all. From the *Śrāvakabhūmi*'s nine stages, to Kamalaśīla's absorption of them in the *Bhāvanākrama* (*Stages of Meditation*), to Tsongkhapa's *Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path* of 1402 — the nine stages, together with the six powers, the four kinds of attention, and that hunter-chasing-elephant chart, were carried over intact into the wall-mounted curriculum every Geluk practitioner recites.[^24] Return to the elephant chart at the head of this paper: it is not the source. It is one downstream uptake left, at the Tibetan station, after this model had travelled for more than a thousand years. The "progress chart for cultivating concentration" that Chinese readers know from the *Lamrim* is, traced upstream, a definitional model the *Yogācārabhūmi* built for calm abiding.

A comparison with another tradition throws the model's character into sharper relief. The Pāli "right concentration" (*sammā-samādhi*) is defined by the four jhānas; and each jhāna is gauged by "which jhāna-factors arise and which are let go."[^25] Its scale is set on the *object* — on the field and its signs — and measured by whether a benchmark is met (access, absorption). The Yogācāra's nine stages run the other way: the scale is set on the *cognizing mind* — on attention and mental force — and measured by the texture of effort (from forced to effortless). Both traditions draw concentration as a mountain to be ascended by stages, but the Pāli measures the *sign* — what the object before the mind presents; the Yogācāra measures the *mental force* — how much effort the mind facing the object spends, and how fine it has become. The same mountain, measured by two different rulers.

Last, the two red lines, each looped back once. First: this paper, from first to last, describes the doctrinal structure the treatise states; it adjudicates no one's practice. How to sit, how to count the breath — that is the responsibility of a different genre, and this paper does not touch it. Second: calm abiding is not swallowed by insight, nor does insight seize calm abiding's place — insight depends upon calm abiding, the yoking sits at the summit, and wisdom is not replaced by nine rungs.

A very faint foreshadowing is left, undeveloped: is a concentration that simply halts at the summit, motionless, the end? That question will close only at the sealing of this volume; here it is touched with one stroke — concentration must, in the end, return to wisdom. And the very next chapter will first ask a question that comes earlier still: when concentration is cultivated to the point of entering and leaving at will, abiding without being bound, what does the bodhisattva's "non-abiding" concentration look like?

One mountain, two rulers — one measures the object, the other the mind. The gradient is a ruler, not a ladder to be climbed.
