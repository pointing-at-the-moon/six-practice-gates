# Mañjuśrī's Sword

**How Prajñā Severs the Subtle Clinging to "Dharmas" within Concentration**

*中文主題：文殊之劍——般若如何斬斷定中對「法」的微細執著*

---

## Abstract

The previous paper (P16) used prajñā to cut away the **coarse** clinging to concentration — craving the flavor of dhyāna, sinking into emptiness and stagnating in quiescence — and from there reached the verdict that *the marks of concentration are empty*. This paper takes up what was left unfinished and turns the same blade upon the finest layer of all: the subtle clinging, within and after absorption, that the meditator forms toward "dharmas" themselves. That clinging has three targets. The first is **the conceit of attainment** — the "I have gained the concentration, I have gained the path" that, at its root, is the conceit of false attainment (*abhimāna*, 增上慢), the imagining of attainment where there is none. The second is **clinging to the dharmas of the absorbed state, ending in dharma-attachment** (*dharma-rāga*, 法愛) — taking samādhi, the meditative state, and even prajñā itself to be a thing that can be obtained, owned, collected. The third is **the residue of rule-and-ritual clinging within concentration** — the hidden conceit of the precept-pure practitioner, "I am pure, others are defiled," which directly receives the subtle clinging handed forward from the *SĪLA* volume. This paper argues that this is the **second-order** blade, the one that comes after precept and concentration: the gross afflictions are first subdued by precept-keeping and merit, and what remains is only the finest residue of craving, view, and conceit, which prajñā alone can sever. The one who wields this blade is Mañjuśrī, carried by two images — "taking up the sword of wisdom, holding the shield of meditative concentration" (the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*) and the episode in the *Māyopama-samādhi-sūtra* in which Mañjuśrī takes up a sword and advances upon the Buddha. The whole paper turns on its final move — **the blade is itself empty**: the prajñā that cuts and the dharmas that are cut share one and the same emptiness; cut to the end, the blade severs even the thought that "there is a dharma to be grasped" (and so a Buddha, a prajñā, a sword). The way out is not the abandonment of cultivation, nor a license to indulge, but to cultivate without clinging to one's cultivation.

**Keywords:** subtle clinging to dharmas within concentration (定中細執), the conceit of false attainment (增上慢 / *abhimāna*), dharma-attachment (法愛 / *dharma-rāga*), clinging to rules-and-observances (戒禁取見 / *sīlabbata-parāmāsa*), Mañjuśrī's sword (文殊之劍), kill skillfully (善害), the blade is itself empty (刀自空), the no-practice of all dharmas (諸法無行 / *Sarvadharma-apravṛtti*), the raft simile (栰喻), the three fetters (三結), the monk Shèngyì (勝意比丘), the sword of wisdom and the shield of meditative concentration (智慧劍·禪定楯), prajñā (般若), samādhi (三昧)

## I. Introduction: The Coarse Cut Made, the Blade Turns to the Finest Layer

The *Sarvadharma-apravṛtti-nirdeśa* — the sūtra of the no-practice of all dharmas — has a line that, on first reading, comes close to grating:

> To be attached to the Buddha-dharma is to be far from the Buddha-dharma; because one is greedy for the unobstructed dharma, one comes round again to suffer affliction.[^1]

Set against the instincts most readers bring to the texts, the sentence is counter-intuitive. We are schooled to thirst for the dharma as for water, to hold it dearer than life, to guard and uphold the true teaching; and here is a line that calls being "attached to the Buddha-dharma" a way of being "far from the Buddha-dharma" — as though piety itself were being arraigned. Yet the line is not counselling indifference to the dharma. It is pointing at a clinging so faint, so hard to catch in oneself, that it does not stand where the five sense-desires of the world stand. It hides, rather, under the robes of "the wholesome," "the true teaching," "the high state."

That hidden clinging is the subject of this paper. The previous paper (P16) has already used the blade of prajñā to cut the **coarse** clinging to concentration — craving the flavor of dhyāna, sinking into the quiescent and empty — and from there reached the judgement that the marks of concentration are empty. P16 closed by giving notice that a subtler layer still remained for a later paper: the meditator's subtle clinging, within and after absorption, to "dharmas" themselves, to be handled with Mañjuśrī's sword of prajñā. This paper redeems that notice.

Why call it the hardest layer to catch in oneself? Because coarse clinging wears a suspect face. To crave the flavor of dhyāna is plainly *greed*; the practitioner himself can grow suspicious of it. Subtle clinging wears the clothing of the good. Having gained the concentration, one feels "I have accomplished something"; having kept the precepts, one feels "I am pure"; having seen emptiness, one feels "I have seen the path." That very "I have accomplished something" is quietly conscripted by the "I" into a new kind of possession — and it does not look like an affliction. It looks like merit.[^2] This is why cutting coarse clinging can draw on the power of "precept-keeping and merit," while cutting subtle clinging cannot be done with anything but prajñā.

Mañjuśrī (*文殊師利*) in the Mahāyāna sūtras is forever holding a sword. The sword is no weapon for subduing demons abroad; it is the icon of the *sword of wisdom* — what it cuts is ignorance, what it cuts is clinging. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, with a figure drawn from the battlefield, arrays the six perfections as the armament of a single body:

> Donning the armor of patience, taking up the sword of wisdom, holding the shield of meditative concentration, and so warding off the arrows of the afflictions — this is called inner patience.[^3]

Note the placement of *sword* against *shield* in this one line: meditative concentration is the shield, the guard; prajñā is the sword, the cut. Once concentration has held its guard, there must still be a sword to cut the subtle clinging that arises within it — and so this single line has already laid out the structure of the present paper. Three earlier volumes (*NIAN*, *DĀNA*, *SĪLA*) kept Mañjuśrī's stage reserved and unopened until exactly this point, precisely so that here, in this paper, the blade could at last be fully drawn.

The thesis of this paper can be set down in a sentence: the coarse cut made, the blade of prajñā turns to the finest layer — the meditator's subtle clinging, within concentration, to "dharmas" themselves; and the one who wields the blade is Mañjuśrī. What follows first explains why this blade is the "second-order" one (§II), then cuts in turn at three targets — the conceit of attainment (§III), the clinging to meditative dharmas that ends in dharma-attachment (§IV), and the residue of rule-and-ritual clinging within concentration (§V) — and finally returns to Mañjuśrī's sword itself, to point out the paper's deepest turn: the blade is itself empty (§VI).

## II. The Second-Order Blade: What Remains After Precept and Concentration

To see why this cut of prajñā is necessary, one has first to see clearly where it falls. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, expounding the stages of cultivation, offers a layered account: the gross and heavy afflictions are first subdued by the power of merit and precept-keeping; once they are subdued, the matter is not therefore closed, for what remains is only a finer layer of craving, view, and conceit, and prajñā then turns to treat this *view of dharmas* and *view of nirvāṇa*.[^4]

The principle holds even in ordinary life. To clean a room long left uncleared, one first carries out the bulky clutter, the obvious refuse — this is the work of precept and merit, answering to the gross afflictions. With that done, the room looks clean; but the fine dust clinging to the floor, lodged in the cracks, is still there. That takes a finer cloth and a closer eye. This cut of prajñā is exactly that very fine cloth. Its concern is not "is there any large refuse left," but "is even this satisfaction of *it is already clean* itself a new layer of dust."

This finest layer of dust has, in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, a name of its own — **dharma-attachment** (*dharma-rāga*, 法愛). Its classic definition can almost serve as the axis of this whole paper:

> Because of this attachment, the love of dharmas, he loves even the unborn-and-unceasing … this bodhisattva, within the patient acceptance of the unborn-and-unceasing dharma that is ultimate emptiness, gives rise to a loving attachment — and it turns, instead, into his affliction! Dharma-attachment is exquisite within the human-and-deva range; it is a burden at the patience of the unborn.[^5]

A point must be marked here especially: **dharma-attachment is not ordinary craving.**[^6] It is not greed for wealth, for sex, for fame, but a subtle loving attachment to the wholesome dharma, to nirvāṇa, to emptiness, to one's own attainment. The treatise sets a remarkably precise gauge — "exquisite within the human-and-deva range, a burden at the patience of the unborn": at the level of human-and-deva wholesome practice, to love the wholesome dharma is a good thing, a step forward; but the moment the practitioner would enter so extreme a realization as the patience of the unborn (*anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti*), even this love of the wholesome becomes a drag. Attachment to the wholesome is harder to sever than attachment to the unwholesome, for everyone knows the unwholesome is to be cut, while the wholesome is held to be the very thing one should guard, clutched and never let go — and so it is the last to be cut.

And precisely because the *material* of this clinging is the wholesome dharma itself, precept and concentration are powerless against it; they may even be turned to its service. The purer the precept is kept, the more the practitioner is liable to a hidden self-satisfaction in that purity; the deeper and steadier the absorption sits, the more he is liable to a sense of possession — "I have gained the concentration" — resting on that depth. To put it otherwise: precept and concentration can subdue the gross afflictions, but they cannot subdue the *clinging to precept and concentration themselves*, because that clinging is parasitic on the very accomplishment, born and grown with it. To sever it, the blade must be a contemplation that "sets up no obtainable mark of any kind," one that can see that *even this attainment has no own-nature, cannot be obtained*. This is exactly prajñā — and prajñā alone. What the previous paper called *the marks of concentration are empty*, brought down to this paper, becomes: even this "concentration I cultivated, this precept I kept, this emptiness I saw" has not one mark that can be grasped.

So the division of labor this paper sets down is clear: **precept treats the gross, concentration lays the ground, prajñā cuts this fine residue.** This also draws the line between this paper and its neighbors — each cut below must land on "the clinging that remains within and after concentration": on the conceit of attainment, on the grasping of meditative states, on the conceit of the precept-keeper. The teaching on "dharma-attachment" in the treatises is itself pitched very broadly (it reaches across all dharmas, every dharma), but this paper takes that breadth only as a *blade*; wherever the edge falls, it falls always on that one point of residue in the practitioner — and does not unfold into a general treatise on emptiness. That latter is the work of the *Prajñā* volume, not the work of this single cut.

## III. The First Target: The Conceit of Attainment — "I Have Gained the Concentration, I Have Gained the Path"

Of the three targets, the first to surface — and the easiest to show itself — is **the conceit of attainment**: taking "I have entered the concentration, I have gained the path" as a thing to display, a thing by which to confirm a self. Its doctrinal name is the **conceit of false attainment** (*abhimāna*, 增上慢).

Here too a term must be set right for the reader at the outset: the conceit of false attainment is not "pride" or "self-satisfaction" in the ordinary sense.[^7] It has a very precise structure — **imagining attainment where there is none, imagining realization where there is none**: when one has plainly not arrived, yet supposes oneself arrived, and exalts the self on that supposition. The *Mañjuśrī-paripṛcchā Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* digs the root of it all the way down:

> As for the mark of awakening, there is no slight dharma whatever that can be obtained — this is called supreme perfect awakening … if one says that one has seen awakening to exist and so has grasped its realization, know that such a one is a person of the conceit of false attainment.[^8]

The cut goes deep. What it says is not "your realization is not high enough, hence the conceit," but: so long as you still suppose "awakening" to be a *thing that can be obtained*, an *object that I can grasp in realization*, the very posture of "grasping the realization" is already the conceit of false attainment — for "there is no slight dharma whatever that can be obtained." The disease of this conceit lies not in "taking a little for enough," but in "supposing there is something to be obtained."

The *Sarvadharma-apravṛtti-nirdeśa* has a more cautionary narrative still, one that lodges the principle in a practitioner's fate. A practitioner, within concentration, thinks thus —

> And he thinks again: "I have now gained liberation within all dharmas; there is nothing further to be done; my own person has gained the path of the arhat." … and because of this doubt, when his life ends he falls into the great hell. Why? Because this person discriminated within the unborn dharma.[^9]

What deserves notice is the "why" of this fall: not that he broke a precept, not that he grew slack — he was, if anything, *within concentration*, applying himself; he falls because he "discriminated within the unborn dharma." That is to say, upon a dharma that by its nature admits no discrimination and has nothing to be obtained, he forcibly produced a discriminating mark — "I have already gained, already realized, already finished." This is a certificate that, no sooner received, one rushes about displaying; and the certificate itself becomes a new fetter.

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* observes the disease more finely still: while *entering* concentration the practitioner may yet not cling, but **after leaving it there is a "lingering scent of attachment"** — a thread of craving and self-satisfaction toward the meditative state just departed.[^10] That thread of "lingering scent" is the most ordinary and least detectable form of the conceit of attainment. It makes no noise; it only murmurs quietly at the bottom of the mind, "that was a fine sitting just now." It is exactly this thread that Mañjuśrī's sword is to cut.

## IV. The Second Target: Clinging to Meditative Dharmas and Dharma-Attachment — When Even Prajñā Becomes a Thing to Grasp

The second target lies a layer deeper than the first. The conceit of attainment clings to "I" — I have gained, I have realized; clinging to meditative dharmas clings to "dharma" — taking samādhi, the meditative state, and even prajñā itself to be a thing that can be obtained, owned, collected.

The *Mañjuśrī-paripṛcchā Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* dismantles outright the premise that "samādhi is a thing to be obtained":

> The conceivable concentration is the mark of what can be obtained; the inconceivable concentration is the mark of what cannot be obtained … the mark of all beings and the mark of the inconceivable samādhi are equal, without distinction.[^11]

Whatever is conceivable falls into "the mark of the obtainable"; the truly inconceivable concentration is "the mark of the unobtainable." The text goes so far as to set "the mark of the inconceivable samādhi" alongside "the mark of all beings" as "equal, without distinction" — meaning that even that mark of samādhi you prized as the high ground, and the mark of the ordinary being you would transcend, have, in the ultimate, no boundary between them for the mind to climb. In other words, to set up samādhi as an object "over there, waiting for me to obtain it" — that very act of positing has already mislaid samādhi.

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* draws out the mechanism of this mislaying further — its most cunning feature is that it will disguise itself as "liberation":

> He turns, instead, to cling to detachment-and-quiescence, and within the markless gives rise to a clinging … clinging where there is nothing to cling to, grasping where there is nothing to grasp … because he relies on love-and-view and clings to the wholesome dharma.[^12]

"Clinging to detachment-and-quiescence," "within the markless gives rise to a clinging" — these phrases repay attention. Having, with great difficulty, set down the marks of the world, the practitioner turns about and, upon "detachment," "quiescence," "the markless" — the very dharmas meant to treat clinging — gives rise to a new clinging: clinging to his own "non-clinging," abiding in his own "marklessness." The treatise puts it with one stroke: "clinging where there is nothing to cling to, grasping where there is nothing to grasp." It is like a man who resolves to quit bad sugar and turns, instead, addicted to the labels "sugar-free, healthy, pure" — the object of the addiction has changed; its structure has not.

This layer is dangerous precisely because it does not look like a step backward but like a step forward. A man still craving the world's pleasures at least knows, himself, that they are desire, that they are a disease; but a practitioner "clinging to the markless" appears, outwardly, to be striving, leaving defilement behind, drawing toward quiescence — even he himself takes this for the high ground of practice, and so this clinging is treasured up, instead, as a fruit of realization. In the same passage, the treatise also gives the direction of the way out: the bodhisattva who has skillful means is one who, "toward all dharmas, does not receive, does not cling." Note that it is "does not receive, does not cling," not "does not cultivate, does not realize" — he still detaches, still grows quiescent, still abides in the markless; only, toward all this, he "does not receive, does not cling," does not slip it into the pocket of "my attainment." To cultivate without receiving, to realize without clinging — this is exactly the way this blade of prajñā is used upon the meditative state.

And at the end of this road lies the **dharma-attachment** set down in the section above: the love of the wholesome dharma, of attainment, of even prajñā itself. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* says that even the Buddha, toward prajñā, "neither dwells on it nor leans upon it"; and it cites the raft simile — the raft is for crossing the river; the river crossed, one should leave the raft at the bank, for no one walks on carrying the raft.[^13] The dharma is the raft for crossing the river; samādhi and prajñā are both rafts; to carry the raft and not let go is dharma-attachment. So the point of this section is not "do not cultivate prajñā" — quite the reverse: it is to cultivate prajñā until even the thought "I possess prajñā" is itself carried across and let go. Attachment to the wholesome, harder than attachment to the unwholesome — the last thing cut is this one thought.[^14]

## V. The Third Target: The Residue of Rule-and-Ritual Clinging within Concentration — "I Am Pure, Others Defiled"

The third target is an explicit handoff between this volume and the preceding one, the *SĪLA* volume. In treating precept-keeping, the *SĪLA* volume already pointed out that there remain, within the keeping of precepts, various exceedingly subtle clingings — the most stubborn of which is the conceit of the precept-pure, "I am pure, others are defiled"; and the *SĪLA* volume stated plainly that the fine treatment of this layer must await the *SAMĀDHI* gate, to be cut by the prajñā within concentration. This paper directly receives that consignment: **precept treats its gross form; the prajñā within concentration treats this fine form.**

The doctrinal name of this clinging is **clinging to rules-and-observances** (*sīlabbata-parāmāsa*, 戒禁取見).[^15] It means mistaking "the keeping of precepts, the keeping of some observance," in itself, for the sufficient condition of liberation. In the early teaching it is one of the *three fetters* — identity-view, clinging to rules-and-observances, and doubt — the three fetters cut through which, one is then called a stream-enterer (*sotāpanna*).[^16] In its residual form upon the meditator, it becomes the conceit of precept-keeping: a subtle conceit arising from one's own precept-purity, and, born of it, a contempt for those who break the precepts.

The *Sarvadharma-apravṛtti-nirdeśa* shapes a figure of remarkable instructive force — the monk Shèngyì (勝意, "lofty intent"). In matters of cultivation and realization he is all but beyond reproach:

> The monk Shèngyì guarded and upheld the precepts, gained the four dhyānas and the four formless attainments, and practiced the twelve ascetic practices … toward keeping the precepts he produced a thought of gain, and toward breaking them a thought of obstruction.[^17]

The four dhyānas and four formless attainments complete, the precepts upheld, the twelve ascetic practices (*dhutaguṇa*) lacking in nothing — if one reckons by "provisions," he is better furnished than the great majority. The trouble is in the last clause: "toward keeping the precepts he produced a thought of gain, and toward breaking them a thought of obstruction." He counted "keeping the precepts" as a profit to be inventoried, and saw "breaking them" as an obstacle to be guarded against; keeping and breaking became, in his mind, a ledger to be discriminated and calculated. The sūtra then passes a verdict of great weight:

> If a person discriminates, "this is keeping the precepts, this is breaking them," and, mad with precept-keeping, holds others in contempt — that person has no awakening, nor any Buddha-dharma, but only sets himself up, dwelling within a view of something-to-be-obtained.[^18]

"Mad with precept-keeping, holds others in contempt" — this is the whole face of "I am pure, others defiled." Its irony is this: a man exceedingly clean, despising others for being dirty because he is clean, finds that the contempt is itself already a kind of dirt — and a dirt harder to wash, because it wears the clothing of "purity." The verdict of the sūtra comes down so heavily — "that person has no awakening, nor any Buddha-dharma" — not because precept-keeping, the ascetic practices, the gaining of concentration are themselves of no use, but because, once all of this is conscripted by "madness for precept-keeping" and "contempt for others," the practitioner has only "set himself up, dwelling within a view of something-to-be-obtained," nailing a whole body of merit fast to a view of "I have gained, I am purer than he." The greater the merit, the deeper that view is nailed.

The monk Shèngyì is in fact the three targets of this paper compressed into a single person: the four dhyānas and four formless attainments complete is the soil of the conceit of attainment (§III); guarding the precepts and the ascetic practices, yet producing a thought of gain toward precept-keeping, is the residue of rule-and-ritual clinging (this section); and forcing upon the unborn dharma a discrimination of "I am pure, others defiled, this keeps, that breaks" is dharma-clinging (§IV). Three diseases gathered in one man — which shows that these three layers of clinging are not in fact three separate things, but three fine branches off one root, and the root is that "something-to-be-obtained" self. For just this reason, what cuts them can only be one and the same blade.

This is also where the division of labor between this volume and the preceding *SĪLA* volume comes to rest. The *SĪLA* volume sets up the precept and guards it, and exposes that there remain, within precept-keeping, various subtle clingings — the most stubborn being this conceit of precept-keeping and the conceit of "I am pure, others defiled"; but the *SĪLA* volume stated plainly that the fine treatment of this layer is beyond what precept can finish, and must await the *SAMĀDHI* gate, to be cut by the prajñā within concentration. Section V here directly receives that consignment. The logic between them must be made plain: **the precept volume sets up the precept; the concentration volume cuts the clinging.** The precept is not negated — Shèngyì's disease lies precisely *not* in his keeping the precepts, but in his "grasping precept-keeping as a profit to be flaunted, grasping precept-breaking as an obstacle to be despised." What this blade of prajñā cuts is that ledger that calculates keeping and breaking, not the precept itself; and after the cut, the precept is kept just as before — only there is no longer an "I" that exalts itself for keeping it.

It is within just this context that the *Sarvadharma-apravṛtti-nirdeśa* speaks its most violent line — words of non-duality of the order of "the very nature of craving is the nature of the Buddha-dharma." This line is exceedingly liable to misreading, and must be read tightly to its context.[^19] What it breaks is the grasping-and-rejecting clinging to dharmas in the pair "pure / defiled," "keeping precepts / breaking precepts" — that is, Shèngyì's ledger of "keeping is profit, breaking is obstacle." It says: when you grasp "the pure" as something to be coveted and grasp "the defiled" as something to be refused, that very structure of grasping-and-rejecting is the deeper bond; ultimately, there is no own-natured "pure dharma" to covet, no own-natured "defiled dharma" to refuse. **This is in no way an opening of the door to indulgence, and in no way a saying that the precepts may be abandoned.** Quite the reverse — what it cuts is exactly Shèngyì's "conceit of precept-keeping"; to read the line as "one may now indulge" is to take a blade meant for the conceit of self and turn it, in the wrong direction entirely, to hack at the root of the precepts. Toward this line, the only safe reading is to let it stop at "cutting the conceit of *I am pure, others defiled*," neither citing it in isolation nor extending it a single step further.

## VI. Mañjuśrī's Sword: Kill Skillfully, for All Arises from Mind — and the Blade Is Itself Empty

The three targets made plain, the paper can return to the sword itself. What best speaks the character of this sword to the bottom is a startling episode in the *Māyopama-samādhi-sūtra*.

In that sūtra, five hundred bodhisattvas, the knowledge of their former lives opening, recall the deeds of transgression they had done in past lives, and, hearts giving way to remorse and dread, cannot rest at peace in the dharma. To break this clinging — "guilt can be obtained, guilt is truly real" — Mañjuśrī does a frightful thing:

> Mañjuśrī thereupon rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, took a sword in his right hand, and strode to where the Buddha was. The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Stop! Stop! Commit no transgression — you should kill skillfully. Why so? Because all arises from mind, and from mind the killing is born."[^20]

Mañjuśrī advancing upon the Buddha with a sword is one of the boldest images in the Mahāyāna sūtras. And the Buddha's response is two utterances. The first, "Stop!", is a barring; the second, "you should kill skillfully," is a permitting — only that the direction of "killing" is wrenched around. The hinge is the reason the Buddha gives: "all arises from mind, and from mind the killing is born."

Here it must be declared to the reader, heavily: **"kill skillfully" is in no way literal killing, and "the sword" in no way an incitement to violence.**[^21] The Buddha at that very moment turns the "sword" into the "mind-knife," the "wisdom-knife" — what is to be cut is the clinging in the hearts of those five hundred bodhisattvas, "I have transgression, guilt is truly real, guilt cannot be dissolved." Since guilt "all arises from mind," then what cuts it can only be the mind, the knife of prajñā upon the mind. The sūtra at once goes on: at these words the five hundred bodhisattvas penetrate to "there is no guilt" — not that transgression is permitted, but that they awaken to the mark of guilt as empty in its very nature, with nothing to be grasped, and so gain the patience of the unborn. The drama of Mañjuśrī holding the sword settles, at this point, into a precise doctrinal operation: with the blade of prajñā, to cut the thought that "grasps dharmas (and 'guilt,' 'realization' are dharmas too) as truly real."

Modern readers have said various things about this sword. One has named, with great precision, a trap of the cultivating life — the "I" will conscript the accomplishments of practice, the states reached within concentration, into a self-confirmation of "I have arrived somewhere," and the higher the accomplishment the more it feeds this hidden sense of possession.[^22] The observation is correct, and this paper is glad to take it up; but a sentence must be added: that is to see through the self's game *on the psychological plane*, and Mañjuśrī's sword goes a step further, severing the root *on the ontological-soteriological plane* — not "I have seen through that there is an I playing games," but rather, fundamentally, "there is no self to be conscripted, no dharma to be collected"; the cutting of dharma-attachment is cut at the unborn. Another has judged "clinging to emptiness," "attachment to dharmas," to be among the hardest views the system has to treat — one who grasps "emptiness," even the Buddhas can scarcely deliver.[^23] The judgement is exactly right; only it is most often phrased as a philosophical caution to be carefully heeded (a "view" one ought to avoid). Mañjuśrī's sword differs in that it is not merely a caution but a demonstration on the spot — and the manner of the demonstration is precisely the deepest, and the last, turn of this paper.

This turn comes at the close of the *Māyopama-samādhi-sūtra*:

> Then the great holy Buddha took the wisdom-knife in his hand and cut the root of birth-and-death … the Tathāgata, the True One, too is like the sharp sword; Mañjuśrī thus, and Śāriputra thus; beings of no-origin thus; and all dharmas thus.[^24]

Look closely at this roster. That the "dharmas" cut are "thus" (empty) is no surprise; but the sūtra puts "the Tathāgata," "Mañjuśrī," "Śāriputra," "beings" all into it — the sword that cuts (Tathāgata, Mañjuśrī) and the dharmas that are cut share **one and the same "thusness," one and the same emptiness.** This is **the blade itself is empty**. Cut to the last, the sword severs even the thought "there is a sword that cuts," "there is a prajñā that can cut," "there is a Mañjuśrī who holds the sword"; otherwise the practitioner has merely exchanged "clinging to self" and "clinging to dharmas" for "I have a prajñā with which to cut the clinging to dharmas" — which is only a more refined layer of clinging. A man who searches the whole world and at last finds the sword that cuts through everything discovers, in the end, that this sword too has no smith who forged it and no iron it cannot cut — and the last thing it must cut is the mark of its own being "a sword that can be grasped."

It must be stressed that this "the blade is itself empty" is not some further mystery outside this paper, but the necessary capstone of the three cuts already made. To cut the conceit of attainment is to cut the "I" of "I have gained the concentration"; to cut the clinging to meditative dharmas is to cut the "dharma" of "the concentration can be obtained"; to cut the residue of rule-and-ritual clinging is to cut the "discrimination" of "I am pure, others defiled." Yet so long as there remains a "prajñā that can cut these three" standing there, the "I possess prajñā, I am able to cut" instantly becomes a fourth and most hidden layer of clinging — harder to detect than the first three, because it is exactly the support the practitioner takes to be most ultimate, most trustworthy. The reason dharma-attachment "is a burden at the patience of the unborn" rests just here: the practitioner would carry, and not let go, even the very raft of prajñā that ferried him to the unborn. So the true sharpness of this sword lies not in its having cut away the myriad dharmas, but in its turning, at the last, upon itself, to cut away the thought "there is a sword that cuts away the myriad dharmas." The cutter empty, the cut empty, the act of cutting itself unobtainable — only at this point are the three targets cut clean; otherwise one has merely moved the clinging to a higher and harder-to-find seat.

This and the previous paper's "the marks of concentration are empty, and even emptiness cannot be obtained" are two vertebrae of one and the same spine: the previous paper cut the coarse, this paper cuts the fine; the previous paper landed on "emptiness," this paper on "the sword and dharma-attachment." And the way out of both papers is wholly the same — **neither abandons cultivation.** What the *Sarvadharma-apravṛtti-nirdeśa* — the no-practice of all dharmas — negates is the four reified marks into which "seeing, cutting, realizing, cultivating" are grasped, not cultivation itself.[^25] As the previous paper held "this is not the abandonment of concentration," this paper's conclusion is "this is neither the abandonment of cultivation nor a license to indulge": what the sword cuts is the thought "I cling to my cultivation," and what it leaves is "to cultivate without clinging to one's cultivation."

With this, Mañjuśrī's word is spent — the sword drawn, it cuts the last thought of clinging, within concentration, to dharmas. Yet the sword is still an "act," still a "cutting"; Mañjuśrī's word stands, and there is a stillness more thoroughgoing still, the silence after this very blade has been laid down — and that is what the next paper (P18) takes a step further to speak, which this paper holds in reserve. And looking further off, the concentration of this volume must, in the end, return into the very prajñā that cuts it: concentration is not the terminus; prajñā is.[^26]

Cut until there is no sword to grasp; where dharma-attachment is severed, the mark of concentration is of itself empty. In a single line:

> **The sword of wisdom, itself empty, severs the love of dharmas.**
