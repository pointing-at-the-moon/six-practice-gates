# Vimalakīrti on Sitting

**The One-Practice Samādhi and the Rendering-Markless of Concentration**

*中文主題：維摩詰宴坐——定的無相化與一行三昧*

---

## Abstract

Prajñā's deconstruction of concentration reaches its end in this paper. The coarse clinging — craving the flavor of dhyāna, sinking into emptiness — was cut in the first paper of this Part (P16); the subtle dharma-attachment within absorption was severed by Mañjuśrī's blade of prajñā in the second (P17); what remains is the last step, in which concentration lets go even of its own **mark of sitting** and its own **speech**. This paper turns on the episode, in the *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa*, in which Vimalakīrti rebukes Śāriputra over his meditative sitting — "this sitting is not necessarily what makes sitting-in-meditation; one who sits in meditation does not manifest body or mind within the three realms" — to argue that concentration lies not in the posture, not in the marks of entering and exiting, but in a markless mind. It then draws on the three commentators collected by Sengzhao (Kumārajīva, Sengzhao, Daosheng) to distinguish "the disciple, his mind hidden yet his body still showing" from "the dharma-body, form and spirit alike extinguished"; takes the One-Practice Samādhi of the *Mañjuśrī Prajñāpāramitā* to show that concentration's object turns from a reified thing into the markless **single mark of the dharma-realm**; takes the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s "sees neither the near shore nor the far shore, no marks of entering or exiting" to conclude that concentration is no longer a state one enters and then leaves; and ends at the apex of Vimalakīrti's silence — Mañjuśrī speaks the wordless, Vimalakīrti enacts the wordless in silence, and five thousand bodhisattvas there attain the forbearance of the unborn dharmas. The paper's conclusion: the deepest reach of concentration is prajñā's marklessness, and the consummation of that marklessness is a silence. This seals the three-paper arc of "Prajñā and Concentration" (flavor-and-mark, dharma-attachment, the markless-and-wordless), and hands the baton on to the Śūraṅgama-samādhi.

**Keywords:** meditative sitting (宴坐 / *pratisaṃlayana*), the One-Practice Samādhi (一行三昧 / *ekavyūha-samādhi*), the single mark of the dharma-realm (法界一相), ultimate emptiness (畢竟空 / *atyanta-śūnyatā*), Vimalakīrti (維摩詰), entering the Dharma-gate of non-duality (入不二法門), silence (默然 / *tūṣṇīṃbhāva*), the rendering-markless of concentration (定的無相化), the forbearance of the unborn dharmas (無生法忍 / *anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti*), prajñā's deconstruction (般若解構), the posture of concentration (定的姿態), Mañjuśrī (文殊師利)

## I. Introduction: When "Sitting" Becomes the Measure of Concentration

In the previous paper, Mañjuśrī took up the sword of prajñā to sever the subtle dharma-attachment within concentration, and that blade, cut to the end, voided even itself — the cutting and the cut returning alike to emptiness. Mañjuśrī's word still hangs in the air; Vimalakīrti's silence is the step beyond. This paper takes up from there and closes the arc of the three papers on "Prajñā and Concentration." The first cut the coarse clinging (craving the flavor of concentration, stagnating in emptiness); the second turned Mañjuśrī's sword on the fine clinging (dharma-attachment within concentration, the blade itself empty); this one takes the last step, bringing prajñā's deconstruction to bear upon **concentration's own posture** and **concentration's own speech.**

For a reader who has put in hours in a meditation hall, or settled body and mind amid the contemporary wave of sitting practice, nothing comes more naturally than to measure "concentration" by "sitting": how long one sits, how steadily, whether the body can stay unmoving and the mind unstirred for the length of a single stick of incense. "Concentration" quietly becomes a measurable accomplishment, and that "self who can sit long" sits ever more solidly within the reckoning. This is the most familiar — and the least guarded-against — of misrecognitions: mistaking a settling of the body for the very substance of concentration. What Vimalakīrti seizes is exactly this measuring-rod that takes sitting as its gauge. One thing must be said at the outset: he is not denying meditative sitting, and this paper, from first line to last, does not argue "do not sit." What he dismantles is only the single thought that *takes the posture of sitting to be concentration itself.*

Bound up with this is a second misrecognition: imagining "concentration" as a special state one can "go into and then come out of" — as though there were a threshold for entering it and a moment for leaving it, as though concentration were another room one could lodge in, and practice were the repeated entering and exiting of that room. This misrecognition is finer than the first, but it nails concentration to a "mark" just the same: entering has its mark, exiting has its mark, attaining has its time, ceasing has its time. The latter half of this paper (§V) takes up the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* to dismantle this "two shores" mark; here it is enough to flag it, so that the reader holds it in view — "concentration as the self that sits long" and "concentration as a state one can enter and leave" are two faces of one and the same grasping, shown once in the body and once in the mind.

This step must also be kept clear of the previous paper's disease of "sinking into emptiness," or the two are very easily confused. Stagnating in emptiness, craving the flavor of quiet concentration, was the wrong road P16 diagnosed — a falling upon the mark of "emptiness" and getting stuck there. What this paper calls **the rendering-markless of concentration** is precisely prajñā's cure for that disease, not the disease itself. The two look alike and run opposite: sinking into emptiness takes an "emptiness-mark" for its resting place; the rendering-markless lets go even of that "emptiness-mark," that "concentration-mark," and seizes neither. So the rendering-markless of concentration does not tell a person to stop sitting or stop cultivating. It is "to sit without grasping the sitting, to speak while knowing that speech is not the last word" — in the deepest abiding, even the thought "I am now in concentration" quietly loosens, and yet the sitting goes on as before, the cultivation as before.

The thesis of this paper can be gathered in a sentence: the terminus of prajñā's deconstruction is that concentration lets go even of its own mark of sitting and its own speech — meditative sitting is not the sitting (it returns to the markless); entering non-duality is not a saying (it returns to the wordless); the deepest reach of concentration is prajñā's marklessness, and samādhi is no longer a state one "enters and then exits" but the immediacy of the single mark of the dharma-realm. Vimalakīrti's opening line already holds the whole:

> Śāriputra — this sitting is not necessarily what makes sitting-in-meditation.[^1]

What follows first uses this line to redefine "meditative sitting" (§II, the paper's pivot); then takes the three classical commentators to distinguish the disciple's sitting from the dharma-body's sitting (§III); then takes the One-Practice Samādhi to show the markless turn of concentration's object (§IV); then takes the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* to make good "no marks of entering or exiting" (§V); and ends at the apex of Vimalakīrti's silence (§VI, the paper's climax), sealing this Part and handing it on to the Śūraṅgama.

## II. Redefining Meditative Sitting: Concentration Does Not Lie in the Posture

Vimalakīrti's rebuke of Śāriputra is the pivot of this paper. Śāriputra is sitting in meditation in the grove; Vimalakīrti comes upon him, takes him to task, and lays out a sixfold "that is sitting in meditation":

> Śāriputra — this sitting is not necessarily what makes sitting-in-meditation. For one who sits in meditation: not to manifest body or mind within the three realms — that is sitting in meditation; not to rise from the attainment of cessation and yet to display the bearings of conduct — that is sitting in meditation; not to forsake the dharma and yet to appear amid the affairs of ordinary beings — that is sitting in meditation; for the mind to dwell neither within nor without — that is sitting in meditation; to be unmoved amid all views and yet to cultivate the thirty-seven factors of awakening — that is sitting in meditation; to enter nirvāṇa without cutting off the afflictions — that is sitting in meditation.[^1]

"Meditative sitting" (宴坐, also written 燕坐, *pratisaṃlayana*) means, in the first instance, secluded quiet contemplation, sitting at ease — the standard posture of the disciple cultivating concentration, and precisely what Śāriputra is doing in the grove. Vimalakīrti arrives and rebukes him, and his edge falls not on the act of "sitting" but on the three words "that is sitting in meditation" — what he seizes is the *mark of sitting*: he pries the term "meditative sitting" loose from "a way of sitting" and re-lodges it in the markless mind of "not manifesting body or mind within the three realms." The body is what sits; but the true meditative sitting comes to rest in "not manifesting one's body and mind within the three realms" — body and mind alike not displaying, to the three realms, any position that could be grasped or set up. In a word: concentration lies not in the posture but in a markless mind. This is the first load-bearing image of the paper, and its pivot. A reader who takes the six clauses for "six premium grades of sitting" has missed the whole of it; Vimalakīrti's syntax does the reverse — with each "that is sitting in meditation," he strips one more mark off the sitting.

The six clauses should be read in order. "Not manifesting body or mind within the three realms" is the master clause, stripping the mark of "there being some place where body and mind can be shown." "Not to rise from the attainment of cessation and yet to display the bearings of conduct" strips the mark of "concentration and motion being walled apart": when the disciple enters the attainment of cessation (*nirodha-samāpatti*), body and mind are alike stilled, and he cannot at the same time bring forth the bearings of walking, standing, sitting, lying — concentration and motion stand as two separate things; Vimalakīrti's meditative sitting need not leave the attainment of cessation in order to display the bearings of conduct, and the still and the moving are no longer set apart into two stretches of time. "The mind dwells neither within nor without" strips the mark of "concentration having some place to dwell" — neither gathered inward into an interior outpost nor reaching outward to an exterior object; concentration has no location to settle in. "To be unmoved amid all views and yet to cultivate the thirty-seven factors of awakening" strips the mark of "concentration requiring the suspension of all activity before it is concentration": amid every kind of view one remains as-is, unmoved, while the thirty-seven factors of awakening are cultivated just as before — being unmoved is not a halt, the markless is not a blank. Read down, layer by layer, the six clauses are a single knife shaving the mark of sitting off in successive layers; and what is left when the shaving is done is not a more refined sitting but marklessness itself.

The first and last clauses call for the most care, the last especially — "to enter nirvāṇa without cutting off the afflictions" — together with its kin, "not to forsake the dharma and yet to appear amid the affairs of ordinary beings." Read in isolation, these two clauses are the easiest to mistake for a license to indulge, and so a correction must be set down here:

❌ **Misreading:** to take "entering nirvāṇa without cutting off the afflictions" and "not forsaking the dharma yet appearing amid the affairs of ordinary beings" as a license to indulge — as though they said "there is no need to cut off the afflictions; one may freely mix with the conduct of ordinary beings."

✅ **Correct reading:** these clauses express the **non-grasping of the dharma-body, seen from the standpoint of the markless** — they do not fall into the duality of "afflictions can be cut off / cannot be cut off, the ordinary and the holy to be taken up as two." The disciple stands on the mark of grasping-and-rejecting — "the afflictions are a thing to be cut off, birth-and-death a realm to be left" — and so there is a whole train of things to be done, of realms to be entered: "cut off the afflictions," "leave birth-and-death." The dharma-body's sitting holds afflictions and nirvāṇa as one and the same marklessness, with nothing to grasp and nothing to discard — it neither sets up "afflictions" as an object to be destroyed nor sets up "nirvāṇa" as a far place to be reached. This is a **deeper purity**, not an excuse to loosen the precepts: it does not say "the afflictions need not be cut," but says that within the markless there is simply no such thing to be found that could be cut off, or kept, as "my afflictions." The misreading is dangerous precisely because it hears "the non-grasping of the markless" as "the indifference of conduct" — reading the most thoroughgoing purity, backwards, into the cheapest indulgence. So these clauses must never be cited in isolation, apart from the three classical commentators of the next section; it is the commentators who lock this "non-grasping" tight to the concentrative ground of "the dharma-body, form and spirit alike extinguished," and keep it from sliding into a worldly maxim.

A point worth distinguishing: this chapter, in which Vimalakīrti rebukes Śāriputra, is not the same as the Upāli chapter (on the original emptiness of the nature of transgression) drawn upon in this series' volume on patience-and-confession. The two come from one and the same sūtra and draw upon one and the same set of classical commentaries, but take two different chapters to two different ends — there, the Upāli chapter to make plain the original emptiness of transgression; here, the Śāriputra sitting-chapter to make plain the rendering-markless of concentration. The two are not to be run together.[^2]

## III. The Three Commentators: The Disciple's Sitting and the Dharma-Body's Sitting

Why is Vimalakīrti's rebuke worded as it is? In the *Commentary on the Vimalakīrti* collected by Sengzhao, the notes of three hands — Kumārajīva, Sengzhao, and Daosheng — parse this rebuke with great precision: the disciple's sitting and the dharma-body's sitting are sharply distinct.

Kumārajīva points straight to the chapter's intent — to make plain the utmost concentration, not merely emptiness:

> [Kumārajīva] This chapter is chiefly to make plain the utmost concentration, instructing those not yet able; it is not solely to make plain emptiness. The bodhisattva settles the mind upon the true realm, his cognition not racing outward — this is the mind not manifesting. The dharma-transformation body transcends the three realms — this is body and mind alike hidden, the very acme of meditative concentration. The disciple, though able to hide the mind's real dharmas, is not yet able to be unseen in his body … hence hidden yet still manifest, not yet well-gathered.[^3]

Kumārajīva's gauge lies between "body and mind alike hidden" and "hidden yet still manifest." The bodhisattva settles the mind upon the true realm, his cognition not racing outward — this is "the mind not manifesting"; the dharma-transformation body transcends the three realms — this is "body and mind alike hidden," and this alone is the acme of meditative concentration. The disciple, though able to hide his mind and cognition within the attainment of cessation, is "not yet able to be unseen in his body" — the mark of his body still shows within the three realms, and so it is "hidden yet still manifest, not yet well-gathered." The disciple's sitting hides the mind while the body still shows — this is the paper's running auxiliary image: concentration's incompleteness lies just there, in that one thread of bodily mark "still showing."

Sengzhao's note pushes further, to "form and spirit alike extinguished":

> [Sengzhao] As for the dharma-body's sitting in meditation: form and spirit alike extinguished, the Way cutting off the realm of the constant, beyond the reach of sight and hearing — how should it again manifest a body within the three realms, or raise up intention and make of it concentration? Śāriputra still has the karmically-recompensed body and the karmically-recompensed mind-faculty, and so … is not yet able to leave form and spirit traceless; hence he draws this rebuke.[^4]

Sengzhao figures the dharma-body's sitting as "form and spirit alike extinguished, the Way cutting off the realm of the constant": a realm beyond the reach of sight and hearing — why should it need to "manifest a body within the three realms, or raise up intention and make of it concentration"? Śāriputra draws the rebuke precisely because he "still has the karmically-recompensed body and the karmically-recompensed mind-faculty" — there is still a body conditioned by karmic recompense, a mind-faculty grounded in it, that can be shown and can be cultivated; he is not yet able to leave form and spirit traceless. One sees, then, that what Vimalakīrti seizes is not the act of "sitting" but that last foothold of there still being a "body" and a "mind" to be shown, on which a mark of concentration could be set up.

Daosheng then carries the matter a layer finer, into a hidden dialectic. His sense may be summarized thus: if one supposes the acme of concentration to lie in "hiding the mind deeper, concealing the body more closely," then this very thought of "hiding, concealing" is itself a mark — there is lodged in the mind an outpost of "I have hidden, I am able to conceal"; and whatever is hidden must still be met and disturbed by "not-hidden, not-concealed," for outside "hiding" there yet stands a "not-hiding" set over against it. So the true meditative sitting lies not in hiding a self away more deeply, but in there being, within the three realms, simply no graspable "place of hiding / not-hiding" to be found at all — even the mark "I have concealed" finds no footing, and the two ends of hidden and not-hidden are alike effaced. The commentarial layer thereby plants "the rendering-markless of concentration" more firmly still: the fullness of concentration is not hiding the self where none can find it, but there being not one mark, not one place, in which to hide.[^5] This is exactly what catches Vimalakīrti's line "the mind dwells neither within nor without" — to hide, there must be a place to hide in; the markless concentration has not even a place to hide.

Taken together, the three commentators run one gauge throughout: the divide between the disciple's sitting and the dharma-body's sitting lies not in "how deep the sitting" but in "whether there remains a mark." The disciple can hide the mind, can enter the attainment of cessation — already the acme of concentration — and yet his body still shows, his mind-faculty still stands; that one thread "still showing" is the last mark not yet shaved away. The dharma-body's sitting has form and spirit alike extinguished, hidden and not-hidden alike effaced: not one mark to show, not one place to hide. Vimalakīrti's rebuke is not that Śāriputra sits poorly but that he should slip free, root and all, from the very fact that "however well one sits, there is still an I that sits."

A frame of reference must be flagged here: the attainment of cessation (*nirodha-samāpatti*) is the highest concentration realized by the disciple, and both Kumārajīva's and Sengzhao's notes take "Śāriputra able to hide the mind while the body still shows" as its instance. This paper draws on it purely as a **point of contrast** — using the disciple's sitting to throw the dharma-body's sitting into relief, marking off the line between "markless" and "not-yet-complete" — and not to unfold the structure or stages of the attainment of cessation itself (that belongs to the earlier Part of this volume, and is not entered here). The value of the three commentators is just this: they read this rebuke not as a disparagement of the disciple but as a marking-out of the true terminus of the science of concentration — a terminus that lies not in a deeper hiding but in the markless.

## IV. The One-Practice Samādhi: The Markless Turn of Concentration's Object

If meditative sitting has had the mark of sitting stripped from it, then upon what does concentration still "concentrate"? The One-Practice Samādhi of the *Mañjuśrī Prajñāpāramitā* (in Mandrasena's translation) answers the question head-on: concentration's object turns from a reified thing into the markless **single mark of the dharma-realm.**

> [The Buddha said] The single mark of the dharma-realm, with the meditative object bound to the dharma-realm — this is called the One-Practice Samādhi. If a good son or good daughter would enter the One-Practice Samādhi, one must first hear prajñā-pāramitā and cultivate it as taught, and only then can one enter the One-Practice Samādhi … inconceivable, unobstructed and markless … One should withdraw to a quiet place, cast off scattered thoughts, not seize upon marks, fix the mind on a single Buddha, exclusively reciting the name … able, upon that one Buddha, to keep the mindfulness unbroken thought after thought — and within that mindfulness one is able to behold the Buddhas of past, future, and present.[^6]

Three layers in this should be kept clear. The first is the layer of the **markless**: "the single mark of the dharma-realm" is precisely the mark of the markless — the dharma-realm has no manifold of differentiating marks, and so it is called "one mark"; yet this "one mark" is not, again, a mark set over against "many marks," but is the markless. To bind the object to the dharma-realm is to bind it to this marklessness of the markless, not to some fixed, reified object — and so the text adds at once "not seizing upon marks," "unobstructed and markless." Concentration's object loosens here from "some thing that I take as object" into "the markless of the dharma-realm" — having an object, yet no graspable mark of an object — and this is a turn of the first importance in the science of concentration. The second is the layer of "**one-practice**": the so-called one-practice is that this samādhi "constantly runs as one," without admixture, with no further sequence of practices to follow (a sense made plainest at the next section's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, "no further sequence of practices"). The third is the layer of "**prajñā as the gate**," and it is the one least to be passed over lightly: the text says plainly, "one must first hear prajñā-pāramitā and cultivate it as taught, and only then can one enter the One-Practice Samādhi." This clause binds the One-Practice Samādhi firmly beneath prajñā — not first cultivating some concentration and then bolting on an understanding of prajñā, as though prajñā were an ornament added once concentration is accomplished, but prajñā itself being the precondition and the threshold of entering this concentration. Without prajñā first, the object remains a graspable mark, and concentration remains an accomplishment with an entering and an exiting; only when prajñā has first illumined away the reality of the object can concentration be truly bound to the markless of the dharma-realm. Concentration and wisdom here are not added one after the other; wisdom is the gate of concentration.

As for the clause "fix the mind on a single Buddha, exclusively reciting the name," a line of defense must be set down lest the boundary be crossed. ⚠️ **Caution:** "fixing the mind on a single Buddha, exclusively reciting the name" is, here, the **expedient gateway into this concentration** — the entry-expedient by which a beginner gathers in his scattered thoughts and, from a single point, contacts "the single mark of the dharma-realm": one takes the concentrated single-practice of unbroken thought-after-thought, and from "being mindful of one Buddha" is led toward the markless of "within that mindfulness, beholding the Buddhas of past, future, and present," coming home at last to the single mark of the dharma-realm. Its landing-point is, from first to last, the **markless** — not "the Buddha one is mindful of as a reified object." **So the fixing of the mind on a single Buddha here is not the Pure-Land recollection-of-the-Buddha samādhi, and this paper does not unfold along the path of recollection-and-rebirth;** recollection of the Buddha and recollective practice as an independent gate have their own broad stage elsewhere, and here the matter is touched and let go, taken only as an entry-step into "the markless concentration."[^7] What this paper takes from the One-Practice Samādhi is purely this **aspect of concentration** — "the single mark of the dharma-realm, the markless."

A point of lineage must also be distinguished, lest the reader be puzzled. The previous paper of this series, on Mañjuśrī's sword of prajñā, also drew on this same *Mañjuśrī Prajñāpāramitā*; but what it took there was the passage on "the conceit of false attainment, samādhi unobtainable," whereas this paper takes the passage on the "One-Practice Samādhi" — one and the same sūtra, but a different locus and a different point made (there, the unobtainability of samādhi, to cut dharma-attachment; here, the marklessness of the One-Practice Samādhi, to render concentration's object markless). The renderings keep throughout to Mandrasena's usage (Mañjuśrī, prajñā, samādhi, the single mark of the dharma-realm), consistent front to back, without conflict.

## V. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*: No Near Shore, No Far Shore — No Marks of Entering or Exiting

The markless sense of the One-Practice Samādhi receives its most precise doctrinal grounding in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*. Its exposition of the One-Practice Samādhi (fascicle 47, in the gloss on the hundred-and-eight samādhis) has a passage that can serve as the eye-dotting stroke for this paper's "rendering-markless":

> The One-Practice Samādhi is a samādhi that constantly runs as one, in correspondence with ultimate emptiness; within it there is no further sequence of practices … Moreover, the bodhisattva, within this samādhi, sees neither the near shore nor the far shore. For the various samādhis, the mark of entering is the near shore, the mark of exiting the far shore; the mark of first-attaining is the near shore, the mark of ceasing the far shore.[^8]

The treatise glosses the "one-practice" of the One-Practice Samādhi by "in correspondence with ultimate emptiness": this concentration is constantly in correspondence with ultimate emptiness, with no further sequence of practice-marks to follow — which catches exactly the sense of "one-practice" from the previous section: no admixed practice, no graded steps, in correspondence with ultimate emptiness alone, of one flavor. The clause that follows at once — "sees neither the near shore nor the far shore" — is the keenest eye-dotting stroke of the whole paper. The remarkable thing is that the treatise will not let "near shore, far shore" rest as a mere figure but itself defines what they point to: for the various samādhis, the "mark of entering" is the near shore, the "mark of exiting" the far shore — at one end the mark of going into concentration, at the other the mark of coming out; and, divided along time, the "mark of first-attaining" is the near shore, the "mark of ceasing" the far shore — at one end the moment of first realizing this concentration, at the other the moment of its passing away. So it is that every single samādhi is held between two shores by these four marks (entering, exiting, first-attaining, ceasing). And the bodhisattva, within the One-Practice Samādhi, sees neither shore: no mark of entering to set up as near shore, no mark of exiting to set up as far shore; no moment of first-attaining as near shore, no moment of passing-away as far shore.

The weight of this line lies in its freeing "concentration," whole and entire, from the structure of a state that is "entered and then exited." Samādhi as ordinarily understood always presupposes a threshold and a span of time: a mark of entering, a mark of exiting, a moment of first-attaining, a moment of passing-away — these four marks together being the frame that nails concentration "between the two shores," and being exactly the doctrinal prototype of the second misrecognition flagged at the head of this paper ("concentration as a state one can enter and leave"). But the One-Practice Samādhi, in correspondence with ultimate emptiness, has no such two shores to set up: neither a mark of "I entered concentration," nor a mark of "I came out of it," nor any mark of "when I attained it, when I lost it." The dimension of concentration's "mark" returns here utterly to the markless. The earlier "not manifesting body or mind within the three realms" of meditative sitting, and the "unobstructed and markless" of the One-Practice Samādhi, here reach their cleanest doctrinal close: **concentration is no longer a place you enter and then leave, but the single mark of the dharma-realm, here and now.** With no place to enter or exit, "the self that sits long" and "the self that enters and leaves" alike lose their footing — these two misrecognitions, being from the first one mark shown in body and in mind, are now alike planed flat by a single stroke of ultimate emptiness.

There are two resonances here with other papers of this volume, both flagged in passing and not unfolded again. The first: the sense of "non-abiding" — the bodhisattva's sovereign entering-and-leaving of concentration, not dwelling on its flavor — was established, on its own ground, in an earlier paper (P14); this paper lands on the side of "the markless mark." The earlier paper set up the *use* of "non-abiding" (entering and leaving freely, not bound by concentration); this paper makes plain the *mark* of "the markless" (within ultimate emptiness there are from the first no two shores of entering and exiting to abide on). The two meet within ultimate emptiness, each holding its own ground. The second: the coarse-clinging critique of "the marks of concentration too are empty" was already cut in the first paper of this Part (P16); this paper's "rendering-markless" must not be run together with the "sinking into emptiness" that P16 diagnosed — sinking into emptiness is a falling upon the mark of "emptiness" and stagnating there, while the rendering-markless seizes neither that "emptiness-mark" nor that "concentration-mark." A further note: this paper draws on the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* only at the fascicle-47 passage glossing the One-Practice Samādhi, and does not stray to the treatise's other fascicles on the perfection of dhyāna, so as not to slip past this paper's scope.

## VI. Vimalakīrti's Silence: The Wordless Concentration, Consummated in a Single Silence

With concentration's "mark" returned to the markless, one last dimension remains: "speech." Mañjuśrī enacts the wordless by speaking; Vimalakīrti enacts the wordless by falling silent — this is the climax of the paper, and the capstone of the whole of "Prajñā and Concentration." In the chapter of the *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa* on entering the Dharma-gate of non-duality, after thirty-two bodhisattvas have each spoken a pair of "non-duality," Mañjuśrī sums up, and then turns the question to Vimalakīrti:

> Mañjuśrī said: "To my mind, with respect to all dharmas, to be without word and without speech, without showing and without cognizing, apart from all question-and-answer — this is entering the Dharma-gate of non-duality." Thereupon Mañjuśrī asked Vimalakīrti […]. Then Vimalakīrti was silent, without a word. Mañjuśrī exclaimed: "Excellent, excellent! To be without so much as letter or word — this is truly entering the Dharma-gate of non-duality." [Among that assembly, five thousand bodhisattvas … attained the forbearance of the unborn dharmas.][^9]

This passage must be held tight to the paper's main thread and not blown up. ⚠️ **Caution:** in the non-duality chapter, the thirty-two bodhisattvas each speak a pair of non-dual marks (arising-and-ceasing, defiled-and-pure, self-and-no-self, and so on); here this paper takes **only Mañjuśrī's one statement and Vimalakīrti's one silence**, and does not enumerate those thirty-two pairs, nor spread the matter out into a general treatise on the Dharma-gate of non-duality or a catalogue of the emptiness of prajñā — that is the province of the science of wisdom (the science of view), which this paper's science of concentration ought not to overstep. What this paper takes is only how this one statement and this one silence make good the apex of "concentration's rendering-markless, rendering-wordless": every step must be drawn back to the markless of concentration, of samādhi, of meditative sitting; non-duality serves only as the frame of that apex.

Its tiers are three, to be distinguished in order. At the first tier, Mañjuśrī enacts the wordless by speaking: "with respect to all dharmas, no word and no speech, no showing and no cognizing, apart from all question-and-answer" — with a single utterance he has *said* the matter of "no word and no speech." This is already an exceedingly high ground, yet it remains "speaking the wordless": the wordless is taken as a content, pointed out by language. At the second tier, Vimalakīrti goes a step further: faced with the same question from Mañjuśrī, he no longer opens his mouth — "silent, without a word." He does not "speak" the wordless; he *does* it, on the spot. This is "enacting the wordless without speaking": the wordless is no longer a content pointed out, but the very fact, demonstrated. At the third tier, Mañjuśrī at once gives his confirmation: "Excellent, excellent! To be without so much as letter or word — this is truly entering the Dharma-gate of non-duality." With his own mouth he judges that this one silence of Vimalakīrti's is more truly "entering" than his own utterance just before. One saying, one silence, one confirmation — three tiers in ascending order, and the landing-point throughout the same single thing: how speech is handed back, utterly, into silence.

These three tiers carry the work of "rendering-markless" done in the first five sections up to that last dimension, "speech." What the first five sections rendered was concentration's "mark" — the mark of sitting, the mark of the object, the marks of entering and exiting, returned layer by layer to the markless; what this section renders is concentration's "speech" — even that last layer of speech, "saying that it is markless," is handed back into silence. So "rendering-markless" and "rendering-wordless" are the two wings of one and the same prajñā-deconstruction: one wing shaves away every mark of concentration's posture, the other shaves away every mark of concentration's speech; both wings done, concentration has not one mark, not one word, to grasp — and this is the deepest reach of concentration. And that this silence is a **consummation** and not a "blank nothing" has one piece of iron proof: "among that assembly, five thousand bodhisattvas … attained the forbearance of the unborn dharmas." If Vimalakīrti's silence were merely the cold pause of having nothing to say, how should five thousand in that one sitting then and there realize the forbearance of the unborn? Precisely because that silence is the on-the-spot, full manifestation of the markless concentration, it can imprint the great dharma in the minds of those who hear.

So Vimalakīrti's silence must be read against the previous paper's Mañjuśrī-word to see exactly where its "step further" lies. The Mañjuśrī of the previous paper, with the sword of prajñā, cut the dharma-attachment within concentration and enacted the wordless by speaking; cut to the end, the blade voided even itself. Vimalakīrti goes further still — laying down "sitting" and "speaking" alike, he enacts the markless by "silence." The previous paper closed on the seed-line "Mañjuśrī's word still stands; Vimalakīrti's silence is the step beyond," and here it is made good: Mañjuśrī has reached the limit of "speaking the wordless"; Vimalakīrti steps over that last threshold from "saying" to "not-saying." This is the paper's second load-bearing image: Vimalakīrti's one silence — without so much as letter or word — and a whole assembly realizing the forbearance in a single sitting.

What this "silence" (*tūṣṇīṃbhāva*) means calls for a correction at the level of the word. ⚠️ **Caution:** Vimalakīrti's silence is **not having-nothing-to-say, not a cold pause, not a mystical ineffability**; it is the on-the-spot demonstration of "the consummation of the markless" — and its fruit is the five thousand bodhisattvas attaining the forbearance of the unborn dharmas. In the writing of it, one must on no account romanticize it into a kind of "Zen-flavored blank"; it must come to rest within the **upward-stepping** structure of "speaking the wordless (Mañjuśrī) → enacting the wordless (Vimalakīrti)" — not silent because nothing can be said, but because concentration, because dharma itself, having already returned to the markless, finds in wordlessness its fullest manifestation.

This silence of Vimalakīrti's has been celebrated through the ages, and later commentators have often praised it as "a silence like thunder." Set within a cross-traditional view, there are three points worth comparison — though all only by way of comparison, never of equation, and so noted briefly.

The first: the limit of speech. In the proposition that closes Wittgenstein's *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* — the seventh — the thought is that of what cannot be spoken, one can only keep silent.[^10] In form this resembles Vimalakīrti's "without so much as letter or word": where language reaches its limit, only silence remains. Yet the difference must be drawn: Wittgenstein's silence is a **logical-epistemic limit** — it marks "what cannot be said"; Vimalakīrti's silence is a **soteriological-performative consummation** — concentration itself returned to the markless, its fruit the five thousand attaining the forbearance of the unborn. The one *marks*; the other *fructifies*. Vimalakīrti is not "unable to speak" but "enacting the markless in silence."

The second: the way of apophatic theology (the *via negativa*). That tradition (as in the writings ascribed to Pseudo-Dionysius) holds that the highest reality can be approached only by negation, by silence — stripping away predicate after predicate to draw near it.[^11] It shares with Vimalakīrti's silence the motif that "the highest is approached only in silence." Yet the difference lies here: the apophatic silence still points toward a **transcendent object** — the stripping of predicates is in order to approach that which lies beyond affirmation and negation; Vimalakīrti's silence has **no referent** — it approaches no thing, but is the marklessness of concentration, of dharma itself, present here and now (in correspondence with ultimate emptiness, no near shore, no far shore). The angle must be locked here on "the wordless of concentration," not on a general discourse of emptiness, lest it overlap the science of wisdom.

The third: the transmission of "a silence like thunder." Later East-Asian commentary, and recent Vimalakīrti scholarship, have largely taken this "thunderous silence" as a much-quoted motif.[^12] It is indeed a celebrated and undimmed saying; but the three commentators on the *Vimalakīrti* (Kumārajīva, Sengzhao, Daosheng) had already locked this silence into the **register of the science of concentration** (the dharma-body's sitting, form and spirit alike extinguished), and so what this paper takes is its **doctrinal operation** — concentration's "rendering-wordless" — and not merely a rhetorical marvel or a Zen-flavored blank. (As for the Chan tradition's later use of "the single silence," that has its own proper place, and is not drawn in here.)

It must be said plainly: Western scholarship has, to this day, no monograph whose core thesis is "the rendering-markless of concentration / Vimalakīrti's silence as the apex of samādhi as such." This paper sits precisely in that empty seat — which is not a gap but the very place where its weight is brought to bear.

With this, the arc of the three papers on "Prajñā and Concentration" closes: the first cut the coarse clinging of flavor-and-mark; the second, with Mañjuśrī's sword, cut the fine clinging of dharma-attachment; this one takes the last step of the markless and the silent — concentration's dimension of "mark" returned to the single mark of the dharma-realm, to ultimate emptiness, to no-entering-and-exiting (the rendering-markless), and concentration's dimension of "speech" returned to a single silence (the rendering-wordless). Prajñā's deconstruction, brought thus to bear on concentration's own posture and own speech, has nothing left to deconstruct.

From here the baton passes to the next Part. Concentration, having been loosened by prajñā to the markless and the wordless, raises the next question: is there a great concentration that is itself no ladder, no enter-and-exit to be measured? The Śūraṅgama-samādhi, the heroic-march concentration, rises to take up just this question — and its very gateway is the brick handed up by the gate of precepts. With this, the paper seals its Part.

And the longer backward glance is this: concentration, gathered to its depth, must in the end return into prajñā; the deepest concentration is one in which even the concentrative state cannot be obtained. Vimalakīrti's seizing of the mark of sitting and his enacting of the single silence are the stillest stretch on that road of return.

To show neither body nor mind: that is sitting in meditation.
