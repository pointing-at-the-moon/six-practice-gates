# Silent Illumination

**Hongzhi Zhengjue and the Concentration in Which Stillness and Illumination Arise at Once — the Native Ground Recovered, Not a State Built Up**

*中文主題：默照——宏智正覺的本地之定*

---

## Abstract

This paper approaches silent illumination (默照), the meditation of the Caodong master Hongzhi Zhengjue (1091–1157), from the side of concentration. Its thesis is that silent illumination is not an answer to the question the sudden-and-gradual debate asked — *how is concentration attained* — but a re-conception of concentration as a wordless seated posture in which silence (止) and illumination (觀) are present together in a single thought, with neither subject nor object, neither before nor after. From two lines of the *Extensive Record of Chan Master Hongzhi* — the "Lancet of Seated Meditation," "knowing without touching objects, illuminating without facing conditions," and the "Inscription on Silent Illumination," "when illumination loses its silence, intrusion appears; when silence loses its illumination, it all becomes leftover dharma" — one can read off the exact structure by which silence and illumination mutually constitute each other: lacking either one, it is no longer silent illumination. Just this is why silent illumination is precisely not the dead concentration Huineng repudiated as "fixing the mind and contemplating stillness," nor the "cold ashes and withered wood" of Dahui Zonggao's polemic. The paper then takes as its warrant the first-hand record, in the Taishō *Record of [Hongzhi's] Life and Conduct*, that Hongzhi at his death entrusted his final affairs to Dahui, who came at once to the mountain and buried him — and argues from it that the ranking of silent illumination against the huatou is a matter of Song-dynasty sectarian rhetoric, not the verdict of the two masters themselves. The two gates are therefore set side by side and not ranked.

**Keywords:** silent illumination (默照), Hongzhi Zhengjue (宏智正覺), the Lancet of Seated Meditation (坐禪箴), the Inscription on Silent Illumination (默照銘), the Caodong school (曹洞宗), calming-and-contemplation (止觀), concentration (*samādhi*, 定), the huatou (看話), Dahui Zonggao (大慧宗杲), the non-duality of concentration and wisdom (定慧不二), dead-wood trance (枯木死定)

## I. Introduction: Reading Silent Illumination from the Side of Concentration

A reader meeting "silent illumination" (默照) for the first time most often receives it as an operable sitting technique — the four characters "just-sitting" (*shikantaza*) have all but become its synonym, read as an instruction to sit. This paper does not take that road. The whole volume runs on concentration (*samādhi*, 定, the capacity to gather and abide) as its spine, re-illuminating one and the same ladder of the four jhānas chapter by chapter; arriving at the Chan line, the question is no longer "how does one enter such-and-such a station of concentration" but how the Chan gate, step by step, rewrote the very meaning of concentration. Silent illumination occupies a definite position along that line, and this paper's work is simply to make that position clear — what it is, and how the tradition reads its doctrinal structure, not how it should be done.

Two disciplines must be declared at the outset and held throughout. First, this paper describes what silent illumination *is* and how the tradition interprets its doctrinal structure; it does not supply guidance in "how to practise silent illumination." Silent illumination, seated meditation, resting — all such words are handled as objects of doctrine, never as operable directions.[^1] This discipline is especially exacting here, because what silent illumination names is the very act of sitting itself — the thing most readily read as a method of sitting. Wherever this paper describes the *state* of silent illumination (the "vast yet never dull," the "illumination without the work of illumining" of the passages below), it is stating the structure of the state as the doctrine describes it, not a doorway by which one is to enter it. Second, silent illumination and the huatou (*huatou*) are set side by side and not ranked: this paper asserts neither line as the standard and pronounces on no hierarchy of their transmission. Where it cites Dahui Zonggao's phrase "the heretical Chan of silent illumination," that phrase is cited only as an instance of Song-dynasty sectarian rhetoric — not endorsed, and not used to judge silent illumination higher or lower.

To place silent illumination on the axis of concentration, one must first recall the Chan gate's two prior steps. Bodhidharma named the Chan "concentration" a *pacifying of the mind* (安心) — Chan's first turn upon concentration, in which concentration ceased to be an entering-into-absorption that clings to one object and bores deeper rung by rung, and became instead the fixed abiding of "outwardly cutting off all conditions, inwardly the mind without panting." Huineng went further and rewrote the relation of concentration and wisdom into the mutual immediacy of substance and function — "concentration is the substance of wisdom, wisdom the function of concentration" — so that concentration and wisdom were no longer two cultivations one after another but the two sides of one body. The preceding papers laid out both steps in full; this paper takes up their conclusions and does not restate them.[^2]

What Hongzhi Zhengjue did is the third step. He no longer spoke of concentration as an entering-into-absorption, nor stopped at expressing the mutual immediacy of concentration and wisdom through the "marks" of substance and function; he brought concentration and wisdom down into a wordless seated posture itself — silence (stillness, calm) and illumination (mirroring, contemplation) present together, with no before-and-after, within a single thought of "seated meditation." What makes this a bringing-down is that it sets up no further conceptual scaffolding to explain the relation of concentration and wisdom (neither a ladder of absorption nor the names of substance and function), but points directly at the seated posture in the present moment: concentration and wisdom are present right here, in this single thought of silent illumination, and there is no other place to speak of them.

This makes plain the root parting between silent illumination and the sudden-and-gradual debate. The sudden and the gradual asked *how concentration is attained* — gained all at once, or gained by stages? Whether one held for the sudden or the gradual, the argument turned on the temporal structure of *attainment*, and concentration and wisdom were still imagined as something to be acquired. Hongzhi's silent illumination does not ask after attainment; it asks whether concentration and wisdom can be present together, non-dual, within a single thought. This reversal is the deepening of the Chan gate's spine of departure — not its completion. The non-duality of concentration and wisdom, as the doctrinal summation of the whole Part, belongs to this Part's later paper; this paper does not reach forward to it, advancing only as far as the single master Hongzhi's posture of "silence and illumination at once."

## II. Silent Illumination as Concentration: Knowing Without Touching Objects, Illuminating Without Facing Conditions

The most precise thing Hongzhi says of seated meditation is the opening lines of the "Lancet of Seated Meditation":

> The essential function of all the buddhas, the functioning essence of all the patriarchs: knowing without touching objects, illuminating without facing conditions. Knowing without touching objects, its knowing is subtle of itself; illuminating without facing conditions, its illumining is wondrous of itself … the knowing has no counterpart, yet is singular … the illumining grasps nothing, yet is wholly clear. The water so clear to the bottom — the fish moves slowly; the sky so vast without bound — the bird flies far and faint.[^3]

The structure of "concentration" here differs at root from the Pāli four-jhāna tradition. Concentration on the four-jhāna ladder is built upon a meditative object (*ārammaṇa*, the object faced): the meditator binds the mind to a single object and, through the object of a meditation-subject (*kammaṭṭhāna*, the meditation-object), bores deeper layer by layer, discarding the coarse for the fine, until abiding in the basic absorption. The structure of this ladder and the sequence by which the jhāna-factors fall away in turn the preceding papers have set out in full, and they are not restated here.[^4] The seated meditation Hongzhi articulates is precisely "not facing conditions" — illuminating with no object, knowing without touching objects. The "knowing" is subtle of itself, awaiting no thing known; the "illumining" is wondrous of itself, awaiting no condition illumined. The knowing "has no counterpart" — no duality of a knower and a known; the illumining "grasps nothing" — it takes up no single mark as the object it illumines.

This is an objectless mirroring. Concentration in the four jhānas takes a single object as the thing faced, gathers the mind upon it and enters; concentration in silent illumination sets up no single object as that which is faced — the illumining body is tied to no thing illumined, and the ten thousand appearances arise and subside of themselves within it. "The water so clear to the bottom — the fish moves slowly; the sky so vast without bound — the bird flies far and faint" — the clarity of the water is not set there to mirror the fish, yet the fish moves slowly through it; the vastness of the sky is not raised up to bear the bird, yet the bird flies far and faint across it. The illumining is like this water, this sky: it sets up no illumining "I" that takes hold of an object illumined, and yet objects stand forth distinct of themselves. This is the hinge that distinguishes the concentration of silent illumination from the concentration entered by binding to a condition — it is not "being concentrated upon something," but "facing not a single thing, and the illumining present of itself."

One must press a step further into the "knowing" of "knowing without touching objects." This "knowing" is not a knowing that discriminates and grasps marks — not a cognition got by clinging to some thing, then judging and dividing it. In the concentration bound to a condition, knowing arises with its object: there being a thing touched, there is a knowing of that thing; the thing gone, the knowing subsides. The "knowing" Hongzhi speaks of is subtle of itself "without touching objects" — it does not come from contacting a thing and seizing a mark, and so "the knowing has no counterpart, yet is singular": no pairing of a knower with a known, and yet the knowing stands singular. In the same way the "illumining" of "illuminating without facing conditions" "grasps nothing, yet is wholly clear": it takes up no mark as the thing illumined, and yet is utterly distinct. This rebuilds "concentration" — from the structure of "gathering the mind and binding it to a single object" into the structure of "binding to no single object, and the knowing and illumining subtle of themselves." Concentration in the four jhānas gathers the scattered mind, binds it to an object and enters by degrees into deep absorption; concentration in silent illumination, in the very moment of taking up not a single thing, is of itself subtle and present in its knowing and illumining. The former enters concentration by *grasping* (taking up one object and abiding in it); the latter illumines by *not grasping* (setting up no object, and the illumining clear of itself). Just this parting of "grasping" and "not grasping" is the shape that fell to Hongzhi's hands after the Chan gate had, all along the line, rewritten the Pāli concentration-on-an-object.

A word of discrimination is needed here. The term "just-sitting" (*shikantaza*), habitually paired with silent illumination, is in fact a Japanese Sōtō term established by Dōgen after he rewrote Hongzhi's "Lancet of Seated Meditation"; it is not Hongzhi's own word for himself.[^5] Hongzhi's own names for his house style are only "silent illumination" and "seated meditation"; "just-sitting" is a later sedimentation of the Japanese Sōtō line reading itself back onto Hongzhi, a stratum in the history of reception. To take it straight as Hongzhi's name for himself does not square with the history. Yet it must equally be said: the doctrinal core of silent illumination — the objectless, non-discriminating mirroring of "knowing without touching objects, illuminating without facing conditions" — is already complete in Hongzhi's own "Lancet of Seated Meditation" and "Inscription on Silent Illumination," and waits upon no Dōgen to be made whole. The later sedimentation of reception should indeed be peeled away; but once it is peeled away, Hongzhi's own teaching stands sufficient and complete in itself. What this paper establishes is Hongzhi's silent illumination, not Dōgen's just-sitting.

## III. Silence and Illumination at Once: Illumination Losing Silence, Silence Losing Illumination

What makes silent illumination silent illumination — its exact structure — lies within the single text of the "Inscription on Silent Illumination." This text is the keystone of the paper:

> Silent, words forgotten; bright, all manifest before you. In the moment of mirroring, all is vast; in the place of abiding, all is numinous … the wondrous abides in the silent place; the work is forgotten within the illumining … the way of silent illumination, the root of departure-and-subtlety … silence is the utmost word, illumination the universal response … when illumination loses its silence, intrusion appears … when silence loses its illumination, it all becomes leftover dharma. The principle of silent illumination is complete — the lotus opens, the dream wakes … silent illumination fully attained belongs to my house; the house's silent illumination penetrates from crown to sole.[^6]

"Silent, words forgotten; bright, all manifest before you" is the core of the proposition: silent to the point where words are forgotten, and the clear illumining present before one — silence and mirroring are not two things, but the two sides of one and the same seated posture. "In the moment of mirroring, all is vast; in the place of abiding, all is numinous" — when there is mirroring, all is vast and boundless; when there is abiding, all is numinous and undimmed; mirroring (illumination) and abiding (silence) are neither two times nor two places. "The wondrous abides in the silent place; the work is forgotten within the illumining" — the wondrous lies just in that silence, while the work (the operation of illumining) is itself forgotten within the illumining, leaving no trace of an illumining agent.

Its decisive safeguard is the pair of lines at the center: "when illumination loses its silence, intrusion appears" — if there is illumining with no silence beneath it, the illumining sinks into clinging, the intrusion of objects upon it; "when silence loses its illumination, it all becomes leftover dharma" — if there is silence with no illumining running through it, the silence sinks into dead sitting, a useless remainder. Lacking either one, it is no longer silent illumination. This double-rejecting structure — "lacking either one, it is not" — is the exact statement of silence and illumination being at once and non-dual: silence cannot stand alone apart from illumination (silence apart from illumination is dead), and illumination cannot run alone apart from silence (illumination apart from silence is clinging); the two must constitute each other at once, and only so are they named silent illumination.

Put in this volume's standing language of calming-and-contemplation (*śamatha-vipaśyanā*, calming and contemplating): silence answers to calming, illumination to contemplation; but the calming-and-contemplation of silent illumination is not the sequence of "first calming, then contemplation" — not the two stages of first cultivating calm to settle the mind and then raising contemplation to mirror the dharmas. The yoked-pair mechanism of calming-and-contemplation the preceding paper set out in full, and only its one point of difference from silent illumination is taken here.[^7] In traditional calming-and-contemplation, calming and contemplating may be cultivated apart and may be set in sequence; the calming-and-contemplation of silent illumination is mutually constituting in a single thought, present at once — within the calming there is the numinous brightness of illumination, within the illumination there is the stillness of silence, with no before-and-after of subject and object to divide. "The way of silent illumination, the root of departure-and-subtlety" — departure (離, the silence of cessation) and subtlety (微, the illumination of mirroring) arise from one root and cannot be split in two.

The two words "departure-and-subtlety" (離微) are originally a term within the Caodong house. Caodong has always taken the reciprocity of the biased and the upright (偏/正) as its principle: the upright within the biased, the biased within the upright, light and dark interpenetrating, mutually containing and entering. Dongshan Liangjie's "Jeweled-Mirror Samādhi" figured the mutual containment of the upright and the biased by "snow heaped in a silver bowl, a heron hidden in the bright moon," and figured silence-containing-illumination by the mirror mirroring the ten thousand appearances while the mirror-body does not stir.[^8] Hongzhi Zhengjue inherits this house style of Caodong, and his "silence and illumination at once" is just the reciprocity of biased and upright cashed out upon the seated posture: silence (upright, still, substance) and illumination (biased, bright, function) are neither two posts in opposition nor a before-and-after succession, but reciprocal as light and dark, inseparable as mirror and image. Once this Caodong background is clear, the double rejection of "illumination losing silence, silence losing illumination" is no momentary admonition of Hongzhi's but has its root in the house's doctrine: the biased cannot stand alone apart from the upright (a biased apart from the upright is a dead biased), the upright cannot stand alone apart from the biased (an upright apart from the biased is a dead upright); the mutual constituting of silence and illumination is the reciprocity of biased and upright settled upon concentration.

This makes plain the parting from the sudden-and-gradual debate. The sudden and the gradual asked *how concentration and wisdom come to hand* — gained all at once, or gained by stages? Whether sudden or gradual, the dispute was over the temporal structure of attainment, and concentration and wisdom were still imagined as something to be acquired. Huineng's "concentration is the substance of wisdom, wisdom the function of concentration," though it had already gathered the two into the mutual immediacy of substance and function, still expressed the principle of their immediacy by way of "marks" (the mark of substance, the mark of function).[^9] Hongzhi's "Inscription on Silent Illumination" no longer asks after attainment, nor sets up the marks of substance and function any further, but speaks of the at-once mutual constituting of silence and illumination right within a single thought of the seated posture — "the principle of silent illumination is complete," the principle already whole in the present thought, awaiting no accumulation of stages and no parsing into substance and function. "The lotus opens, the dream wakes" — like the lotus opening, like the dream waking, complete at the very moment, with no gradual sequence to wait upon. Hongzhi says of himself "silent illumination fully attained belongs to my house," and again "the house's silent illumination penetrates from crown to sole," naming his house's style outright by the two characters "silent illumination" and declaring that this style runs through from top to bottom without a leak.

## IV. Not Dead-Wood Trance: Hongzhi's Own Safeguards

The misreading silent illumination most readily invites is to take it for the dead sitting of a stagnant pool — silent yet without awareness, entered into absorption yet sunk in torpor. Against this, Hongzhi set his own safeguards within the text long ago. The "Dharma-Talk for Vice-Director Jin Ke," in fascicle six, says:

> The place of true work is only sitting quietly in silent inquiry … the mind being empty, it can contain; the illumining being wondrous, it can take true measure … vast and solitary, yet never dull; numinous, free of all opposition, yet self-attained.[^10]

"The mind being empty, it can contain" — the mind, empty, can hold the ten thousand appearances; "the illumining being wondrous, it can take true measure" — the illumining, wondrous, can take the measure of the dharmas: this is no realm of dull, awareless blankness. It is worth noting that Hongzhi takes "sitting quietly in silent inquiry" as "the place of true work": silence is not without inquiry — within the quiet sitting there is precisely "silent inquiry," silent and yet inquiring, inquiring and yet still. The word "inquiry" is already illumination: the silence of silent illumination has never been a blank that halts awareness, but a stillness holding within it the inquiry of mirroring. Hence "vast and solitary, yet never dull" — vast and standing alone, not sunk in torpor and dimness; "numinous, free of all opposition, yet self-attained" — numinous and free of all duality, and self-possessed of its attainment. The same fascicle says again, "in the silent illumining place the heavens are clear as autumn; illumination without the work of illumining, light and shadow alike cut off" — the illumining of silent illumination is clear as an autumn sky, yet "illumination without the work of illumining": it illumines without setting up the agency of an illumining function and leaves no image of an illumining and an illumined, hence "light and shadow alike cut off." A convocation in fascicle one sets its own question still more pointedly — "do you yet know how not to fall into mere static fixity and turn back round to the wondrous?" — warning plainly that silent illumination must not fall into "static fixity," the hollow congealing of a frozen concentration. The three gates of "never dull," "free of all opposition," and "not falling into mere static fixity," set together, establish an objectless yet numinous silent illumining — precisely not the torpid, dead concentration.

❌ The claim, then, that "silent illumination just is dead-wood trance" is a common misreading. This misreading runs exactly counter to the dead concentration Huineng repudiated in the preceding paper as "fixing the mind and contemplating stillness is a disease, not Chan" — read rightly, silent illumination is the very reverse of that dead concentration of fixing the mind and contemplating stillness: it does not abide in one mind as its concentration, nor take one stillness as the object it contemplates, and so is free of the grasping disease of "fixing" and "contemplating." What Huineng repudiated was a contrived concentration that holds the mind down and guards the stillness; the "silence" of Hongzhi's silent illumination is a "silence within illumination," its "stillness" a "stillness numinous and never dull" — alike in face to one who holds the mind and guards stillness, but parted in truth.[^9]

A finer discrimination is needed of Hongzhi's phrase "cleansing and polishing," which all too easily merges with the wiping of the gradual gate. Fascicle six says:

> The field is empty and vast — what has been one's own from the very first — and is to be kept by cleansing and polishing.[^11]

⚠️ "Polishing" looks, on its face, close to Shenxiu's "wipe it clean time and again," and yet the two are opposite in sense. The wiping of Shenxiu's verse is an ongoing labor upon the dust of defilement, undertaken to gain purity by degrees; its structure is the acquiring of "at root not pure, now wipe it and afterward pure." The premise of Hongzhi's "cleansing and polishing," by contrast, is that "the field is empty and vast — what has been one's own from the very first": that empty and vast field is natively complete, one's own from the start; the polishing is not to *bring about* a purity but to clear away the obscuring film so that what was always there re-appears. ❌ To read "polishing" as a gradual-gate seeking of purity in Shenxiu's manner would misjudge the concentration of silent illumination as "a state built up by stages"; in truth the concentration of silent illumination is "the recovery of the native ground," not "a structure raised up by stages." Concentration here is not a thing awaiting construction but the ground natively one's own; the work of seated meditation lies not in "achieving a concentration" but in cleansing and polishing, in bringing the empty and vast field that has been one's own from the first to manifest anew. Just this is one more place where silent illumination is set off against the sudden-and-gradual debate as its anchor: the concentration of the gradual gate is wiped and thereby "gained," the concentration of silent illumination is polished and thereby "recovered" — alike in the face of the words, opposite in their drift, and the reader must not fail to tell them apart.

## V. Silent Illumination and the Huatou, Side by Side: The Dispute Is Sectarian, Not Between the Two Masters

To treat concentration in Song-dynasty Chan, one cannot avoid the dispute of silent illumination and the huatou. The Chan of the huatou had its great master in Dahui Zonggao, and the mechanism of his sensation of doubt and critical phrase is the proper province of this volume's later paper; it is not unfolded here.[^12] What this section takes up is only the one strand of Dahui's censure of "silent illumination," so as to see where the ranking arose and how it is to be suspended.

Dahui's most-quoted words run thus:

> There is a kind of heretical silent-illumination Chan that, seeing the literati blocked by the toils of the world … straightway teaches them to become cold ashes and withered wood, a single strip of white silk.[^13]

And again, "even when at leisure one must sit quietly … but if one grasps the place of stillness as the ultimate, then one is held fast by the heretical Chan of silent illumination," and further, of himself, "I forcefully drive out the heretical silent-illumination Chan that blinds people's eyes." The words are fierce enough. Yet here one discipline must be strictly kept: Dahui's "heretical silent-illumination Chan" sort of language is cited in this paper only as an instance of Song-dynasty sectarian rhetoric — not endorsed, not made the ground for judging silent illumination higher or lower, and not turned about into a counter-attack upon the line of the huatou. The aim of citing it is precisely to show the rhetorical character of "the ranking" itself, not to join in the ranking.

Why say this censure cannot serve as a settled verdict on the two masters' standing? Three things dissolve it. First, what Dahui attacks is an unnamed "false teacher" — his words are "a kind of heretical silent-illumination Chan," a general pointing at a class of corruptions that grasp stillness as the ultimate, structurally just like the way the "Northern school" was set up as a target in the history of Chan: what is attacked is a constructed opposite, not necessarily Hongzhi himself.[^14] Second, Dahui himself teaches sitting quietly — "even when at leisure one must sit quietly" came from his own mouth; one sees that what he repudiates is the bias of "grasping the place of stillness as the ultimate," not sitting quietly, and not silent illumination itself. Sitting quietly being allowed by Dahui, what the "cold ashes and withered wood" rebukes is only the disease of making stillness the terminus, of sitting dead there and raising no illumining function. Third, and most decisive: the "Inscription on Silent Illumination" itself already rejects this disease from within — the very line "when silence loses its illumination, it all becomes leftover dharma" is Hongzhi's own safeguard against "dead sitting become leftover dharma." In other words, the "cold ashes and withered wood" Dahui forcefully drives out is exactly the deviant path the "Inscription on Silent Illumination" itself means to exclude — what is attacked is a distortion of silent illumination, not silent illumination read rightly. When a gate is rebuked by way of a corruption it plainly rejects of itself, the rebuke fails to land upon the gate itself.

A word of historical and geographical background is needed, to show the character of this dispute. In the Southern Song, the rise and fall of a monastery hung upon the patronage of the literati and upon imperial standing; the waxing and waning of a school or a line lay often not in the height of its doctrine but in whether it could win the favor of patrons and establish its orthodoxy in the contest of the great monasteries. The Caodong line was able to revive in Hongzhi's day, the gate of the huatou flourished greatly in Dahui's, and the two stood within one and the same field of contention. The censure of "heretical silent-illumination Chan," set in this situation, is then not purely a doctrinal adjudication but carries the rhetorical color of a rivalry between houses — to hold up one's own house's essential function (the huatou's sensation of doubt) and to set against it a constructed image of "grasping stillness, sitting dead" is a commonplace of sectarian discourse. Seeing this, one may read Dahui's fierce words as the language of the courtyards, not necessarily his private estimate of Hongzhi.

What most suffices to suspend the ranking is a piece of first-hand evidence, recorded in the Taishō *Record of Life and Conduct*, of the friendship between the two masters. As Hongzhi was passing away:

> He looked to his attendant, asked for a brush, and wrote a letter, leaving it for Chan master Dahui, entrusting to him his final affairs … [his death-gāthā] "a dream, an illusion, a sky-flower — sixty-seven years" … Dahui, receiving the letter that night, came at once to the mountain … reverently bore the master's whole body and buried it in the East Valley stūpa.[^15]

At his death Hongzhi wrote with his own hand a letter to Dahui, entrusting his after-affairs to him; Dahui, receiving the letter that night, hastened through the night to the mountain, bore Hongzhi's whole body, and buried it in the stūpa of the East Valley. This is recorded in the Taishō *Record of Life and Conduct*, a first-hand source, not the legend of a later memorial inscription or chronicle. Consider it: the master of a "heretical Chan" rebuked by another as "blinding people's eyes" entrusted, at his death, his greatest after-matter to the very one who so rebuked him — and that one, hearing of it, hurried through the night to the mourning and laid him to rest with his own hands. Were there truly a settled verdict ranking silent illumination and the huatou, did the two masters truly hold each other's gate to be heretical, this would be unintelligible. The reasonable reading is the one modern scholarship has given: the dispute of silent illumination and the huatou was in fact a contest over the patronage, the orthodox standing, and the lay following of the Southern-Song Chan forest, constituted by the situation of the great monasteries of a moment, and not the two masters' private verdict on the height of each other's gate. The dispute lay in the courtyards, not in the mutual knowing of the two masters.[^14]

❌ To take Dahui's censure of "heretical silent-illumination Chan," then, as a settled verdict that "silent illumination falls short of the huatou" is a methodological misplacement — it reads a stretch of sectarian rhetoric as a doctrinal adjudication, and reads a courtyard rivalry of a moment as the two masters' settled view. On this ground the paper suspends the ranking: silent illumination and the huatou are set side by side, each showing one face of "concentration" — silent illumination presents silence and illumination together within a single thought, the huatou takes the sensation of doubt as its essential function (its mechanism the province of the later paper); both are the Chan gate's bringing-down of concentration, and neither line is taken as the standard, nor any hierarchy of transmission pronounced. The two masters' friendship at the deathbed is just this suspension's most forceful textual warrant.

## VI. Conclusion: Chan's Third Re-Embodiment of Concentration

**In sum:** silent illumination is not the "how is concentration come by" of the sudden-and-gradual debate, but a wordless seated posture in which concentration and wisdom are present together within a single seated thought, with no before-and-after of subject and object. The "Lancet of Seated Meditation"'s "knowing without touching objects, illuminating without facing conditions" establishes its objectless ground; the "Inscription on Silent Illumination"'s "when illumination loses its silence, intrusion appears; when silence loses its illumination, it all becomes leftover dharma" establishes the exact structure of its at-once mutual constituting — lacking either one, it is no longer silent illumination. Hongzhi sets his own safeguards too, in "vast yet never dull," "illumination without the work of illumining," and "cleansing and polishing so the native ground re-appears," so that silent illumination is precisely not the dead concentration Huineng repudiated, nor the withered wood of Dahui's censure. Bodhidharma named concentration a pacifying of the mind, Huineng rewrote concentration and wisdom by substance and function, Hongzhi brought it down into a wordless posture of silence and illumination at once — this is the Chan gate's third turn upon and re-embodiment of "concentration," and the spine of departure is hereby deepened.

**Placing it within the scholarship.** Three lines may be told apart in this paper's materials. The first is the social history of sectarian dispute (Schlütter): the contest of silent illumination and the huatou was a struggle over the patronage and orthodox standing of the Southern-Song monasteries; the Caodong school revived in the line of Hongzhi (descending through Furong Daokai and Danxia Zichun), and Dahui's censure was a strategic rhetoric aimed at a distorted image biased toward stillness — a caricature, not Hongzhi in person — the two masters' relations being in fact cordial. This account the paper accepts in full, for what the suspension of the ranking needs is precisely the seeing-through of this sectarian rhetoric. Yet this is a social-historical approach and does not reconstruct, on the doctrinal side, the structure of silent illumination's "concentration"; what the paper extends is the return to the texts of the "Lancet of Seated Meditation" and "Inscription on Silent Illumination," reading off from them the conclusion of "silence and illumination at once" — a reading on the axis of concentration that still stands on the texts themselves once the sectarian genealogy is deconstructed. The second is the history of reception (Bielefeldt): "just-sitting" is a term Dōgen set up after rewriting the "Lancet of Seated Meditation," which the paper has granted; yet its doctrinal core is already wholly within Hongzhi, and so the paper establishes Hongzhi's silent illumination, not Dōgen's just-sitting. The third is translation and sympathetic study (Leighton): the English rendering of Hongzhi and a practitioner's reading may be consulted; yet this approach leans toward practice (how to do silent illumination), and the line between it and the doctrinal-historical study this paper keeps (what silent illumination is) must be drawn clearly, lest one slide into the register of a practice manual.[^16]

What must be honestly disclosed: Western scholarship has as yet no monograph whose central thesis is "silent illumination as the simultaneous-non-dual reading of concentration, grounded in the 'Inscription on Silent Illumination''s 'illumination losing silence / silence losing illumination,' held side by side with the huatou and unranked." Its building-blocks — the sectarian history, the history of reception, the translation — are all present in the three approaches above; but gathering them into this synthesis along the axis of concentration is the paper's own offering.

**Open questions.** Silent illumination's bringing-down of "concentration" still leaves two questions for the later papers to take up. First, what Hongzhi established is the single patriarch's posture of "silence and illumination at once"; "the non-duality of concentration and wisdom, the living concentration that is apart from marks within marks," as the doctrinal summation of the whole Part, is to be sealed in this Part's later paper, and the present paper, advancing only this far, does not reach forward to it. Second, once concentration has been gathered into samādhi, it must in the end turn back toward *prajñā* — this question of "concentration returning to *prajñā*" is the matter of the volume's sealing paper. Silence to the place where words are forgotten, and illumination already wholly present:

**Silence, with words forgotten; brightness, all manifest before you.**
