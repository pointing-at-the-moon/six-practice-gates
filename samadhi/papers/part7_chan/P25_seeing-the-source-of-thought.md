# Watching the Head of Speech

**Dahui's Reflexive Samādhi — Turning the Light Back upon the One That Originates Thought, and Seeing the Original Face**

*中文主題：看話頭——迴光返照能起念者·見本來面目之定*

---

## Abstract

This paper takes up the *huatou* Chan (kàn huàtóu, 看話頭) transmitted by Dahui Zonggao and argues that it re-conceives concentration as a concentration of *turning the light back upon the one that originates thought*. The *huatou* is the head of speech — the moment before a single thought arises; to watch the *huatou* is to turn the mind's light back and reflect upon the source that gives rise to thought and lifts up the *huatou*, pointing ultimately at seeing the original face (本來面目). Zhaozhou's "*wú*," "the myriad dharmas return to one," and "who is dragging this corpse?" are interchangeable *gates*: the matter lies not in the phrase watched but in the backward turn of reflection, and the sensation of doubt (疑情) together with the doubt-mass (疑團) is the engine that sustains that turn. This reflexive structure is not a later imposition; it is already complete in Dahui's own "turning the light back" and "after all, what is this? — just look right here," and in Gaofeng's "after all, it turns out to be this very fellow." The gathering of doubt is concentration; the bursting-open of doubt into a seeing of the source is wisdom; the *huatou*'s non-duality of concentration and wisdom is thus the two faces of one and the same reflexive event, not a sequence of concentration-then-wisdom. Dahui further guards the gate against dead concentration with "do not use the mind to dam up [thoughts]," and against becoming a technique for awakening with "pay no heed to whether you awaken or not," while Gaofeng's three essentials — great faith, great zeal, great doubt — give the reflection its frame. Carrying the line of Bodhidharma, Huineng, and Hongzhi, the *huatou* is set beside Silent Illumination — Silent Illumination as the simultaneous co-presence of reflection, the *huatou* as the doubt-fusion of reflection — both reflecting the source, placed side by side and not ranked.

**Keywords:** watching the head of speech (看話頭), the huatou (話頭), the sensation of doubt (疑情), the doubt-mass (疑團), turning the light back (迴光返照), the original face (本來面目), the non-duality of concentration and wisdom (定慧不二), Dahui Zonggao (大慧宗杲), Gaofeng Yuanmiao (高峰原妙), Silent Illumination (默照), Zhaozhou's "wú" (趙州無字), seeing the nature (見性)

## I. Introduction: Another Gate

Silent Illumination asked: within a single sitting, can concentration and wisdom be present together in one thought — silence (止) and illumination (觀) co-present, neither one before the other, neither replacing the other? The preceding paper gave its account of Hongzhi Zhengjue's structure of Silent Illumination, and that account is not restated here. What this paper takes up is another gate opened in the same age.

The *huatou* Chan (kàn huàtóu) transmitted by Dahui Zonggao (1089–1163) does not reach outward for some further meditative object to abide in. The reflection of Silent Illumination lets silence and illumination stand co-present in a single seated posture without taking up any mark; the *huatou* presses that reflection into a single ball of fire. It turns the mind's light back around — not looking outward, but looking back — to reflect upon the source "that originates thought, that lifts up the *huatou*." The *huatou* (話頭), the head of speech, is the moment before a single thought arises; and to watch the *huatou* is just this turning of the light back, illuminating the original face that is prior to the arising of thought. Dahui has a line of his own that marks the threshold of this gate exactly:

> A thousand doubts, ten thousand doubts, are only one doubt. When the doubt is broken upon the *huatou*, the thousand and ten thousand doubts break all at once … and yet do not take hold at the place where it is raised, and do not reckon or calculate.[^1]

Three things easily mistaken must be said clearly first. The first: the *huatou* is not "that phrase." Zhaozhou's "*wú*" is not something one is meant to chew over for the meaning of the word "*wú*"; "*wú*" is only a gate, interchangeable with "the myriad dharmas return to one — to what does the one return?" and with "who is dragging this corpse?" — different gates, all opening on one thing. The matter does not lie in the phrase watched, but in this one backward turn of reflection. The second: this paper is a doctrinal analysis, not a manual of practice; the patriarchs' sayings cited below all serve to set out what watching the *huatou* *is* and how the tradition interprets its reflexive, concentration-and-wisdom structure — not to teach the reader "how to watch the *huatou*, how to raise the sensation of doubt." The third: the *huatou* and Silent Illumination are two gates, set side by side and not judged higher or lower; written after Silent Illumination, this paper must hold that centre-line all the more.

As for "who is reciting the Buddha's name" — that specific lexical genealogy of the *huatou*, running from Zhaozhou down to Zhongfeng and Yunqi — that is a thread of mindfulness turned inward, of the traffic between Chan and Pure Land, and belongs to the mindfulness gate's own volume; it is not taken up here.[^2] What this paper takes up is the concentration-face of the *huatou*: that it re-conceives concentration as a concentration of turning the light back upon the one that originates thought. The *huatou* is the source before a single thought arises; to watch the *huatou* is to reflect back upon the true self that originates thought, pointing ultimately at seeing the original face; and the sensation of doubt, together with the doubt-mass, is the fire that sustains this reflection.

This re-conception carries forward a long line in Chan's handling of concentration. Bodhidharma, with wall-gazing pointing directly, named the Chan "concentration" and set it flowing apart from the *jhāna* attained rung by rung; Huineng rewrote the substance-and-function of concentration with "concentration is the substance of wisdom, wisdom the function of concentration," and drove off the dead concentration of withered sitting; Hongzhi brought concentration and wisdom down into a seated posture of Silent Illumination present at once.[^3] What Dahui does is to push this line to a new turn: concentration no longer reaches outward to abide in an object, but turns back to illuminate the very source that issues this mind and gives rise to this single thought. In other words, watching the *huatou* turns concentration over — from "an outward abiding" into "an inward reflection." This is the proposition the paper lays out.

It is worth marking at the outset the parting of Silent Illumination and the *huatou* on the point of reflection, so as to see Dahui's newness. The reflection of Silent Illumination is to let the self-illumining awareness and its silence be present together within a posture that takes up no mark — reflection here is "co-presence at once." The reflection of the *huatou* is not content with co-presence; it presses reflection into a single ball of doubt that allows no looking aside, pressing until the partition of subject and object dissolves in the bursting — reflection here is "the fusion of doubt." Both reflect the source; the one lets it be present, the other lets it burst forth. The five sections below follow the order: the *huatou* as the head of speech — reflecting back upon the one that originates thought — the two floodgates — Silent Illumination and the *huatou* side by side — and the close.

## II. The Huatou as the "Head of Speech"

To understand watching the *huatou*, the first step is to peel the two characters "*huatou*" away from "the content of the gong'an."

A modern Chan master's concise formulation gathers up this sense most simply: the *huatou* is the moment before a single thought arises, and to watch the *huatou* is to turn the light back and reflect upon the original face.[^4] The "head" of speech is the verge before speech rises — that instant in which the thought has not yet taken form and language has not yet landed. What watching the *huatou* watches is not the phrase already formed, but the light turned back upon the source "that can give rise to this speech, that can lift up this thought." The mind's light runs outward by nature, reaching after the marks of objects; watching the *huatou* turns that one beam back, illumining the light-giver itself. The Chan gate divides by "the live word" (活句) and "the dead word" (死句): what falls into meaning, available to be construed, is the dead word; what is stripped of meaning, leaving the mind no road to walk so that it can only turn back and illumine the source, is the live word. The *huatou* is a live word precisely because it presses the mind back to the ground before a single thought.

For just this reason, a gong'an such as "a monk asked whether a dog has buddha-nature; Zhaozhou said *wú*"[^5] functions in the watching of the *huatou* in a way wholly unlike its function in the savouring of letters. In the savouring of letters, the gong'an is a complete narrative available to be annotated, held up, and appraised; in the watching of the *huatou*, "*wú*" is stripped of meaning, no longer a construable object, and becomes instead a gate at which the mind has nothing to reach for — and the mind, having nowhere to reach, can only turn back, illumining the source that, even at the point where there is nothing to reach for, is still "raising this *wú*." "The myriad dharmas return to one — to what does the one return?" and "who is dragging this corpse?" function in just the same way: they are not problems whose answers are to be "thought out," but devices that press consciousness back to the threshold before a single thought. The phrase watched may therefore be exchanged at will, but the turn of reflection is one and the same; the phrase is only the gate, and reflection is the matter.

Robert Buswell's reconstruction of the mechanism of *kanhua* is of real help here. He observes that the *huatou* is a critical word stripped of meaning (as against the gong'an as complete narrative), upon which the mind has no purchase for intellection to construe, and that this "cutting off of reaching" is just where the "short-cut" of *kanhua* lies — not detouring through the gradual stages of contemplative work, but driving the mind straight to the end of intellection.[^6] This layer of the mechanism the paper grants in full: stripping away meaning to cut off reaching is indeed a real part of how watching the *huatou* operates. But a stroke must be added. Buswell reads the *huatou* chiefly as a "contentless object," and weighs the whole apparatus of *kanhua* within the frame of a soteriology of sudden awakening; whereas the true axis of watching the *huatou* lies not in that object but in the turn of reflection — the *huatou* is the source before a single thought, and watching the *huatou* is the turning of the light back upon the one that originates thought. To read the *huatou* as an object sees only one of its faces (the stripping of meaning to cut off reaching), and does not yet see its reflexive dimension: the mind, once driven to the end of intellection, does not halt upon a blank object but turns back to illumine the source that is "right now being driven, right now raising the phrase."

This addition does not negate Buswell; it restores his reconstructed mechanism to the direction that was its own. Stripping away meaning is useful precisely because it serves the turning-back: were the *huatou* still a construable dead word, the mind would halt upon construal and reach after that object; only when it is forged into a live word with no road to walk does the mind, at the place where the road runs out, have no choice but to turn its head and illumine the very self that has all along been "seeking a road, raising the phrase." So the function of "*wú*" is not to be understood but to be used up — used until intellection has no further place to apply its force, whereupon reflection occurs of itself. A gate is a gate just in that it lets one pass through and does not let one halt; the *huatou* is a *huatou* just in that it sends the mind back to the source before a single thought while itself withdrawing from view. Seen so, the interchangeability of "*wú*," "the myriad dharmas return to one," and "who is dragging this corpse?" is no surprise at all: they are only different doorways onto one and the same turning-back.

And because watching the *huatou* is a turning of the light and not a watching of content, its settling of "concentration" differs from ordinary methods of calming. Dahui says:

> When deluded thoughts arise, do not use the mind to dam them up. To stop motion and return to stopping — the stopping grows the more agitated. Just at the very place of motion-and-stopping, watch the *huatou* … this is the *wú* that is the true *wú*, not the empty, hollow *wú* of the world.[^6]

The concentration of watching the *huatou* is not a pressing-down of deluded thoughts, not a forcing of the mind into stillness — "the stopping grows the more agitated," the more one presses the more it stirs; it is, just at the alternation of motion and stopping, the turning-back of the light. This "*wú* that is the true *wú*" is not the world's blank and vacant dead emptiness, but the "*wú*" within which reflection has already fused wisdom. Whether deluded thoughts rise or stop, there is no need to spend separate effort countering them; one need only, at the very place of their rising and stopping, turn the light back and illumine the source. The next section will show that this turn of reflection is just where concentration and wisdom are forged into one.

## III. Reflecting Back upon the One That Originates Thought: The Fusion of Concentration and Wisdom

This is the pivot of the paper. Watching the *huatou* re-conceives concentration as a concentration of turning the light back upon the one that originates thought — and this reflexive structure is not an interpretation imposed by later hands but is already complete in Dahui's own words.

Dahui says of himself: "Turning the light back [upon its source] is the buddhas' medicine."[^7] And: "After all, what is this? — just look right here."[^8] The point of the doubt has never been to "solve that word *wú*," but to reflect upon "this — that which can think, can doubt, can raise the *huatou* — after all, what is it?" The "watching" of watching the *huatou* is a watching back upon the watcher; what is investigated is not the phrase, but the source that lifts the phrase. The line "after all, what is this? — just look right here" is exacting: what is watched is not the head of the *huatou*, but "right here" — the present source that can raise this phrase, that can raise this doubt.

By the time of Gaofeng Yuanmiao (1238–1295), the aim of this reflection is pressed to its limit. With "who is dragging this corpse?" Gaofeng presses upon the one who can drag this body of blood and flesh — the body is a dead thing; who is the one able to drag it? And the place of his own breaking-through, by his account, is just the illumining of the source that originates thought as the original face:

> After all, it turns out to be this very fellow. The other day, questioned by the old monk on the phrase about dragging the corpse, all at once it shattered … cut off and then revived.[^9]

"It turns out to be this very fellow" — it was just this all along. Reflected upon to the bottom, what is seen is no other thing: just that which has all along been originating thought, raising the phrase, dragging this body — the original face. "Cut off and then revived": once the reaching of intellection is utterly cut off, the light of reflection bursts out from the partition of subject and object and illumines its own source. The ultimate of watching the *huatou* is here laid bare: the direction of concentration no longer reaches outward to abide in an object, but turns back to illumine the source of itself. This "turning back to illumine the source" runs of one kind with the *Śūraṅgama*'s Guanyin "turning the hearing back to hear the self-nature" — not following sound outward, but rotating the hearing-nature to illumine itself; the detailed mechanism of that perfect penetration through the faculty of hearing is the proper province of another paper, and only its kinship of reflection is taken here, not its structure restated.[^10]

On what, then, can this reflection sustain itself, fuse into a single piece, and at last burst open into a seeing of the source? On the sensation of doubt. The sensation of doubt is not an intellectual reservation or skepticism, but the whole-bodied urgency of "after all, who is this that can think, can doubt, can raise the *huatou*?" — a single pressing force that allows no looking aside, locked against day and night. Dahui says again and again that until the root of the doubt of birth-and-death is pulled out, this ball of doubt is raised without break, present in walking, standing, sitting, and lying, until all at once it bursts open.[^11] Gaofeng states the inner structure of this pressing force most clearly of all:

> Know that doubt has faith for its substance, and awakening has doubt for its function … A thousand doubts, ten thousand doubts, are only one doubt. Resolve this doubt, and there is no doubt left over.[^12]

Doubt has faith for its substance: this ball of doubt is no rootless wavering; its ground is the great faith that "here there must be an original face to be seen." Awakening has doubt for its function: the awakening that sees the source is just the matter of this ball of doubt bursting open. And the line "a thousand doubts, ten thousand doubts, are only one doubt" lets the two patriarchs cross-confirm each other — Dahui too says, "a thousand doubts, ten thousand doubts, are only one doubt; when the doubt breaks upon the *huatou*, the thousand and ten thousand doubts break all at once."[^1] Every kind of doubt comes down in the end to the one doubt, "after all, who is the one that originates thought?"; this one doubt bursting open upon the *huatou*, all doubts break at once.

Here, then, concentration and wisdom are fused into one matter. The single-pointedness of the sensation of doubt, its refusal to stray, its locked solidity, is the face of concentration — it gathers the scattered mind into one ball, and this gathering yields nothing in concentration to the abiding-upon-one-object of the four jhānas and eight attainments, yet takes up no outer object as the thing faced. The bursting of the doubt-mass into a seeing of the source that originates thought is the face of wisdom — it bursts the light of seeing the nature out from the extremity of gathering. The non-duality of concentration and wisdom in watching the *huatou* is not a sequence of cultivating concentration first and wisdom after, but the two faces of one and the same reflexive event: the gathering of doubt is concentration, the bursting of doubt into a seeing of the source is wisdom. Being the inside and outside of one and the same ball of doubt, they admit of no division into before and after, nor of any further relation set up beyond taking one for substance and the other for function. This structure of fused concentration and wisdom differs much from the concentration discussed in earlier chapters of this volume, and is worth the comparison. On the ladder of the four jhānas and eight attainments, concentration is an abiding that, following the stages, discards the coarse and takes up the fine, entering deeper by degrees, while wisdom is a contemplation raised separately within or after concentration; even when concentration and wisdom assist each other, they remain two matters. In watching the *huatou* there is no object available to be abided in or entered by degrees — the doubt-mass is not an object but a pressing force that drives the reaching mind back to its source; so its concentration is not "abiding in some place" but "having no place to abide, and the mind gathering of itself." Nor is its wisdom a contemplation raised separately once concentration is complete; it is the immediate seeing of the source at the moment this pressing force bursts. The one who watches and what is watched are here two ends of one matter: what is watched is the source that originates thought, and the one who watches is just the light that source gives off; when the doubt-mass bursts, inner and outer beaten into one piece, the partition of subject and object dissolves, and there is no "source illumined out there" standing over against a "mind that illumines in here" — and this is why "after all, it turns out to be this very fellow" is a seeing of the original face, and not the seeing of an object.

Wumen Huikai puts this ball of doubt and the moment of its bursting most vividly:

> A monk asked Zhaozhou whether a dog has buddha-nature. Zhaozhou said *wú* … Rouse a ball of doubt through your whole body, investigate this word *wú*, raise it day and night … like swallowing a red-hot iron ball you can neither spit out nor get down … Long and long, ripened through, and of itself inner and outer are beaten into one piece.[^13]

A whole-bodied ball of doubt, a red-hot iron ball swallowed and not to be spat out — this is the portrait of the sensation of doubt as the gathering of concentration; long-ripened, inner and outer beaten into one piece — this is the moment of seeing the source, gathering carried to its limit until the partition of subject and object dissolves. "Inner and outer beaten into one piece" is the crux: the watching mind and the watched source, inner and outer, subject and object, dissolve into non-duality at the bursting of the doubt-mass, and this is the very moment of reflection carried to the bottom, the seeing of the original face. Worth setting one against the other: Silent Illumination settles reflection as co-presence at once (silence and illumination present together in a single seated posture); watching the *huatou* presses reflection into the fusion of doubt (the turned light forcing out a ball of doubt that breaks into a seeing of the source). The two roads differ, but what each reflects is the one source that originates thought.

## IV. Two Floodgates: Neither Dead Concentration nor a Technique for Attaining Awakening

Because watching the *huatou* takes the sensation of doubt for its concentration, it is all too easily misread as two things: read as the dead concentration of pressing down thoughts in withered sitting, or read as a technique for procuring "awakening." In the words of Dahui and Gaofeng, a floodgate is set against each of these misreadings.

The first floodgate guards against falling into dead concentration. Already cited above is Dahui's "do not use the mind to dam up [thoughts]; the stopping grows the more agitated; just at the place of motion-and-stopping, watch the *huatou*"[^6] — the concentration of watching the *huatou* does not lie in suppressing deluded thoughts into stillness. Huineng long ago refuted the dead concentration of "fixing the mind and contemplating stillness is a disease, not Chan," and that is not restated here;[^14] Silent Illumination too guards itself against sinking into torpid withered sitting with "vast yet never dull," as the preceding paper discussed.[^15] The self-guarding words of watching the *huatou* are "do not use the mind to dam up" and "the *wú* that is the true *wú*, not the empty, hollow *wú* of the world" — within reflection wisdom is already fused, so its concentration is not the dead halt of blank emptiness but a living, pressing force. Worth noting: because this ball of doubt is raised without break at the alternation of motion and stopping alike, the concentration of watching the *huatou* is not confined to seated stillness upon the cushion but can continue through walking, standing, sitting, and lying — and its living quality lies just there.

The second floodgate guards against degenerating into an instrument for procuring awakening. Dahui says plainly:

> Right in the topsy-turvy of daily affairs, just watch the word *wú*. Pay no heed to whether you awaken or not, whether you penetrate or not.[^16]

"Pay no heed to whether you awaken or not, whether you penetrate or not," together with the earlier "do not reckon or calculate, do not take hold at the place where it is raised"[^1] — these all say: reflection is not a means operated in order to procure a result called "awakening." The moment the mind harbours the thought "I mean to awaken by this," another outwardly-reaching object has been raised, and reflection at that instant falls empty — the thought of seeking awakening is itself a new "object faced," running exactly counter to the direction of watching the *huatou*, which turns the light back to illumine the source. "Do not take hold at the place where it is raised" is especially exacting: even when the *huatou* is raised and the sensation of doubt is at its height, one must not, at that place of raising, take hold of some "this is it" as a state to grasp — for once there is a taking-hold, the opposition of subject and object is fallen into again, and the purity of reflection is at once destroyed. The watching of watching the *huatou* is a watching that does not expect what it might gain; and just because it does not expect a gain, reflection is able to stay pure.

Gaofeng sets up "the three essentials" as the frame of this reflection — the first, a great root of faith; the second, a great resolve of zeal; the third, a great sensation of doubt.[^17] Great faith is the substance: first to trust that here there must be an original face to be seen, so that reflection has somewhere to aim; great zeal is the momentum: to raise the doubt to its limit with fierce resolve, so that reflection can sustain itself without retreating; great doubt is the function: once the doubt-mass forms into a single piece, its bursting is the seeing of the source. With all three present, reflection can form into a ball without scattering, press without sinking. It must be said again: the sayings cited above — "pay no heed to whether you awaken," "do not reckon or calculate," "after all, what is this? — just look right here," "the three essentials" — all describe the inner bounds and frame of this reflexive mechanism of watching the *huatou*, and do not confer upon the reader a set of operating steps.

## V. Silent Illumination and the Huatou Side by Side: A Spine That Refuses to Rank

Within the Song-dynasty Chan gate, Silent Illumination and the *huatou* did indeed have their dispute; the political context of that piece of history, and its polemical writings, are the proper province of the preceding paper, and here only the structure of the concentration-teaching is set side by side, not restated and not judged for height.[^15]

As to the structure of reflection: Silent Illumination is the co-presence at once of reflection, the *huatou* the doubt-fusion of reflection. The former, within a single seated posture, lets silence and illumination stand co-present; the latter, within a single ball of doubt, fuses gathering and bursting into one thought. Buswell marks the *huatou* as a method with the phrase "short-cut gate": not detouring through gradual contemplation and long nurturing, but driving the mind straight to the end of intellection so that it turns in a single thought.[^6] This description of the "short-cut" may be set against Silent Illumination's "co-presence without taking up a mark" — both, each in its own way, leap over the laddered gradualism and come to rest upon reflection. The two gates differ in approach, yet both alike press concentration past the laddered abiding of the four jhānas and eight attainments,[^18] and both alike point at the source that originates thought. They are two gates onto one and the same room, set side by side and not judged better or worse.

Three pieces of historical record must be set in place with a neutral historian's eye, and on no account turned over into grounds for ranking. The first: Dahui did indeed gibe at "the heretical Chan of silent illumination," but the object of that polemic, its wording, and its political background are the province of the preceding paper, and this paper cites it without unfolding it, neither endorsing it nor using it to counter-attack Silent Illumination — moreover Dahui himself taught people to sit quietly, and what he gibed at was an unnamed lopsided distortion, not Silent Illumination itself.[^15] The second: Hongzhi at his death entrusted his final affairs to Dahui, and the depth of friendship between the two masters itself refuses any warrant to read this dispute as a verdict on the two men's standing; this matter is recorded in the preceding paper, and only its presence is noted here.[^15] The third: it is reported by tradition that Dahui burned the printing blocks of his master Yuanwu Keqin's *Blue Cliff Record* — a matter semi-legendary in character, its historical substance still hard to establish,[^19] and even were it certain, all it could show is that watching the *huatou* departs from the mark of letters (reflection lies not in savouring the complete narrative of a gong'an), and it can by no means be read as "the *huatou* surpassing the savouring of letters." Yuanwu Keqin was precisely Dahui's own master, and the *Blue Cliff Record* stands as the very model of the way of savouring letters; teacher and disciple in succession, this paper does not frame Dahui as one who forsook his master, nor does it rank the *huatou* and the savouring of letters as higher and lower.

As for the social diffusion of watching the *huatou*, Miriam Levering's study is of real value for reference. Dahui transmitted watching the *huatou* to literati lay practitioners, and from this developed a "raise it constantly in walking, standing, sitting, and lying" work-within-activity, so that watching the *huatou* was not confined to the cushion but could be carried about with one; nor were the figures of women practitioners absent, the gate of watching the *huatou* standing open alike to householders and to women.[^20] What this paper takes from this social-historical observation is the doctrinal structure that shows through behind it — turning the light back within activity shows precisely that the concentration of watching the *huatou* is not a still concentration of damming-up but a reflection that can continue through walking, standing, sitting, and lying — and not the social history itself. In other words, watching the *huatou* can be "raised within activity" precisely because its concentration is bound to no single still posture or object, but to the source that is always originating thought and can be illumined back upon anywhere.

## VI. Conclusion: A Gate of Reflecting Back upon the Source

Bodhidharma, with wall-gazing pointing directly, named the Chan "concentration"; Huineng rewrote the substance-and-function of concentration with "concentration is the substance of wisdom, wisdom the function of concentration"; Hongzhi brought concentration and wisdom down into a seated posture of Silent Illumination present at once; and Dahui — together with the later Gaofeng and Wumen — pressed concentration into the sensation of doubt that turns the light back upon the one that originates thought. Chan's re-conception of "concentration" has here come to rest once more upon "turning the light back, illumining the original face that originates thought." This is a deepening spine, not yet complete; how concentration and wisdom are utterly beaten into one piece, in the mark and in the function, is the matter of the next paper, and only touched here.[^21]

As for the scholarly placement: Western scholarship on *huatou* Chan has a solid accumulation. Buswell reconstructed the mechanism of the *huatou*'s "short-cut" and the phases of the sensation of doubt; Levering disclosed its diffusion among literati and lay practitioners and the form of its work-within-activity; and other scholars have given close treatment to the political context of the Silent Illumination–*huatou* dispute (a context that is the preceding paper's province).[^15] Yet to define the *huatou* expressly as "a re-conception of concentration — re-conceived as a concentration of turning the light back upon the one that originates thought and at last seeing the original face, with the sensation of doubt as the engine that sustains this reflection, held side by side with Silent Illumination and not ranked," taking this reflexive synthesis of the concentration-face as the central thesis: no monograph yet appears that has done so. This is just what the present paper means to supply; while the materials that compose this synthesis — the mechanism, the social diffusion, the dispute's context — lie already scattered across the hands named above. A modern Chan master's summation, "the *huatou* is the moment before a single thought arises, and watching the *huatou* is turning the light back upon the original face," also gives this concentration-face its simplest modern statement.[^4]

Watching the *huatou* is not the watching of the word "*wú*," but the turning of the light back upon the source that raises "*wú*," that can think and can doubt. Zhaozhou's "*wú*," "the myriad dharmas return to one," and "who is dragging this corpse?" — different gates, one thing pointed at: after all, who is this that originates thought? The sensation of doubt is the fire that sustains this reflection, and at the moment the doubt-mass bursts open, concentration (the single-pointedness of doubt) and wisdom (the seeing of the source) fuse in one thought — and what is seen is, as Gaofeng says:

> After all, it turns out to be this very fellow.
