# The Five Hindrances and What Jhāna Clears
***Subtraction, Suppression, and the Return to Wisdom***

*中文主題：五蓋作為定之所對治——離，是定的入口*

**"Seclusion is the door to concentration."**

**Abstract**

Open almost any modern meditation manual and the "five hindrances" appear under a single heading: how to overcome them — what to do about drowsiness, how to handle distraction, how to dissolve ill-will. This chapter does not ask that question. It asks a prior one: when the canon says one "secludes oneself from" the five hindrances and thereby "enters the first jhāna," what is this seclusion? Drawing on the Dīrgha Āgama's *Poṭṭhapāda Sutta*, the Madhyama Āgama's *Assapura* discourse, and the awakening-factor *saṃyukta* of the *Saṃyukta Āgama*, the chapter argues that the five hindrances (*pañca nīvaraṇā*) are not a set of "difficult emotions to be managed" but a *concentration-relative category*: they obtain only in the relation of being-secluded-from, and strip away the reference to concentration and to the path, and the category dissolves. Concentration, in other words, is defined not by what it accumulates but by what it sheds — what remains once the hindrances are stilled simply *is* concentration. The chapter then sets side by side two later models of "what jhāna clears" — the Southern per-factor pairing (Vimuttimagga) and the Northern sequential-block scheme (Abhidharmakośa) — noting that both are commentators' instruments, not the surface of the sūtras. It closes on a seam that runs through the whole volume: concentration can only *suppress* the hindrances (temporarily, *vikkhambhana*), while only wisdom can *sever* them (by the root, *samuccheda*) — and so concentration must, in the end, return to wisdom.

**Keywords:** the five hindrances (五蓋 / *pañca nīvaraṇā*), concentration (定 / *samādhi*), seclusion (離 / *viveka* / *vivicca*), first jhāna (初禪), suppression (伏 / 鎮伏 / *vikkhambhana*), severance (永斷 / *samuccheda*), Vimuttimagga (解脫道論), the *Poṭṭhapāda Sutta* (布吒婆樓經), the awakening-factor *saṃyukta* (覺支相應), concentration-relative category (定—相對範疇), the covering sense of "hindrance" (蓋＝覆), mindfulness and concentration (念與定), śamatha–vipaśyanā (止觀), remorse not wrongdoing (惡作＝追悔)

## I. The Question — *"Overcoming" versus "What It Is"*

Open a modern guide to meditation, turn to the chapter on the five hindrances, and you can all but predict its heading: how to overcome drowsiness, how to handle distraction, how to subdue ill-will and doubt. The whole chapter is an operator's checklist — meet this hindrance, apply that remedy. This is useful, of course, and it is the real work done day after day in the meditation hall. But it presupposes a way of reading, and that way of reading is worth pausing over before we go on: it treats the five hindrances as five separate enemies, each to be beaten back in turn.

This chapter does not take that road. It asks the prior question that the checklist skips over: in the formula that recurs throughout the discourses — "secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful states … one enters the first jhāna" — what is that *seclusion*?

Three readings that crowd in too easily must be barred at the door from the start. First, "secluding oneself from the five hindrances" is not "developing a stronger focus that overpowers the five" — that is addition, and the canon's verb is one of subtraction. Second, the "five hindrances" are not "five negative emotions to be soothed" — to read them as emotion-management is already to cancel the very reference on which they stand. Third, "overcoming the five hindrances" is not the title of this chapter — "how to seclude oneself from the hindrances" is the headline question of the meditation manual, whereas what this chapter sets out to describe is what *seclusion itself is*, not to teach anyone how to do it.

The distinction sounds like splitting hairs; it is in fact the hinge of the whole essay. Robert Sharf has repeatedly reminded scholars that contemporary descriptions of Buddhist meditation tend, without noticing, to read a modern set of assumptions — about how mental states are technically regulated — back into ancient texts that were talking about something else. The five hindrances are among the easiest objects of just such a reading. They look so much like our familiar "distraction," "agitation," "low energy," that we almost reflexively connect them to "how to correct it." The work of this chapter is to set that reflex aside and return to the texts with a single question: in the world of the Āgamas and the early treatises, what are these five things taken to be? And concentration — on which side of them does it stand?

Two redlines must therefore be drawn from the outset, and they run through the whole chapter. The first is *non-operation*: this chapter describes what the hindrances are and what jhāna clears; these are statements of what-is, not instructions in how-to. "Seclusion" is a *definitional fact about jhāna*, not a step the reader is to carry out. The second is *non-psychologization*: the five hindrances are not a set of "difficult emotions" that can stand on their own, apart from concentration and the path — they are a category, and the conditions under which that category obtains are what the next section will take apart.

## II. What the Hindrances Are — *Concentration's Named Complement*

First, the list, set out plainly. The five hindrances (*pañca nīvaraṇā*) are: sensual desire (*kāmacchanda*), ill-will (*byāpāda*), dullness-and-drowsiness (*thīna-middha*), restlessness-and-remorse (*uddhacca-kukkucca*), and doubt (*vicikicchā*).

The first thing on this list worth stopping over is that its arithmetic is not honest. "Five" hindrances — but counted out, they come to seven factors. Dullness-and-drowsiness is in fact two things reckoned as one: *thīna* (惛沈, the dullness of mind) plus *middha* (睡眠, the drowsiness of body). Restlessness-and-remorse is likewise two: *uddhacca* (掉舉, the wavering of mind) plus *kukkucca* (惡作, remorse). The *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 21, gives this arithmetic its rule:

> "Though two, they are established as one hindrance (dullness and drowsiness as one, restlessness and remorse as one); because they obstruct the same aggregate, there are only five."[^1]

That is to say: the reason the treatise-masters are willing to count two factors as a single hindrance is that the pair shares its nutriment, shares its antidote, shares its function — the aggregate each obstructs is the same, and so they may be reckoned together. Just now this looks like nothing more than a bookkeeping trick; but in Section IV it will turn out to matter. Any tidy table that pairs "five hindrances" against "five somethings" must first remember that the ledger here is really seven against five, not five against five.

The second thing worth stopping over is a translation-trap lodged in the terms. The "remorse" of restlessness-and-remorse is *kukkucca* (惡作), and *kukkucca* means regret, after-the-fact worry — the anxious looking-back over what one has done or left undone. The *Kośa*, fascicle 4, says it outright: "remorse is what regret rests upon."[^2] It is *not* "doing evil," not the committing of wrong. The Chinese characters 惡作 are far too easily read as the latter, and the trap is one to bat away by hand whenever one reads the old treatises. (Regret as a practice-theme in its own right is treated at length in this series' Volume Three, *Repentance* (KṢAMĀ); here it is noted with a single touch and not developed.)

Now to this section's real question: why are these five (seven factors) bound into one sheaf and called "hindrances"?

The key is in the word "hindrance" (蓋, *nīvaraṇa*) itself. The *Mahāvibhāṣā*, fascicle 34, gives a spare gloss:

> "The sense of covering is the sense of hindrance; the sense of binding is the sense of fetter … all the defilements can cover and obstruct the noble path and the roots of good that prepare for it, and therefore all are called hindrances."[^3]

This sentence does something very precise. It tells us that one and the same batch of defilements can be cut differently and renamed according to the facet from which it is seen. Cut along the facet of *binding*, it is called a "fetter"; along the facet of *latency*, a "latent tendency" (隨眠, *anuśaya*); and along the facet of *covering-and-obstructing* — covering what, obstructing what — it is called a "hindrance." The *Saṅgītiparyāya*, fascicle 12, spreads this "covering" sense of "hindrance" into an eight-word gloss: a hindrance is what "obstructs, screens, presses down, conceals, covers, veils, entangles, and wraps" the mind.[^4] Eight words circling one meaning: it covers the mind over, so that the mind cannot be clear and bright, cannot be put to work.

So a "hindrance" is not a separate class of defilement, but a defilement *seen under the aspect of what it covers*. Then what does it cover? The *Saṃyukta Āgama*, discourse 707, puts the answer flatly:

> "These five hindrances — sensual desire, ill-will, drowsiness, restlessness-and-remorse, doubt — cover and hinder the mind, defile it, and can enfeeble wisdom … these five cover over the world, so that beings, deeply attached and hard to ferry across, are screened off and do not see the right path."[^5]

What it covers is wisdom, the "right path," the capacity to know and see things as they are. This lays bare the condition under which the five hindrances obtain at all: they are defined *relative to the path of concentration and wisdom*. Take away that path — take away the thing that *ought to be lit and is instead screened over by them* — and there is no "hindrance" left to speak of.

This section has one last piece to fit, and it is the hardest piece of canonical evidence for the non-psychologization redline. The awakening-factor *saṃyukta* of the *Saṃyukta Āgama* (fascicle 27), in defining each of the five hindrances item by item, fixes the same verdict on each — and the syntax of that verdict is a word-for-word mirror, reversed, of the definition of the seven awakening-factors. Each of the seven awakening-factors — the concentration factor among them — "is knowledge, is awakening, leads onward to nirvāṇa"; and each of the five hindrances is:

> "just a hindrance — not knowledge, not awakening, does not lead onward to nirvāṇa."[^6]

Mark the structural force of this opposition. The *Saṃyukta Āgama*, discourse 725, presses it into a single banner:

> "What is called the wholly unskillful heap is the five hindrances … what is called the wholly purified heap is the seven awakening-factors."[^7]

The sole ground on which a hindrance is a hindrance is that it "does not lead toward awakening, does not lead toward nirvāṇa." It is not a hindrance because it "feels unpleasant"; not because it "is a negative emotion" — it is a hindrance because it stands on the far side of the path that leads to nirvāṇa. This is a thoroughly *path-relative*, *concentration-relative* criterion. Remove the reference to concentration and to the path, and the five hindrances are no longer the five hindrances; only five mental phenomena remain, neither good nor bad. This is exactly why reading the hindrances as "five difficult emotions" misses the mark: that reading strips away the very reference that makes them "hindrances" at all.

One editorial detail is worth a word: the *Saṃyukta Āgama*'s "hindrance" material is not a section standing on its own, but is set entirely inside the "awakening-factor" *saṃyukta* (discourses 705–710 in fascicle 26, 713–725 in fascicle 27). Each core discourse is itself the opposition of "the five hindrances (which obstruct awakening)" against "the seven awakening-factors (which assist it)." In other words, "what is contrary to concentration" and "what assists concentration" are, in the very arrangement of the text, the two faces of one thing that one set of discourses set out to say.

## III. Seclusion, the Threshold — *Concentration Defined by Subtraction*

Now we can return to the formula set aside in Section I.

The doorway into the first jhāna is opened, again and again in the discourses, by a single verb: *seclusion* (離). The Pāli is *vivicca kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi* — secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful states. What does this "seclusion" mean? The Dīrgha Āgama's *Poṭṭhapāda Sutta* glosses it as clearly as could be wished:

> "Having extinguished the five hindrances that cover the mind … putting away sensual pleasures and unskillful states, with applied thought and sustained thought, with the rapture and pleasure born of seclusion, one enters the first jhāna."[^8]

"Seclusion" is glossed, explicitly, as "extinguishing the five hindrances." The act of entering the first jhāna is not the *developing* of something, but the *removing* of something — the removing of the five hindrances that cover the mind. The Madhyama Āgama's *Assapura* discourse (fascicle 48; the *Discourse on Knowing* in fascicle 49 is its doublet) carries this subtraction all the way to the fourth jhāna, and gives the five hindrances a compound name:

> "Having severed these five hindrances — defilements of the heart that weaken wisdom — secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful states … one dwells having attained the fourth jhāna."[^9]

Here the five hindrances receive a wonderfully vivid compound name: "defilements of the heart that weaken wisdom" (心穢、慧羸) — stains upon the mind, an enfeebling of wisdom. And the attainment of the fourth jhāna is described as something that follows upon "severing these five hindrances." (A translation detail worth noting: the Madhyama Āgama here renders the first hindrance as "covetous prying" (貪伺), and renders restlessness-and-remorse one way in fascicle 48 and slightly differently in fascicle 49 — these are synonymous renderings of the same items the *Saṃyukta Āgama* calls "sensual-desire hindrance" and "restlessness-and-remorse hindrance.")

Set the *Poṭṭhapāda* and the *Assapura* discourses side by side, and the load-bearing proposition of this chapter stands up: concentration is defined not by what it accumulates but by what it sheds. It is a subtraction. What remains once the five hindrances are stilled simply *is* concentration.

This supplies the volume's load-bearing image with its last missing piece. The earlier chapters each asked their own question: how deep does concentration run (the vertical scale of the volume), which gate it is, where it stands on the eightfold path, how it arises in due sequence. This chapter asks — concentration is what is left once what is subtracted? The previous chapter argued that "concentration is a fruit that is given rise to"; this one answers what that fruit, once arisen, contains: the absence of the five hindrances. The result of the arising (the previous chapter) is the seclusion (this one).

It is exactly here that the non-operation redline of Section I shows its positive content. When we say "seclusion is a definitional fact about jhāna," we mean that "seclusion" describes the *structure* of concentration-as-concentration, not a procedure the reader is to complete. "The absence of the five hindrances is concentration" is a statement about what concentration *is*, not an imperative about "how you should go about getting rid of the five hindrances." Seclusion is the door to concentration — but this "door" says "this is what concentration looks like," not "to walk in through here you must first do these five things."

This is the right place to set a distinction that recurs throughout the series, and that this chapter grounds most cleanly of all in the discourses — *mindfulness is not concentration*. This same set of five hindrances is, in the establishments of mindfulness (*satipaṭṭhāna*), an object that is *watched*; in jhāna, it is the field one is *secluded from*. The Pāli "hindrance section" of the discourse on the establishments of mindfulness (DN 22 / MN 10) speaks of knowing as it is:

> when there is sensual desire within, one knows "there is sensual desire within me"; when there is none, one knows "there is no sensual desire within me"; and how unarisen desire comes to arise, and how arisen desire comes to be abandoned — all this one knows as it is.[^10]

This is the work of mindfulness (*sati*): it watches the presence, the absence, the arising, and the ceasing of the hindrances. The work of jhāna is not to "watch" but to be "secluded from" — it is the very absence of the hindrances. One set of five hindrances, two relations: mindfulness watches the hindrances, concentration is secluded from them. This distinction is a main thread in this series' Volume One, *Mindfulness* (NIAN); here it is made fast by the early texts — both poles are in the discourses, both are canonical, neither is a later commentator's partition.

(The Pāli tradition also has the well-known similes of "being secluded from the five hindrances as a casting-off of burdens" — as the discharge of a debt, recovery from disease, release from prison, freedom from slavery, the safe crossing of a desert — together with the simile of "the five hindrances as five kinds of fouled water." These similes have no corresponding original in the surviving Chinese Āgamas, and so are not quoted here verbatim; but the Chinese Āgamas have their own equivalent images: the *Saṃyukta Āgama*, discourse 706, likens the hindrances' covering of the mind to "darkness, sightlessness," and discourse 708 likens them, in their stunting of the roots of good, to "a seed under the shade of a tree."[^11] The sense is the same: a mind covered by the five hindrances is like the blind, like a seed pressed under shadow; secluded from them, it sees light, it grows.)

## IV. What Jhāna Clears — *Two Models, South and North*

If concentration is "the stilling of the five hindrances," then it is natural to ask next: *how*, exactly, does concentration clear the five hindrances? One hindrance matched to one antidote, cleared in correspondence — or is there an order, a sequence?

To this the discourses themselves give little answer — what they give is the seed-image "secluded from the five hindrances, one enters the first jhāna," and the skeleton of the good/unskillful opposition. What actually takes apart "what jhāna clears, and by what structure" is the later treatise literature. And here, intriguingly, the Southern and Northern traditions give two very different models. Setting them side by side is this chapter's analytic centerpiece.

The South takes the road of *per-factor pairing*. The Vimuttimagga (解脫道論, the Chinese sister-text of the *Visuddhimagga*), fascicle 4, pairs each of the first jhāna's five factors with one of the five hindrances, and attributes the scheme explicitly to "what is stated in the Tipiṭaka":

> "One-pointedness is the antidote to sensual desire; rapture is the antidote to ill-will; applied thought is the antidote to dullness-and-drowsiness; pleasure is the antidote to restlessness-and-remorse; sustained thought is the antidote to doubt."[^12]

Decoded: one-pointedness (*ekaggatā*) counters sensual desire; rapture (*pīti*) counters ill-will; applied thought (*vitakka*) counters dullness-and-drowsiness; pleasure (*sukha*) counters restlessness-and-remorse; sustained thought (*vicāra*) counters doubt. One jhāna-factor to one hindrance, five pairs, clean. The same treatise carries this subtraction up the ladder of the four jhānas:

> "By the first jhāna one severs the five hindrances; by the second, applied-and-sustained thought; by the third, rapture; by the fourth, pleasure."[^13]

What the first jhāna severs is precisely the five hindrances — which aligns with this volume's vertical-scale chapter, where "the jhāna-factors fall away layer by layer"; the point is touched here and not re-walked.

The North takes another road. The *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 21, gives no per-factor table but a *sequential-block* model — arranging the five hindrances by the position at which each obstructs the process of entering concentration, laid out along an arc in time:

> sensual desire and ill-will obstruct the mind that is *about to enter* concentration (at the threshold); dullness-and-drowsiness and restlessness-and-remorse obstruct, "respectively, in their order, śamatha and vipaśyanā" (within concentration, the one obstructing serenity, the other insight); doubt obstructs the discernment of dharmas at the *emerging* from concentration (afterward).[^14]

This is a timeline: before entering, two hindrances block the way; within, two hindrances damage serenity and insight respectively; after emerging, one hindrance impedes the discerning investigation of the teaching. It is not a one-to-one pairing; it is the arc of a process.

Now, with the two models set side by side, three things must be said plainly, on pain of error — and these three are exactly where this volume's standing discipline, *the commentator's grid is not the Buddha's gateway*, comes to ground in this chapter.

First, that elegant per-factor table — "applied thought cures dullness, sustained thought cures doubt, rapture cures ill-will, pleasure cures restlessness-and-remorse, one-pointedness cures sensual desire" — is *Southern* (the Vimuttimagga, word-for-word the same pairing as the *Visuddhimagga*'s fourth chapter); it cannot be hung in the mouth of the *Kośa* or the *Vibhāṣā*. The North gives the sequential-block arc above. The *Vibhāṣā* does contain fragments such as "tranquillity counters dullness, equanimity counters restlessness" — but that belongs to another discussion, "how the jhāna-factors are established" (the territory of this series' next stage of papers); imported here, it yields only the bare fact that "such a pairing exists," not the mechanism behind it. In other words, the juxtaposition "Southern per-factor / Northern sequential-block" is *this chapter's* synthesis, not the original wording of any one treatise; one must not fabricate a stitched-together pseudo-citation of the form "the treatise says: the five jhāna-factors counter the five hindrances in order."

Second, return to the ledger laid down in Section II: the five hindrances are really seven factors. So any tidy table pairing "five hindrances against five jhāna-factors" is a treatise-master's tidying that treats the double hindrances (dullness-drowsiness, restlessness-remorse) as single factors — not the clean five-against-five of the sūtra surface. The pairing-table is a serviceable map, but the map has simplified here.

Third, "which hindrance obstructs which aggregate, which training" is disputed even *inside* the *Kośa* itself (whether dullness-and-drowsiness obstructs wisdom or obstructs concentration, for instance, the treatise states more than one way). So wherever such correspondences are listed, one must honestly set the variant views side by side, and not assert one clean single correspondence.

Gather the three into one sentence: the Southern per-factor table and the Northern sequential-block arc are both analytic maps that the treatise-masters drew in order to make plain "what jhāna clears." They are achievements, and deserve respect; but they are maps, not the terrain — and one must not write the commentators' after-the-fact grid back into the mouth of the Āgamas, taking it for a gateway the Buddha himself laid down. (This chapter owns "the pairing as a clearing-of-hindrances"; the *mechanism* of the jhāna-factors themselves it leaves to a later paper, P10.)

## V. Suppression, Not Severance — *Jhāna Only Subdues; Only Wisdom Cuts*

By now this chapter still owes one most-crucial qualification, and that qualification connects directly to the proposition of the whole volume.

Does concentration clear the five hindrances *thoroughly*?

It does not. This is the central seam of this chapter — and of the whole volume: concentration (and every mundane, defiled path) can only *suppress* the five hindrances; only wisdom (the supramundane path) can *sever* them. "Suppression" is *vikkhambhana*, a temporary subduing; let the conditions reassemble and the hindrance rises again. "Severance" is *samuccheda*, the cutting-off at the root, permanent.

The Vimuttimagga states this most clearly within its framework of the "five kinds of release," where it distinguishes "release by suppression" from "release by severance":

> "One who, practicing now, suppresses the hindrances by the first jhāna — this is called release by suppression … as for release by severance, one cultivates the supramundane path, which can extinguish the remaining fetters."[^15]

What the practice of the first jhāna yields is the "release by suppression" that *subdues* the hindrances; the "release by severance" that truly extinguishes the remaining fetters at the root depends on "cultivating the supramundane path" — that is, on wisdom. Concentration presses the hindrances down; wisdom pulls them up by the root. The *Kośa*, fascicle 24, gives the textual ground for the same verdict from another angle:

> "Only the undefiled path can free one from attachment to the peak of existence — not the defiled path."[^16]

The defiled path (mundane concentration included) can subdue but cannot sever for good; only the undefiled path (wisdom) can free one from defilement finally. The *Vibhāṣā* puts it still more bluntly: the mundane path of the sense-sphere "cannot even temporarily sever a hindrance and check an entanglement so that it does not arise again — how much less finally."[^17]

This seam is worth nailing down at the level of vocabulary, lest the terms slide. This chapter's use of "suppress" and "subdue" for concentration's temporary pressing-down of the hindrances is apt (the very word "presses down," 鎮, among the *Saṅgītiparyāya*'s eight senses — "obstructs, screens, presses down, conceals" — supports the usage). But note: the three-character compound "suppress-subdue-sever" (鎮伏斷) is in fact Yogācāra vocabulary (the *Yogācārabhūmi*, the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*); the four Sarvāstivāda treatises (the *Kośa*, the *Vibhāṣā*, the *Saṅgītiparyāya*, the *Dharmaskandha*) do not have those three characters run together — where they express the same idea they use phrasings like "temporarily sever and check," "subdue," "damage and subdue." So this chapter takes the *sense* of "suppress / subdue," without hanging the Yogācāra term "suppress-subdue-sever" in the mouth of the *Kośa*.

Set this "suppression ≠ severance" distinction back into the context of the whole volume, and it is no longer merely a technical qualification but a directional momentum. An earlier chapter pointed to a seam: concentration runs through the defiled, whereas the awakening-factors are purely undefiled. "Suppression ≠ severance" is the downstream consequence of that very seam — and it upgrades the seam from a fact of classification into a verdict about the direction of practice: since concentration's clearing of the five hindrances is only temporary, concentration cannot be the terminus — it must turn back toward wisdom. For one who holds the five hindrances down by concentration alone, let the concentration ebb and the hindrances rise again; only wisdom's severance-at-the-root keeps those five things from returning.

This seed the volume will harvest in its final chapter (the volume-closing paper that returns to prajñā). Here only this one sentence is planted: concentration's clearing is a subtraction, but the subtraction is *reversible*; to make it irreversible, one must hand it over to wisdom.

## VI. Modern Dialogue, the Two Redlines, and a Coordinate Revisited

Set this chapter into the contemporary discussion of early Buddhist meditation, and three positions in the conversation are worth marking.

First, Rupert Gethin has long argued that the five hindrances should be understood as a structured *set*, belonging — together with the seven awakening-factors and the rest of the thirty-seven factors of awakening — to one and the same developmental framework, rather than as a scatter of separate emotions.[^18] This reading the chapter accepts whole — it is precisely the best gloss on the anti-psychologization point: the five hindrances are a structured category, not a string of stray moods. On top of Gethin's "set," the chapter lays two further claims: first, that the set is *subtractive* — it is concentration's named complement (the things concentration does *not* contain); and second, that concentration's clearing of this set is suppression and not severance — severance-at-the-root belongs to wisdom and to the path of seeing, not to concentration. This is not opposition to Gethin; it is two further courses laid on the structure he has built.

Second, Bhikkhu Anālayo, in his work on the establishments of mindfulness, has expounded with great clarity the face of the five hindrances *as an object of satipaṭṭhāna* — how their presence, absence, arising, and abandonment are known as they are within mindfulness.[^19] This face the chapter likewise accepts. The only thing to add is one sentence: that is the relation of the establishments of mindfulness, not the relation of jhāna. Mindfulness watches the hindrances; concentration is secluded from them — one set of hindrances, two relations. This is the scholarly version of the distinction drawn in Section III.

Third, in the living field of contemporary jhāna instruction there is a dispute between "active antidote" and "natural subtraction": one side stresses the active technique of countering (noticing, naming, applying a targeted remedy), the other stresses a natural shedding (not countering at all, letting the hindrances retire of themselves in the stilling). This chapter takes no side, and names no living teacher or center — because what the chapter means to answer is a doctrinal question already written into the texts, not to pass judgment on anyone's teaching. And the chapter has in fact already furnished the tools to dissolve the dispute: the two models of Section IV tell us that "per-factor countering" was itself a map drawn by the treatise-masters (and a Southern one at that), not the definition of concentration; the "suppression ≠ severance" of Section V tells us that, whether by active countering or natural shedding, all concentration can do is *suppress*. "Seclusion" is the definitional fact of concentration; "countering" is one described route by which one arrives at it — the two are not on the same plane, and so they need not exclude one another.

Having come this far, let the two redlines be drawn together.

On *non-operation*: from start to finish this chapter has described what the five hindrances are and what concentration clears — statements of what-is. The chapter has not, and does not mean to, teach anyone "how to overcome the five hindrances" — no noting technique, no second-person remedial procedure, no "three steps to seclusion." This is not out of withholding, but because "seclusion" simply *is* the definitional fact of concentration, not an operating instruction for the reader. To write it as an operating instruction would be to miss precisely the thing this chapter sets out to say.

On *non-psychologization*: the five hindrances are not "difficult emotions to be managed and soothed." They are a *concentration-relative category* (Section II's "not knowledge, not awakening, does not lead onward to nirvāṇa" is the condition under which the category obtains); strip away the reference to concentration and to the path, and they fall apart into five indifferent mental phenomena. To connect them to the modern vocabulary of performance-management and emotion-regulation is to demote a category about "the path to nirvāṇa" into a category about "how to live more comfortably" — and that is exactly to cancel a "hindrance" as a "hindrance."

Last, a backward glance over the road just travelled. This volume's Part II has used three chapters to view one and the same concentration from three different coordinates: one looked at its *place* on the noble eightfold path; one at how it *arises* in the sequence of the seven awakening-factors; and this one at the *seclusion* by which it is constituted, the subtracting-away of the five hindrances. Place, sequence, seclusion — three coordinates, one concentration. Taken together, the three complete what Part II set out to picture as "the Āgama view of concentration": it is a factor with a fixed position on the path, a fruit that arises in dependent sequence, and the content of that fruit is the absence of the five hindrances.

What remains is left to the next part. If concentration is "the stilling of the five hindrances," then — which objects of cultivation, which supports, best still them? Once that question is asked, the abhidharmic work of systematization opens in earnest: the forty meditation-subjects (*kammaṭṭhāna*) will be the starting point of the next paper. And the foreshadowing planted in Section V — that suppression must in the end yield to severance — will accompany us all the way to this volume's final chapter, where concentration will look back upon itself and hand itself over to wisdom. This chapter stays with "what-is"; it is neither "here-is-how" nor "this-too-is-empty."
