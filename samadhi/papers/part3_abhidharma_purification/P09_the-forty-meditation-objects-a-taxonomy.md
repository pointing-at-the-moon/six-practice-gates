# The Forty Meditation-Objects
***A Taxonomy — Matching the Remedy to the Illness: Temperament, Antidote, and the Multi-Axis of the Meditation-Objects***

*中文主題：四十業處綱目——應病與藥*

*Matching Object to Temperament*

**Abstract**

Open a meditation app and the first question is often "which type are you?" — answer a few prompts, receive a type, and the type hands you a recommended practice. Practice is thereby loaded into a frame of "find your type, pick the method you like." This chapter asks whether the system in the treatise literature that *matches objects to people* is really an ancient personality quiz of that kind. Taking as its core text the Vimuttimagga's chapters "Distinguishing Temperaments" and "Distinguishing Meditation-Subjects" — its reduction of fourteen temperament-types to three, and the "by-person" passage that pairs temperament with object — and setting beside it the *Abhidharmakośa* and *Mahāvibhāṣā* "two gateways to the deathless," with the *Saṃyukta Āgama* episode at the Vaggumudā river (the praise of foulness drove monks to such revulsion at the body that the Buddha shifted them to mindfulness of breathing) as the canonical anchor, it argues that the matching of object to temperament is not a "find-your-type, choose-what-you-love" personality typing but a diagnostic-prescriptive system: identify which defilement is currently dominant, and prescribe the object that counteracts it. What is matched is not a preference that flatters the self, but a remedy that treats a poison. The chapter further shows that temperament is only one of several axes along which the objects are sorted: the treatise-master himself says they are to be understood "by nine dimensions," so the system is a multi-dimensional matrix, not a single-line menu. The Southern temperament-grid and the Northern two-gateways are two traditions arriving independently at the same insight — set side by side, compared, not ranked. Match the remedy to the illness, and when the medicine takes, the gate of concentration opens.

**Keywords:** meditation-object (業處 / *kammaṭṭhāna*), temperament (性行 / *carita*), object of cultivation (所緣), antidote (對治 / counteractive), matching-the-remedy-to-the-illness (應病與藥), the two gateways to the deathless (二甘露門 / *amṛta-dvāra*), contemplation of foulness (不淨觀 / *asubha*), mindfulness of breathing (安那般那念 / *ānāpānassati*), the *Vimuttimagga* (解脫道論), the *Visuddhimagga* (清淨道論), diagnosis-and-prescription, the Vaggumudā episode (婆裘園), the forty meditation-objects (四十業處), calm (止 / *samatha*)

## I. The Question — *The Object Is Prescribed for "Which Defilement Dominates," not Chosen for "Who You Are"*

Open almost any meditation app and the first question it asks is some version of: "What kind of person are you?" Answer a handful of prompts, and out comes a type; the type is matched to a recommended set of practices. In a personalized practice, the "type" is an *identity you can affirm* — it tells you, "this is the sort of person you already are, and so this method suits you." Within that frame, choosing a method is like choosing a comfortable pair of shoes: the test is fit, ease, the feeling of *this is so me*.

The treatise literature does contain a system that matches objects to people. The treatise-masters sort practitioners by which defilement is currently dominant in them, and then match each sort to a different object of cultivation. On the surface this is uncannily close to the app's quiz — both run "diagnose the person first, then assign the method." But return to the texts and three things turn out *not* to be what the system is doing.

First, it is not a preference-menu that flatters personality. What it matches is not "what you like" but "what you most need to counteract right now." Second, it is not an affirmable identity-typing. The question it asks is not "who are you?" but "which defilement is now in command?" — and a defilement is a liability to be dissolved, not a character-trait to be embraced. Third, it is not merely a teacher picking, by intuition, a method that happens to suit a student's palate. The matching is governed by the analysis of defilement: which poison dominates decides which antidote is prescribed. All three readings miss.

The load-bearing image of this system is the physician's: *match the remedy to the illness*. The object is the medicine; the defilement is the illness; temperament (the currently-dominant tendency to a particular defilement) is this patient's presenting disease. The physician matching a drug does not consult the patient's preferred flavor; he consults where the disease is. The earlier chapters of this volume have each asked their own question in turn: how deep concentration can be cultivated (P04), which gates the meditation-objects open and what level each leads to (P05), where right concentration stands on the eightfold path (P06), how concentration arises through the sequence of the awakening-factors (P07), what the hindrances require to be subtracted (P08). This chapter asks the last and the most human of the questions: **for whom is each of these objects prescribed?**

And the fact that *a wrong prescription can harm* is no scholastic nicety. The *Saṃyukta Āgama*'s Vaggumudā episode gives the sharpest canonical evidence for it. The Buddha praised the contemplation of foulness to the monks; having cultivated it, they became so violently disgusted with their own bodies that some took their own lives, and some had others kill them. Ānanda then asked the Buddha to "teach some other Dharma," and the Buddha shifted them to mindfulness of breathing — a "subtle abiding," he said, that settles arisen and unarisen agitation as a great rain settles the dust already risen and not yet risen.[^1] The very same medicine that cures a defilement, given to the wrong patient, given too strong, becomes a lethal poison. This is exactly why the matching matters.

Two redlines must be drawn at the outset. The first: the determination of temperament discussed here is not a self-quiz for the reader. The treatise-masters list various marks to observe — the bearing of one's standing, walking, sitting, and lying down; the manner of eating; the way one goes about a task — but these are written for a *teacher*, on "how to observe a student," not for a student on "how to self-diagnose your type." This chapter reports the treatise-masters' method of discernment strictly in the third person, and offers no self-help steps of the "if you want to know your type, notice whether you…" kind. The second: temperament is not a personality type in the modern sense. It is not an MBTI-style label to be tagged with, identified with, and worn for life, but a category defined *relative to* defilement — one that is meant to be counteracted and dissolved. It describes "what now binds you"; once the antidote takes effect, the type itself loosens.

With this chapter, the systematizing labor of the treatise literature formally opens.

## II. What Temperament Is — *A Diagnosis of the Currently-Dominant Defilement*

### 2.1 From Fourteen to Three: The Reduction of Temperament-Types

The Vimuttimagga, in its chapter "Distinguishing Temperaments," opens by declaring that the practitioner is to be taught fourteen "temperaments":

> "One should teach this practitioner the fourteen temperaments: the greed-temperament, the hate-temperament, the delusion-temperament, the faith-temperament, the intelligence-temperament, the speculative-temperament; the greed-and-hate, the greed-and-delusion, the hate-and-delusion; the even-share; the faith-and-intelligence, the faith-and-speculative, the intelligence-and-speculative; and the even-share."[^2]

These fourteen are six "root temperaments" (greed, hate, delusion, faith, intelligence, the speculative or discursive cast of mind) set alongside a number of "compound temperaments" (greed-and-hate, greed-and-delusion, hate-and-delusion, and the faith-with-intelligence, faith-with-speculative, intelligence-with-speculative blends). The treatise-master at once draws them in: the fourteen are abridged to seven, and the seven are then "reduced, by the root defilement, to three" — the greed-temperament, the hate-temperament, the delusion-temperament.[^3] However finely the surface is divided, in other words, everything finally returns to one of the three poisons — greed, hate, delusion — as the dominant. The point of the typing is not to enumerate the kinds of human being, but to locate "which one of the three poisons is, in this person, now in command."

Two terms here need pinning down, because their correspondence cannot be read off the Chinese characters. The "intelligence" of the intelligence-temperament corresponds to the *buddhi* line — a quick, analytically inclined cast of faculty. The "awakening" graph in the speculative-temperament (覺) does *not* mean awakening; it renders *vitakka* — the tendency of the mind to coarse movement, scattered thought, ceaseless reaching after objects. Neither correspondence is got by reading the graphs at face value; both are established *backwards*, by the antidotes that follow. The intelligence-type is matched to objects requiring deep, fine reflection — defining the four elements, recollection of death (so we know the faculty in question is wisdom); the speculative-type is matched to counting the breath "in order to cut off *vitakka*" (so we know the disease lies in the agitated movement of discursive thought). To read this system one must let the *matching* define the name of the temperament, and not be misled by the surface of the characters.

### 2.2 Causes and Discernment: The Map Is Not the Terrain

Where does temperament come from? The treatise-master gives three sources: past karma, a predominance among the four elements, and a difference in the bodily humors. This etiology carries the imprint of ancient Indian medicine and cosmology; read today it looks more like an explanatory framework than a verifiable pathology. The point is not whether the causes hold up, but that they support a practical conclusion: temperament leaves traces, and is therefore observable, and therefore treatable.

As for how it is observed, the treatise-master lists a number of marks of discernment — from the way a person regards what is before them and the manner in which defilement arises in them, to their habits of walking, of wearing the robe, of eating, of doing a task, of lying down and sitting. All of these can serve a teacher as clues to the dominant defilement. These marks are *descriptive observational items*; they are the treatise-master teaching a teacher "how to look at a person," and emphatically not a "test which type you are" questionnaire. To read them as a self-quiz is to mistake a diagnostic map drawn for a teacher's reference for a fixed personality table that the reader must slot themselves into.

This is exactly the measure the series keeps returning to: the map is not the terrain; the commentator's grid is not the Buddha's gate. The temperament-grid is a diagnostic map the treatise-masters drew for the clinical purpose of prescribing; its ingenuity deserves the respect due a genuine feat of systematization. But it remains a map — not a deterministic chart that decrees "you belong by nature to such-and-such a type." The treatise-masters' ordering work is to be held with the ingenuity of the map in one hand and the restraint of "this is not the Buddha's fixed type" in the other, the two regarded together.

## III. Matching Is Antidote — *The Prescription*

### 3.1 The "By-Person" Passage: A List of Remedies

The discourses already scattered such object-and-defilement pairings here and there — "cultivate the contemplation of foulness to cut off greed; cultivate loving-kindness to cut off hate; cultivate the perception of impermanence to cut off conceit; cultivate mindfulness of breathing to cut off discursive thought."[^14] What the treatise-masters did was to gather these scattered clues and systematize them. The "by-person" passage in the Vimuttimagga's chapter "Distinguishing Meditation-Subjects" is the heart of that whole systematizing labor. It pairs temperament with object, one by one:

> "The greed-temperament should cultivate the perception of foulness and mindfulness of the body, for this counteracts greed. The hate-temperament should cultivate the four immeasurables, for this counteracts hate; or should cultivate the colour-kasiṇa, since the mind follows after it. The faith-temperament should cultivate the six recollections, with recollection of the Buddha foremost, for the sake of the concentration that faith first finds. The intelligence-temperament should cultivate defining the four elements, the perception of repulsiveness in food, recollection of death, and recollection of peace, for these are deep. Further, the intelligence-temperament meets no obstacle in any object whatever. The speculative-temperament should cultivate counting the breath, in order to cut off discursive thought. The delusion-temperament should, by asking after the Dharma in words, hearing the Dharma in season, and dwelling with a teacher in reverence for the Dharma, cause wisdom to grow."[^4]

Read remedy by remedy, the logic of the matching is consistent throughout — it is always "medicine against illness." The greed-type is matched to the contemplation of foulness and mindfulness of the body, because seeing the body's foulness is the most direct antidote to craving. The hate-type is matched to the four immeasurables (loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity), to moisten hatred with loving-kindness — or to the colour-kasiṇa, since when the mind is coarse with hate, the clear bright sign of the colour-disc gives it something to follow and settle on. The faith-type is matched to the six recollections (of the Buddha, the Dharma, the Saṅgha, and so on), recollection of the Buddha foremost, because one strong in the faculty of faith most easily reaches concentration through calling the Three Jewels to mind. The intelligence-type is matched to deep, fine objects — defining the four elements, the perception of repulsiveness in food, recollection of death, recollection of peace — because that faculty can bear the work of deep reflection; and the treatise-master notes especially that the intelligence-type "meets no obstacle in any object whatever," able to put almost any object to use. The speculative-type is matched to counting the breath, precisely because the in-and-out of counted breaths gives the scattered, reaching mind a measured beat to be drawn in by — "in order to cut off discursive thought."

Only the delusion-type is an exception: the treatise-master gives it no fixed object, but teaches it to ask after the Dharma often, hear the Dharma often, and dwell with a teacher in reverence, "so that wisdom may grow." The disease of delusion is a dull, powerless dimness, with no capacity to hold an object on its own; so one must first lean on a good teacher to prop wisdom up, and only then speak of prescribing. This stroke shows the clinical character of the system best of all: the medicine must match a patient who can receive it, and one who cannot receive it must first have the constitution restored.

What the whole matching addresses is the illness of "the currently-dominant defilement," and what it prescribes is the *antidote* to that defilement — not the "thing one finds agreeable" that flatters the person's preference. This differs from the approach the series took to the five hindrances in P08. P08's antidote was "jhāna-factor against hindrance" (a positive concentration-factor suppressing a negative hindrance); the antidote here is "object against temperament" (a fitting medicine treating the dominant illness). The two cut along different axes and are not to be run together.

### 3.2 Suitable for All: The Broad-Spectrum Remedies in the Cabinet

The matching is not a rigid one-to-one. The treatise-master notes that some objects are broad-spectrum, "suitable for all temperaments":

> "Among the thirty-eight subjects, according to what one delights in, recollection of death and defining the four elements are supreme … the six are reduced to three … the kasiṇas and counting the breath, growing through space, are without obstruction suitable for all temperaments."[^5]

Recollection of death and defining the four elements are, among the thirty-eight objects, "supreme" — usable by a person of any type; the kasiṇas and counting the breath are likewise "without obstruction suitable for all temperaments," clashing with no temperament. This means the matching system has two layers: a layer of "specific prescriptions" (a particular defilement matched to a particular medicine) and a layer of "broad-spectrum base remedies" (usable by anyone, hard to get wrong). Clinically, the broad-spectrum remedy is often the safe opening move — which echoes the lesson of the Vaggumudā river: when the strong medicine of foulness had brewed disaster, the mindfulness of breathing the Buddha turned to instead is just such a broadly suitable, subtle, hard-to-harm base remedy. It is the very object the Buddha called "the noble abiding, the divine abiding, the holy abiding, and even the blissful abiding of the adept here and now" — one in which everyone from the beginner to the adept can rest.[^15]

The matching is then crossed by a second dimension: the keenness or dullness of the faculties. Among greed-types, the one of dull faculty is suited to the contemplation of foulness (strong and direct), while the one of keen faculty can bear a finer practice such as mindfulness of the body; and the wholly faculty-less, dull-and-deluded person the treatise-master will not even "teach" — not abandoning them, but waiting, for the time is not yet, and wisdom must first be grown. So "which defilement dominates" (temperament) and "keen or dull faculty" (faculty) interweave into a two-dimensional prescription table: one and the same illness draws a heavier or lighter medicine according to the keenness or dullness of the person.

## IV. Not One Axis — *The Meditation-Objects as a Multi-Dimensional Matrix*

Temperament is only one axis along which the objects are sorted. The Vimuttimagga states that these thirty-eight objects are to be understood, in their excellence, from nine sides:

> "These thirty-eight meditation-subjects are best understood by nine dimensions: first, by jhāna; second, by transcendence; third, by extensibility; fourth, by yield-condition; fifth, by object-substance; sixth, by excellence; seventh, by plane; eighth, by mode of apprehension; ninth, by person."[^6]

"By person" (matching according to temperament) is the climax of these nine dimensions, and the theme of the foregoing — but it is only one in nine. The remaining dimensions are *orthogonal* to "person," each sorting the same set of objects afresh from a different angle. Take three.

First, "by object-substance" sorts whether an object is a concept or something real. The treatise-master says: "What is object-substance? Twenty-one subjects have a concept for their object; twelve subjects have a real for their object; five subjects are to be called neither concept nor real."[^7] Some objects have for their field a conceptual designation (a concept); some have an ultimately real dharma (a real); and some fall on neither side. The same list of objects, seen from the angle "is the field a concept or a real?", at once divides into three classes — an axis that has nothing to do with depth, a sorting along another dimension entirely.

Second, "by excellence" sorts what each object is best at accomplishing. The treatise-master gives examples: the eight kasiṇas and the four formless attainments are strong at accomplishing *concentration*; the ten perceptions of foulness and the perception of repulsiveness in food are strong at accomplishing *perception*; the ten recollections are strong at *mindfulness*; defining the four elements is strong at *wisdom*.[^8] Objects equally able to lead a person into concentration, sorted by "which mental quality each is best at strengthening," yield yet another sorting.

Third, "by yield-condition" sorts what fruit an object leads toward. Some objects are a condition for the higher knowledges; some are a condition for insight (*vipassanā*) — a sorting by where the object is headed.

These three axes (together with the rest — plane, mode of apprehension, and the others) make one key point plain: two objects falling at the same jhāna-depth can still be sorted into different cells by "person," by "object-substance," by "excellence," by "yield-condition." Depth is only one axis among many. As for the depth-distribution of the objects, the channel by which the learning-sign and counterpart-sign are internalized, the linguistic seam between access and absorption, and the seam between the count of thirty-eight and forty — the preceding chapter, P05, *The Object-Structure of Absorption*, treated these in full; this chapter does not re-walk them, and hands back to P05 in this one sentence.

Stack these axes together, and the meditation-object system is not a single-line menu of "pick one you like," but a multi-dimensional matrix: each object is located at once along the person-axis, the substance-axis, the excellence-axis, the yield-axis, the jhāna-axis, and more. The matching can be precise precisely because the treatise-master holds a multi-dimensional sorting table, able to set a stone at the intersection of "this person, this illness, this faculty, this field, this fruit." A matrix, not a menu — this is the most distinctive achievement of the treatise literature's systematizing labor.

A note in passing: the other standard Southern manual, the Visuddhimagga, compresses temperament into a flat six types (greed, hate, delusion, faith, intelligence, the speculative), whereas the Vimuttimagga enumerates fourteen and then reduces three times over. One person-axis, two treatises — one spare, one full: this is the fingerprint of two distinct recensions, not a doctrinal contradiction — one inclined to compress into a table, the other to enumerate into a list. (The Visuddhimagga is cited here only in paraphrase, never verbatim; its temperament-grid, matching-table, and count of six types are presented through restatement of the sense and cross-comparison.)

## V. South and North Side by Side — *The Temperament-Grid and the Two Gateways*

Move the view from the Southern tradition to the Northern, and one finds another tradition arriving independently at the same insight. The most stable matching-unit in Northern Abhidharma is the "two gateways to the deathless." The *Abhidharmakośa* announces it in a verse and then expounds it in prose:

> "The two essential gateways for entering cultivation: the contemplation of foulness and mindfulness of breathing; one in whom greed or discursive thought predominates should cultivate them respectively, in this order. Treatise: there are two essential gateways for rightly entering cultivation — first, the contemplation of foulness; second, mindfulness of breathing … such a being is called a greed-temperament practitioner, and by contemplating foulness rightly enters cultivation. One whose much discursive thought scatters the mind is called a discursive-temperament practitioner, and by mindfulness of breathing rightly enters cultivation."[^9]

The greed-type cultivates the contemplation of foulness, the discursive-type cultivates mindfulness of breathing — "two gateways to the deathless," meaning these two gates are like the deathless nectar (*amṛta*), the two essential portals for entering the path of cultivation. The *Mahāvibhāṣā* likewise sets up these two: "Again, two kinds of contemplation of form are, for entering the Buddha-Dharma, gateways to the deathless — namely, the contemplation of foulness and mindfulness of breathing."[^10]

The North does not stop at giving the matching; it supplies the rationale for the antidote. The *Kośa* explains that mindfulness of breathing has "few objects" and "turns at the inner door" — its object is simple and inward-drawing — and so it can still the scattered, reaching discursive thought; the contemplation of foulness has "many objects" and "turns at the outer door" — its object is manifold and outward-running — and so it cannot treat the scatter of discursive thought; conversely, just because the contemplation of foulness reaches outward, grasping the various signs of foulness, it matches the outward-grasping greed of the greedy mind.[^11] This explanation of the mechanism — *why this medicine matches this illness* — is the distinguishing mark of the Northern matching-theory.

More striking still, the framing language of the North differs from the South's. The *Mahāvibhāṣā* marks the two gateways by the analysis of dharmas: what the contemplation of foulness contemplates is *derived matter* (the form-dharmas that are produced), while what mindfulness of breathing contemplates is the *primary elements* (the great elements that produce).[^12] The North frames the object by the dharma-categories of "primary elements / derived matter"; the South frames it by the person-axis of "temperament." One and the same pair of matchings (foulness/breath against greed/discursive thought), framed by one tradition through dharma-analysis and by the other through the person — an exact contrast.

Did the North, then, sort practitioners into five types matched neatly against five gateways? Here one must be careful. The *Mahāvibhāṣā* does mention several types of practitioner, but explicitly states only three: "Again, if a greed-temperament practitioner comes and sits in the assembly, he hears the Buddha teach the meaning of the contemplation of foulness. If a hate-temperament practitioner comes and sits in the assembly, he hears the Buddha teach the meaning of the contemplation of loving-kindness. If a delusion-temperament practitioner comes and sits in the assembly, he hears the Buddha teach the meaning of the contemplation of dependent origination. The conceit-temperament and the rest are to be understood by analogy."[^13] Greed matched to foulness, hate to loving-kindness, delusion to dependent origination — three types explicitly stated; conceit and the rest are passed over with the single phrase "understood by analogy," and no neat five-gateway scheme is set out here at all.

The "five gateways for stilling the mind" so familiar in later Chinese Buddhism — the contemplation of foulness, of loving-kindness, of dependent origination, of the analysis of the elements, and counting the breath, neatly matched against greed, hate, delusion, conceit, and scatter — this tidy five-against-five table is in fact *not found* in the original wording of the *Mahāvibhāṣā* or the *Kośa*. It is the mature synthesis of a later, Tiantai- and Cien-era line. To read it back into the Vibhāṣā and the Kośa as an "the treatise says: five gateways against five temperaments" original doctrine is an inverted misreading. The stable Northern unit is the two gateways to the deathless (two against two); the Vibhāṣā adds three explicitly stated types; the "five gateways" is the later Chinese arrangement and completion of these materials (supplying, for instance, the analysis of the elements to match conceit) — and each must be returned to its proper place.

A last word is owed. This chapter sets the Southern temperament-grid and the Northern two-gateways side by side and compares them, but this comparison is the chapter's own synthetic work, not a unified statement ready-made in any single treatise. There is no treatise in the world that says "concentration has a Southern and a Northern method of matching to the person." The Southern "by-person" is from the Vimuttimagga; the Northern two-gateways are from the *Kośa* and the *Mahāvibhāṣā*; each has its own source. The chapter sets them side by side to show that two traditions arrived, independently, at the same structural insight — *matching the object to the currently-dominant defilement*. Set side by side and compared, not ranked, with no judgment of higher and lower: this is the consistent discipline the series keeps in handling material from more than one tradition.

## VI. Modern Dialogue and Conclusion

Three scholars' work is worth being in dialogue with as this system is set into the coordinates of modern research.

P. V. Bapat's comparative study of the *Vimuttimagga* and the *Visuddhimagga* is the foundation for the Southern-internal comparison. He set the two meditation manuals against each other item by item, recording the structural divergences — the six versus fourteen temperaments, the thirty-eight versus forty objects — and read both as two recensions of one tradition (the *Vimuttimagga* the *Path of Freedom* preserved in Chinese, the *Visuddhimagga* the standard text of the Mahāvihāra). This chapter's footing — "two Southern recensions set side by side, so the divergence is data, not error" — rests precisely on his philological work. Bapat's project, however, is textual-comparative (who said what, where they differ); this chapter's is doctrinal-functional (why the matching system is as it is — the counteraction of defilement). For this chapter, those numerical divergences are just what shows that the stable core is the *logic of the matching*, not any exact list. And his account is confined to the two Southern texts; the Northern arm — the two gateways — lies outside its frame. (Specific pages and edition await verification.)

Sarah Shaw's work on Buddhist meditation reads the objects and the temperament-matching as a pastoral, pedagogical system: the object is something a teacher *gives* to an individual student, and the forty objects are a teacher's repertoire of methods, not a self-service menu the student draws from. Her emphasis on the relational, pedagogical dimension is this chapter's strongest ally — it is the very opposite of the app's self-administered quiz. But this chapter must hold the doctrinal-structural side: the matching is finally governed by the analysis of defilement (which defilement dominates), not by a teacher's individual intuition alone. An over-pastoral reading slides, without noticing, back into the very "find what suits you" frame this chapter criticizes; so the chapter accepts the relational character of the matching with one hand and re-anchors it in the analysis of defilement with the other — what is matched is the antidote aimed at a defilement, not an expedient for building therapeutic rapport. (Specific pages and edition await verification.)

K. L. Dhammajoti's systematic study of Sarvāstivāda Abhidharma gives a clear English ordering of the Northern two gateways and the logic of "object-as-antidote." Without such a Northern scholarly voice, this chapter's side-by-side comparison would have only the Southern half and would lose the Northern arm. But it should be said that what he orders is a *description* of the Northern system; this chapter's contribution is the cross-traditional synthesis — the observation that South and North arrived independently at the one insight, *matching the object to the dominant defilement* — and that synthesis is the chapter's own, built on his work rather than framed by it. (Specific pages and edition await verification.)

Return to the inversion of the opening. Modern personalized practice takes self-expression for its premise: the type is an identity to be affirmed, and choosing a method is like picking an agreeable product. The temperament system in the treatise literature understood "matching to the person" more than a thousand years before this line of thought — but the "person" it matched to is "which defilement dominates," not "who you are"; and what it matched is the antidote aimed at that poison, not the product that flatters the customer. This is the clinching of the two redlines: temperament is not a personality type for self-diagnosis and self-identification, and the matching is not a preference-procedure for the picking — it is the one fitting medicine the physician takes from the cabinet, against the patient's presenting illness right now.

**A précis.** The temperament-matching of the forty meditation-objects is not an ancient personality quiz of "find your type, choose what you love," but a diagnostic-prescriptive system of "identify the currently-dominant defilement and prescribe the object that counteracts it." What is matched is not a preference that flatters the self, but a remedy that treats the defilement. And temperament is only one of several axes along which the objects are sorted: the treatise-master himself says they are understood "by nine dimensions," so the objects form a multi-dimensional matrix, not a single-line menu. The two traditions — the temperament-grid and the two gateways — arrived independently at the same insight, set side by side and compared, not ranked.

**A word for practice.** The real reminder of this system is not "which type am I?" but a plainer question: *right now, which defilement is in command in me?* The order of prescribing follows from that — a strong medicine (such as the contemplation of foulness) must match a patient who can receive it, while the broad-spectrum base remedies (counting the breath, recollection of death, defining the four elements) are the safe, hard-to-harm opening move. Match the remedy to the illness, and when the medicine takes, the gate of concentration opens.

**An open question.** The matching settles "which object to cultivate"; but once the object is fixed and the mind has entered concentration, how does the inside of concentration work — how do applied and sustained thought come to rest, how do tranquillity and equanimity arise? That is the internal anatomy of the jhāna-factors, left to the next chapter. And even when the medicine is matched and the dominant defilement suppressed, "suppression" is in the end not "severance": temperament reduces to the three poisons, and the final severing of the three poisons awaits the direct seeing of wisdom — a thread this volume will draw closed in its last chapter. The treatise literature's systematizing labor has done the person-axis in this chapter, will do the jhāna-factor axis in the next, and the absorption-nature axis after that; the outline once spread, the entries can be counted off one by one.

[^4]: *Vimuttimagga*, fascicle 3, "Distinguishing Meditation-Subjects," chapter 7, "By Person": "The greed-temperament should cultivate the perception of foulness and mindfulness of the body, for this counteracts greed. The hate-temperament should cultivate the four immeasurables, for this counteracts hate; or should cultivate the colour-kasiṇa, since the mind follows after it. The faith-temperament should cultivate the six recollections, with recollection of the Buddha foremost, for the sake of the concentration that faith first finds. The intelligence-temperament should cultivate defining the four elements, the perception of repulsiveness in food, recollection of death, and recollection of peace, for these are deep. Further, the intelligence-temperament meets no obstacle in any object whatever. The speculative-temperament should cultivate counting the breath, in order to cut off discursive thought. The delusion-temperament should, by asking after the Dharma in words, hearing the Dharma in season, and dwelling with a teacher in reverence for the Dharma, cause wisdom to grow." Taishō vol. 32, No. T1648. The mapping of "intelligence-temperament" to wisdom (*buddhi*) and "speculative-temperament" to discursive thought (*vitakka*) is established backwards by the objects matched (the intelligence-type matched to deep wisdom-objects, the speculative-type matched to counting the breath in order to cut off *vitakka*), not by the surface of the Chinese characters.

[^5]: *Vimuttimagga*, fascicle 3, "Distinguishing Meditation-Subjects," chapter 7, "among the thirty-eight subjects, according to what one delights in, recollection of death and defining the four elements are supreme … the six persons are, in what is distinguished, abridged to three … the kasiṇas and counting the breath, growing through space, are without obstruction suitable for all temperaments," Taishō vol. 32, No. T1648.

[^6]: *Vimuttimagga*, fascicle 3, "these thirty-eight meditation-subjects are best understood by nine dimensions: first, by jhāna; second, by transcendence; third, by extensibility; fourth, by yield-condition; fifth, by object-substance; sixth, by excellence; seventh, by plane; eighth, by mode of apprehension; ninth, by person," Taishō vol. 32, No. T1648.

[^7]: *Vimuttimagga*, fascicle 3, "What is object-substance? Answer: twenty-one subjects have a concept for their object; twelve subjects have a real for their object; five subjects are to be called neither concept nor real," Taishō vol. 32, No. T1648.

[^8]: *Vimuttimagga*, fascicle 3, "What is excellence? … the eight kasiṇas and the four formless attainments … are called excellence in concentration … the ten perceptions of foulness and the perception of repulsiveness in food are called excellence in perception … the ten recollections are called excellence in mindfulness … defining the four elements is called excellence in wisdom," Taishō vol. 32, No. T1648.

[^9]: *Abhidharmakośa-bhāṣya*, fascicle 22, "the two essential gateways for entering cultivation: the contemplation of foulness and mindfulness of breathing; one in whom greed or discursive thought predominates should cultivate them respectively, in this order. Treatise: there are two essential gateways for rightly entering cultivation — first, the contemplation of foulness; second, mindfulness of breathing … such a being is called a greed-temperament practitioner, and by contemplating foulness rightly enters cultivation. One whose much discursive thought scatters the mind is called a discursive-temperament practitioner, and by mindfulness of breathing rightly enters cultivation," composed by Vasubandhu, translated by Xuanzang, Taishō vol. 29, No. T1558.

[^10]: *Abhidharma-mahāvibhāṣā-śāstra*, fascicle 74, "Again, two kinds of contemplation of form are, for entering the Buddha-Dharma, gateways to the deathless — namely, the contemplation of foulness and mindfulness of breathing," translated by Xuanzang, Taishō vol. 27, No. T1545.

[^11]: *Abhidharmakośa-bhāṣya*, fascicle 22, the rationale that mindfulness of breathing has "few objects" and "turns at the inner door" and so stills scattered discursive thought, while the contemplation of foulness has "many objects" and "turns at the outer door," with the closing "by these two gateways the mind comes to be concentrated," Taishō vol. 29, No. T1558.

[^12]: *Abhidharma-mahāvibhāṣā-śāstra*, fascicle 127, "the true gateway to the deathless … the contemplation of foulness contemplates derived matter; mindfulness of breathing contemplates the primary elements," Taishō vol. 27, No. T1545.

[^13]: *Abhidharma-mahāvibhāṣā-śāstra*, fascicle 79, "Again, if a greed-temperament practitioner comes and sits in the assembly, he hears the Buddha teach the meaning of the contemplation of foulness. If a hate-temperament practitioner comes and sits in the assembly, he hears the Buddha teach the meaning of the contemplation of loving-kindness. If a delusion-temperament practitioner comes and sits in the assembly, he hears the Buddha teach the meaning of the contemplation of dependent origination. The conceit-temperament and the rest are to be understood by analogy," translated by Xuanzang, Taishō vol. 27, No. T1545. Here only three types — greed, hate, delusion — are matched explicitly; conceit, the discursive, and the rest are not. The neat five-against-five of the later "five gateways for stilling the mind" is the mature synthesis of the Tiantai and Cien lines, not an original doctrine fixed in the Vibhāṣā or the Kośa.

[^14]: For the canonical kind of this pairing, see *Saṃyukta Āgama*, fascicle 29, discourse 815, "cultivate the contemplation of foulness to cut off greed; cultivate loving-kindness to cut off hate; cultivate the perception of impermanence to cut off conceit; cultivate mindfulness of breathing to cut off discursive thought," Taishō vol. 2, No. T0099. The treatise-masters' temperament-matching system may be seen as a systematizing upgrade of these object-and-defilement pairings scattered through the discourses.

[^15]: For the broad suitability of mindfulness of breathing, see *Saṃyukta Āgama*, fascicle 29, discourse 807, "mindfulness of breathing is the noble abiding, the divine abiding, the holy abiding, and even the blissful abiding of the adept here and now," Taishō vol. 2, No. T0099.
