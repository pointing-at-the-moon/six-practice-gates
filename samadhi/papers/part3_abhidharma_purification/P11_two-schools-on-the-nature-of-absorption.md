# Two Schools on the Nature of Absorption
***Is Concentration a Thing, or a Name? Sarvāstivāda and Theravāda on the Own-Substance of Samādhi***

*中文主題：定體——兩個學派論定之自性*

*Two Schools on the Nature of Absorption*

**Abstract**

Concentration is not a checklist of real items one cultivates and then keeps, loses, and re-enters; it is not three different practices — relished, pure, undefiled; and it is not a tribunal that rules which tradition's meditation counts as real. This chapter asks one thing only: when you set concentration (定 / *samādhi*) on the ontological scale, does it register a weight of its own — is it, in the end, a thing with an own-substance? The Āgamas long ago called concentration a *faculty* (the concentration-faculty is samādhi, is the four jhānas) but never pressed the further question, "what is concentration *in itself*." That question is the scalpel the Abhidharma laid where the suttas left off. The title says "two schools," but the truth is that the Sarvāstivāda and the Theravāda stand on the same side of the realist line: the Sarvāstivāda rules concentration to be one-pointedness of mind, a universally-present mental factor (*mahābhūmika*) found in every mind-moment, a really-existent separate dharma (*dravya*) that perdures across the three times; the Theravāda rules one-pointedness (*cittass'ekaggatā*) an ultimate real (*paramattha-dhamma*), a momentarily arising-and-ceasing mental factor that nonetheless has its own nature (*sabhāva*). The two answer one question on different chassis. What genuinely unsettles this consensus is a third voice — the Sautrāntika (Dārṣṭāntika), recorded by Vasubandhu in his own *Kośa*: the so-called concentration is no more than "the state of the mind being fixed on a single object … there need be no further, separate mental factor … its substance just is the mind." Vasubandhu sets it down and half-grants its reasoning without making it his own ("though this reasoning has merit, it is not the tenet of my school"). The fault-line therefore runs *through the Kośa itself*. The chapter unfolds along three axes — the dispute over the substance of absorption; the discrimination of the three terms *samādhi / samāpatti / samāhita*; and the classification of absorption as relished, pure, or undefiled — read throughout against the Sautrāntika's subtraction and the Theravāda's momentary real, and so seals the three axes of Part III: the axis of the person, the axis of the jhāna-factor, the axis of the nature of absorption.

**Keywords:** the substance of absorption (定體), one-pointedness of mind (心一境性 / *cittaikāgratā* / Pāli *cittass'ekaggatā*), samādhi / concentration (三摩地), a universally-present mental factor (大地法 / *mahābhūmika*), the composed state (等引 / *samāhita*), attainment (等至 / *samāpatti*), relished / pure / undefiled absorption (味淨無漏 / *āsvādana / śuddhaka / anāsrava*), really-existent separate dharma (*dravyasat*), own-nature (自性 / *svabhāva*), ultimate real (*paramattha-dhamma*), momentary (*khaṇika*), the Sautrāntika (經部 / Dārṣṭāntika), the Sarvāstivāda (有部), the Theravāda (上座部), the ontological scale, Abhidharma

## I. The Question: The Āgamas Name Concentration a Faculty but Never Ask What It Is

The Āgamas knew very well how to speak of concentration. They list it among the five faculties, set it as the eighth limb of the noble eightfold path, and tell you without hedging what it contains:

> "What is the concentration-faculty? The samādhi that arises in one who has first given rise to the mind set on awakening in the Tathāgata — this is called the concentration-faculty."[^1]

The concentration-faculty is samādhi. Elsewhere the content of that one faculty is opened out directly into the four jhānas:

> "What is the concentration-faculty? If a monk, secluded from sensual desires, secluded from unwholesome states, with awareness and with examining, with rapture-and-bliss born of seclusion … on up to the fourth jhāna, enters and abides — this is called the concentration-faculty."[^2]

So in the suttas "concentration" is clear enough: it is a capacity, a path-limb, a course one can enter by leaving sensual desire behind and deepen layer by layer. But notice what the Āgamas do say and what, throughout, they never say. They tell you what concentration *does* (it settles the mind, turns it toward good destinations, exhausts suffering); what it *looks like* (the falling-away sequence of the four jhānas); what it is *for* (knowing and seeing as things really are). What they never ask is whether concentration is *itself a thing* — whether it has its own substance, distinct from the mind. Is it one and the same item that, once cultivated, can be held in possession, lost, and re-entered? Or is it a state that has to be freshly produced moment after moment? That question the suttas do not raise.

The one who pried it open was the Abhidharma; what this chapter tracks is precisely what happened after that scalpel went in. Three boundaries must be drawn first, or the whole discussion will list to one side.

First, **concentration is not a checklist.** To treat it as a set of capacities one acquires item by item, pockets, and keeps in long-term possession is to misread the Sarvāstivāda doctrine of "one dharma in five roles" (which comes below) as a kind of warehousing.

Second, **concentration is not three practices.** The relished, pure, and undefiled absorptions met below are three *states* one and the same absorption may be in — defiled by craving, a pure mundane good, or undefiled with the noble path — not three roads one may choose to walk.

Third, **concentration is not a tribunal.** To collate two traditions, and a third voice, is to see clearly how each answers the ontological question "does concentration have an own-substance," not to rule which tradition's meditation is the orthodox article. This is diagnosis, not verdict.

One inherited line must be set in place at once: **concentration is not mindfulness.** One-pointedness of mind, *ekaggatā* — both traditions classify it as a mental factor distinct from mindfulness (*sati*). Mindfulness is the function of clear, unforgetting registration; concentration is the function of the mind's being one upon its object. Conflate the two and every later step warps.[^3]

As to its place in Part III: the first chapter (the axis of the person) asked "who cultivates concentration," the second (the axis of the jhāna-factor) asked "of what constituents is concentration built, and how do they fall away layer by layer." That second chapter, at its close, said plainly that what it had not yet touched was the substance of absorption itself — whether concentration is nothing but one-pointedness, how the relished / pure / undefiled triad divides, where the difference among the composed, the attained, and the held-in-concentration lies. This chapter takes up exactly that, and seals the three axes.

## II. The Common Ground: The Sutta Seeds, and a Question the Suttas Never Asked

Neither tradition built its ontology out of thin air; both raised it on language the Āgamas had already supplied.

The concentration-faculty is the four jhānas, is samādhi (quoted above); so too right concentration. The *Madhyama Āgama* glosses right concentration with "secluded from sensual desires, secluded from unwholesome states … he enters and abides in the fourth jhāna," and takes "the mind made one" as its core verb. The *Saṃyukta Āgama* goes further and cuts right concentration into two layers:

> "Right concentration is of two kinds. There is a right concentration that is worldly and ordinary, with taints, with grasping, turning toward good destinations; and there is a right concentration that is noble and supramundane, untainted, without grasping, rightly exhausting suffering … if the mind dwells unscattered, unmoving, gathered in, stilled — samādhi, the mind one — this is called right concentration."[^4]

Two things here are worth keeping. First, "the mind made one," "the mind one," "the mind dwells unscattered" — these are the very seeds of the later Abhidharma term *cittaikāgratā*, one-pointedness of mind; the suttas already say the mind is "one," and the treatises only cast that into a technical term. Second, the suttas already divide tainted from untainted right concentration; this taint / no-taint axis has a canonical warrant, and it will grow, in §V, into one limb of the relished / pure / undefiled triad.

But this is exactly where the common ground ends. The suttas gave "the concentration-faculty is the four jhānas," gave "the mind made one," gave "tainted and untainted"; what they did not give is whether concentration has an own-substance. They never take concentration as an *object* and ask whether, under ultimate analysis, anything of it is left over — whether it is a real dharma or merely a phase of the mind. To put it in the one sentence this chapter most wants kept: **the Sarvāstivāda realism about the substance of absorption, and the Theravāda thesis that *ekaggatā* is an ultimate real, are both answers to a question the Āgamas never posed.** The two answers are not the same; but the question they answer is one and the same sutta-unasked question. It is precisely these two answers — and a third — to "what is concentration" that this chapter sets side by side.

(The internal mechanics of the jhāna-factors — how applied and sustained thought come to rest, how the intermediate dhyāna is set up — belong to the preceding chapter; so does the construction of the four-jhāna ladder itself. Here only their conclusion is taken as floor, not re-walked.)

## III. Axis One — The Dispute over the Substance of Absorption: Is One-Pointedness a Separate Real Dharma?

This is the chapter with the strongest mechanism of the three. The question is plain: is concentration — that "being one" by which the mind is fixed on a single object — a dharma distinct from the mind, one that stands on its own feet?

### The Sarvāstivāda answer: yes, and it is a universally-present factor

The Sarvāstivāda definition makes concentration, from the outset, a dharma with an own-nature:

> "As to the substance of meditative absorption (*dhyāna*), spoken of in sum: it is, on the side of the wholesome, one-pointedness of mind, for its own-nature is wholesome samādhi; if taken together with its helpers, it has the five aggregates for its nature. What is one-pointedness? It means being fixed on a single object."[^5]

The foot-treatises of the Abhidharma speak with one voice. The *Prakaraṇapāda* gives the tersest line: "What is concentration? One-pointedness of mind."[^6] And it lays this out into a full chain of synonyms: "It makes the mind dwell, dwell evenly, dwell settled, dwell close, dwell firm — unscattered, undistracted, gathered, held-in-samādhi, one-pointed; this is called concentration."[^7] The *Mahāvibhāṣā*, for its part, separates this one factor cleanly from the whole event of absorption: "One-pointedness of mind is dhyāna, for samādhi is its own-nature" — the event of dhyāna has the five aggregates for its substance, while the *factor* within it called "concentration" has, for its own-nature, that one mental factor: samādhi, one-pointedness of mind.[^8]

The Sarvāstivāda then pushes this really-existent factor onto a decisive footing: it is a **universally-present factor** (*mahābhūmika*), present in every single mind-moment.

> "Moreover, samādhi is a universally-present factor; it should be that in every mind there is a turning toward one object."[^9]

The price of this line is immediately visible: if samādhi is in every mind, then even a scattered mind — even this very mind-moment of yours, restless as a monkey — has a portion of samādhi in it. The *Mahāvibhāṣā* says exactly that: "even the scattered mind has that samādhi"; in a distracted mind, the faint continuity within it is samādhi too, only so weak you do not notice it.[^10] The Sarvāstivāda accepts the inference: the samādhi of non-concentrated states is merely *weak*, not absent.[^11] This step is crucial: it means that "concentration" is not something present only on entering absorption and gone on leaving it; it is a real dharma always on the scene, differing only in strength. Set it on the ontological scale, and the scale really does carry one extra item with a weight of its own.

### The engineering proof: one dharma in five roles

How does the Sarvāstivāda stabilize this real dharma and fit it into the whole doctrinal frame? The single clearest piece of evidence is in the *Dharmaskandha*:

> "All the mind's dwelling, dwelling evenly … up to one-pointedness of mind, associated with undefiled attention, is together named concentration; it is also named the concentration-faculty, also the concentration-power, also the concentration awakening-factor, also right concentration — the noble, supramundane, untainted, ungrasping path that runs with the path, is co-existent with the path, turns with the path."[^12]

One own-substance — one-pointedness of mind — threaded onto four doctrinal roles: among the five faculties it is the concentration-faculty; among the five powers, the concentration-power; among the seven awakening-factors, the concentration awakening-factor; among the eight path-limbs, right concentration. The *Mahāvibhāṣā* puts it more bluntly still: "yet samādhi may be spoken of as one … among the five faculties it is named the concentration-faculty, among the five powers the concentration-power, among the seven awakening-factors the concentration awakening-factor, among the eight path-limbs right concentration."[^13] Count carefully: this is not five independent items but **one substance of concentration given a separate name under each of the four classes** — faculty, power, awakening-factor, path-limb. One thing, many names — this is the clearest textual fingerprint of *reifying* concentration into a stable entity. The scattered "concentration"-words of the various path-set lists in the suttas are, in the treatises' hands, gathered back into one and the same it.

### The third voice: the Sautrāntika says the scale carries nothing extra

By this point the Sarvāstivāda and the Theravāda are in fact already on the same side — the next section develops the Theravāda, but both set concentration up as a really-existent dharma with an own-nature. What genuinely unsettles that consensus is a third voice, and it surfaces in the most unexpected of places: inside the *Kośa*'s own chapter on concentration.

Vasubandhu first states the Sarvāstivāda position (the "its own-nature is wholesome samādhi" definition quoted above), and then immediately records the counter:

> "If that is so, then it is just the state of the mind being fixed on a single object that the name 'samādhi' is set up upon; there need be no further, separate mental factor. [To claim] a separate dharma that makes the mind turn upon a single object and call *that* samādhi — [no]: its substance just is the mind."[^14]

This is the Sautrāntika (Dārṣṭāntika) voice. It says: what you call "concentration" is nothing but *the state of the mind being concentrated on a single object*; you need not erect, over and above the mind, a further separate dharma named "samādhi" to explain it. The mind's being one upon its object — that fact is itself concentration; concentration has no separate substance; its substance just is the mind. Set concentration on the scale, and the Sautrāntika says: what you are weighing is only this un-scattered mind itself; there is no extra counterweight named "concentration" on the pan.

More telling still is Vasubandhu's own posture. He records this not once; in treating inner clarity (*sampasāda*), samādhi, and applied-and-sustained thought, he pushes the same subtraction wider:

> "Some masters hold: this inner clarity, this samādhi, this applied-and-sustained thought, all have no separate substance. — If they had no separate substance, they could not be set up as mental factors. [Reply:] even a difference of phase in the mind may be named a mental factor. Though this reasoning has merit, it is not the tenet of my school."[^15]

"Though this reasoning has merit, it is not the tenet of my school" — these words are the pivot of this chapter. Vasubandhu concedes that the "no separate substance" account has merit (*though this reasoning has merit*), and plainly says it is not the position he holds (*it is not the tenet of my school*). This is record-plus-respect, not endorsement. The honest reading: the *Kośa* is a commentary on the Vaibhāṣika, from the hand of an editor sympathetic to the Sautrāntika; it marks the tension out without resolving it for you. **So one must never take "concentration has no separate substance" as Vasubandhu's settled doctrine**; it is the counter-view he sets down with his own hand, half-grants, and yet finally declines to make his school's. The fault-line is not outside the *Kośa*; it runs through the *Kośa* itself.

### The orthodox counterstroke: Saṃghabhadra holds the separate substance

The one who hardens the Sarvāstivāda orthodoxy and braces explicitly against that editorial lean is Saṃghabhadra, in the *Nyāyānusāra*. His argument has three layers, each worth seeing in turn, for each answers the Sautrāntika subtraction head-on.

First, a functional division: settling the mind and cognizing are two different operations, and so require two different substances.

> "The function of samādhi is that it can settle the mind; cognizing an object is the function of consciousness … Moreover, the scriptures attest that samādhi really has a separate substance, as a sutta says: two things are to be cultivated, namely calm (*śamatha*) and insight (*vipaśyanā*). If one denied any real samādhi, one would contradict these and countless suttas."[^16]

"Settling the mind" is concentration's function; "cognizing an object" is the mind's (consciousness's) function; functions differing, the substances should not be one. And he calls the Āgamas to witness: the suttas plainly say calm and insight are *both* to be cultivated — if concentration had no real separate substance, why would the suttas rank it alongside calm-and-insight as a thing to be cultivated?

Second, a slippery-slope reductio: if concentration just is the mind, then feeling, perception, and volition should also just be the mind.

> "Moreover, if the substance of samādhi just is the mind, then perception and the rest should likewise have no separate nature … the mind's receiving agreeable and disagreeable objects would be called feeling; the mind's fashioning wholesome and unwholesome deeds would be called volition. Then there is mind alone, and there should be no [other] three [aggregates of mental factors]."[^17]

If you grant that "concentration is no more than a phase of the mind," the same subtraction can swallow feeling, perception, and volition — until only a mind is left and every mental factor is cancelled. The cost, Saṃghabhadra holds, is too high.

Third, an argument from degree: association admits no more-and-less, whereas samādhi admits more-and-less; therefore the two cannot be one and the same thing.

> "Mind and mental factors are of the nature of association, not the nature of being-held-in-samādhi. Why? Because in every state their association is without more or less, whereas the nature of samādhi has more and less across the states."[^18]

The "association" of mind and mental factors is the same in every state, with no question of deeper or shallower; but the "holding" of concentration plainly has degrees — the concentration of the first jhāna is shallower than that of the fourth. Since concentration admits of more-and-less while association does not, concentration cannot be merely another name for "the mind being in association"; it has to be a separate dharma with a more-and-less of its own.

The Chinese world holds one further echo of the third voice. Harivarman's *Satyasiddhi-śāstra* — a treatise of relatively even hand, which stages both a no-mental-factors chapter and an establishing-mental-factors chapter — on the side it there argues, comes close to the Sautrāntika:

> "Therefore know: there is only mind, and no separate mental factors. … I do not say there are no mental factors; I say only that, because they are differentiations of the mind, they are named mental factors."[^19]

Citing this side calls for honesty: the *Satyasiddhi* stages both sides, and what is taken here is its "only-mind, nothing-separate" face, not the whole of its conclusion.

By this point the three answers to "does concentration have an own-substance" are complete: the Sarvāstivāda says yes, and a really-existent separate dharma present in every mind at that; the Sautrāntika says no, what you weigh is only the un-scattered mind itself; and Vasubandhu records the latter, half-grants its reasoning, yet declines it as his tenet. This chapter, on the volume's footing, holds a conversation with the Sautrāntika without adopting it — for the whole concentration sequence is unfolded on the side that takes concentration to have an *analyzable factor-structure*; we let the Sautrāntika subtraction be present, speak, and press, but do not let it serve as the final verdict.

## IV. Axis Two — Samādhi, Samāpatti, Samāhita: Three Terms, Not One Circle

This is the sharpest single stroke of the three axes, and it is purely philological. Chinese translators used several near-synonymous Sanskrit terms to render concepts around "concentration," and Western renderings then flatten them all back into *samādhi*; the result is that three terms of different extension get pressed into one. The *Kośa* itself, by a small chapter-boundary, proves they are not synonyms.

The most direct evidence is this chapter-turn:

> "The attainments (*samāpatti*) have now been treated. What is the holding-in-concentration (*samādhi*)?"[^20]

"We have finished treating the attainments. Now, what is concentration-holding?" — a treatise that took the two for synonyms would never break the chapter and ask afresh like this. The three terms are each at their widest along a different axis; take them one by one.

**Samādhi** (等持, "concentration-holding"; one-pointedness of mind) — this is that samādhi which is a universally-present factor, present even in a scattered mind (weak, not absent, as seen above). Along the axis of *which minds it pervades*, it is the widest.

**Samāpatti** (等至, the attainment one enters) — its peculiarity is that it **subsumes the two mindless attainments**. The attainment of non-perception and the attainment of cessation are *acittaka* (mindless): in them there is no mind, and so none of that mental factor one-pointedness; yet they are still counted as samāpatti.[^21] So along the axis of *which absorptive states it covers*, samāpatti is wider than samādhi: some attainments — those two mindless ones — contain no samādhi at all.

**Samāhita** (等引, the composed / evenly-led state) — the *Kośa* gives it no flat "*samāhita* means …" definition; its sense has to be reconstructed from usage. The treatise says the two mindless attainments are "produced from a mind that is composed" — produced by a mind in a prior composed state.[^22] Reconstructing from this: *samāhita* is the state, produced-by-leading, in which mind and mental factors run on level and continuous; it excludes the scattered mind. (Because the *Kośa*'s chapter on concentration gives no nominal definition, the sense of *samāhita* here is reconstructed from the verb-usage, not a verbatim line of the treatise.)

The *Mahāvibhāṣā* arranges the difference between samādhi and samāpatti into several paired contrasts, which confirm precisely that they are not co-extensive:

> "Question: what is the difference between samādhi and samāpatti? Answer. Some say: samādhi has one thing for its substance, samāpatti the five aggregates. Some say: samādhi is of one instant, samāpatti is continuous. Some say: every samādhi is a samāpatti; there are samāpattis that are not samādhi — namely the attainment of non-perception and the attainment of cessation."[^23]

Samādhi has one thing (that single mental factor) for its substance, samāpatti the five aggregates; samādhi can be spoken of in a single instant, samāpatti is continuous; and the most decisive line — "there are samāpattis that are not samādhi," namely the two mindless attainments of non-perception and of cessation: they are attainments, yet not samādhi (having no one-pointedness of mind).

How, then, do the three terms close? Each is widest along one axis; they overlap without coinciding — like three half-interlocking circles. Samādhi is widest along *which minds it reaches* (down to the scattered mind), samāpatti widest along *which absorptive states it covers* (down to the mindless attainments), samāhita the level state produced by leading. One line is worth adding: when the discussion is of the term *concentration* as such, samādhi and concentration are different in name, one in substance — "samādhi and concentration are different in name, one in substance … this too is named one-pointedness of mind"[^24]; the discrimination of the three terms does not cancel this core identity, it only returns each of the neighbouring terms — so often conflated with it — to its proper place.

Here one comparative break should be stated honestly. The Theravāda does **not** replicate this "holding / attainment / composed" architecture of three terms. What the southern tradition divides is *samādhi* (the mental factor), *samāpatti* (the attainment one dwells in), and *jhāna*; and, beyond that, the pair *appanā* and *upacāra* (absorption-concentration and access-concentration), a distinction for which the Sarvāstivāda has no counterpart. To force the southern vocabulary into the *Kośa*'s three-term grid would distort it. This section therefore cannot lay out a one-to-one comparison table — and that is exactly where the discipline of "set the two traditions side by side, do not force the seam" applies: in some places the comparison simply breaks, and marking honestly *where* it breaks is better than welding a false bridge across the gap.

## V. Axis Three — Relished, Pure, Undefiled: Absorption Classified by Defilement

This is the safest and most far-reaching of the three axes: it is pure Abhidharma ontological classification, touching neither the jhāna-factors nor the awakening-factors. The *Mahāvibhāṣā* sets it out cleanly:

> "The first dhyāna is of three kinds: relished-associated, pure, and undefiled. The relished-associated is the craving-associated. Craving can hold the mind in a streaming flow upon its object; because its character accords with concentration, it alone takes the name [relished]. … 'Pure' means the wholesome-with-taints. 'Undefiled' means the noble path."[^25]

Keep firmly in mind that this is the **defilement axis**. Relished, pure, and undefiled are not three methods one may choose to cultivate, but three moral-ontological states one and the same absorption may be in. The **relished absorption** is an absorption defiled by craving — you crave the very absorptive state you have entered, and so this absorption is "stuck fast" by craving. The **pure absorption** is a clean, mundane, wholesome absorption. The **undefiled absorption** is an absorption associated with the noble path. One and the same first jhāna may be relished, pure, or undefiled, depending on what mental factor accompanies it. The four dhyānas and the lower three formless attainments all admit these three; only the peak — neither-perception-nor-non-perception — having no noble path, admits only the relished and the pure.

The *Mahāvibhāṣā* fixes the ontological status of the relished absorption precisely:

> "Here the relished-associated dhyānas and formless attainments are all obstructive-yet-indeterminate. Because they can obstruct the noble path and its preparatory effort, they are called obstructive (*nivṛta*); because they do not summon a ripened fruit, they are called indeterminate (*avyākṛta*)."[^26]

The relished absorption is "obstructive-yet-indeterminate" (*nivṛta-avyākṛta*) — it can obstruct the noble path (hence "obstructive"), yet it summons no ripened karmic fruit (hence "indeterminate"). This is a *diagnostic* category: the tradition uses it to name the fault of "craving one's own meditative absorption." Read it as description, never as guide: there is no method here of "how to savour your absorption," only an ontological classification of "how the tradition names a particular fault within absorption."

One important qualification must be added. A rather affecting account — that "the object of the relished absorption is the pure absorption of the preceding instant" — must be handled with care. The *Mahāvibhāṣā* does record this account, but it is a **canvassed opinion ("some masters hold"), set aside by the verdict**:

> "Some masters hold: this means that immediately after the pure absorption there arises the relished-associated absorption, and the pure absorption is what is relished, being its object … The verdict: know that the first account is the better. For here, in entering and emerging, the discussion is framed throughout on the relished absorption itself."[^27]

"The verdict: know that the first account is the better" — the *Mahāvibhāṣā*'s ruling is that the earlier account is the right one. So if one wishes to use the affecting frame of "the relished absorption craving the prior pure absorption," one must flag it: that is an opinion the *Mahāvibhāṣā* records and the verdict sets aside, not the Sarvāstivāda's settled doctrine.

Finally, return to this axis's ontological yield: samādhi is one and the same substance of concentration, and the so-called "three kinds" of difference are set up by reference to the different things it counters — this is "one substance, many names" restated along another dimension:

> "Question: the substance of concentration is one only — namely, among the mental factors, samādhi. How are three kinds of difference set up? Answer: by the close countering of three obstructions. The emptiness-samādhi closely counters the view of a real self; the wishless-samādhi closely counters the clinging to rules-and-vows; the signless-samādhi closely counters doubt."[^28]

But here lies the chapter's highest risk of confusion, and it must be prised apart on the spot: **the *Mahāvibhāṣā* contains three orthogonal "triads of concentration," which must never be laid over one another**.

First, **relished / pure / undefiled** — the defilement axis (what moral state the absorption is in).

Second, **emptiness / signless / wishless** — the liberation-gate axis (by which mode of emptiness an undefiled absorption inclines toward release). Note: all three of these belong *to the undefiled*; the whole family lives inside the single "undefiled" cell of the relished / pure / undefiled triad. They are not parallel alternatives to relished / pure / undefiled, but a sub-division beneath it.

Third, **with-applied-and-sustained / with-sustained-alone / with-neither** — the applied-thought-stage axis (handled in the preceding chapter; flagged here only for its location[^29]).

The three axes are orthogonal, and whenever they appear together each must be labelled by its own axis. One point demands special vigilance: the Āgamas, too, speak of "three (or four) samādhis," but those belong to the *liberation-gate* family —

> "There is the boundless-mind samādhi, the signless-mind samādhi, the nothingness-mind samādhi, the emptiness-mind samādhi … What is the emptiness samādhi? It is when a noble disciple [contemplates that] the world is empty — contemplates the world as empty as it really is, abiding unchanging, not-self, not-mine; this is called the emptiness-mind samādhi."[^30]

The Āgamas' "three samādhis" (emptiness, signless, nothingness/boundless) are the liberation-gate family, subsumed under the exhaustion of greed, hatred, and delusion; they are **homonymous-but-not-identical with the Abhidharma's relished / pure / undefiled defilement triad, with zero overlap of members**. Holding to this is the lifeline that keeps the chapter from anachronism.

## VI. One Scale, Three Readings: Modern Interlocutors and the Close of Part III

Gather the whole chapter into one image: the ontological scale. Three houses each set the same "concentration" on the same scale and read off different answers to "is there anything there, and what is it." The Sarvāstivāda says there is indeed an item on the scale — a really-existent separate dharma, present in every mind, perduring across time — so that the concentration you cultivate can be held in possession, lost, and re-entered as one and the same it. The Sautrāntika says there is no extra item on the scale — what you weigh is only this un-scattered mind itself, and "concentration" is merely the name of its phase of being one.

And the Theravāda? This is the third reading held in reserve above and now to be supplied. The Theravāda, too, sets concentration on the scale, and it too reads off "something there": one-pointedness of mind (*ekaggatā*) is, in the southern Abhidhamma, an ultimate real (*paramattha-dhamma*), a really-existent mental factor, not a mere nominal designation. But its "something there" is not the Sarvāstivāda's: the Sarvāstivāda's is a substance that perdures across the three times (*dravya*), the Theravāda's a momentarily arising-and-ceasing (*khaṇika*) real that has its own nature (*sabhāva*) and lapses in the very instant. In other words, the weight on the scale, as the Theravāda reads it, falls away each instant and must be raised again instant by instant. **So one must never caricature the Theravāda as a nominalist** — it affirms that dharmas have own-natures, and is no flat anti-realism; its difference from the Sarvāstivāda lies not in "real versus name" but on the finer graduation of "perduring substance versus momentary real." (The southern account here is carried throughout by doctrinal paraphrase; this chapter quotes no Pāli Abhidhamma or commentarial text verbatim.[^31])

So the truth behind the title's "two schools" emerges: the Sarvāstivāda and the Theravāda are in fact allies — both set concentration up as a really-existent dharma with an own-nature, differing only in chassis (perduring substance versus momentary real). What unsettles that consensus is the third voice that surfaces inside the *Kośa*.

Western scholarship hands us three serviceable tools here. Collett Cox's reconstruction of how, and why, the Sarvāstivāda pushed these contested dharmas (concentration among them) from a *slot in a doctrinal list* all the way to *dravyasat*, a really-existent thing, remains unsurpassed for precision; her thesis "from category to ontology" is the very spine of this chapter's third section.[^32] But this chapter pushes her question one step further: Cox reads the dispute as an intra-Indian problem of existence; this chapter reads it as a *soteriological* one — for a meditator, the ruling on "is concentration a thing" bears directly on "is the concentration you cultivate one and the same item you can acquire, hold, lose, and re-enter, or a being-one that must be freshly produced instant by instant." The ontological ruling carries a contemplative consequence Cox does not pursue — but that is a diagnosis, not a manual of practice.

The southern arm rests on Y. Karunadasa's analysis of the *paramattha-dhamma* framework: dharmas are the irreducible reals reached by ultimate analysis, momentary, each with its own nature, yet wholly conditionally arisen; *ekaggatā* is just such a really-existent mental factor and not a perduring substance.[^33] He holds the line that the Theravāda is no nominalism. This chapter reads the southern momentary-and-own-natured account against the Sarvāstivāda substance-and-mental-factor account, making "the two schools" a structured contrast, and so raising the cross-tradition question that Karunadasa, treating one tradition alone, would not pose.[^34] As for the modern reconstruction of the Sarvāstivāda *caitta* architecture — why concentration is listed universally-present, how relished / pure / undefiled are systematically differentiated — K. L. Dhammajoti's northern survey can be consulted (the facet drawn on here is the mental-factor ontological status of concentration and the relished / pure / undefiled classification, distinct from the meditation-object taxonomy borrowed in the preceding chapter).[^35]

The thinness of the scholarship must be stated honestly: there is no sustained Western monograph taking, as its specific object, "the Sarvāstivāda and the Theravāda collated on the ontological status of *concentration*"; the question is reached only obliquely, through the dharma-theory of unmanifest matter and the mental factors (Cox, Dhammajoti), or through the two systems' general dharma-frameworks (Karunadasa). The two-school collation of the substance of absorption offered here is one small filling-in of that thin commentary.

Two red lines, to be hooked back in at the close. First, this chapter is diagnosis, not verdict: we see clearly how each house answers "does concentration have an own-substance," but rule nothing on whose meditation is the true article. Second, concentration is not mindfulness: the one-pointedness, the *ekaggatā*, treated throughout has been, from first to last, a mental factor distinct from mindfulness (*sati*) — a point both traditions share, marked plainly here once.

So Part III closes. The first chapter asked "who cultivates concentration" (the axis of the person); the second, "of what is concentration built" (the axis of the jhāna-factor); this one, "is concentration a thing" (the axis of the substance of absorption). Taken together, the three axes are the Abhidharma's complete dissection of a word — "concentration" — that the suttas had long used but never once taken up as an object of inquiry.

And the scalpel will be whetted once more. Part IV turns to the Mahāyāna: when the perfection of meditative absorption is taken over by the operator that is prajñā, the question "does concentration have an own-substance" will be re-framed — not by reverting to the Sautrāntika's "no separate substance" subtraction (the Sautrāntika is *not* Madhyamaka; never conflate the two), but by setting the whole scale, reading and all, inside emptiness and asking again. That is for later. This chapter only whets the ontological blade and leaves it here:

**Is absorption a thing, or a name?**
